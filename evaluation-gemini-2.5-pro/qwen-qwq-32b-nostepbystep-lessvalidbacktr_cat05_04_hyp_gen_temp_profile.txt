**Anomalies Identified in the Temporal Profile Model:**

Based on the provided temporal profile, the following anomalies related to average time intervals and standard deviations between activities are noted:

1.  **Receive (R) to Approve (P):** The average time is approximately 25 hours (90000 seconds), but the standard deviation is extremely low at 1 hour (3600 seconds). This indicates unusually high consistency in the time taken for this phase, suggesting a lack of natural process variation.
2.  **Approve (P) to Notify (N):** There is a very long average delay of 7 days (604800 seconds) between approval and customer notification. Furthermore, the standard deviation is high at 2 days (172800 seconds), indicating significant inconsistency and variability in this step's duration.
3.  **Assign (A) to Close (C):** The average time of 2 hours (7200 seconds) seems suspiciously short. Given the expected intermediate steps (Evaluate, Approve, Notify), closing a claim just 2 hours after assignment might suggest that these crucial steps are frequently bypassed.
4.  **Evaluate (E) to Notify (N):** An average time of only 5 minutes (300 seconds) between evaluation and notification is remarkably fast. This rapid transition might imply that the 'Approve' (P) step is often skipped or happens almost instantaneously with evaluation for certain claims.

**Hypotheses for Anomalies:**

Potential reasons for these observed anomalies include:

1.  **R-P (Low STDEV):**
    *   *Automation/Rigid Rules:* An automated system might be configured to advance claims from 'R' to 'P' after a fixed duration (e.g., 25 hours), eliminating natural processing time variations.
    *   *Data Entry Practices:* Timestamps for 'P' might be systematically recorded based on the 'R' timestamp plus a constant offset, rather than reflecting the actual event time.
    *   *Simplified Workflow:* A subset of very simple or specific claim types might follow a highly standardized, fast-tracked path to approval with minimal deviation.

2.  **P-N (Long Average, High STDEV):**
    *   *Notification Bottleneck:* A manual review, batch processing system for notifications, or resource constraints in the notification team could lead to significant and variable delays after approval.
    *   *Process Variability:* Different claim types, complexities, or required communication methods (e.g., physical mail vs. email) might have vastly different P-N timelines, contributing to the high standard deviation.
    *   *External Dependencies:* Waiting for external information or customer responses required before notification could introduce long and unpredictable delays.

3.  **A-C (Short Average):**
    *   *Expedited Closure Rules:* Business rules might allow immediate closure after assignment for certain clear-cut cases (e.g., obvious non-coverage, duplicate claims, minor value claims immediately settled/rejected).
    *   *Logging Errors:* Intermediate 'E' or 'P' events might not be logged consistently for all claims, making it appear as if closure happens directly after assignment.
    *   *Adjuster Discretion:* Adjusters might have the authority to close certain straightforward claims directly, bypassing the full standard workflow.

4.  **E-N (Short Average):**
    *   *Automated Notification Trigger:* The system might automatically trigger the 'N' event immediately upon completion of 'E' for certain evaluation outcomes (e.g., automated approval for standard cases or immediate rejection notices), bypassing or integrating the 'P' step.
    *   *Integrated System Logging:* The 'E' and 'N' events might be logged nearly simultaneously by the system, even if representing logically separate actions.
    *   *Implicit Approval:* For certain claim types or outcomes, the evaluation itself might serve as the approval, making a distinct 'P' step redundant and leading to immediate notification logging.

    -- Find claims closed <= 3 hours after assignment and check for missing intermediate E/P steps
    WITH QuickClose AS (

            a.claim_id,

        qc.claim_id,
        qc.ac_duration_hours,
