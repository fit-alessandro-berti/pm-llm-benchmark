## 2. Building a compliant trace step-by-step

Let us try to order the activities according to the positive constraints, temporarily ignoring those that denote impossiblity.

### Step 1 – Must start with R
`Init(R)` forces `R` as the very first activity.

### Step 2 – P follows R, exactly once
`Response(R,P)` demands P after R. `Exactly_one(P)` guarantees P appears precisely one time. So the start is **R → ... → P**.

### Step 3 – P leads to G
`Succession(P,G)` says P must precede G and G must eventually follow P. Because G is mandatory (existence), we must have **P → ... → G**. Combined with the above the prefix is **R → P → ... → G**.

### Step 4 – G leads to Q
`Altsuccession(G,Q)` requires G before Q and Q after G. So now we have **R → P → ... → G → ... → Q**.

### Step 5 – Q and A are a direct chain
`Chainsuccession(Q,A)` forces Q to be **immediately** followed by A. Therefore after G we cannot insert anything between Q and A. The flow becomes **R → P → ... → G → ... → Q → A** with Q→A instantaneous.

### Step 6 – A triggers T immediately
`Chainresponse(A,T)` requires T to appear right after A, with no other activity in between. So the sequence extends to **R → P → ... → G → ... → Q → A → T**.

### Step 7 – T eventually leads to N
`Altresponse(T,N)` and `altprecedence(N,T)` together mean T must occur before N and whenever T happens, N eventually follows. Thus N comes after T. So far the trace ends with **... → T → ... → N**.

### Step 8 – Where does C go?
We have several constraints on C:
- `Coexistence(G,C)`: G and C must co-occur → C is mandatory (since G is).
- `Precedence(Q,C)`: C must appear **after** Q.
- `Chainprecedence(C,P)` demands that P be immediately preceded by C. This is the first critical problem.

**Conflict #1 – Immediate precedence clash**
`Chainprecedence(C,P)` says P must be directly preceded by C, i.e., the pattern must be **C → P** with nothing between them. But we already know P happens early (after R) and C must occur after Q (which is much later). It is impossible for C to be before P and simultaneously after Q (unless the process loops, but P appears exactly once and there is no loop back). Therefore the model cannot satisfy both `chainprecedence(C,P)` and the ordering `Q→C`.

### Step 9 – Even if we ignore that impossible rule, can we place C?
Without `chainprecedence(C,P)`, we need C after Q, but Q→A and A→T are immediate. So C must be placed either:
- Between G and Q: forbidden, because precedence says Q before C.
- Between Q and A: impossible, immediate chain.
- Between A and T: impossible, immediate chain.
- After T, before N.

Let's test **R → P → G → Q → A → T → C → N**. Check all constraints:
- `Coexistence(G,C)`: G and C both occur ✔
- `Precedence(Q,C)`: Q occurs before C ✔ (Q is before C in this trace)
- `Noncoexistence(T,R)`: **violation** – both T and R appear.
- `Nonchainsuccession(C,N)`: C is directly followed by N (C→N) → **violation**.
- All other positive constraints respect this order.

**Conflict #2 – Noncoexistence between Transfer_Funds and Receive_Application**
`Noncoexistence(T,R)` explicitly forbids T and R from appearing in the same case. Yet R is mandatory (existence) and T is forced by the chain. Hence any valid trace is immediately impossible.

**Conflict #3 – Nonchainsuccession forbids C→N directly**
If we put C before N (the only possible position), they would be adjacent unless we insert something else. No extra activities exist, so C→N is unavoidable → violation.

Thus the DECLARE model is **over-constrained and has zero conforming traces**.

## 3. The intended real-world process (ideal operational flow)

Leaving aside the contradictory constraints, the *intent* of the model—judging by the naming and the majority of positive rules—can be reconstructed.

A sensible loan-approval sequence would be:

1. **Receive_Application** – The bank captures the customer's loan request.
2. **Preliminary_Credit_Check** – An initial credit assessment filters out clearly ineligible applicants before wasting effort.
3. **Gather_Additional_Documents** – If the initial check passes, the applicant supplies pay stubs, IDs, etc.
4. **Quality_Assurance_Review** – A compliance/quality team confirms all documents and underwriting rules are met.
5. **Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package** – With everything validated, the loan terms, rates, and legal documents are prepared.
6. **Authorize_Contract_Terms** – A senior officer or system gives formal approval ("signs off") on the final contract.
7. **Transfer_Funds** – Money is disbursed to the customer's account.
8. **Notify_Customer** – The customer is informed that the loan is approved and funded.

In this ideal flow:
- The **response** and **succession** constraints guarantee a linear progression from intake through credit check and documentation.
- **Altsuccession** and **chainsuccession** ensure that quality review happens *after* gathering documents, and that the offer is assembled *immediately* after the review, so no unverified documents reach the packaging stage.
- **Precedence** forces authorization *after* quality assurance, ensuring no loan is finalized without a compliance sign-off.
- **Chainresponse** from Assemble to Transfer_Funds links packaging and funding tightly, reducing the risk of funds being transferred before the offer is complete.
- **Altresponse/altprecedence** between Transfer_Funds and Notify_Customer ensure the customer is always notified after the money has moved, avoiding premature notifications that might create confusion.

The **prohibitions** (absence of X, nonsuccession, nonchainsuccession) serve to block forbidden shortcuts:
- *Proceed_Without_Compliance* may never occur – this would represent skipping all checks.
- *Nonsuccession* (N followed by P) prevents a scenario where the customer is notified and then a credit check is run, which would indicate a broken process.
- *Nonchainsuccession* (C directly before N) might be intended to keep authorization and customer notification separated by at least one activity (e.g., the actual transfer), so that the customer is only contacted after funds have actually moved.

## 4. Real-world motivations behind these rules

**Regulatory compliance**
Mandatory credit checks, documentation gathering, and quality assurance are standard requirements for "Know Your Customer" (KYC) and anti-money-laundering rules. The absence constraint directly mimics a forbidden "skip compliance" action that could incur regulatory fines.

**Fraud prevention & risk management**
The sequence **credit check → documents → QA → authorization** creates a multi-stage verification chain that makes it difficult for a fraudulent application to pass unnoticed. The immediate link from offer assembly to funds transfer (chainresponse) reduces the window during which terms could be altered.

**Operational best practices**
Forcing a clear separation of duties (e.g., QA before authorization) ensures that the person who verifies compliance is not the same as the person who signs off, following the "four-eyes principle". Immediate successions (chainsuccession, chainresponse) can reflect system-driven hand-overs where no manual delay or variation is allowed.

**Customer experience**
Notifying the customer only after funds have been transferred (altresponse/altprecedence) avoids confusing "approved" messages that are not yet followed by actual money. It also prevents customers from calling before the disbursement has been processed.

## 5. Consequences of deviations and omitted steps

**If mandatory steps are skipped**
- Omitting the credit check (P) would mean granting loans to high-risk applicants, potentially leading to defaults and regulatory censure.
- Removing document gathering (G) invalidates KYC proof, making the institution liable for money-laundering violations.
- Skipping quality assurance (Q) could result in erroneous loan offers with incorrect interest rates, breaching fair-lending laws and inviting customer disputes.

**If activities occur out of order**
- Performing *Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package* before *Quality_Assurance_Review* (violating chainsuccession) means the offer is built on unverified data; changes needed after QA would waste time and cause rework.
- Executing *Transfer_Funds* before *Authorize_Contract_Terms* (violating precedence and chain constraints) would disburse money without a signed contract, making it impossible to legally enforce repayment.
- Notifying the customer before the funds transfer (violating altprecedence) leads to a poor experience—"You are approved, but where is the money?"—and potential harm if the transfer later fails.
- Permitting *Proceed_Without_Compliance* would open a direct path from application to disbursement, bypassing all safeguards, and almost certainly result in fraudulent or non-compliant loans.

The conflicting constraints in the model (noncoexistence of T and R, impossible placement of C due to immediate chains, and the absurd chainprecedence(C,P)) would in reality **deadlock the process**: no loan could ever be completed. In an actual system, such conflicts would be detected during verification and would require the process designer to resolve them, e.g., by removing the noncoexistence rule, relaxing some immediate successions, or reordering the authorization step to after the offer assembly but before the transfer.
