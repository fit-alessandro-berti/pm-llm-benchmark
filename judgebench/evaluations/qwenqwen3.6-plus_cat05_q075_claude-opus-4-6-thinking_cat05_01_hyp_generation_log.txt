#### 2. SQL Query Technical Audit (Significant Deductions)
- **Query 1 (Critical Logical Flaw):** The approach to out-of-order detection is fundamentally broken for partial traces. By joining `order_event_log` to a fixed `expected_order` list and comparing `ROW_NUMBER() OVER (ORDER BY timestamp)` against `expected_step`, the query conflates *missing activities* with *sequence deviations*. If a case skips step 2, step 3 becomes `actual_step=2` but retains `expected_step=3`. The `WHERE actual_step <> expected_step` clause will falsely flag step 3, 4, 5, etc., as out-of-order. In process mining, this is a known anti-pattern that produces massive false positives on non-conformant traces. A correct implementation requires pairwise predecessor/successor checks or `LAG(expected_step)` comparisons, not positional offsets.
- **Query 2 (Acceptable but Fragile):** Uses `CROSS JOIN` with `LEFT JOIN` anti-pattern to find missing activities. Syntactically correct for PostgreSQL, but assumes the `orders` table contains 100% of `case_ids`. If event logs contain orphan cases, this misses them. Given the prompt, it's passable.
- **Query 5 & 6 (Scope Imprecision):** Query 6 joins `anomalous_cases` back to the full `order_event_log` table. This means `ARRAY_AGG(DISTINCT oel.activity)` aggregates *every* action a resource performed in an anomalous case, not just the problematic step. It dilutes investigative focus.
