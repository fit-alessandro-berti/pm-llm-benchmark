### 2. Some process-mining concepts are used a bit loosely
A few methodological points are not precise enough:

- **Role discovery via k-means on skill vectors** is not really classic process-mining role discovery.  
  In process mining, role discovery is typically based on **event log behavior** (activities performed, frequencies, handovers), not just HR skill vectors.

- **Centrality = bottleneck** is oversimplified.  
  High in-degree or centrality in a handover network does not automatically indicate a bottleneck; it may just indicate a coordinator or a frequently collaborating agent.

- The answer does not sufficiently distinguish:
  - **waiting time**
  - **service time**
  - **handover delay**
  - **queueing delay**
  
  In an ITSM context, that distinction matters a lot for diagnosing assignment inefficiencies.

### 3. The strategies are useful, but some are under-designed or logically shaky
Examples:

#### Strategy 1 score formula
```text
Score = (Skill_Match * 0.5) + (Proficiency * 0.3) + (1/Workload * 0.2)
```
Problems:
- It is not normalized.
- `Proficiency` on a 1–5 scale can dominate the binary `Skill_Match`.
- `1/Workload` is unstable and undefined at zero unless adjusted.
- It does not include **SLA urgency**, **queue age**, **calendar availability**, or **business hours**.

So while the idea is good, the formula is too naive for a high-quality answer.

#### Strategy 2
- “If all skilled agents are busy, escalate to next tier” is risky and may worsen overload at higher tiers rather than solve it.
- It does not explain when to queue, when to defer, and when cross-skill substitution is acceptable.

#### Strategy 3
- “If predicted skill is rare, auto-escalate to L3” is too simplistic and potentially wrong. Rare skill does not imply L3 ownership.
