#### b) “Bottleneck discovery via social network analysis / betweenness centrality” is dubious
This is one of the weaker parts. Bottlenecks in this context are about **capacity, queue lengths, occupancy, and synchronization delays**.  
Betweenness centrality in a process graph is not a reliable way to identify whether cold-packing or batching is causing delay. This is at best tangential and at worst misleading.

A strict grader should penalize this.

#### c) Cold-packing queue-time measurement is poorly specified
It says:

> Compare START timestamp of a Cold-Packing task with the COMPLETE timestamp of the prior task on any Cold-Packing station.

This is not a correct way to measure waiting time. Waiting should be computed from:
- the order’s **ready/enabled time** for packing, typically predecessor completion,
- to the actual packing **start time**.

Then resource contention should be inferred by checking whether all eligible cold stations were occupied during that interval.

Comparing with the “prior task on any cold station” is simplistic and may be wrong in multi-server settings.

#### d) Hazardous-material impact is under-measured
The answer focuses on:

But the scenario asks for the **impact** of the constraint, not only compliance violations. Important missing measures include:
- time spent at or near the cap,
- hazardous-order queueing caused by the cap,
- throughput loss attributable to the cap,
- blocking of non-hazardous flow due to shared resources.

Counting only breach events is insufficient.

#### e) Within-instance vs between-instance distinction is conceptually sloppy
The answer says:
- within-instance = processing time,
- between-instance = queue time.

But the prompt asks to differentiate **waiting time** caused by within-instance factors versus between-instance factors.  
Calling processing time a form of “waiting” is conceptually imprecise.

A stronger answer would separate:
