### Why it is not higher

#### 1. Queue-mining methodology is only partially correct
- The answer defines waiting time as  
  **start of next activity - completion of current activity**, which is a reasonable baseline.
- However, it then says that if there are multiple possible next activities, one should take the **earliest subsequent start timestamp**. That is a **methodological weakness**:
  - It can **hide queues** for specific downstream activities.
  - It does not properly handle **parallelism**, optional branches, or synchronization points.
  - In healthcare, different “next activities” can have very different meanings and constraints, so this simplification can distort results.
- It also does not distinguish between:
  - **pure queue time**
  - **clinical waiting that is intentional/necessary**
  - **patient-caused delays**
  - **handover delay**
  - **resource-ready vs case-ready delay**
  
That omission is significant in a healthcare queue-mining answer.

#### 2. Important queue metrics are missing
The answer includes basic descriptive metrics, but queue mining should also consider:
- **queue length over time**
- **arrival rate**
- **service rate**
- **work-in-process / concurrent cases**
- **resource occupancy and calendars**
- **sojourn time decomposition** (service vs waiting)
- **time-to-start after becoming enabled**
  
Without these, the analysis is more like basic performance reporting than strong queue mining.

#### 3. Root-cause analysis is generic rather than rigorous
The listed causes are sensible, but the answer is somewhat **template-like**:
- It names standard causes without detailing how they would be empirically separated.
- It does not explain how to tell whether long waits are driven by:
  - demand spikes,
  - long service times,
  - poor routing,
  - batching behavior,
  - resource calendars,
  - or prioritization of urgent cases.
- It does not explicitly address that **process mining reveals associations, not definitive causality**. That is an important analytical caveat.
