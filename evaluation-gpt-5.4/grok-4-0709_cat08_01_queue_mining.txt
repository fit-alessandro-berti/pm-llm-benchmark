### 2. Some methodological claims are shaky or oversimplified
There are several technical oversimplifications:

- **Little’s Law** is invoked rather casually for queue length estimation.  
  That is risky because outpatient flows are often **time-varying and non-stationary**, especially by hour-of-day/day-of-week. You cannot just infer queue length cleanly from average arrival rate × average waiting time without checking assumptions.

- The mention of modeling queues as **M/M/1 systems** is a weak point.  
  That is a strong queueing assumption and usually unrealistic for a multi-specialty clinic with:
  - multiple servers,
  - priority patients,
  - appointment scheduling,
  - non-exponential service times,
  - branching pathways.  
  Under strict grading, this is a meaningful flaw.

- “Use app-based notifications to start tests during consultations if predictable” is not well justified and can be operationally or clinically unsound.  
  In many cases, a test is ordered **after** the physician evaluates the patient, so this recommendation can conflict with care logic.

- “Low room utilization” being cited alongside queueing is not explained carefully.  
  If utilization is low yet waiting is high, the root cause must be something more specific such as:
  - poor synchronization,
  - batching,
  - mismatched calendars,
  - priority interruption,
  - information delay.  
  The answer gestures at this but does not unpack it rigorously.
