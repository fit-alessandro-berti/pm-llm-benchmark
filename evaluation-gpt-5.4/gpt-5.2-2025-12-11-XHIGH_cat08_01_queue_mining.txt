## Why it is **not** a 9+ answer

Despite being good, it has several important shortcomings.

### 1. It slightly oversimplifies “waiting time”
This is the biggest methodological weakness.

It defines queue time as:

\[
Q(A \rightarrow B)=t_{start}(B)-t_{complete}(A)
\]

But in real outpatient processes, the patient is not always truly **ready** for B immediately when A completes. There may be:
- hidden tasks
- transport/escort steps
- room-cleaning/turnover
- physician order release delays
- clinical decision points

So the answer should have distinguished between:
- **observed inter-activity time**
- **true queue time after readiness**

Without that distinction, the analysis risks misclassifying all inter-step gaps as “queue,” when some are clinically necessary or structurally embedded.

A more rigorous answer would explicitly say that queue mining should infer or approximate a **readiness timestamp** where possible, not just use the previous completion blindly.
