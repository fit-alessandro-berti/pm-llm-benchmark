### Why the score is low
The biggest problem is that many of the SQL queries are unreliable or outright wrong for PostgreSQL interval analysis.

#### 1. Several interval calculations are incorrect
The answer repeatedly uses expressions like:
- `EXTRACT(DAY FROM (n.timestamp - p.timestamp))`
- `EXTRACT(MINUTE FROM (cl.timestamp - a.timestamp))`
- `EXTRACT(SECOND FROM (n.timestamp - e.timestamp))`

This is a major issue.

In PostgreSQL, `EXTRACT(MINUTE FROM interval)` gives the minute component, not total minutes.  
So:
- 3 hours 5 minutes becomes `5`, not `185`
- 1 day 2 hours 4 minutes becomes `4`, not `1564`

That makes several filters badly wrong:
- `EXTRACT(MINUTE ...) < 120` is logically broken
- `EXTRACT(MINUTE ...) < 5` is also broken
- `EXTRACT(DAY ...) > 14` ignores sub-day precision and can misrepresent elapsed time

This alone seriously damages the answer.

#### 2. The queries do not properly use the temporal profile ranges
The prompt is about verifying anomalies relative to the temporal profile model, including mean and standard deviation.  
But the answer mostly uses arbitrary thresholds:
- `> 14 days`
- `< 120 minutes`
- `< 5 minutes`

That is much weaker than checking against expected ranges such as mean ± k·stdev. It does not actually operationalize the temporal profile very well.

The queries join events only on `claim_id` and activity type, for example joining all `R` rows to all `P` rows for the same claim.

For temporal analysis, that is a serious omission.
