## Major technical and logical problems

### 2. Several metric definitions are flawed or imprecise

#### First-call resolution definition is weak
The answer defines FCR for L1 as:

> `Assign L1 -> Work L1 End` without a subsequent Escalate event

That is not a robust definition.
- A ticket can end L1 work and still not be resolved.
- For email/web tickets, “first-call resolution” is not even always the right concept; better would be **L1 resolution without escalation** or **first-touch resolution**.
- It should require some notion of **resolution/closure at L1**, not merely the absence of escalation in the immediate trace segment.

This is a meaningful methodological issue.

#### Workload based only on count of assignments is too crude
Counting assigned tickets per agent/tier is only a partial view.
- It ignores **concurrency**,
- ignores **ticket complexity**,
- ignores **touch time vs waiting time**,
- ignores **reopened incidents**,
- ignores **shift length / availability**.

A stricter answer would explicitly distinguish:
- assigned volume,
- active work time,
- queue length over time,
- occupancy/utilization,
- case mix adjusted workload.

#### Skill matching via exact/partial string matching is naïve
The answer repeatedly proposes exact/partial matching on `Required Skill` and `Agent Skills`.
That is risky and simplistic:
- skill taxonomies usually need **controlled normalization**,
- there may be synonyms, hierarchical skills, stale profiles, inferred skills, or composite skills,
- “partial match” is especially weak and error-prone.

A top answer would have emphasized **master data cleaning / ontology mapping** before analysis.

That is inaccurate. **Heuristics Miner** and **ILP-based discovery** are process discovery algorithms, not decision mining methods.
