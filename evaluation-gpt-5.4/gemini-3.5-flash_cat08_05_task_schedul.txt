### 2. Some metrics are **technically weak or incorrect**
Several formulas or definitions are problematic:

- **Queue time** is oversimplified:
  - \( t_{queue} = t_{setup\_start} - t_{queue\_entry} \)
  - This assumes setup immediately precedes processing for that job and ignores cases like machine waiting, operator delay, blocking, or batching nuances.
- **Utilization decomposition** omits important manufacturing realities:
  - It ignores **shift calendars**, planned downtime, non-working hours, blocking, starvation, and off-shift time.
  - So the idle-time calculation is potentially misleading.
- **Makespan** is defined for a “batch or shift,” but the scenario is a job shop with continuous arrival; this is not wrong, but it is not particularly well contextualized.
- **Schedule adherence** is treated mostly as due-date adherence. That is incomplete.
  - The prompt asks about scheduling performance; the answer should also measure **planned vs. actual start/end times**, dispatch-sequence deviations, and rescheduling frequency.
- **MTTR** is used under “impact of disruptions,” but that is really a repair-duration metric, not a complete disruption-impact metric.
  - The answer should also quantify incremental tardiness, queue spillover, starvation propagation, and recovery of schedule stability after disruption.

Issues:

This is a good conceptual strategy, but the formula itself is not production-grade as written.
