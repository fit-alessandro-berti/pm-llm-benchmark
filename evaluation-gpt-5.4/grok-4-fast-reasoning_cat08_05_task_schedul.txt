#### 2. Some important technical inaccuracies
A few issues are serious enough to materially reduce the score.

- **Makespan is defined incorrectly.**  
  The answer says:
  > “Makespan ... would be computed shop-wide by summing active processing periods, excluding idle times.”

  That is wrong. Makespan is the elapsed time from the start of the schedule/horizon (or first release) to the completion of the last job, not the sum of active processing times.

- **Dispatching score formula has logical sign problems.**  
  It proposes:
  > Score = w1*(Due Date Slack / Remaining Processing Time) + w2*Priority Factor + w3*Estimated Setup Time + w4*Downstream Load Index

  As written, larger slack gives a larger score, which would perversely favor *less urgent* jobs unless the sign is inverted. Likewise, adding estimated setup time positively would reward jobs with longer setups unless the weight is negative. This is a nontrivial logic flaw.

- **Conformance checking is used too loosely.**  
  The answer treats conformance checking as a main tool for schedule adherence and tardiness. In reality, schedule adherence is more naturally measured by comparing actual start/completion times to planned start/completion times and due dates, not by conformance to an “ideal process model.” Conformance may help, but it is not the central metric here.

- **Breakdowns as “variants” is conceptually weak.**  
  A breakdown is a resource-state event, not just a trace variant. Treating disruptions mainly as process variants is too simplistic for this problem.

- **Bullwhip effect is not the right term here.**  
  The answer uses “bullwhip effect” for internal WIP oscillations on the shop floor. That is at best imprecise. The classical bullwhip effect refers to amplification of demand/order variability upstream in a supply chain, not general WIP variability inside a job shop.
