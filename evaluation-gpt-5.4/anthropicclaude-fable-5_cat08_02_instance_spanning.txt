### Why it does **not** deserve a very high score
Several nontrivial issues prevent a 9+ score:

#### 1. Waiting-time attribution is somewhat oversimplified
The answer proposes a **counterfactual availability test** like:
- if all eligible resources were busy, then waiting is between-instance/resource contention;
- if a resource was idle, then it is within-instance/organizational.

That is too simplistic for rigorous attribution. It ignores:
- queue discipline,
- setup/cleanup time,
- shift calendars,
- resource qualification nuances,
- release policies,
- hidden routing/scheduling rules.

A resource can be “idle” and yet the case can still be waiting because of a legitimate between-instance scheduling decision. So the attribution logic is directionally good, but too cleanly stated.

#### 2. The hazmat “token” strategy contains a real conceptual flaw
This is the biggest issue.

The regulation says no more than 10 hazmat orders can be **undergoing Packing or Quality Check simultaneously**.  
The answer proposes holding a token **from Packing start until QC complete**.

That means if an order finishes Packing and then waits before QC, it would still consume a token, even though it is not undergoing either activity during that waiting gap. That is stricter than the actual rule and could artificially reduce throughput. A more precise design would treat the cap over the union of the two active states, not necessarily the full interval from Packing START to QC COMPLETE.

That is a material modeling/design error.

#### 3. Preemption detection is weaker than it first appears
The answer suggests detecting preemption via overlap with an express order on the same resource, or via abnormal duration. Reasonable as a heuristic, but:
- if the log only contains START/COMPLETE and no suspend/resume events,
- and if the resource cannot truly handle two orders simultaneously,
then overlap-based inference may be ambiguous or impossible depending on logging semantics.

This should have been framed more carefully as an inference problem requiring assumptions, not as a fairly straightforward detection.

#### 4. Some recommendations are good, but not fully justified
A few optimization ideas are plausible but insufficiently disciplined:
- **Reserving one cold station for express** could materially hurt standard throughput when express demand is misforecast.
- **Express bypass of batching** may undermine route optimization economics.
- **Releasing a batch without a hazmat order** may improve flow but could create operational fragmentation downstream.

The answer acknowledges trade-offs, but under strict grading I would expect tighter explanation of decision criteria or threshold policies.

#### 5. The answer is more “operations analytics” than explicitly “process mining” in a few places
It uses process mining correctly overall, but sometimes drifts into general queueing/scheduling language. For a top score I would expect more explicit use of:
