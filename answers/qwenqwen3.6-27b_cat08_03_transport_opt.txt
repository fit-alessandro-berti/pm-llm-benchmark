### 1. Process Discovery and Conformance Checking

**Data Preprocessing and Integration**
To apply process mining, heterogeneous data sources must be transformed into a standardized **event log** schema. The foundational attributes required are `case_id`, `activity`, `timestamp`, and `event_type` (start/end), supplemented by contextual attributes.
* **Case Concept Definition:** The optimal `case_id` for last-mile logistics is typically `VehicleID_ShiftDate` (e.g., `V12_20241205`). This represents a single operational instance from depot departure to return.
* **Activity Mapping & GPS Segmentation:** Raw GPS data is continuous and high-frequency. It must be segmented into discrete process activities using rule-based inference:
  * Speed > 5 km/h  `Travel`
  * Speed  5 km/h & Dwell > 2 mins  `Stop`
  * Stop at known customer GPS + Scanner event  `Service` or `Failed Delivery`
  * Stop at depot  `Depot Depart/Return`
  * Maintenance log overlap  `Scheduled/Unscheduled Maintenance`
* **Integration Challenges:**
  * **Time Synchronization:** Clock drift between GPS, scanners, and dispatch systems requires time normalization to a single UTC/Local standard.
  * **Data Granularity Mismatch:** GPS is continuous; scanners are discrete. Missing scanner events must be inferred from GPS dwell times at customer coordinates.
  * **Location Matching:** GPS coordinates must be matched to dispatch stop addresses using geocoding and spatial tolerance thresholds to link `Arrive Customer` scanner events to the correct planned stop.
  * **Noise & Outliers:** GPS drift, signal loss, or scanner errors require cleaning (e.g., removing impossible speeds, filtering micro-stops < 30 seconds).

**Process Discovery & Visualization**
* **Algorithm Selection:** Given the high variability and noise in last-mile routes, **Inductive Miner** or **Heuristic Miner** (with noise reduction parameters) is recommended over the basic Alpha Miner. These algorithms handle non-sequential patterns (e.g., drivers occasionally swapping stops) and produce sound process models (Process Trees or Petri Nets).
* **Visualization:** The discovered model visualizes the actual end-to-end flow: `Depart Depot`  `Travel`  `Arrive Customer`  `Service/Failed`  `Depart Customer`  (Loop)  `Return Depot`. Deviations like `Unscheduled Stop`, `Maintenance`, or `Route Swap` appear as alternative branches or loops. Color-coding by frequency highlights the most common actual route variants.

**Conformance Checking**
* **Methodology:** Compare the discovered actual process model against the *planned* route model derived from the Dispatch System. Techniques like **Alignment-Based Conformance Checking** or **Token-Based Replay** calculate conformance scores per case.
* **Deviations to Identify:**
  * **Sequence Deviations:** Stops completed out of planned order (e.g., Stop 4 before Stop 3).
  * **Timing Deviations:** Arrival/departure outside planned time windows or excessive inter-stop travel time.
  * **Unplanned Activities:** `Unscheduled Maintenance`, `Long Idle/Parking Search`, or `Failed Delivery` not accounted for in the plan.
  * **Resource Deviations:** Wrong driver/vehicle assignment or capacity overloads.
  * **Attribute Deviations:** Planned travel time vs. actual GPS travel time discrepancies.

### 2. Performance Analysis and Bottleneck Identification

**Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) & Calculation from Event Log**
| KPI | Definition & Calculation from Log |
| :--- | :--- |
| **On-Time Delivery Rate (OTDR)** | `% of 'Delivery Success' events where Scanner Timestamp falls within Dispatch Time Window.` |
| **Avg Time per Delivery Stop** | `Mean(Depart Customer Timestamp - Arrive Customer Timestamp) per case.` |
| **Travel vs. Service Time Ratio** | `Sum(Travel Activity Durations) / Sum(Service Activity Durations).` High ratio indicates routing inefficiency; low ratio suggests excessive dwell/service time. |
| **Fuel Consumption per km/Package** | `Proxy: (Total GPS Distance ﾗ Base Fuel Rate + Idle Time ﾗ Idle Fuel Rate) / Packages Delivered.` (Actual fuel data can replace proxy if available.) |
| **Vehicle Utilization Rate** | `(Active Operational Hours / Total Available Hours) or (Packages Delivered / Max Vehicle Capacity).` |
| **Traffic Delay Frequency/Duration** | `Count & Sum of 'Low Speed/Idle' events in predefined traffic hotspot polygons during peak hours.` |
| **Failed Delivery Rate** | `Count('Failed Delivery') / Count('Arrive Customer') per route/driver.` |

**Bottleneck Identification Techniques**
Process mining techniques transform raw durations into quantified bottlenecks:
* **Performance Coloring & Variance Analysis:** Color process map activities by average duration and standard deviation. High variance in `Service` or `Travel` between specific stops highlights unpredictable bottlenecks.
* **Bottleneck Detection Algorithms:** Apply queueing theory-based PM algorithms to identify activities where work accumulates. For logistics, this translates to locations where vehicles consistently queue (e.g., difficult parking zones, complex apartment blocks) causing cascading delays.
* **Quantifying Impact:** Calculate a **Delay Propagation Score**. If a bottleneck at Stop 3 adds 15 minutes, conformance checking reveals how many subsequent stops miss their time windows. This quantifies the downstream impact of specific bottlenecks.
* **Segmented Analysis:** Filter the event log by `Time of Day`, `Driver ID`, `Vehicle Type`, or `Route Zone`. Compare performance metrics across segments to isolate bottlenecks (e.g., Driver D105 has 40% longer service times in Zone B; Vehicle V12 has higher idle times during 14:00-16:00).
