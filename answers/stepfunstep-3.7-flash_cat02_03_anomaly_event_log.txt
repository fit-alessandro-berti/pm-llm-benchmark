### Baseline Standard Procure-to-Pay (P2P) Process Flow
The universally accepted standard P2P sequence, designed to mitigate financial, compliance, and operational risk, follows these steps in strict order:
1. Create Purchase Requisition (PR): Internal request for required goods/services
2. Approve Purchase Requisition: Gatekeeping step to validate budget, business need, and stakeholder authorization before any purchase commitment is made
3. Issue Purchase Order (PO): Formal, binding contract sent to the vendor outlining order terms (price, quantity, delivery, etc.) based on the approved PR
4. Receive Goods/Services: Confirmation that the vendor delivered the ordered items per PO terms
5. Vendor Invoice Received: Vendor submits a bill for the delivered goods/services
6. Match Invoice to PO (Three-Way Match): Preventive control to validate that the invoice amount, items, and terms align with both the pre-approved PO and the actual goods receipt
7. Release Payment: Disburse funds to the vendor only after the match is approved and all prior steps are complete
8. Close Case: Finalize the process and archive all records for audit purposes.

Any deviation from this sequence (unless a documented, pre-approved exception like a vetted advance payment) is considered anomalous and introduces material risk.

### Baseline Standard Procure-to-Pay (P2P) Process Flow
The universally accepted standard P2P sequence, designed to mitigate financial, compliance, and operational risk, follows these steps in strict order:
1. Create Purchase Requisition (PR): Internal request for required goods/services
2. Approve Purchase Requisition: Gatekeeping step to validate budget, business need, and stakeholder authorization before any purchase commitment is made
3. Issue Purchase Order (PO): Formal, binding contract sent to the vendor outlining order terms (price, quantity, delivery, etc.) based on the approved PR
4. Receive Goods/Services: Confirmation that the vendor delivered the ordered items per PO terms
5. Vendor Invoice Received: Vendor submits a bill for the delivered goods/services
6. Match Invoice to PO (Three-Way Match): Preventive control to validate that the invoice amount, items, and terms align with both the pre-approved PO and the actual goods receipt
7. Release Payment: Disburse funds to the vendor only after the match is approved and all prior steps are complete
8. Close Case: Finalize the process and archive all records for audit purposes.

Any deviation from this sequence (unless a documented, pre-approved exception like a vetted advance payment) is considered anomalous and introduces material risk.

### Identified Anomalies and Associated Risks
#### 1. Case 1002: Multiple Critical Control Bypasses
This case has severe out-of-sequence activities that reverse core P2P controls:
- **Anomaly 1: Purchase Order issued before Purchase Requisition approval**
  The PO is issued on 2024-02-01 08:40, 2 full days before the PR is approved on 2024-02-03 08:00.
  *Risk*: This bypasses the first mandatory gate of the P2P process. The PR approval step exists to confirm the purchase is necessary, within budget, and authorized by relevant stakeholders. Issuing a PO without approval creates a legal and financial commitment for unauthorized, potentially unbudgeted spending, increasing risk of fraud, maverick spending, and budget overruns.
- **Anomaly 2: Payment released before PR approval, goods receipt, and invoice matching**
  Payment is released on 2024-02-03 07:15, 45 minutes before PR approval, 26 hours before goods are received, and 2 days before the invoice is matched to the PO.
  *Risk*: This defeats the entire purpose of the P2P verification workflow:
  - No confirmation that the ordered goods were actually delivered, creating high risk of payment for non-existent or undelivered goods (a common fraud vector).
  - No validation that the invoice amount aligns with PO terms, creating risk of overpayment for inflated or incorrect invoices.
  - No supporting documentation exists for the payment at the time of disbursement, creating a major audit trail gap and financial reporting risk.
  - The three-way match is performed after payment, rendering this core preventive control useless, as discrepancies cannot be resolved before funds are spent.

#### 2. Case 1003: Payment Released Before Invoice Receipt and All Verification Steps
This case violates the standard post-PO sequence entirely:
- **Anomaly: Payment released 1 day before the vendor invoice is received, and 2–3 days before goods receipt and invoice matching**
  Payment is processed on 2024-02-08 11:00, while the vendor invoice is not received until 2024-02-09 12:00, goods are not received until 2024-02-10 13:00, and the three-way match is not completed until 2024-02-11 14:00.
  *Risk*: This is a critical control failure with severe consequences:
  - No invoice documentation exists to support the payment at the time of disbursement, meaning the company has no record of what the payment is for, what amount was agreed upon, or what goods/services were purchased. This creates high risk of unsupported, incorrect payments and fails basic financial compliance requirements.
  - The three-way match cannot be performed before payment, as two of the three required documents (invoice and goods receipt) do not exist at the time of payment. Even the post-payment match is useless for preventing discrepancies, as funds have already been spent.
  - Payment is made before goods are received, so the company has no leverage to withhold payment if goods are damaged, incorrect, or never delivered, leading to unrecoverable losses.
  *Note*: While pre-approved advance payments are a rare exception in P2P, they require formal documentation specifying advance terms and reconciliation requirements, which is absent here. The subsequent standard post-PO steps indicate this is an uncontrolled process failure, not a valid exception.

#### 3. Case 1004: Goods Received Before Purchase Order Issuance
This case violates pre-receipt procurement controls:
- **Anomaly: Goods received 15 minutes before the Purchase Order is issued**
  Goods are received on 2024-02-06 09:00, while the PO is not issued until 2024-02-06 09:15.
  *Risk*: The PO is the formal, pre-agreed contract that defines the terms of the purchase. Receiving goods before the PO is issued creates multiple risks:
  - No pre-agreed terms exist for the purchase at the time of delivery, so there is no contractual basis to dispute incorrect pricing, wrong quantities, or defective goods with the vendor.
  - This bypasses procurement approval controls, as the company accepts a financial commitment (by taking possession of goods) before completing the required PR approval and PO issuance steps, leading to unbudgeted, unplanned spending.
  - The three-way match is performed against a retroactively issued PO, which may be adjusted to match the actual delivery rather than the original agreed terms, defeating the purpose of the match as a preventive control to catch discrepancies between ordered and delivered goods.
