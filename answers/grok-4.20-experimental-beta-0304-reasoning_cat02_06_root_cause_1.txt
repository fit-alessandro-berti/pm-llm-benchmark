**Analysis of Customer Support Ticket Resolution Event Log**

### 1. Cases with Significantly Longer Resolution Times

Total cycle time (Receive Ticket  Close Ticket):

- **Case 101**: 2 hours 15 minutes
- **Case 103**: 1 hour 20 minutes
- **Case 102**: 25 hours 10 minutes
- **Case 104**: 24 hours 10 minutes
- **Case 105**: 49 hours 5 minutes

**Conclusion**: Cases 102, 104, and 105 have significantly longer cycle times (roughly 10–20× longer than the fast cases). Cases 101 and 103 represent normal/good performance (under 3 hours).

### 2. Root Causes of Delays

The main performance issues are driven by two primary factors:

**A. Escalations to Level-2 (Strongest Pattern)**
- Cases 102 and 105 both contain an "Escalate to Level-2 Agent" step.
- These are the two slowest cases (25h and 49h).
- Post-escalation delays are severe:
  - Case 102: Escalate (11:30)  Investigate (14:00) = 2.5 hours
  - Case 105: Escalate (10:00 on Mar 1)  next Investigate (14:00 on Mar 2) = ~28 hours
- After escalation, the second "Investigate Issue" step is often performed late in the day (14:00), which then leads to overnight delays.

**B. Long Waiting Times Between Key Activities (Especially Investigate  Resolve)**
All three slow cases show a consistent ~19-hour gap between "Investigate Issue" and "Resolve Ticket":

- Case 102: Investigate (Mar 1 14:00)  Resolve (Mar 2 09:00)  19 hours
- Case 104: Investigate (Mar 1 13:00)  Resolve (Mar 2 08:00)  19 hours
- Case 105: Second Investigate (Mar 2 14:00)  Resolve (Mar 3 09:00)  19 hours

This strongly suggests a **business-hours constraint** — tickets investigated in the afternoon are typically not resolved until the next morning.

**Additional Contributing Factors:**
- **Morning overload**: All five tickets arrived within a 25-minute window (08:00–08:25). This creates a queue that affects triage and assignment speed in slower cases (e.g., Case 104 waited 3.5 hours after assignment before investigation).
- Case 104 is interesting because it has no explicit escalation yet still shows the same overnight delay pattern as the escalated cases. This suggests some tickets are complex enough to require extended investigation time even at Level-1.

### 3. Insights and Recommendations

**Key Insights:**
- The process performs very well when tickets are handled entirely at Level-1 and resolved the same day (Cases 101, 103).
- Escalation is a major cycle time killer — not just because of the extra step, but because it pushes work into late afternoon, triggering overnight delays.
- The largest single contributor to long cycle times is the **systematic overnight handoff** between investigation and resolution. This appears to be a structural issue (working hours + capacity) rather than random variation.
- High arrival clustering in the early morning exposes capacity bottlenecks in both Level-1 and Level-2 teams.

**Recommendations to Reduce Cycle Time:**

1. **Reduce Escalation Rate**
   - Provide additional training or knowledge base resources to Level-1 agents to handle more issues without escalation.
   - Implement better initial triage routing to send complex tickets directly to Level-2 when possible.

2. **Address Capacity and Scheduling**
   - Add afternoon/evening coverage or staggered shifts so that investigations started at 13:00–14:00 can be completed same-day rather than pushed to the next morning.
   - Consider a "same-day resolution" target for non-escalated tickets with clear SLAs at each handoff point.

3. **Process Improvements**
   - Introduce a "Pending Customer/Input" activity to distinguish between active work delays and waiting-for-external-input delays.
   - Smooth out morning arrival peaks if possible (e.g., better self-service options to reduce ticket volume during the 8:00–8:30 rush).
   - Set performance thresholds: flag any ticket that sits more than 2 hours between Assign and Investigate, or more than 4 hours post-escalation.

4. **Monitoring**
   - Track escalation rate and average post-escalation wait time as key performance indicators.
   - Use process mining to automatically detect the "overnight handoff" pattern in future logs.

**Summary**: The primary bottlenecks are escalation-induced delays and the structural overnight gap between investigation and resolution. Addressing agent capacity during peak afternoon hours and reducing unnecessary escalations should yield the largest improvements in overall cycle time.
