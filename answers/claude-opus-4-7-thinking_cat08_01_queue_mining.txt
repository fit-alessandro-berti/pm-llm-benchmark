# Queue Mining Analysis for Outpatient Clinic Optimization

## 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

### Defining Waiting Time from the Event Log

Since the event log contains both `START` and `COMPLETE` timestamps, I can decompose each patient's visit into two distinct time components per activity:

- **Service Time (Activity Duration)** = `COMPLETE(A_i)`  `START(A_i)` — value-adding time with the resource.
- **Waiting Time (Queue Time)** = `START(A_i+1)`  `COMPLETE(A_i)` — non-value-adding time between the completion of one activity and the start of the next.

For each case, I would reconstruct the activity sequence sorted by timestamp and compute a **per-transition waiting time vector**. These transitions (e.g., *Registration  Nurse Assessment*, *Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation*, *Doctor  ECG*) become the unit of analysis for queue mining.

A subtlety: "waiting" is not always pure queue time. The gap may include patient walking time, physiological delays (e.g., fasting before a blood draw), or legitimate buffer. I would therefore establish a **minimum expected transition time** per activity pair (e.g., 2 minutes walk between registration and nursing). Anything above this threshold is treated as queue time.

### Key Queue Metrics

For every activity-to-activity transition, I would compute:

| Metric | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Mean / Median waiting time | Central tendency; median resists outlier skew |
| 90th & 95th percentile | Captures tail experience patients complain about |
| Max waiting time | Worst-case signal |
| Standard deviation / CV | Variability — key predictor of queue formation (per Kingman's formula) |
| Queue frequency | % of cases where wait > threshold (e.g., >15 min) |
| Total waiting time contribution | % of total visit duration spent in this queue |
| Queue length over time | Number of concurrent patients waiting (time-series) |
| Arrival rate  vs. Service rate  | Utilization  = / per resource pool |

### Identifying the Most Critical Queues

I would rank queues using a **composite criticality score** rather than a single metric, because a short-but-universal wait can impact more patients than a rare extreme wait. Criteria:

1. **Impact magnitude**: `(Mean wait) × (Frequency of cases affected)`  total person-minutes lost.
2. **Patient experience**: 90th-percentile wait (the number patients actually remember).
3. **Equity**: Are urgent or vulnerable patient types disproportionately affected?
4. **Downstream propagation**: Does this queue cause cascading delays (e.g., doctor waits push ECG into lunch)?

In a typical outpatient clinic, I would hypothesize the **Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation** and **Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Test** transitions will dominate — consistent with the V1001 example, where the patient waits ~20 minutes before seeing the doctor.

## 2. Root Cause Analysis

Identifying *where* queues occur is only half the problem. The root cause falls into several categories, each diagnosable using specific process mining techniques:

### Resource Bottlenecks
- **Resource utilization analysis**: Compute  = busy time / available time per resource (Dr. Smith, Nurse 1, Room 3, ECG machine). Resources operating at  > 80% are predictive of exploding queues even under small variability.
- **Resource availability profiles**: Heatmaps of staffing per hour of day vs. arrivals reveal staffing gaps (e.g., only one clerk during 9–10 AM peak).

### Variability in Service Times
- **Activity duration distribution analysis**: Compute CV for each activity. High variability (e.g., cardiology consultations ranging 10–45 min) causes downstream schedule collapse. Break down by doctor, patient type, and urgency to detect outliers.

### Arrival Patterns & Scheduling
- **Arrival rate analysis**: Bucket patient arrivals per 15-minute interval. Compare planned appointments vs. actual arrivals. "Block scheduling" (e.g., 8 patients at 9:00 AM) causes immediate registration pile-ups.
- **Early/late arrival analysis**: Quantify deviation from scheduled time as a covariate for queue length.

### Handovers and Dependencies
- **Handover-of-work analysis (social network mining)**: Identify resource pairs with high handover volumes and delays. If every cardiology patient needs Dr. Smith *and* Tech X's ECG, their schedules must be coupled.
- **Performance-annotated process models**: Overlay mean waits on the discovered process graph (using Directly-Follows Graphs or Heuristic Miner) to visualize bottleneck edges.

### Variant Analysis
- **Trace clustering by patient type / urgency / specialty**: Do "New" patients experience 2× the wait of "Follow-up"? Does Cardiology versus Orthopedics have systematically different queuing? Variant analysis isolates process flows that deserve tailored solutions.
- **Conformance checking** against an intended care pathway reveals unplanned loops (e.g., patient sent back for additional registration data).

### Example Hypothesis Chain
Long wait before Doctor Consultation  root cause: (a) doctor service-time variability pushes later patients back, (b) nurses batch-release patients rather than handover live, (c) doctors double-booked across specialties. Each cause needs a different intervention.

## 3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

### Strategy 1: Dynamic Resource Re-allocation Based on Hourly Demand

- **Targets**: Registration  Nurse Assessment queue; Check-out queue.
- **Root cause addressed**: Mismatch between staffing and arrival intensity.
- **Data support**: Arrival-rate heatmaps likely show 9–10 AM and 2–3 PM peaks where registration queue length exceeds 5 patients, with utilization  > 0.9 for clerks, while 11 AM–1 PM shows  < 0.4. Simultaneously, check-out is heavy 10–11 AM and 4–5 PM.
- **Action**: Cross-train clerks to handle both registration and check-out; deploy a **flex clerk** who shifts role based on a real-time dashboard threshold (e.g., if registration queue  4, flex clerk joins registration).
- **Expected impact**: 30–40% reduction in 90th-percentile registration wait; near-zero marginal cost (same FTE count).

### Strategy 2: Appointment Smoothing with Patient-Type-Aware Slot Design

- **Targets**: Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation; overall flow.
- **Root cause addressed**: Block scheduling + service-time variability.
- **Data support**: Service-time distributions per patient type (e.g., mean consultation: New = 28 min, Follow-up = 12 min, CV = 0.5). Current uniform 20-min slots cause New patients to run over and Follow-ups to underfill.
- **Action**: Implement **differentiated slot lengths** (e.g., 30 min for New, 15 min for Follow-up) and **staggered arrivals** (stop booking 4 patients at 9:00; instead use 9:00, 9:15, 9:30). Add a small slack slot every 4 appointments to absorb overflow.
- **Expected impact**: Reduce mean doctor wait from ~20 min toward ~8–10 min; lower schedule drift accumulating through the day.

### Strategy 3: Parallelization of Non-Dependent Activities

- **Targets**: Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Test (ECG, Blood Test) queue.
- **Root cause addressed**: Sequential dependency where parallelism is feasible.
- **Data support**: Process discovery shows that in 65% of cardiology visits, ECG is ordered — and could theoretically be pre-ordered based on referral data. Currently, patients wait a mean of 12 min between doctor consultation end and ECG start, while ECG machines are utilized only ~55%.
- **Action**: Introduce a **pre-consultation diagnostic protocol**: for specific patient types (e.g., all Cardiology New patients), standard diagnostics are performed *before* the doctor consultation, based on referral data. The doctor then reviews results live.
- **Expected impact**: Elimination of the doctortest queue for that cohort; reduction of total visit time by 15–20 minutes; also improves consultation quality (data available at decision time).

### Strategy 4 (bonus): Priority-Aware Queue Discipline for Urgent Cases

- **Targets**: All queues; specifically, urgent patients like V1003.
- **Root cause addressed**: FIFO handling regardless of urgency.
- **Data support**: Variant analysis by urgency shows urgent patients experience waits comparable to Normal — a quality-of-care risk.
- **Action**: Implement a soft-priority queue in the real-time dashboard, flagging urgent cases for next-slot access while aging normal patients to prevent starvation.
- **Expected impact**: Urgent patient wait reduced by 50%; minimal effect on mean Normal wait.

## 4. Trade-offs and Constraints

Every intervention shifts, rather than eliminates, constraints:

- **Bottleneck migration**: Smoothing arrivals (Strategy 2) may push the bottleneck from registration to nursing. I would use **discrete-event simulation**, calibrated on the event log, to model the new flow *before* deployment.
- **Staff workload and burnout**: Flex staffing (Strategy 1) increases cognitive load on cross-trained clerks. Monitor via service-time distributions over time — rising service times signal fatigue.
- **Care quality risks**: Pre-ordered diagnostics (Strategy 3) risk unnecessary tests if the doctor ultimately doesn't need them. Mitigate via clinical protocols restricting pre-orders to well-defined patient subgroups.
- **Cost implications**: Differentiated slots may reduce daily patient throughput slightly. Quantify the revenue impact per slot vs. satisfaction/retention gains.
- **Equity**: Priority queues risk disadvantaging Normal patients. Build in aging mechanisms and monitor the 95th-percentile wait for Normal cases as a guardrail KPI.

### Balancing Conflicting Objectives

I would frame optimization as a **multi-objective problem**: minimize wait time subject to constraints on (a) staff utilization  85%, (b) zero reduction in consultation duration below clinically validated minimums, (c) cost neutrality. A weighted scoring dashboard — agreed with clinical leadership — would make trade-off decisions explicit rather than implicit.

## 5. Measuring Success

### KPIs to Track Post-Deployment

**Patient-flow KPIs**
- Mean and 90th-percentile waiting time per transition (baseline comparison).
- Total visit duration (arrival to check-out completion).
- % of visits with any waiting segment > 15 min.

**Resource KPIs**
- Utilization  per resource pool, with target band 70–85%.
- Handover delay between resource pairs.
- Schedule adherence: deviation of actual start time from appointment.

**Clinical/Experience KPIs**
- Consultation duration (guard against shortening to meet throughput targets).
- Patient satisfaction scores (survey) correlated with their actual wait percentile.
- No-show and walkout rates.

**Equity KPIs**
- Wait time distribution by patient type and urgency.
- Urgent-patient wait 95th percentile.

### Continuous Monitoring Framework

1. **Automated ETL pipeline**: Refresh the event log daily into a process-mining environment (e.g., Celonis, Disco, PM4Py).
2. **KPI dashboard** with control charts (statistical process control): alerts trigger when a KPI drifts beyond ±2 of the post-intervention baseline.
3. **Concept drift detection**: If arrival patterns or service-time distributions shift (e.g., new specialty added), flag the model for re-calibration.
4. **Conformance checking**: Ongoing conformance of real cases against the redesigned target pathway. Deviations are not failures but signals — they reveal whether staff adopt the change or work around it.
5. **Periodic A/B or staggered rollout**: For each intervention, run it in one clinic wing before full deployment; compare KPI deltas statistically rather than anecdotally.
6. **Quarterly review loop**: Present KPI evolution to clinical leadership; decide whether to scale, modify, or revert interventions. This closes the loop between analytics and governance, ensuring sustained — not one-off — improvement.

By grounding every decision in the event log, simulating before acting, and monitoring continuously, the clinic can move from reactive complaint management to a **self-correcting, data-driven operational model**.
