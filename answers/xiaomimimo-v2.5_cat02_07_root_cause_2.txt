### **2. Root Cause Analysis**
I analyzed how the event-level attributes (Resource, Region, Complexity) correlate with these extended lead times.

#### **a. Complexity is the Primary Driver**
- **Low complexity** (Cases 2001, 2004): No additional documents requested  processed in under 2 hours.
- **Medium complexity** (Case 2002): One document request  added ~20 hours of waiting time.
- **High complexity** (Cases 2003, 2005): Multiple document requests (2–3 cycles)  each cycle added 19–30 hours, leading to multi-day delays.

**Mechanism:** The “Request Additional Documents” activity creates a **waiting period** where the claim is halted until the claimant provides the required information. More complex claims trigger more requests, multiplying these waiting periods.

#### **b. Resource and Region Are Secondary Factors**
- **Region:** Both Region A and Region B show the same pattern—low-complexity cases are fast, high-complexity cases are slow. Region itself does not directly cause delays.
- **Resource:** 
  - **CSRs** (Customer Service Reps) handle submit/close quickly across all cases.
  - **Adjusters** (Mike and Lisa) both manage high-complexity claims with multiple document requests. However, Adjuster_Lisa (Region B) made **three** requests for the high-complexity case 2005, while Adjuster_Mike (Region A) made **two** for case 2003. This could indicate a difference in adjuster behavior or claim characteristics, but with only one high-complexity case per adjuster, we cannot confirm a systematic issue.
  - **Managers:** High-complexity claims are approved by Manager_Bill, while low/medium are approved by Manager_Ann. The approval step itself is fast, but the timing depends on when documents are received—so the manager is not the bottleneck.

#### **c. Timeline Analysis of Waiting Periods**

- **Case 2003:** After the last document request, the claim waited **23 hours** for approval.

### **3. Proposed Explanations and Mitigation Strategies**

#### **Why These Attributes Contribute to Delays**
- **High complexity** naturally requires more verification and documentation, but the current process handles this reactively—making multiple sequential requests that each introduce a full waiting cycle.
- **Adjuster behavior** may vary in how they request documents (e.g., batching requests vs. piecemeal), but the data is insufficient to confirm.
- **Resource constraints** could play a role if certain adjusters or managers are overloaded, but the logs show that delays occur while waiting for external document submission, not during active processing.

#### **Suggestions to Mitigate Root Causes**
1. **Front-load documentation requirements:**  
   Provide a clear checklist of required documents at the time of claim submission (especially for high/medium complexity) to reduce back-and-forth requests.

2. **Implement parallel processing:**  
   While waiting for documents, proceed with steps that do not depend on them (e.g., initial assessments, eligibility checks).

3. **Use automated reminders:**  
   Send automated notifications to claimants when documents are pending, and escalate if there’s no response within a set timeframe.

4. **Adjuster training:**  
   Train adjusters to request all necessary documents in a single communication where possible, minimizing request cycles.

5. **Predictive triage:**  
   Use historical data to predict claim complexity early and allocate experienced adjusters or pre-emptively request common documents for high-risk claims.

6. **Monitor and balance workloads:**  
   Ensure high-complexity claims are evenly distributed among adjusters and managers to prevent bottlenecks.

By addressing the root cause—inefficiencies in handling documentation for complex claims—the process can significantly reduce waiting times and improve overall performance.
