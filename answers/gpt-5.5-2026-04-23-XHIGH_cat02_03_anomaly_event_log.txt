A typical Procure-to-Pay flow would usually look like:

1. Create Purchase Requisition  
2. Approve Purchase Requisition  
3. Issue Purchase Order  
4. Receive Goods / Services  
5. Receive Vendor Invoice  
6. Match Invoice to PO / goods receipt  
7. Release Payment  
8. Close Case  

Using that as the baseline, the log contains several anomalous cases.

| Case ID | Assessment | Anomalous Sequence | Why It Is Problematic |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1001** | Appears normal | None obvious | The case follows the expected P2P sequence: requisition creation, approval, PO issuance, goods receipt, invoice receipt, matching, payment, closure. |
| **1002** | Anomalous | PO issued before requisition approval; payment released before approval, goods receipt, and invoice matching | This suggests control bypass. A purchase order should generally not be issued until the purchase requisition has been approved. More seriously, payment was released before goods were received and before the invoice was matched to the PO. This creates risk of paying for unauthorized, incorrect, or undelivered goods. |
| **1003** | Highly anomalous | Payment released before invoice received, before goods received, and before invoice matching | This is a major red flag. Payment should normally be based on an invoice and supported by matching against the PO and goods receipt. Releasing payment before receiving an invoice or confirming delivery can indicate fraud risk, duplicate or erroneous payment, or an uncontrolled advance payment. |
| **1004** | Anomalous | Goods received before PO was issued | Receiving goods before a purchase order exists suggests an after-the-fact PO or “maverick buying.” This weakens procurement controls because the organization may have accepted goods before formally authorizing the purchase, agreeing terms, or confirming budget availability. |

### Key Anomalies Identified

#### Case 1002: Payment before proper controls
Sequence excerpt:

- Create Purchase Requisition  
- Issue Purchase Order  
- Vendor Invoice Received  
- Release Payment  
- Approve Purchase Requisition  
- Receive Goods  
- Match Invoice to PO  

Issues:

- The **PO was issued before the requisition was approved**.
- The **payment was released before goods were received**.
- The **payment was released before invoice-to-PO matching**.
- Approval occurred only after payment, which looks like a retrospective approval.

This is problematic because payment was made without confirming that the purchase was authorized, that goods were delivered, or that the invoice matched the PO.

#### Case 1003: Payment before invoice and delivery
Sequence excerpt:

- Create Purchase Requisition  
- Approve Purchase Requisition  
- Issue Purchase Order  
- Release Payment  
- Vendor Invoice Received  
- Receive Goods  
- Match Invoice to PO  

Issues:

- Payment occurred before the vendor invoice was received.
- Payment occurred before goods were received.
- Payment occurred before invoice matching.

This is the most severe anomaly. In standard P2P, payment should usually occur only after invoice validation and, where applicable, three-way matching between PO, invoice, and goods receipt.

#### Case 1004: Goods received before PO
Sequence excerpt:

- Create Purchase Requisition  
- Approve Purchase Requisition  
- Receive Goods  
- Issue Purchase Order  
- Vendor Invoice Received  
- Match Invoice to PO  
- Release Payment  

Issue:

- Goods were received before the PO was issued.

This may indicate an emergency purchase, but in a standard controlled P2P process it is unusual. The organization accepted goods before a formal purchase order existed, which can lead to disputes over pricing, quantity, contract terms, or authorization.

### Overall Conclusion

- **Case 1001** follows the expected process.
- **Cases 1002, 1003, and 1004** contain process deviations.
- The most serious anomalies are in **1002 and 1003**, where payment is released before key controls such as approval, goods receipt, and invoice matching.
- **Case 1004** is also problematic because goods are received before a formal PO is issued, suggesting possible unauthorized or retrospective purchasing.
