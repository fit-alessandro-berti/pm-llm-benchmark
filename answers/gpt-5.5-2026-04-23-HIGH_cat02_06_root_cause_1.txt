## 2. Potential root causes of performance issues

### A. Escalations appear to increase cycle time

Two of the three delayed cases involve escalation:

| Case ID | Escalation Present? | Total Cycle Time |
|---|---|---:|
| 102 | Yes | 25h 10m |
| 105 | Yes | 49h 05m |

Case **105** is especially delayed. It is escalated at **Mar 1, 10:00**, but the next investigation does not happen until **Mar 2, 14:00**.

That is a waiting time of:

> **28 hours between escalation and Level-2 investigation**

This suggests a major bottleneck in the Level-2 queue or handoff process.

Case **102** also has escalation-related delays:

- Assigned to Level-1 Agent at **09:00**
- Escalated to Level-2 Agent at **11:30**
- Investigation after escalation starts at **14:00**

There is a combined delay of roughly **5 hours** between assignment and actual investigation by the escalated team.

### B. Long waiting time before investigation

Case **104** is delayed even though it was not escalated.

Key delay:

- Assigned to Level-1 Agent at **09:30**
- Investigation starts at **13:00**

That is a delay of:

> **3h 30m before investigation begins**

Compared to normal cases:

- Case 103: assignment to investigation = **15 minutes**
- Case 101: assignment to investigation = **40 minutes**
- Case 105: assignment to first investigation = **10 minutes**

So Case 104 appears to wait too long in the Level-1 queue before active work starts.

### C. Overnight delays between investigation and resolution

A repeated pattern appears in the delayed cases:

| Case ID | Investigation | Resolution | Waiting Time |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| 102 | Mar 1, 14:00 | Mar 2, 09:00 | **19h** |
| 104 | Mar 1, 13:00 | Mar 2, 08:00 | **19h** |
| 105 | Mar 2, 14:00 | Mar 3, 09:00 | **19h** |

This suggests that once a ticket reaches a certain point in the afternoon, resolution may be deferred until the next working day.

The consistent **19-hour delay** is a strong indicator of an operational bottleneck, possibly caused by:

- End-of-day processing cutoffs
- Lack of after-hours coverage
- Tickets being paused overnight
- Waiting for specialized approval or technical input
- Low prioritization of aging tickets

### D. Case 105 combines multiple delay factors

Case **105** is the longest-running case because it contains several bottlenecks:

1. It is escalated.
2. It waits **28 hours** after escalation before investigation resumes.
3. After Level-2 investigation, it waits another **19 hours** before resolution.
4. It takes an additional **30 minutes** to close after resolution.

This case suggests a serious handoff or capacity issue at the Level-2 stage.

## 3. How these factors increase cycle time

The main contributors to increased cycle time are not the simple administrative steps such as receiving, triaging, or closing. Those are generally short.

The major delays occur in three places:

### 1. Waiting for investigation to begin

If a ticket is assigned but not actively investigated for several hours, the total cycle time increases even before any real diagnostic work starts.

This is visible in:

- **Case 104**: 3h 30m from assignment to investigation
- **Case 102**: 5h from Level-1 assignment to post-escalation investigation

### 2. Escalation handoffs

Escalation introduces extra waiting. A ticket may sit in a Level-2 queue before someone takes ownership.

This is especially visible in:

- **Case 105**: 28h from escalation to Level-2 investigation

Escalation itself is not necessarily bad, but delayed escalation handling can significantly increase resolution time.

### 3. Overnight deferral

The delayed cases all show long overnight gaps before resolution. This suggests that tickets not resolved the same day are pushed to the next business day, creating large cycle-time increases.

---

## Recommendations

### A. Introduce escalation SLAs

Set clear service-level targets for escalated tickets, for example:

- Level-2 must acknowledge escalated tickets within **30 minutes**
- Level-2 investigation must begin within **2 hours**
- Aging escalations should trigger alerts

This would directly address the issue seen in Case 105.

### B. Improve early ticket classification

If a ticket is likely to require Level-2 support, it should be identified during triage and routed directly to the specialized team.

This could reduce unnecessary Level-1 waiting time and prevent late escalations.

### C. Monitor waiting time between assignment and investigation

The process should track queue time after assignment. Long waits, such as the **3h 30m** delay in Case 104, indicate workload or prioritization problems.

Useful metrics include:

- Average time from assignment to investigation
- Maximum queue wait by agent/team
- Number of tickets waiting more than 1 hour after assignment

### D. Address overnight resolution delays

Because several tickets wait around **19 hours** between investigation and resolution, the organization should investigate whether there is an end-of-day bottleneck.

Possible improvements:

- Add late-day coverage for open investigations
- Prioritize same-day resolution for tickets already investigated
- Create handover procedures for unresolved tickets at the end of the day
- Use automated reminders for tickets idle overnight

### E. Prioritize aging tickets

Tickets that have already been open for many hours should receive higher priority. This would prevent cases like 105 from sitting in the escalation queue for more than a day.

## Summary

The delayed cases are **102, 104, and 105**. The strongest performance issues are:

1. **Escalation delays**, especially in Case 105.
2. **Long waits before investigation**, especially in Cases 102 and 104.
3. **Overnight delays between investigation and resolution**, visible in Cases 102, 104, and 105.

The biggest bottleneck appears to be around **handoffs and waiting queues**, particularly for escalated tickets and tickets that remain unresolved near the end of the working day.
