**1. Identifying Instance-Spanning Constraints and Their Impact**

I would begin by importing the three-month event log into a process mining platform (e.g., Celonis, Disco, PM4Py, or Apromore) and enriching it with the provided case attributes (Order Type, Destination Region, Requires Cold Packing, Hazardous Material).

**Discovery and Quantification Techniques:**

- **Shared Cold-Packing Stations:**  
  Use resource-activity filtering to isolate ōPackingö events where Requires Cold Packing = TRUE. Apply interval-based analysis on START/COMPLETE timestamps to reconstruct concurrent usage of the 5 specialized stations. Calculate the **contention ratio** (number of cold orders ready but waiting for a cold station) and **queue time due to cold station unavailability**. I would also mine a multi-instance dependency graph showing which orders were blocked by which other orders.

- **Shipping Batching:**  
  Group cases by Destination Region and Shipping Label Generation timestamp. Identify ōbatch completion waiting timeö as the difference between an orderÆs Quality Check completion and its Shipping Label Generation timestamp when multiple orders share the same batch identifier. Compute the **batch waiting time distribution** and **batch size frequency** per region.

- **Priority Handling (Express Orders):**  
  Detect preemption by looking for overlapping Packing/Quality Check intervals where an Express order START occurs while a Standard order is still active on the same resource. Measure **preemption delay** (time the Standard order was paused or pushed back) and **knock-on effect** on subsequent activities.

- **Hazardous Material Limits:**  
  Use a sliding time-window analysis (e.g., 30-minute windows) to count concurrent hazardous orders in Packing or Quality Check. Flag any violations of the ōmax 10ö rule and, more importantly, identify periods where the count approached 10 and non-hazardous orders experienced artificial waiting. Calculate **throughput loss** during high-hazard periods.

**Differentiating Within-Instance vs. Between-Instance Waiting Time:**

I would apply **waiting time decomposition**:
- Within-instance waiting = time between COMPLETE of previous activity and START of next activity for the *same case* when the required resource is idle and no batch is pending.
- Between-instance waiting = time when the resource is occupied by another case, a batch is incomplete, or the hazardous limit is at capacity.  
This is achieved by cross-referencing the caseÆs ready time with the actual resource occupancy timeline reconstructed from all cases (using interval algebra). The difference yields the ōinstance-spanning waiting timeö metric, which is the primary KPI for each constraint.

**2. Analyzing Constraint Interactions**

The constraints are not independent; they create compound effects:

- An Express cold-packing order can preempt a Standard order at a cold station, which in turn increases the cold-station queue for other cold orders, amplifying contention.
- When several Hazardous orders share the same Destination Region, the batching constraint forces them to wait together. Because they count toward the hazardous limit, they can block the entire Packing/Quality Check area for non-hazardous orders even if the batch is not yet full.
- Priority Express orders going to a region with slow batch accumulation can simultaneously occupy limited cold stations *and* delay the batch, creating a double penalty for Standard orders in the same region.

Understanding these interactions is essential because optimizing one constraint in isolation (e.g., larger batches) can worsen another (e.g., hazardous limit violations or cold-station starvation). Process mining correlation analysis between the four constraint-related waiting metrics will reveal the strongest interaction pairs, guiding where joint optimization is most valuable.

**3. Developing Constraint-Aware Optimization Strategies**

**Strategy 1: Dynamic Cold-Packing Allocation with Priority-Aware Preemption Rules (Addresses Shared Cold-Packing + Priority Handling)**  
Introduce a real-time scheduling rule: when an Express cold order arrives and all 5 stations are busy, the system identifies the Standard cold order with the *least remaining packing time* (predicted via regression on historical data) and allows controlled preemption only if the Standard order has not already been preempted once. Use predictive analytics (trained on the event log) to forecast cold-station demand 30 minutes ahead and temporarily reclassify non-perishable stations as cold-capable when demand spikes.  
*Expected outcome:* Reduction in cold-station queue time by 35-50% and lower preemption-induced delays for Standard orders, directly lowering instance-spanning waiting time.

**Strategy 2: Adaptive Batching with Hazardous-Aware Batch Triggers (Addresses Shipping Batching + Hazardous Limits)**  
Replace fixed batching by region with a dynamic trigger that combines: (a) minimum batch size, (b) maximum wait time (shorter for Express), and (c) current hazardous concurrency level. If hazardous count is 8, the system lowers the batch size threshold for hazardous-heavy regions or creates a ōhazardous-firstö sub-batch that bypasses normal batching but still respects the max-10 rule. Historical region-volume and hazardous patterns are used to set optimal thresholds via simulation (see below).  
*Expected outcome:* Reduced batch waiting time for Express orders and prevention of hazardous-induced throughput drops, improving overall on-time delivery.

**Strategy 3: Capacity-Buffered Hazardous Flow Lane + Process Decoupling (Addresses Hazardous Limits + Interactions)**  
Physically or logically designate a dedicated ōHazardous Flow Laneö with its own 2-3 packing/quality stations that are excluded from the general hazardous count (still respecting the global max-10 across all lanes). Orders flagged as hazardous are routed to this lane unless it is saturated. Additionally, decouple the Quality Check from Packing for non-hazardous orders by allowing parallel quality checks at non-station resources when hazardous concurrency is high. The routing decision uses a lightweight predictive model that estimates remaining hazardous load for the next hour.  
*Expected outcome:* Maintained regulatory compliance while increasing effective packing throughput by 15-25% during peak hazardous periods and reducing interaction effects with batching.

**4. Simulation and Validation**

I would build a hybrid simulation model (discrete-event + agent-based) in tools such as AnyLogic, SimPy, or Celonis Process Simulation, seeded with the discovered process model, resource calendars, and probabilistic distributions from the event log.

**Key modeling requirements to capture instance-spanning constraints:**
- Entities carry attributes (Express, Cold, Hazardous, Region).
- Shared resources are modeled as limited pools with preemption rules.
- A global ōHazardous Concurrency Monitorö that blocks new hazardous entries when count 10.
- A batching agent that holds completed orders until batch criteria are met, using the same adaptive logic proposed above.
- Realistic arrival patterns and attribute correlations learned from the log.

I would run Monte-Carlo experiments comparing the baseline (current) model against each proposed strategy and their combinations, measuring:
- End-to-end cycle time (mean and 95th percentile)
- On-time delivery rate
- Average instance-spanning waiting time per constraint
- Resource utilization and violation frequency (hazardous limit, cold-station queue length)

Only strategies that improve KPIs without increasing regulatory violations would be selected for pilot implementation.

**5. Monitoring Post-Implementation**

I would establish a live process mining dashboard with the following views:

- **Constraint-Specific Waiting Time Breakdown:** Daily stacked bar chart showing within-instance vs. between-instance waiting for each of the four constraints.
- **Cold-Station Contention Heatmap:** Real-time and historical view of queue length and utilization of the 5 cold stations, with alerts when contention exceeds a threshold.
- **Hazardous Concurrency Gauge:** Live counter of concurrent hazardous orders in Packing/QC, with compliance trend line.
- **Batch Efficiency Metrics:** Average batch size, batch waiting time distribution, and percentage of orders exceeding maximum allowed batch wait, segmented by Express/Standard and region.
- **Interaction Impact Score:** A composite metric that correlates cold-station queue time with hazardous levels and batch delays, highlighting periods where interactions are strongest.

Automated conformance checking would continuously verify that the new scheduling and routing rules are followed and that instance-spanning waiting times trend downward. Weekly root-cause analysis on any remaining high-waiting orders would feed back into model recalibration.

This comprehensive, data-driven approach ensures that optimization efforts explicitly acknowledge and manage the interdependencies between process instances rather than treating each order in isolation, which is the core limitation of traditional process mining when applied to highly shared-resource environments.
