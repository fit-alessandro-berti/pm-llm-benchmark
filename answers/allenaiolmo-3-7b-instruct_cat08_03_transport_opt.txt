## 1. Process Discovery and Conformance Checking

Map different systems’ event names to a consistent schema using mapping rules and, if necessary, manual review of sample logs.
- **Temporal Resolution:** Ensure all events are timestamped with sufficient granularity (e.g., seconds or minutes).
- **Attribute Enrichment:** Attach relevant attributes to each event: vehicle ID, driver ID, package ID (if applicable), location (latitude/longitude), route step number, planned vs. actual time, status codes, and flags for delays or exceptions.
- **Data Cleansing:** Handle missing data (e.g., interpolate or flag gaps), resolve duplicates (e.g., multiple scanner events for the same physical event), and remove noise (e.g., erroneous GPS fix points).
- **Context Enrichment:** Where possible, link maintenance events to the affected vehicle/date to trace maintenance windows and their potential impact on operations.

**Process Discovery:**
- Use **process discovery algorithms** such as Alpha, Heuristic Miner, or Inductive Miner to automatically infer the *as-is* process model from the integrated event log. Since this is a delivery operation, I’d expect a model with sequences like:
    1. Start shift at depot
    2. Load packages (if visible)
    3. Begin planned route (from dispatch)
    4. Travel between stops (with potential delays)
    5. Customer pick-up/delivery attempts
    6. Possible failed delivery / re-attempt
    7. Return to depot (possibly with maintenance or delay)
    8. End shift

## 2. Performance Analysis and Bottleneck Identification

### **Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)**
To diagnose and monitor efficiency, I would calculate the following logistics-specific KPIs from the event data:

1. **On-Time Delivery Rate (OTDR):**
   - % of packages delivered within the customer’s time window (derived from arrival/departure timestamps at the customer’s location).
2. **Average Delivery Time (ADT):**
   - Total elapsed time from leaving the customer to returning to the depot (or completing the shift), per delivery.
3. **Average Travel Distance per Delivery / Time per Stop:**
   - Use GPS data to compute actual route distances and time spent in transit versus at customer sites.
4. **Fuel Consumption per km or per delivery:**
   - Aggregate fuel usage data (if available) with route distances to identify inefficiencies in driving behavior (e.g., excessive idling or detours).
5. **Vehicle Utilization Rate:**
   - Ratio of actual miles/km driven to maximum possible (based on vehicle capacity and scheduled routes).
6. **Frequency of Delays and Failed Deliveries:**
   - Count and analyze the causes of failed deliveries (e.g., customer unavailable, address error) to assess process robustness.
7. **Traffic Delay Incidence:**
   - Correlate GPS speed data with timestamps to quantify exposure to traffic congestion and its impact on schedule.
8. **Maintenance Downtime:**
   - Calculate total time vehicles are out of service due to scheduled or unscheduled maintenance.
9. **Driver Overtime Frequency:**
   - Track end-of-shift times vs. scheduled shifts to identify overtime patterns.

### **Process Mining for Bottleneck Identification**
- **Performance Metrics Over Process Flows:** Use *process performance analysis* (e.g., performance indicators in Inductive Miner or Petri net extensions) to identify steps (e.g., specific stops, transitions) where delays or high variance are concentrated.
    - For example, if "Arrive at Customer" always takes >10 minutes, it may indicate long customer interaction times or parking challenges.
- **Case Splitting and Cohort Analysis:** Use variant mining or case splitting to compare high- and low-performing drivers or routes, revealing where process variability is greatest (e.g., certain drivers are late more often due to navigation issues).
- **Time Dependency Analysis:** Use time-distributed modeling (e.g., in process mining software like ProM or Disco) to see how long tasks take under different conditions (e.g., rush hour, bad weather).
- **Root cause of delays:** Correlate event logs with traffic APIs or external data (if available) to see if specific locations or times of day are consistently problematic.

*Example findings:*
- Bottlenecks may include frequent traffic jams near a particular road (detected as a "high delay" transition in the process model).
- A particular driver’s route consistently deviates from the plan, leading to more stops and slower times.
- Certain delivery zones have higher failed delivery rates due to inaccurate addresses or customer unavailability.

## 3. Root Cause Analysis for Inefficiencies

Process mining enables **root cause analysis** by revealing *why* bottlenecks and failures occur, beyond just where they happen.

### **Potential Root Causes (with Process Mining Evidence):**

#### a. **Suboptimal Route Planning / Static vs. Dynamic Routing**
- **Evidence:** If the discovered process shows drivers frequently detouring or not following the planned route (e.g., via conformance checking), this indicates the dispatch system is static and ignores real-time conditions.
- **Analysis:** Use *categorical splits* in the process model to see which planned stops are most frequently skipped or reordered.

#### b. **Inaccurate Travel Time Estimation**
- **Evidence:** If GPS data shows that planned travel times are consistently underestimated (e.g., actual time to customer A is 2x the planned time), this suggests poor estimation in dispatch or outdated maps.
- **Analysis:** Compare planned vs. actual timestamps for travel transitions using process mining’s “time deviation” analysis.

#### c. **Traffic Congestion and External Factors**
- **Evidence:** High variability in travel times between similar locations at different times suggests traffic hotspots.
- **Analysis:** Integrate GPS speed and timestamp data with process events to visualize when and where traffic delays are most severe and how often drivers are affected.

#### d. **Customer Service Variability**
- **Evidence:** Large variance in “Time at Customer” events may indicate issues with customer interaction (e.g., finding the address, handling signatures).
- **Analysis:** Use *activity duration distributions* to flag areas with abnormal delays.

#### e. **Vehicle Maintenance Issues**
- **Evidence:** Frequent unscheduled maintenance stops or long downtimes indicated by maintenance logs during operational shifts.
- **Analysis:** Correlate maintenance events with delivery schedules using process extensions to see if maintenance coincides with peak delivery periods, causing delays or missed deliveries.

#### f. **Driver Behavior / Skill Gaps**
- **Evidence:** If some drivers have higher failure rates or longer completion times, process mining can perform *cohort analysis* to compare behaviors.
- **Analysis:** Use *sequence mining* to identify common error patterns in driver execution (e.g., repeated missed stops, improper scanning).

#### g. **Failed Delivery Attempts**
- **Evidence:** High rates of failed deliveries linked to specific steps (e.g., customer not home) may reveal issues with customer communication or address data entry.
- **Analysis:** Use *conditional splitting* to see which process paths lead to failed deliveries and whether these are due to systemic issues (e.g., wrong addresses in dispatch) or external factors.
