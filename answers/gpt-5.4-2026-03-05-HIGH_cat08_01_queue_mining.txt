Below is a practical process-mining approach I would use for this clinic. The key idea is to use the **start and complete timestamps** to separate:

- **service time**: how long an activity actually takes once it begins, and  
- **waiting time / queue time**: how long the patient waits before the next activity starts.

That distinction is essential: long visits are not always caused by slow clinical work; often they are caused by **idle time between steps**.

---

## 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

### 1.1 Define waiting time correctly

For each visit (case), I would first reconstruct the patient journey in timestamp order and pair each activity’s `START` and `COMPLETE`.

From this, I would derive:

- **Activity service time**  
  `service time(activity) = complete timestamp - start timestamp`

- **Inter-activity waiting time / queue time**  
  For two consecutive activities \(A \rightarrow B\):  
  `wait time(AB) = start(B) - complete(A)`

In plain terms, **waiting time** is the elapsed time between the moment the patient is ready for the next step and the moment that next step actually starts.

### Important nuance
In more complex flows, the “next activity” may depend on multiple prior steps. For example, if a specialist review can only start after both a test and a nurse note are complete, then the true wait is:

- `start(Specialist Review) - max(completion of all required predecessor steps)`

So ideally I would not rely only on simple direct-follow pairs; I would use a discovered process model to determine when an activity became **enabled**.

### Special caveat: “waiting for registration”
If the first event in the log is `Registration START`, then the log does **not** directly capture time from patient arrival to registration start. To analyze that queue, the clinic would need either:

- patient arrival timestamp,
- kiosk check-in timestamp, or
- scheduled appointment time.

Without one of those, I can analyze **in-clinic waiting after registration starts**, but not the pre-registration queue itself.

---

### 1.2 Metrics to characterize queues

For each key transition (for example, `Registration  Nurse Assessment`, `Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation`, `Doctor Consultation  ECG`, `Final Clinical Step  Check-out`), I would calculate:

#### Queue-time distribution metrics
- **Average wait time**
- **Median wait time**  
  Often more reliable than average when a few extreme delays skew the mean.
- **Maximum wait time**
- **90th / 95th percentile wait time**  
  Very useful for patient experience, because dissatisfaction is often driven by the “long tail”.
- **Standard deviation / IQR**  
  High variability matters because unpredictable waits feel worse and destabilize operations.

#### Queue volume metrics
- **Frequency of the queue**
  - How many visits encounter this transition?
  - What % of all visits experience it?
- **Total waiting hours accumulated**
  - Very important for prioritization.  
  A moderate delay that affects many patients may be more important than a rare severe delay.
- **# and % of cases with excessive waits**
  - e.g., >15 min, >30 min, or specialty-specific thresholds
- **Queue length over time**
  - Number of patients ready for an activity but not yet started
  - Example: patients whose nurse assessment is complete but whose doctor consult has not started yet

#### Case-level flow metrics
- **Total visit duration**
- **Total service time**
- **Total waiting time**
- **Flow efficiency**
  - `total service time / total visit duration`
  - In clinics, this is often surprisingly low; much of the visit can be waiting.

#### Stratification dimensions
All metrics should be broken down by:
- specialty
- doctor
- nurse/team
- diagnostic type
- time of day
- day of week
- patient type (new vs follow-up)
- urgency
- resource/room/equipment

This avoids masking problems. For example, “doctor wait” may look acceptable overall but be severe for **new cardiology patients on Monday mornings**.

---

### 1.3 How to identify the most critical queues

I would not rank queues on average wait alone. A better prioritization combines:

1. **Severity**  
   Long median / p90 wait

2. **Frequency**  
   How many patients are affected

3. **Contribution to total delay**  
   How much of all patient waiting time comes from this queue

4. **Clinical / experience importance**  
   Waiting for a doctor or urgent triage is usually more sensitive than waiting for checkout

5. **Segment impact**  
   Does it disproportionately affect:
   - urgent patients,
   - new patients,
   - certain specialties,
   - elderly/high-risk patients?

A practical prioritization score could be:

**Criticality = volume × p90 wait × risk weight**

Where risk weight is higher for:
- urgent pathways,
- clinically sensitive transitions,
- high-complaint touchpoints.

### Example of prioritization logic
A queue should receive immediate attention if it has one or more of these characteristics:

- large share of total patient waiting hours
- p90 wait far above target
- high breach rate of service targets
- affects high-volume pathways
- affects urgent or vulnerable patients
- consistently recurs at specific times/resources

In many outpatient settings, the most critical queues are often:
- registration during arrival peaks,
- nurse-to-doctor handoff,
- doctor-to-diagnostic test queue,
- diagnostic-to-specialist review queue.

But I would validate this from the log rather than assume it.

---

## 2. Root Cause Analysis

Once the worst queues are identified, the next step is to determine **why** they occur. Queue mining tells you where the waiting is; root cause analysis explains whether it is caused by **capacity, coordination, variability, or scheduling design**.

### 2.1 Likely root-cause categories

| Root cause category | What it looks like in the data | Relevant mining analysis |
|---|---|---|
| **Resource bottleneck** | Long waits coincide with high resource utilization and busy rooms/equipment | Resource analysis, utilization, workload over time |
| **Handover / coordination delay** | Patient waits even when downstream resource appears available | Handover analysis, idle-gap analysis |
| **Service-time variability** | Some doctors/activities take much longer or are less predictable | Duration analysis, provider comparison |
| **Appointment scheduling mismatch** | Queue spikes at top of hour, morning peaks, after lunch | Arrival pattern analysis, schedule overlay |
| **Patient mix differences** | New patients, urgent patients, or certain specialties wait longer | Cohort/variant analysis |
| **Rework / extra steps** | Some visits loop through repeated assessments or unexpected diagnostics | Variant analysis, loop detection |
| **Shared constrained assets** | Multiple specialties compete for one room/equipment | Cross-resource bottleneck analysis |

---

### 2.2 How process mining helps pinpoint the cause

#### A. Resource bottleneck analysis
Using resource and room/equipment data, I would measure:

- utilization by hour/day
- concurrency of active work
- queue length versus available capacity
- whether waits happen when all qualified resources are busy

Examples:
- If `Doctor Consultation (Cardio)` wait peaks when all cardiology rooms and cardiologists are occupied, that is a **true capacity bottleneck**.
- If `ECG Test` wait spikes when Room 3 is occupied but the technician is free, the bottleneck may be **room/equipment**, not staffing.

A common operational insight: once utilization gets very high (roughly >85–90% sustained), queues grow rapidly.

---

#### B. Handover and coordination analysis
Not every queue is a capacity problem.

I would look for cases where:
- nurse completes,
- doctor/room is available shortly afterward,
- yet consult starts much later.

That suggests:
- poor signaling,
- manual patient calling,
- batching behavior,
- incomplete prep work,
- patients not being moved promptly,
- documentation or coordination lag.

Process mining is very good at exposing these “hidden waits between boxes”.

---

#### C. Service time variability analysis
Queueing gets worse when service times are highly variable.

I would compare duration distributions by:
- doctor
- specialty
- patient type
- urgency
- first visit vs follow-up
- diagnostic type

Examples:
- New patients may require much longer registration and consultation than follow-ups.
- One provider may have a much higher average consultation time or more variability.
- Some tests may be short on average but very inconsistent, which destabilizes downstream flow.

This is important because the fix may not be “more staff”; it may be **better slot design**.

---

#### D. Variant analysis
I would compare:
- **fast visits vs slow visits**
- **patients with diagnostics vs no diagnostics**
- **new vs follow-up**
- **urgent vs normal**
- **specialty-specific pathways**

This reveals whether long visits are associated with:
- extra handoffs,
- rework,
- certain routing patterns,
- certain specialties,
- certain providers.

For example, a variant may show:

`Registration  Nurse  Doctor  ECG  Doctor Review  Check-out`

If this variant has much longer waiting than a pathway where ECG is completed before the doctor review without extra handoff delay, that creates a redesign opportunity.

---

#### E. Arrival pattern and scheduling analysis
I would overlay:
- patient arrivals / case starts
- appointment templates
- no-show rates (if available)
- urgent add-ons
- staffing calendars

Typical findings:
- too many appointments released at 8:00 or 9:00
- same slot length used for new and follow-up patients
- urgent patients inserted without protected capacity
- doctors start late relative to nurse/registration buildup
- shared diagnostics receive synchronized peaks from several specialties

---

#### F. Predictive explanation
Beyond descriptive mining, I would build a simple explanatory model for long waits, such as:

**Target:** wait > 30 minutes before doctor  
**Predictors:** hour, day, specialty, provider, patient type, urgency, prior activity duration, queue length, room utilization

Even a decision tree or regression can quantify which factors most strongly drive delays.

---

## 3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

I would test changes using the mined process and, ideally, a **discrete-event simulation / what-if model** before full rollout. That helps quantify impact and avoid moving the bottleneck elsewhere.

Below are four concrete strategies.

---

### Strategy 1: Demand-aligned staffing and room allocation

**Targets**
- Registration queue
- Nurse assessment queue
- Diagnostic queues (ECG, X-ray, blood draw)
- Doctor queue during known peaks

**Root cause addressed**
- Capacity-demand mismatch by hour/day
- Peak overload while other periods are underused

**How data supports it**
If event logs show that:
- arrivals surge between 8:00–10:00,
- queue length spikes at those times,
- clerk/nurse/tech utilization is near saturation,
then static staffing is misaligned with demand.

**Actions**
- Shift start times to match actual peaks
- Stagger lunches/breaks
- Use float nurse/tech capacity during morning peaks
- Assign rooms dynamically by specialty demand
- Rebalance staff from low-demand periods to high-demand periods before hiring

**Potential impact**
After simulation/calibration, a common target would be:
- **20–35% reduction in p90 wait** for the affected queue
- lower morning congestion
- improved total visit duration without major headcount growth

**Why this is attractive**
It often improves flow with **limited cost increase**, because the first move is reallocation, not expansion.

---

### Strategy 2: Redesign appointment templates and scheduling rules

**Targets**
- Wait from nurse to doctor
- Wait from doctor to diagnostics
- End-of-session backlogs and overtime

**Root cause addressed**
- Bunched arrivals
- Same slot size for unequal visit types
- No buffer for urgent insertions
- Misalignment between clinic consult load and diagnostic capacity

**How data supports it**
If logs show:
- top-of-hour queue spikes,
- new patients taking materially longer than follow-ups,
- urgent cases causing cascading delays,
- diagnostics overloaded right after consultation blocks,
then appointment design is a major driver.

**Actions**
- Separate slot lengths for new vs follow-up patients
- Stagger starts instead of releasing many patients at the same time
- Reserve limited urgent slots per session
- Dynamically release unused urgent slots later
- Synchronize specialty schedules with downstream diagnostic capacity
- Avoid creating a morning “front-loading” wave

**Potential impact**
A realistic target after pilot/simulation:
- **15–30% reduction in average doctor wait**
- reduced variability
- lower overtime and fewer late-running sessions

**Healthcare-specific benefit**
This preserves care quality better than simply compressing consultation times.

---

### Strategy 3: Fast-track and partial parallelization for eligible patients

**Targets**
- Registration  nurse wait
- Doctor  diagnostic wait
- Overall visit duration for routine pathways

**Root cause addressed**
- Overly serial patient flow
- Unnecessary handoffs
- Administrative work done too late in the visit

**How data supports it**
If variant analysis shows that routine follow-up patients or predictable diagnostic cohorts have long waits mainly because of sequence design—not clinical complexity—then parts of the process can be moved earlier or run in parallel.

**Actions**
- Pre-visit digital forms and insurance verification
- Self check-in / kiosk for low-complexity patients
- Standing-order vitals or labs before doctor for defined cohorts
- Direct-to-room or fast-track path for stable follow-up patients
- Pre-order common diagnostics where clinically appropriate

**Potential impact**
For eligible cohorts, typical targets would be:
- **30–50% reduction in registration service + wait burden**
- **10–25% reduction in total visit duration**
- improved patient experience for follow-up visits

**Important safeguard**
Only use fast-track or pre-workups where clinical governance is clear. New/complex/urgent patients should not be inappropriately standardized.

---

### Strategy 4: Real-time flow coordination and queue visibility

**Targets**
- Nurse  doctor handoff
- Doctor  diagnostic handoff
- Diagnostic  checkout handoff
- “Invisible” delays when the next resource is actually available

**Root cause addressed**
- Poor coordination rather than lack of capacity
- Delays in calling the next patient
- Batch behavior
- Lack of real-time view of who is ready and who is free

**How data supports it**
If logs show long waits despite downstream idle time, the issue is not staffing volume; it is orchestration.

**Actions**
- Real-time dashboard of patients “ready for next step”
- Automatic alerts when activity completes
- Queue view by specialty/resource/room
- Prioritization rules visible to all staff
- Escalation when wait exceeds threshold
- Next-available assignment logic for rooms/equipment

**Potential impact**
Where handover delay is a major component:
- **10–20% reduction in handoff waits**
- improved predictability
- better perceived flow without adding clinical staff

**Why it matters**
This is often one of the cheapest ways to reduce waste if the clinic already has decent EHR/event capture.

---

## 4. Trade-offs and Constraints

Any intervention can create side effects. In outpatient flow, the biggest risk is **solving one queue by creating another**.

### Likely trade-offs

#### A. Staffing changes
- May improve waits but increase labor cost
- Shift changes can affect staff satisfaction
- A float role may reduce continuity

**Mitigation:** start with reallocation and staggered shifts before adding FTEs.

---

#### B. Appointment smoothing
- Longer slots for new patients may reduce daily appointment capacity
- Protected urgent slots may go unused if poorly managed

**Mitigation:** dynamically release unused urgent slots; optimize for both access and flow.

---

#### C. Fast-track / parallelization
- Risk of inappropriate standardization
- Could miss clinical nuances if eligibility rules are weak
- Digital pre-visit tools may disadvantage some patient groups

**Mitigation:** use strict inclusion criteria, manual override, and non-digital alternatives.

---

#### D. Real-time dashboards
- IT cost and change-management effort
- Requires clean timestamp discipline
- Can create alert fatigue

**Mitigation:** keep alerts focused on exceptions; pilot with one specialty first.

---

### Balancing conflicting objectives

The clinic should treat this as a **multi-objective optimization** problem:

1. Reduce waiting time
2. Control cost
3. Maintain care quality
4. Protect staff workload
5. Preserve fairness across patient groups

I would recommend using:
- **guardrail metrics** for quality and staff burden
- **what-if simulation** before rollout
- **phased pilots** in one specialty or one queue first
- **case-mix-adjusted evaluation**, so success is not overstated due to easier patient mix

Examples of guardrails:
- no drop in average consultation duration below clinically acceptable levels
- no increase in rework or repeat visits
- no sustained overtime increase
- no deterioration for urgent patients while improving normal queues

---

## 5. Measuring Success

### 5.1 KPIs after implementation

I would monitor success at three levels: patient, process, and resource.

#### Patient-flow KPIs
- **Median total visit duration**
- **90th percentile total visit duration**
- **Median and p90 wait time** for each critical queue
- **% of visits within target total duration**
- **% of patients with excessive wait** by queue
- **Flow efficiency** = service time / total visit time

#### Access / fairness KPIs
- Wait metrics by:
  - patient type
  - urgency
  - specialty
  - provider
  - hour/day
- This ensures improvements are not achieved by shifting delays to a subgroup.

#### Resource KPIs
- Utilization of clerks, nurses, doctors, rooms, diagnostics
- Overtime / late-running sessions
- Idle time where relevant
- Queue length by time of day

#### Experience and quality guardrails
- Patient satisfaction / complaints about waiting
- No-show / abandonment / “left before completion” if available
- Rework rates / repeat assessments
- Clinical quality indicators relevant to the clinic

---

### 5.2 Ongoing process monitoring

The same event-log structure can support continuous monitoring.

#### Monitoring approach
- Refresh dashboards daily/weekly
- Use control charts for:
  - median wait,
  - p90 wait,
  - total visit duration,
  - queue length peaks
- Segment by specialty, urgency, patient type, and time of day
- Detect process drift:
  - new variants,
  - rising wait in one specialty,
  - changes in provider duration,
  - shift of bottleneck from doctor to diagnostics or checkout

#### Evaluation design
To show true impact, I would compare:
- **pre vs post implementation**
- ideally with a control group (another specialty/unit)
- adjusted for seasonality and patient mix

A strong evaluation method would be:
- interrupted time series, or
- phased rollout comparison.

---

## Final recommendation

The clinic should not start by “adding staff everywhere.” The right sequence is:

1. **Mine the queues** and quantify where waiting really occurs  
2. **Separate capacity problems from coordination problems**  
3. **Segment by specialty, patient type, urgency, and time of day**  
4. **Pilot targeted interventions** supported by simulation  
5. **Monitor the same log continuously** to sustain gains

In many outpatient clinics, the biggest improvements come from a combination of:

- better peak-hour resource alignment,
- smarter appointment templates,
- fast-tracking predictable patient types,
- and tighter handoff coordination.

That usually reduces both **average wait** and **p90 wait** while controlling cost better than broad staffing increases.
