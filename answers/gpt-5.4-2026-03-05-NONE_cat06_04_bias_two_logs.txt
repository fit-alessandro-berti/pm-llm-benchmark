**The biased log is Group B (Unprotected Group).**

### Why Group B shows bias
In **Group B**, applicants with:
- `LocalResident = TRUE`, and
- membership in a specific `CommunityGroup` (e.g. **Highland Civic Darts Club**)

receive a **positive score adjustment**: `+10 (Community Boost)`.

This adjustment appears in:
- **U001**: 720  730  **Approved**
- **U003**: 695  705  **Approved**

By contrast:
- **U002**, who is also a local resident but has `CommunityGroup = None`, gets **no boost** and is **Rejected**.
- In **Group A**, all applicants have `LocalResident = FALSE`, `CommunityGroup = None`, and **no score adjustment is ever applied**.

### How the bias manifests
The bias is not just in outcomes, but in the **process**:
1. **Protected group applicants never receive the community boost.**
2. **Unprotected group applicants sometimes receive an extra +10 solely due to community-linked attributes.**
3. This changes the effective score used for review and final decision.

That means **Group B has access to a favorable mechanism unavailable to Group A**, creating **systematic advantage**.

### Evidence of systematic difference
Compare similar cases:
- **P002**: score 710, no adjustment  **Rejected**
- **U002**: score 710, no adjustment  **Rejected**
- **U003**: original score 695, +10 adjustment to 705  **Approved**

This suggests the boost can help borderline applicants cross a decision threshold. If a protected-group applicant had the same raw score profile as U003, they would likely not receive the same benefit.

### Role of the attributes
- **LocalResident** and **CommunityGroup** act as proxy-favoring variables.
- Even if protected status is not explicitly used, these variables may correlate with group membership.
- The `ScoreAdjustment` operationalizes that advantage, embedding it into the decision pipeline.

### Conclusion
**Group B’s log exhibits bias** because applicants in the unprotected group receive preferential treatment through a **community-based score boost**, while protected-group applicants do not have access to the same adjustment. This creates **unequal treatment** and can produce **disparate outcomes in final approvals**.
