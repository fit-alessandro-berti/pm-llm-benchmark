# Process Mining Analysis: Manufacturing Assembly Line Event Log

## Overview

After analyzing the sensor readings across all 15 events, I identified **six distinct phases** based on clear transitions in the numerical patterns. Below is my complete analysis.

---

## Phase Identification and Labeling

### Phase 1: **Idle / Machine Startup** (Events 1–3)

| Sensor | Readings |
|--------|----------|
| Temperature | 20–21°C (ambient) |
| Pressure | 1 bar (baseline) |
| Vibration | 0 Hz |
| Material Flow | 0 units/s |
| Tool Position | 0 mm (home position) |
| Energy | 0.40–0.50 kWh (minimal draw) |

**Rationale:** All readings are at or near baseline/ambient levels. The machine is powered on (there is a small energy draw) but no mechanical work is occurring. The tool is at its home position (0 mm), no material is flowing, and there is zero vibration. This is characteristic of an **idle state or startup phase** where the machine is initialized and awaiting the start of a production cycle.

### Phase 2: **Cutting Metal** (Events 4–6)

| Sensor | Readings |
|--------|----------|
| Temperature | 505558°C (rapidly rising) |
| Pressure | 11.5 bar (moderate increase) |
| Vibration | 303538 Hz (high and escalating) |
| Material Flow | 5 units/s (steady, significant) |
| Tool Position | 5710 mm (progressive advancement) |
| Energy | 2.002.503.00 kWh (high and increasing) |

**Rationale:** This phase exhibits the hallmark signatures of a **cutting operation**:

- **Rapidly rising temperature** results from friction between the cutting tool and the workpiece.
- **High vibration levels (30–38 Hz)** are consistent with a saw, mill, or lathe engaging material — cutting generates substantial mechanical oscillation.
- **Steady material flow (5 units/s)** indicates raw material being continuously fed into the cutting zone.
- **Progressive tool advancement (5710 mm)** shows the cutting tool moving deeper or along the workpiece in a controlled path.
- **Escalating energy consumption** reflects the significant power required to drive a cutting tool through metal.
- The **moderate pressure increase** may reflect clamping force or coolant pressure applied during cutting.

### Phase 3: **Assembling Parts** (Events 7–8)

| Sensor | Readings |
|--------|----------|
| Temperature | 40°C (moderate, stable) |
| Pressure | 2 bar (elevated, steady) |
| Vibration | 10 Hz (low, steady) |
| Material Flow | 2 units/s (reduced, steady) |
| Tool Position | 15 mm (fixed) |
| Energy | 2.00 kWh (moderate, constant) |

**Rationale:** This phase shows **stable, moderate conditions** across all sensors, which is characteristic of **assembly operations**:

- **Temperature has dropped** from the cutting phase but remains above ambient — consistent with mechanical joining rather than high-friction operations.
- **Pressure is at its highest (2 bar) and steady**, suggesting a pressing or fitting operation where parts are being pushed together or fastened under controlled force.
- **Low, steady vibration (10 Hz)** indicates controlled mechanical activity — e.g., a press fit, screw driving, or component insertion — rather than aggressive material removal.
- **Reduced material flow (2 units/s)** suggests smaller components (fasteners, sub-assemblies) being fed in, rather than bulk raw material.
- **Fixed tool position (15 mm)** indicates the tool/fixture is holding position to align or join parts.
- **The repetition of identical readings across Events 7 and 8** reinforces that this is a steady, controlled process — assembly is typically more uniform than cutting or welding.

---

### Phase 4: **Welding** (Event 9)

| Sensor | Readings |
|--------|----------|
| Temperature | 80°C (sharp spike — highest in log) |
| Pressure | 2 bar |
| Vibration | 50 Hz (spike — highest in log) |
| Material Flow | 0 units/s |
| Tool Position | 15 mm (unchanged from assembly) |
| Energy | 5.00 kWh (spike — highest in log) |

**Rationale:** Event 9 is the most dramatic in the entire log, and its signature is unmistakably **welding**:

- **Temperature spikes to 80°C** — the highest reading, consistent with the intense localized heat generated by welding (note: 80°C is the ambient sensor reading near the weld zone; actual weld temperatures would be far higher but are attenuated by sensor distance).
- **Vibration peaks at 50 Hz** — welding processes (especially arc or resistance welding) produce strong electromagnetic and mechanical vibrations.
- **Energy consumption surges to 5.00 kWh** — welding is among the most energy-intensive manufacturing operations, requiring high current for arc generation or resistance heating.
- **Material flow drops to 0** — no new material is being fed in; instead, previously assembled parts are being fused together at their joint.
- **Tool position remains at 15 mm** — the weld is being performed at the same station where assembly occurred, which is logical (assemble parts, then weld them in place).
- **The brevity (single event / ~5 seconds)** is consistent with a spot weld or tack weld on the assembled component.

**Rationale:** After the high-energy welding event, conditions dramatically calm down, which is consistent with a **quality inspection / testing phase**:

- **Temperature is rapidly cooling** — the part is no longer being actively processed with heat-generating operations; it's being examined.
- **Pressure returns to baseline** — no mechanical force is being applied.
- **Very low vibration (1–5 Hz)** — this could be from a measurement probe, coordinate measuring machine (CMM), or non-destructive testing (NDT) equipment performing light contact measurements.
- **Minimal material flow (0–1 units/s)** — possibly representing calibration fluid, air for pneumatic gauging, or test specimens being sampled.
- **Tool position advances to 20 mm** — the inspection probe or sensor is moving to measurement positions along the completed part.
- **Low but non-trivial energy consumption** — inspection equipment (laser scanners, vision systems, ultrasonic testers) requires some power but far less than cutting or welding.
- **The steady, repeating pattern of Events 11–12** suggests systematic measurement at a fixed point — characteristic of dimensional inspection or weld quality testing.

## Key Insights

1. **The process follows a logical manufacturing sequence:** raw material is cut  cut pieces are assembled  assembled joints are welded  the welded product is inspected  the machine resets. This is a coherent and common manufacturing workflow.

2. **Energy consumption serves as the strongest single discriminator:** Idle (~0.45), Cutting (~2.5), Assembly (~2.0), Welding (5.0), Inspection (~1.4).

3. **Material flow is a powerful differentiator:** High flow = cutting (material being consumed); moderate flow = assembly (components being fed); zero flow = welding (fusion of existing parts); near-zero flow = inspection.

4. **The temperature-vibration pairing** effectively distinguishes welding (both peak) from cutting (both high but below welding) from assembly (moderate temp, low vibration) from inspection (near-ambient temp, minimal vibration).
