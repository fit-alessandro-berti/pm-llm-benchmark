# Strategic Process Mining & Scheduling Optimization Framework for Precision Parts Inc.

## Executive Summary
Precision Parts Inc. operates in a complex, high-mix environment where static scheduling rules are insufficient to handle sequence-dependent setups, resource contention, and dynamic disruptions. This framework leverages **Process Mining (PM)** to transform raw MES event logs into actionable intelligence. By reconstructing the actual flow of value, we identify bottlenecks, quantify inefficiencies, and deploy adaptive, data-driven scheduling strategies that prioritize throughput over rigid adherence to simple rules.

---

## 1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics

To move beyond descriptive reporting, we must use Process Mining to reconstruct the "actual" process and compare it against the "planned" process. The MES event logs serve as the source data for this reconstruction.

### A. Reconstructing Actual Flow & Execution
We will ingest the Event Log into a PM tool (e.g., Celonis, RPA, or custom Python/R pipeline) to generate an **Event Log** containing:
*   **Traceability:** Linking `Job Released` to `Task End`.
*   **Resource Paths:** Mapping which machines processed which jobs.
*   **State Transitions:** Identifying transitions between states (e.g., `Idle` -> `Setup` -> `Processing`).

### B. Specific Process Mining Metrics & Techniques

| KPI Category | Process Mining Technique | Metric Definition & Calculation from Logs |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Flow Time / Lead Time** | **Performance Measurement** | $\text{Lead Time} = \text{Timestamp(Task End)} - \text{Timestamp(Job Released)}$. We will aggregate this per job to calculate the distribution of lead times (mean, variance, percentiles). |
| **Task Waiting Times** | **Process Variance Analysis** | For each task transition from Machine A to B: $\text{Waiting Time} = \text{Start Time at B} - \text{End Time at A}$. We analyze the distribution of this gap per work center (e.g., MILL-03). |
| **Resource Utilization** | **Throughput Analysis** | Calculate $\frac{\text{Total Active Time}}{\text{Total Shift Time}}$. <br> *Active Time* = Setup + Processing. <br> *Idle Time* = Gaps between jobs or breakdown recovery time. We distinguish between "Starvation" (waiting for resources) and "Blocking" (holding resources). |
| **Sequence-Dependent Setups** | **Correlation Analysis** | Group events by `Machine ID`. For each job sequence $(J_i, J_{i+1})$, calculate $\text{Setup Duration} = \text{Time(Setup End for } J_i) - \text{Time(Setup Start for } J_i)$. We correlate the properties of $J_i$ and $J_{i+1}$ to find setup duration patterns. |
| **Schedule Adherence** | **Conformance Checking** | Compare `Due Date` against `Task End Time`. Calculate Tardiness ($\text{Actual} - \text{Due}$). We classify jobs as "On-Time," "Early," or "Late." |
| **Disruption Impact** | **Causal Analysis** | Identify events tagged as `Breakdown`. Measure the recovery duration (Start to Finish of Breakdown) and the delay caused to subsequent tasks. Calculate $\text{Delay} = \text{Recovery Time} + \text{Queue Buildup during Recovery}$. |

## 2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies

By visualizing the mined data, we can identify specific pathologies that current dispatching rules (e.g., simple FCFS/EDD) fail to address.

### A. Bottleneck Identification & Impact
*   **Diagnosis:** Process Mining will reveal machines with high utilization but long queues. For example, if `MILL-03` consistently shows long waiting times for `JOB-7001` (as seen in the log snippet), it may be a bottleneck.
*   **Evidence:** We will calculate the **Work-in-Process (WIP)** inventory per machine using Little's Law: $\text{WIP} = \text{Flow Rate} \times \text{Lead Time}$. A spike in WIP at `MILL-03` indicates a bottleneck causing downstream starvation or upstream congestion.
*   **Pathology:** Over-allocation of resources on critical machines, leading to job queuing and increased lead times.

### B. Poor Prioritization & Tardiness
*   **Diagnosis:** If "High Priority" jobs are not being processed before "Medium" priority jobs despite their due dates.
*   **Evidence:** We will perform a **Variant Analysis** comparing the flow of "On-Time" vs. "Late" jobs. If "Late" jobs cluster around specific times or machines, it suggests they were blocked by other jobs that were not prioritized correctly (e.g., older low-priority jobs occupying resources).
*   **Pathology:** Ignorance of due date urgency in favor of arrival time or simple FCFS logic.

### C. Suboptimal Sequencing & Setup Waste
*   **Diagnosis:** Jobs with similar material properties being processed out of order, leading to long setups.
*   **Evidence:** Correlation analysis on `Setup Required` and `Previous Job`. If the log shows `JOB-6998` (Steel) followed by `JOB-7001` (Aluminum), but setup time was 23.5 mins (vs planned 20), this indicates friction. If similar jobs are processed sequentially, we expect shorter setups.
*   **Pathology:** Lack of sequence optimization at the workstation level.

### D. Starvation & Bullwhip Effect
*   **Diagnosis:** Downstream machines waiting for upstream output that never arrives (Starvation) or WIP fluctuating wildly due to scheduling instability.
*   **Evidence:** We will analyze the **Inter-Arrival Time** at downstream work centers. If `MILL-02` is often idle while `CUT-01` is overloaded, it indicates poor coordination and lack of look-ahead scheduling.

## 3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness

Process Mining helps differentiate between **Capacity Constraints** (machines are physically full) and **Scheduling Logic Errors** (machines are free but jobs aren't assigned).

### A. Limitations of Static Dispatching Rules
*   **Root Cause:** Current rules (e.g., FCFS/EDD) are reactive and myopic. They do not consider the *future* state of the shop floor.
*   **PM Insight:** PM reveals that "High Priority" jobs often wait in queues for machines because the scheduler didn't anticipate a breakdown or upstream delay. The rule does not account for **Resource Contention**.

### B. Inaccurate Estimation & Data Quality
*   **Root Cause:** Planning relies on optimistic "Planned" durations, which do not match "Actual" durations (e.g., 60 min vs 66.4 min in the log).
*   **PM Insight:** By analyzing the variance between `Planned` and `Actual`, we quantify the **Error Rate**. If this error is high (>10%), the scheduler cannot trust its input data, leading to failed delivery dates.

### C. Sequence-Dependent Setup Blindness
*   **Root Cause:** The scheduling software likely treats setup time as a fixed value or ignores it entirely in favor of simple processing time.
*   **PM Insight:** We can cluster jobs by their "Setup Profile." If the log shows that Job A always requires 30 mins after Job B, but only 5 mins after Job C, the current scheduler is not exploiting this knowledge, leading to unnecessary setup time accumulation.

### D. Inadequate Disruption Handling
*   **Root Cause:** Breakdowns are treated as "recovery" events without rescheduling.
*   **PM Insight:** Analysis of `Breakdown Start` events shows that the system waits for maintenance completion before re-sequencing, rather than dynamically rerouting jobs to free resources (if available).

## 4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies

Based on the analysis above, we propose three sophisticated strategies that replace static rules with dynamic, data-informed logic.

### Strategy 1: Adaptive Look-Ahead Priority Rules (ALPR)
**Core Logic:** A dispatching rule that calculates a composite score for each job considering not just its own attributes, but also the *state of the resource and downstream dependencies*.
*   **Formula:** $\text{Score} = w_1 \cdot \text{DueDatePenalty} + w_2 \cdot \text{RemainingProcessingTime} + w_3 \cdot \text{SetupCost}$
*   **PM Insight Integration:**
    *   $w_3$ (Setup Cost) is derived from the correlation analysis of Strategy 3. If Job A and B have low setup cost together, they get a bonus score if queued together.
    *   The system uses the mined distribution of `Task Duration` to calculate **Remaining Processing Time** probabilistically (e.g., using P90 instead of Mean), rather than point estimates.
*   **Addressing Pathology:** Mitigates tardiness by accounting for setup time and lookahead capacity, preventing bottlenecks.
*   **Expected Impact:** Reduced Tardiness by 15-20%, Improved Resource Utilization.

### Strategy 2: Probabilistic Predictive Scheduling (PPS)
**Core Logic:** Instead of scheduling based on single-point estimates, the scheduler generates a schedule based on probability distributions for task durations and setup times.
*   **PM Insight Integration:**
    *   Use historical logs to train a regression model or Bayesian inference engine. Input: `Job Type`, `Operator ID`, `Previous Job`. Output: $\text{Duration} \sim \text{Normal}(\mu, \sigma)$.
    *   The scheduler schedules the job such that it finishes by the Due Date with 95% probability (using the P90 value of the distribution).
*   **Addressing Pathology:** Accounts for inherent process variability and operator skill variance (e.g., OP-105 vs others) which static rules ignore.
*   **Expected Impact:** More realistic Lead Times, Reduced "False Commitments" to customers.

### Strategy 3: Sequence-Dependent Setup Learning (SDSL)
**Core Logic:** A localized optimization strategy specifically for bottleneck machines that groups similar jobs or sequences them to minimize total setup time per shift.
*   **PM Insight Integration:**
    *   Identify "Setup Clusters" from the log. E.g., Jobs with material X always require 10 mins after Material Y, but 45 mins after Material Z.
    *   Implement a **Genetic Algorithm** or **Simulated Annealing** at the bottleneck machine (e.g., `MILL-03`) that minimizes $\sum \text{Setup Times}$.
*   **Addressing Pathology:** Directly reduces setup time waste and improves flow rate through bottlenecks.
*   **Expected Impact:** Significant reduction in WIP accumulation, faster throughput at critical work centers.

## 5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement

To ensure these strategies do not introduce new risks (e.g., over-scheduling causing starvation), we must validate them rigorously.

### A. Discrete-Event Simulation (DES)
We will build a Digital Twin of the shop floor using parameters extracted from the Process Mining data.

*   **Parameterization:**
    *   `Task Duration`: Modeled as distributions (e.g., Weibull) derived from the "Actual" column in logs, not just mean values.
    *   `Breakdown Probability`: Derived from frequency of `Breakdown Start` events over time.
    *   `Setup Time`: Modeled with sequence-dependent logic based on Strategy 3 insights.
*   **Testing Scenarios:**
    *   **Stress Test:** Simulate 10% higher load to see if ALPR maintains throughput.
    *   **Shock Test:** Randomly inject breakdowns at `MILL-02` to test the robustness of PPS and SDSL.
    *   **Comparison:** Run simulations with Baseline (FCFS/EDD), Strategy 1, Strategy 2, and Strategy 3 in parallel over a "virtual year" (e.g., 5,000 simulated jobs).

### B. Evaluation Metrics for Simulation
*   **Throughput:** Jobs completed per shift.
*   **Tardiness Rate:** % of jobs finishing after due date.
*   **WIP Level:** Average number of jobs in system at any time $t$.
*   **Resource Utilization:** % of time machines are active (including setup).

### C. Continuous Monitoring & Adaptation Framework
Once deployed, the system must evolve to prevent "Drift" (where the model becomes obsolete due to process changes).

1.  **KPI Dashboarding:** Real-time tracking of Tardiness, WIP, and Utilization against simulation baselines.
2.  **Automated Drift Detection:**
    *   If `Actual Task Duration` consistently deviates from `Predicted Duration` (Strategy 2) by >5% for a specific job type, trigger an alert to retrain the predictive model.
    *   If `Setup Time` patterns shift (e.g., new material introduced), flag the SDSL algorithm for re-clustering.
3.  **Feedback Loop:**
    *   The MES logs continue to be ingested daily.
    *   A "Learning Phase" runs weekly where simulation parameters are adjusted based on observed performance vs. target KPIs.
4.  **Exception Handling:**
    *   If a job is marked as `Urgent` (Priority Change), the system must automatically re-run the ALPR algorithm to see if the job should jump the queue, ensuring responsiveness without disrupting flow unnecessarily.

### Conclusion
By shifting from static rules to a **Process Mining-Informed Adaptive Framework**, Precision Parts Inc. will transition from reactive firefighting to proactive optimization. The combination of **Look-Ahead Rules**, **Probabilistic Scheduling**, and **Setup Learning** addresses the core root causes of inefficiency, ensuring that the MES system is not just a recording tool, but a strategic engine for production excellence.
