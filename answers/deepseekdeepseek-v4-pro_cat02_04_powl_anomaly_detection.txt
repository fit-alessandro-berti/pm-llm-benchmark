The provided POWL model represents an “Order-to-Cash” process, but its structure deviates from standard practices due to two significant anomalies introduced by XOR and LOOP operators.

---

### 1. Anomalies Identified

#### a) Optional Credit Check (`XOR(C, skip)`)
```python
xor_credit = OperatorPOWL(operator=Operator.XOR, children=[C, skip])
```
- The model allows a choice between executing the **Check Credit** step (`C`) or taking a **silent transition** that bypasses it entirely.
- **Why it breaks expectations**: In any credible Order-to-Cash process, credit verification is a mandatory financial control. By making it optional, the process permits an order to proceed without any assessment of the customer’s creditworthiness.

#### b) Unbounded Loop between Approval and Invoicing (`LOOP(A, I)`)
```python
loop_approve_invoice = OperatorPOWL(operator=Operator.LOOP, children=[A, I])
```
- The `LOOP` operator defines a block where the “do” part (`Approve_Order`) is executed, followed by an optional “redo” part (`Issue_Invoice`). If the invoice is issued, the loop repeats from approval again, allowing **any number** of approval–invoice cycles.
- The loop also permits **zero invoices** – after the first approval, the process can exit the loop without ever issuing an invoice, moving directly to *Receive Payment*.
- **Why it breaks expectations**:  
  - Normally, an order is approved **once**, and a single invoice is generated before payment.  
  - Multiple approvals of the same order suggest redundant or unauthorized reauthorisation, possibly to correct errors.  
  - Issuing multiple invoices for one order can confuse the customer, cause duplicate payments, or reflect systematic rework.  
  - Skipping invoicing entirely is equally anomalous: a payment would be received without a formal payment request, bypassing standard revenue recognition controls.
