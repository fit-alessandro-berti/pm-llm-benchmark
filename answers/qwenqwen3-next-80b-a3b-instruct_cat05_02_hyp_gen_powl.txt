## **1. Identification of Anomalies in the POWL Model**

The given POWL model introduces several **structural anomalies** that deviate from the intended ideal process flow:

### **Anomaly 1: Infinite Loop Between Evaluation and Approval (E  P  E  P …)**
- **Description**: The `Operator.LOOP` construct with children `[E, P]` implies that after evaluation (`E`), the process may proceed to approval (`P`), but then **loop back** to `E` again — potentially indefinitely.
- **Why it’s anomalous**: 
  - In the ideal flow, evaluation happens **once**, followed by a **single approval**.
  - Repeated evaluation and approval suggest a **feedback loop** that should not exist in standard insurance claim handling unless there is a formal "rework" or "reassessment" path — which is not implied here.
- **Impact**: Could lead to claims being stuck in approval-evaluation cycles, inflating processing time and resource usage.

### **Anomaly 2: Optional Customer Notification via XOR with Skip**
- **Description**: The `XOR` between `N` (Notify Customer) and `skip` (silent transition) means customer notification is **optional** — it may be omitted entirely.
- **Why it’s anomalous**:
  - Notification is a **critical compliance and customer service step** in insurance workflows.
  - Skipping it violates service level agreements (SLAs), regulatory transparency requirements (e.g., GDPR, insurance disclosure rules), and customer trust.
- **Impact**: Customers may be unaware of claim decisions, leading to disputes, complaints, or legal exposure.

### **Anomaly 3: Premature Claim Closure via Direct Edge from A  C**
- **Description**: The partial order includes `A  C` (Assign Adjuster  Close Claim), **bypassing** evaluation, approval, and notification entirely.
- **Why it’s anomalous**:
  - Closing a claim immediately after assignment — without any assessment — is **operationally nonsensical**.
  - This violates the fundamental logic of insurance claims: **no evaluation = no basis for closure**.
- **Impact**: Claims may be falsely closed as "rejected" or "nullified" without justification, leading to financial loss, audit failures, or fraud.

### **Anomaly 4: Lack of Strict Ordering Between XOR and C**
- **Description**: There is **no enforced edge** from `xor` to `C`, only from `loop` to `xor` and `A` to `C`.
- **Why it’s anomalous**:
  - The model allows `C` (Close Claim) to occur **before** the XOR branch completes — meaning a claim can be closed **before** the customer is notified (or even before the system decides whether to notify).
- **Impact**: Creates **race conditions** in execution — closure could happen before notification, violating procedural integrity.

### **Anomaly 5: Partial Ordering as a Source of Non-Determinism**
- **Description**: The use of `StrictPartialOrder` (instead of a fully sequential or tree-based model) allows **concurrent or out-of-order execution** where dependencies are not fully constrained.
- **Why it’s anomalous**:
  - Process models for compliance-sensitive domains (like insurance) should be **deterministic** and **audit-trail friendly**.
  - Partial ordering introduces ambiguity: "Can C happen before E? Can N happen after C?" — questions that should have clear answers.

## **3. Database Queries to Verify Anomalies**

We now use the actual event data in `claim_events` (joined with `claims`) to **detect real-world occurrences** of the anomalies.

### **Query 1: Detect Claims Closed Without Evaluation or Approval**
> *Verifies Anomaly 3 (A  C) and Anomaly 4 (C before XOR)*

```sql
SELECT 
    c.claim_id,
    c.customer_id,
    c.claim_amount,
    MIN(ce_submission.timestamp) AS received_at,
    MIN(ce_assign.timestamp) AS assigned_at,
    MIN(ce_close.timestamp) AS closed_at,
    MAX(CASE WHEN ce_eval.activity = 'E' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS has_evaluated,
    MAX(CASE WHEN ce_approve.activity = 'P' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS has_approved,
    MAX(CASE WHEN ce_notify.activity = 'N' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS has_notified
FROM claims c
JOIN claim_events ce_submission ON c.claim_id = ce_submission.claim_id AND ce_submission.activity = 'R'
JOIN claim_events ce_assign ON c.claim_id = ce_assign.claim_id AND ce_assign.activity = 'A'
JOIN claim_events ce_close ON c.claim_id = ce_close.claim_id AND ce_close.activity = 'C'
LEFT JOIN claim_events ce_eval ON c.claim_id = ce_eval.claim_id AND ce_eval.activity = 'E'
LEFT JOIN claim_events ce_approve ON c.claim_id = ce_approve.claim_id AND ce_approve.activity = 'P'
LEFT JOIN claim_events ce_notify ON c.claim_id = ce_notify.claim_id AND ce_notify.activity = 'N'
WHERE ce_close.timestamp < COALESCE(ce_eval.timestamp, '9999-12-31')
   OR ce_close.timestamp < COALESCE(ce_approve.timestamp, '9999-12-31')
GROUP BY c.claim_id, c.customer_id, c.claim_amount, received_at, assigned_at, closed_at
HAVING 
    MAX(CASE WHEN ce_eval.activity = 'E' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) = 0
    OR MAX(CASE WHEN ce_approve.activity = 'P' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) = 0
ORDER BY closed_at DESC;
```

> **Interpretation**: Any claim where `closed_at` occurs before any `E` or `P` event is a **premature closure**. This directly confirms the `A  C` anomaly in practice.

### **Query 2: Detect Claims with Multiple Approvals (Loop Anomaly)**
> *Verifies Anomaly 1: Repeated E  P cycles*

```sql
SELECT 
    c.claim_id,
    COUNT(CASE WHEN ce.activity = 'P' THEN 1 END) AS approval_count,
    COUNT(CASE WHEN ce.activity = 'E' THEN 1 END) AS evaluation_count,
    STRING_AGG(ce.activity, '  ' ORDER BY ce.timestamp) AS event_sequence

WHERE ce.activity IN ('E', 'P')
GROUP BY c.claim_id
HAVING COUNT(CASE WHEN ce.activity = 'P' THEN 1 END) > 1
ORDER BY approval_count DESC;
```

> **Interpretation**: Any claim with **more than one approval** (`P`) likely experienced a loop. If evaluation count is also >1, it confirms the E-P-E-P cycle. This validates the `LOOP` construct is active in production.

### **Query 4: Detect Claims with Out-of-Sequence Events (Partial Ordering Anomaly)**
> *Verifies Anomaly 5: General process drift*

```sql
WITH ordered_events AS (
    SELECT 
        claim_id,
        activity,
        timestamp,
        ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY claim_id ORDER BY timestamp) AS seq
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity IN ('R', 'A', 'E', 'P', 'N', 'C')
),
expected_sequence AS (
    SELECT 'R' AS activity, 1 AS pos UNION ALL
    SELECT 'A', 2 UNION ALL
    SELECT 'E', 3 UNION ALL
    SELECT 'P', 4 UNION ALL
    SELECT 'N', 5 UNION ALL
    SELECT 'C', 6
)
SELECT 
    oe.claim_id,
    STRING_AGG(oe.activity, '  ' ORDER BY oe.timestamp) AS actual_sequence,
    STRING_AGG(es.activity, '  ' ORDER BY es.pos) AS expected_sequence
FROM ordered_events oe
JOIN expected_sequence es ON oe.activity = es.activity
GROUP BY oe.claim_id
HAVING COUNT(*) != 6  -- missing steps
   OR MAX(CASE WHEN es.pos = 3 AND oe.activity != 'E' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) = 1  -- E not in position 3
   OR MAX(CASE WHEN es.pos = 4 AND oe.activity != 'P' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) = 1  -- P not in position 4
   OR MAX(CASE WHEN es.pos = 5 AND oe.activity != 'N' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) = 1  -- N not in position 5
   OR MAX(CASE WHEN es.pos = 6 AND oe.activity != 'C' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) = 1; -- C not in position 6
```

> **Interpretation**: Identifies claims with **missing, reordered, or duplicated** steps — confirming widespread process deviation.
