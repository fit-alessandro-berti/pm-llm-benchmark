# Data-Driven Queue Optimization for a Multi-Specialty Outpatient Clinic

## 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

### Defining "Waiting Time" in the Event Log

In the event log, every activity for a given Case ID (Visit ID) has a START and a COMPLETE timestamp. Waiting time (queue time) is derived **between activities** for the same case:

> **Waiting Time between Activity A and Activity B = `Start_Timestamp(B) – Complete_Timestamp(A)`**

If the result is positive, the patient is waiting in a queue. If negative, the next activity had already begun (overlap, possibly due to reordering or shared resources); we would flag this for data quality investigation rather than treating it as a negative wait.

We also distinguish three related concepts:
- **Service Time (Processing Time):** `Complete_Timestamp(A) – Start_Timestamp(A)` — the actual duration the resource is busy.
- **Idle Time (for resources):** Periods when a resource is staffed but has no patient.
- **Lead Time / Cycle Time:** Total visit duration = `Complete_Checkout – Start_Registration`.

This decomposition is essential because long waiting times can be caused by long service times, by handovers, by resource unavailability, or by poor scheduling — each pointing to a different root cause.

### Key Metrics to Characterize Queues

For every pair of consecutive activities (A  B) — e.g., Registration  Nurse Assessment, Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation, Doctor Consultation  ECG Test — we calculate:

| Metric | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Mean waiting time** | A simple indicator of average burden — but can be misleading due to outliers. |
| **Median & IQR (P25–P75)** | Robust to outliers, reflects the "typical" experience. |
| **90th / 95th percentile** | Captures the worst-case experience for the long tail of patients — a key driver of complaints. |
| **Maximum waiting time** | Identifies extreme pain points; important for urgent/fragile patients. |
| **Queue frequency (% of cases affected)** | How many patients actually hit this queue — affects the "average" patient, not just unlucky ones. |
| **Service time distribution (mean, std dev, CV)** | High variability is often a hidden source of queue build-up (Kingman's formula). |
| **Resource utilization** | Per-resource % busy time during working hours — the classic bottleneck indicator. |
| **% of patients exceeding a clinical threshold** (e.g., >30 min wait) | Patient-centric KPI, directly tied to satisfaction. |

We can also compute **cohort-level metrics** sliced by:
- Patient Type (New vs. Follow-up)
- Urgency (Normal vs. Urgent)
- Specialty (Cardio, Ortho, etc.)
- Time-of-day / Day-of-week
- Specific resource (e.g., Dr. Smith vs. Dr. Jones)

### Identifying the *Most Critical* Queues

A queue that is "long on average" is not necessarily the worst if only a handful of patients experience it. A queue that is "short on average" may still be the worst if *thousands* of patients hit it daily. We therefore use a **composite prioritization framework**:

> **Criticality Score = (Mean Wait) × (Frequency) × (Impact Weight)**

Where Impact Weight can be adjusted by:
- The 90th percentile (penalizing high-variance queues),
- The proportion of urgent/fragile patients affected,
- The downstream effect on total visit duration (e.g., a 20-minute ECG wait may be more tolerable than a 20-minute wait *before* the doctor, because the patient is now in the consultation area and the perceived wait is shorter).

We would then rank pairs and apply a **Pareto analysis (80/20 rule)**: typically, 3–5 transition pairs explain the majority of total waiting pain. These are the focus of the root-cause analysis and optimization. For instance, our preliminary expectation in such clinics is often:
- "Registration  Nurse Assessment" (registration bottleneck)
- "Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Test" (doctor + diagnostics decoupling)
- "Diagnostic Test  Specialist Review / Check-out" (test result turnaround)

## 2. Root Cause Analysis

Once we know *which* queues dominate, the next step is to understand *why* they form. The event log data enables a rich set of process mining analyses.

### A. Resource Bottleneck Analysis

- **Resource utilization per role and per individual:** Compute % time each Clerk, Nurse, Doctor, Tech, and room is busy. A utilization >85% typically indicates a structural bottleneck; >95% is almost certainly the source of queueing.
- **Resource availability vs. demand curves:** Plot resource load per hour vs. number of patient arrivals per hour. Mismatches (e.g., 2 doctors available but 8 nurse-handoffs arriving in a 15-min window) directly cause queues.
- **Multi-resource activity analysis:** For activities like ECG, both *Tech X* and *Room 3* are involved. Mine which combinations of resource-pair are involved in long waits to identify whether it is a *people* bottleneck or a *room/equipment* bottleneck.

### B. Handover and Coordination Analysis

- **Resource handover networks:** Build a social-network-like graph where edges represent handovers (e.g., Clerk A  Nurse 1). Hubs with high in-degree (e.g., a single popular nurse) are vulnerable points.
- **Inter-activity delay analysis:** A long wait *between* activities is not just about resource unavailability — it can also reflect coordination failures (e.g., the nurse forgets to call the patient, the doctor is unaware the patient is ready). Mine the *variance* of inter-activity delays: if a particular (Resource A  Resource B) pair has consistently high variance, it suggests a process coordination issue, not a capacity issue.

### C. Service Time Variability (Kingman's Law)

A key insight from queueing theory: **queue length is more sensitive to service-time variability than to mean service time.** Using the event log we can compute the **Coefficient of Variation (CV = std dev / mean)** of each activity's service time. A high CV (e.g., >1.0) on, say, Doctor Consultation explains why patients back up even if the doctor is "averaging" their workload.

### D. Appointment Scheduling & Arrival Patterns

- **Time-of-day queue analysis:** Plot waiting time by hour of registration. A "hump" of long waits at 9–10 AM suggests either over-booking at that hour or arrival clustering.
- **Arrival pattern analysis:** Compare scheduled vs. actual arrival times (if available). Early/late arrivals combined with rigid scheduling cause peaks.
- **Block scheduling impact:** If the clinic uses block scheduling (e.g., "all cardio patients in the morning"), queueing patterns may systematically differ across specialties.

### E. Patient-Type and Urgency Effects

- **Variant analysis (process maps):** Mine the process variants — the distinct end-to-end paths patients take. Compare variants for *New* patients vs. *Follow-up* patients and *Urgent* vs. *Normal*. We expect New patients to have longer overall visit durations due to extra paperwork/assessment; Urgent patients may inappropriately wait behind Normal patients if no triage policy is enforced.
- **Discriminative analysis:** Build a regression/decision-tree model predicting waiting time using features extracted from the log (patient type, urgency, specialty, time-of-day, attending resource). Features with high importance are candidate root causes.

### F. Performance Spectrum and Dotted Chart Analysis

Process mining tools (Celonis, ProM, Disco, etc.) allow:
- **Dotted chart** visualization: each case is a line, each dot is an event. Gaps = waiting time, dense regions = queue accumulation, vertical color bands = resource utilization.
- **Performance spectrum:** Identifies the bottlenecks along the critical path.
- **Throughput analysis:** Cases per unit time, filtered by activity pair.

By combining all of the above, we can attribute the critical queues to specific root causes — for example: *"The 35-minute average wait between Doctor Consultation and ECG is driven by (a) Room 3 utilization of 96% between 10:00–11:30 AM, and (b) Doctor Smith's habit of ordering ECG only at the end of the consultation rather than mid-way."*

## 3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

Based on typical patterns observed in such clinics, here are **four** concrete, data-supported strategies. (Strategies are presented generally; the actual queue they target should be selected based on the analysis above.)

### Strategy 1: Demand-Capacity Reshaping via Right-Sized Appointment Slot Templates

**Target Queue(s):** "Registration  Nurse Assessment" and "Nurse  Doctor" (front-of-house queues).

**Root Cause Addressed:** A mismatch between patient arrival patterns and resource availability — arrivals are clustered (e.g., 70% of patients arrive in the first 90 minutes), but resources are scheduled uniformly.

**How Data Supports It:**
- Analyze per-hour arrival counts vs. per-hour resource availability. The log shows the times patients START registration, which approximates arrival.
- Calculate the **inter-arrival time distribution** and **service time distributions** for each activity.
- Use **queueing simulation (M/G/c approximation or discrete-event simulation)** calibrated from the log to test new scheduling templates (e.g., staggered slots, dynamic overbooking for low-no-show patient types, longer slots for New patients).

**Specific Proposal:** Replace uniform "every 15 min" slots with a **staggered, patient-type-aware template**: e.g., a New-patient slot of 30 min followed by two Follow-up slots of 15 min, repeated, with no slot scheduled exactly on the hour to prevent arrival clusters. Allocate an extra clerk during the 9:00–10:30 AM peak identified from the data.

**Expected Impact (illustrative):** Queueing theory and similar published case studies suggest that re-staggering can reduce average front-office waiting time by **25–40%** without adding resources.

### Strategy 2: Pre-Order Diagnostics & Parallelization ("Parallel-Track Diagnostic Ordering")

**Target Queue(s):** "Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Test" and the downstream "Test  Check-out" queue.

**Root Cause Addressed:** Sequential flow where the patient waits for the doctor to order a test, then waits for the test slot, then waits again. This is a classic "process design" bottleneck rather than a resource bottleneck.

**How Data Supports It:**
- Variant analysis shows that patients who undergo diagnostics spend **on average 60 min more** total visit time, with most of the wait occurring *between* the doctor's order and the test starting.
- Service time analysis shows the *Doctor* spends only 2–3 min on the order itself; the rest of the doctor's time is unrelated to the test.

**Specific Proposal:** Implement a **clinical decision support rule** in the EHR: for top-3 high-frequency, low-risk symptom patterns per specialty (e.g., chest pain  ECG + troponin; chronic knee pain  weight-bearing X-ray), the system **auto-suggests pre-ordering** the diagnostic the moment the patient is roomed with the nurse, not after the doctor. The patient goes from nurse assessment directly to the test, then to the doctor with results in hand.

**Expected Impact:** Based on parallelization theory and analogous primary-care implementations, this can reduce total visit duration for affected patients by **20–30%** and reduce post-doctor queue length by **40%+** because tests are already in progress when the doctor finishes.

### Strategy 3: Dynamic Resource Pooling & Task Shifting for Nurses

**Target Queue(s):** "Nurse  Doctor" (when nurse assessment is the bottleneck).

**Root Cause Addressed:** Rigid role boundaries, where a nurse specialized in one area is unavailable, even though a colleague in a different specialty could perform the assessment.

**How Data Supports It:**
- Resource analysis shows Nurse 1 has 95% utilization while Nurse 2 has 60% during overlapping hours.
- Activity analysis confirms Nurse 2's competencies (from historical cases) include 90%+ of the same assessment types.
- Handover network analysis reveals a hub-and-spoke pattern (everyone hands off to Nurse 1) creating a structural bottleneck.

**Specific Proposal:** Create a **"floating nurse pool"** that handles generic Nurse Assessment activities across specialties. Implement a real-time worklist (e.g., a digital queue board) that auto-assigns the next available nurse regardless of specialty, with a 30-min overlap block of cross-training already in place.

**Expected Impact:** In a published analogous case (van der Aalst-style healthcare analyses), workload balancing across a nurse pool reduced wait times before nurse assessment by **30–50%** with no net new FTE.

### Strategy 4: Real-Time Queue Monitoring & Slack-Based Patient Calling

**Target Queue(s):** All transitions, but especially "Registration  Nurse" and "Doctor  Test/Check-out."

**Root Cause Addressed:** Latency between a patient *being ready* and a resource *becoming aware* they are ready (the "information" bottleneck, not the capacity bottleneck). Also, unpredictable test durations causing check-out staff to be idle or overwhelmed.

**How Data Supports It:**
- Service time variability analysis (high CV on diagnostic tests) shows the duration of the upstream activity is unpredictable, so downstream staff cannot be scheduled tightly.
- The log reveals many cases where a downstream START occurs seconds after the upstream COMPLETE — evidence that patient-handling is prompt once initiated, but the *delay between activities* is the problem.

**Specific Proposal:** Deploy a **digital patient-flow management system** (kiosk-based or staff tablet) that updates a real-time dashboard. When a patient completes a stage, the next available resource's tablet alerts them within seconds. Implement a "**5-minute slack rule**": if a patient has waited >5 min in any queue, the system escalates to a coordinator. Add a **predictive check-out queue** that uses the in-progress test status to pre-stage the check-out clerk's workload.

**Expected Impact:** Reduces *information* latency to near-zero, which alone often reduces average inter-activity wait by **10–20%** even without changing capacity, and virtually eliminates the long-tail (>30 min) waits that drive most complaints.

## 4. Trade-offs and Constraints

Every optimization has a downside. A rigorous analyst must surface them.

### Shifting the Bottleneck
Reducing the queue in front of the nurse may overload the doctor. The clinic must run a **system-wide simulation** of each proposed change, not optimize each queue in isolation. Kingman's effect tells us improving a *non-bottleneck* stage is wasted effort, while over-tuning a bottleneck can push the constraint to the next stage. The "Theory of Constraints" framing is essential: optimize the bottleneck, then re-discover the new bottleneck.

### Increased Operational Costs vs. Marginal Benefit
- Adding a floating nurse (Strategy 3) requires hiring or cross-training — a real FTE cost. A break-even calculation should compare the cost of the new FTE to the value of the saved patient-hours (or to the revenue enabled by additional throughput, if applicable).
- A new patient-flow software (Strategy 4) carries licensing, training, and change-management costs.
- The analysis should include a **cost-per-minute-of-wait-saved** metric and a **sensitivity analysis** on patient volume.

### Staff Workload and Morale
- Parallelization (Strategy 2) may make the doctor's day less predictable, since they are no longer "in control" of the diagnostic timing.
- Floating nurse pools (Strategy 3) can erode specialty expertise and reduce job satisfaction if not designed with input from staff.
- Any process change must involve frontline staff in co-design — *prescription-from-data* without engagement typically fails.

### Quality of Care
- **Pre-ordering diagnostics (Strategy 2)** must be clinically validated. Ordering a test before the doctor sees the patient can cause unnecessary testing, patient anxiety, and clinical risk if false positives are common. A **clinical governance committee** should approve any protocol change. Pilot it on a low-risk subset first.
- Reducing waiting time should *never* come at the cost of clinical thoroughness. The event log should be augmented with **clinical outcome data** (e.g., 30-day readmission, missed diagnoses) to ensure quality doesn't degrade.

### Equity and Patient-Type Effects
- An optimization that reduces average wait but inadvertently makes *Urgent* patients wait longer (e.g., by prioritizing easy Follow-up patients in scheduling) is a net loss.
- We must analyze every proposal separately for each patient cohort (New, Follow-up, Urgent, Normal) and ensure no cohort is harmed.

### Data Privacy and Technology
- Real-time tracking systems (Strategy 4) handle sensitive patient location data. GDPR/HIPAA compliance, role-based access, and patient consent are non-negotiable.

### Balancing Conflicting Objectives
Use a **multi-objective Pareto framing**:
- *Objective 1:* Minimize average total visit duration.
- *Objective 2:* Minimize 90th-percentile waiting time (fairness).
- *Objective 3:* Maintain quality (no increase in adverse events).
- *Objective 4:* Limit incremental operating cost (e.g., <X% of current).
- *Objective 5:* Maintain staff satisfaction (e.g., no increase in overtime >10%).

Recommend the solution on the **Pareto frontier** that the clinic leadership prefers (e.g., "smallest cost increase that delivers the largest wait-time reduction while not worsening the 90th percentile for Urgent patients").

### Ongoing Process Monitoring

The same event log structure supports **continuous process mining**. We propose:

1. **Daily/Weekly Automated Dashboards:** Stream the live event log into a process mining platform. Each KPI is updated automatically. The dashboard shows trend lines, control charts (statistical process control), and alerts when any KPI breaches a threshold (e.g., 90th percentile wait > 25 min for 3 consecutive days).

2. **Conformance Checking:** Define a *target process model* (e.g., the optimized flow with parallelization). Run **conformance checking** weekly to detect deviations — e.g., if a previously retired step reappears, or if a new variant emerges at >5% frequency, an analyst investigates.

3. **A/B / Pilot-and-Rollout Approach:** Implement each strategy in one department or on one day-of-week first. Use the **same KPI framework** to compare pilot vs. control. Roll out only if statistically significant improvement is observed without degradation in other KPIs.

4. **Periodic Deep-Dive Re-Analysis (Quarterly):** Re-run the full queue mining analysis every quarter. Bottlenecks shift as the system improves; what was a constraint last quarter may no longer be the binding one. This is the **Theory of Constraints** discipline.

5. **Root-Cause Alerting for Regressions:** If a queue that was reduced begins to grow again (e.g., due to staff turnover or seasonal volume change), the system flags it. An analyst drills in using the same techniques described in Section 2 to find the *new* root cause.

6. **Closed-Loop Feedback with Patients and Staff:** KPIs are necessary but not sufficient. Combine quantitative monitoring with quarterly focus groups of patients and frontline staff to capture qualitative issues that the log cannot see (e.g., perceived rushedness, communication quality).

By systematically decomposing waiting times from the event log, ranking queues by a frequency × impact × severity composite, drilling into root causes using resource, handover, variability, and variant analyses, and applying targeted strategies (staggered scheduling, parallelization, dynamic resource pooling, and real-time monitoring), the clinic can reduce waiting times and total visit duration substantially. The key is to do this **iteratively, holistically, and with clinical and staff co-design** — using the event log not just for one-time diagnosis, but as a **continuous improvement instrument** that ensures gains are sustained.
