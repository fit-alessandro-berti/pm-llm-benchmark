1. Queue Identification and Characterization

Definition of waiting time  
In this clinic context, waiting time (queue time) is the time between the completion of one activity and the start of the next activity for the same patient visit. Using the event log:

Waiting Time = Start Time of Activity B  Completion Time of Activity A

Example:  
If Nurse Assessment completes at 09:25:10 and Doctor Consultation starts at 09:45:55, then the queue before the doctor is about 20 minutes and 45 seconds.

Steps to calculate queue times
- Sort events by Case ID and timestamp.
- Pair each COMPLETE event with the next START event for the same case.
- Compute time differences.
- Label queues by transition (e.g., Registration  Nurse Assessment, Nurse  Doctor, Doctor  ECG).

Important queue metrics
To characterize queues, the following metrics should be calculated per transition and per activity:

Queue duration metrics
- Average waiting time
- Median waiting time
- 90th and 95th percentile waiting time (captures extreme delays)
- Maximum waiting time

Queue frequency metrics
- Number of patients experiencing the queue
- Percentage of visits encountering that queue
- Number of cases exceeding threshold wait times (e.g., >30 minutes)

Process impact metrics
- Contribution of each queue to total visit duration
- Queue time as percentage of total visit time
- Variability (standard deviation)

Segmented metrics
Queues should also be analyzed by:
- Patient type (New vs Follow-up)
- Urgency (Normal vs Urgent)
- Specialty (Cardiology vs others)
- Time-of-day / day-of-week
- Resource (specific doctor, nurse, room)

Identifying critical queues
Critical queues are those with the highest operational and patient experience impact. I would prioritize queues based on:

- High average or 90th percentile wait times
- High frequency (many patients affected)
- High contribution to total visit time
- High variability (unpredictable delays)
- Impact on urgent patients
- Association with patient complaints

Example of typical critical queues in clinics:
- Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation
- Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Tests
- Diagnostic Test  Doctor Review
- Arrival  Registration during peak hours

A ìQueue Severity Scoreî can be created combining wait time, frequency, and patient impact to rank bottlenecks.

2. Root Cause Analysis

Once queues are identified, process mining techniques can reveal why they occur.

Resource bottlenecks
Event logs include resource identifiers (doctor, nurse, technician). This enables:

- Resource utilization analysis
- Activity load per staff member
- Parallel work capacity

Indicators of bottlenecks:
- One doctor consistently associated with long queues
- Diagnostic rooms operating near 100% utilization
- Few nurses compared to arrival rate

Example:  
If queues before cardiology consultations are long and Dr. Smith handles most of them, the doctor becomes the bottleneck.

Activity dependencies and handovers
Healthcare processes often have sequential dependencies.

Example:
Registration  Nurse  Doctor  Diagnostics

Process mining can reveal:
- Where handover delays occur
- How long patients wait after handoffs
- Whether certain sequences create delays

Service time variability
Some activities take highly variable time.

Using event logs:
Service Time = COMPLETE  START

High variability creates queues even with adequate capacity.

Example:
Doctor consultation may range from 5 minutes to 40 minutes depending on case complexity.

This variability propagates delays downstream.

Appointment scheduling policies
If appointment slots are uniform but consultation durations are not, backlog builds.

Analysis:
- Compare scheduled arrival times vs actual start times
- Identify overbooking periods
- Identify synchronized arrivals (e.g., many patients at 9 AM)

Patient arrival patterns
Event logs can reveal:

- Arrival spikes (morning peak)
- Day-of-week variation
- Specialty-specific demand patterns

Example:
Large patient influx between 9:00ñ10:30 causing registration queues.

Patient segmentation effects
Process variants can differ by patient type.

Examples:
- New patients may require longer nurse assessment
- Urgent patients may bypass queues
- Follow-up visits may not need diagnostics

Variant analysis helps detect:
- Different pathways
- Different queue structures
- Different service times

Advanced process mining tools help with:

- Bottleneck detection
- Conformance checking
- Throughput time decomposition
- Resource workload visualization

3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

Strategy 1: Dynamic resource allocation during peak periods

Target queues
Registration  Nurse  
Nurse  Doctor

Root cause addressed
Demand peaks and uneven staff workload.

Data support
Event logs will reveal:
- Time-of-day queue spikes
- Staff utilization patterns
- Patient arrival distribution

If most patients arrive between 9ñ11 AM, static staffing leads to queues.

Solution
- Increase clerks and nurses during peak arrival windows
- Shift staff schedules based on demand curves
- Reassign underutilized staff temporarily

Example change:
- Add one extra registration clerk from 8:30ñ11:00
- Add floating nurse for triage during peak hours

Expected impact
- Registration waiting time reduction by 30ñ50%
- Earlier start of clinical processes
- Reduced cascading delays throughout visit

Cost control
Uses schedule optimization rather than hiring new staff.

------------------------------------------------

Strategy 2: Separate fast-track pathway for follow-up patients

Target queues
Registration  Doctor  
Nurse  Doctor

Root cause addressed
Mixing simple follow-up visits with complex new visits creates queue inefficiency.

Data support
Variant analysis will show:
- Follow-up visits have shorter consultation time
- Often skip diagnostics

Solution
Introduce a fast-track workflow:

Follow-up patients:
Registration  Doctor  Check-out

Or:

Self-check-in  Doctor  Check-out

This avoids unnecessary nurse triage for simple visits.

Expected impact
- Reduced doctor queue congestion
- Faster throughput for routine visits
- 20ñ40% reduction in waiting time for follow-ups

Secondary effect
Doctors can allocate more time for complex cases.

------------------------------------------------

Strategy 3: Pre-ordering and parallelizing diagnostic tests

Target queues
Doctor  Diagnostic tests  
Diagnostic test  Specialist review

Root cause addressed
Diagnostics often occur sequentially after consultation, causing queues.

Data support
Process mining can identify frequent patterns like:

Doctor  ECG  Doctor review

If the doctor nearly always orders ECG for certain symptoms, tests can be anticipated.

Solution
Introduce nurse-driven or protocol-driven pre-orders.

Example:
If patient complaint = chest pain:
Nurse orders ECG before doctor visit.

New flow:
Registration  Nurse  ECG  Doctor

Expected impact
- Eliminates waiting between doctor and diagnostics
- Reduces total visit duration by 15ñ25 minutes per patient
- Improves doctor productivity

------------------------------------------------

Strategy 4: Smart appointment scheduling

Target queues
Morning registration congestion  
Doctor consultation backlog

Root cause addressed
Clustered appointment times and variability in consultation length.

Data support
Arrival pattern analysis may show many appointments scheduled at the same time (e.g., 9:00).

Solution
Implement:

- Staggered appointments
- Different slot lengths
- Buffer slots for complex cases

Examples:
- New patient: 25-minute slot
- Follow-up: 10-minute slot
- 10-minute buffer every hour

Expected impact
- Reduced doctor queue buildup
- More predictable waiting times
- 20ñ30% improvement in throughput stability

4. Trade-offs and Constraints

Shifting bottlenecks
Improving one stage can overload another.

Example:
Faster registration may overload nurses.

Mitigation
Simulate full process flow before implementation.

Cost implications
Additional staff during peak hours increases cost.

Mitigation
Use part-time shifts or schedule redistribution instead of hiring.

Staff workload and burnout
Increasing throughput may increase workload.

Mitigation
- Monitor utilization levels
- Maintain sustainable workload thresholds (e.g., <85%).

Quality of care
Fast-track systems may risk oversimplification.

Mitigation
- Include triage checks
- Allow escalation to full consultation if needed.

Operational complexity
Multiple patient pathways increase management complexity.

Mitigation
Use digital workflow management systems.

5. Measuring Success

Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

Patient flow metrics
- Average total visit duration
- Median visit duration
- 90th percentile visit duration

Queue metrics
- Average waiting time per activity transition
- Maximum queue times
- Percentage of patients waiting >30 minutes

Operational metrics
- Resource utilization (doctors, nurses, equipment)
- Patient throughput per day
- Number of patients processed per doctor

Experience metrics
- Patient satisfaction scores
- Complaint frequency related to waiting

Monitoring approach

Use continuous process mining dashboards based on the same event logs:

- Daily queue monitoring
- Weekly bottleneck analysis
- Resource workload dashboards

Control charts can detect deviations in waiting times.

Example monitoring cycle
- Monthly process mining review
- Compare KPIs before vs after interventions
- Adjust staffing or scheduling if queues reappear

Sustained improvement

Implement automated alerts when:
- Queue times exceed thresholds
- Resource utilization exceeds limits
- Patient flow deviates from expected patterns

This turns process mining from a one-time analysis into a continuous operational intelligence system that supports long-term patient flow optimization.
