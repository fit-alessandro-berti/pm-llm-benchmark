### Anomalies Identified

1.  **Receive to Approve (R to P):** The average time between receiving a claim and approving it is approximately 25 hours, but the standard deviation is extremely low (1 hour). This suggests a highly regimented or artificially fixed approval cycle that lacks realistic variance.
2.  **Evaluate to Notify (E to N):** The average time between claim evaluation and customer notification is only 5 minutes, with a standard deviation of 1 minute. This is suspiciously rapid and suggests that the step of evaluation is either severely compressed, or intermediate communication steps are being skipped.
3.  **Approve to Notify (P to N):** The average delay between claim approval and customer notification is an exceptionally long 7 days, with a high standard deviation of 2 days. This indicates significant variability, suggesting that some claims experience massive delays while others are handled almost immediately, pointing to severe bottlenecks or uneven workflow distribution.
4.  **Assign to Close (A to C):** The average time between assigning an adjuster and closing the claim is 2 hours. While not necessarily an anomaly, the low variability (1 hour) suggests that many claims might be closed without fully observing the subsequent evaluation and approval steps, potentially indicating premature closure.

### Hypotheses on Anomaly Causes

1.  **R to P (Receive to Approve) Rigidity:** The low standard deviation suggests a bottleneck or an automated, rigid approval workflow enforced by system constraints, possibly ignoring real-world human intervention or delays caused by manual review steps.
2.  **E to N (Evaluate to Notify) Acceleration:** The extremely short time frame suggests that the evaluation phase is being executed rapidly, perhaps by automated systems or a single reviewer, bypassing necessary human review or communication steps, leading to less accurate notifications.
3.  **P to N (Approve to Notify) Backlog and Disparity:** The high average time and high standard deviation strongly suggest that the delay in notification is heavily dependent on the specific claim or adjuster. Long delays likely occur when the claim is complex or assigned to a specific region/adjuster (bottleneck), while short delays occur for simple claims.
4.  **A to C (Assign to Close) Premature Closure:** The short time suggests that claims are sometimes closed immediately after assignment without fully completing the evaluation or approval cycles, indicating potential administrative shortcuts or process bypasses.

### Proposed Verification Approaches using SQL

#### 1. Identify Claims with Unusual Timing Spans

These queries calculate the actual time differences between sequential events and compare them against the expected profile averages, flagging deviations.

```sql
WITH TimeGaps AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        activity AS current_activity,
        timestamp AS current_ts,
        LEAD(timestamp) OVER (PARTITION BY claim_id ORDER BY timestamp) AS next_ts,
        activity AS prev_activity
    FROM claim_events
),
CalculatedGaps AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        prev_activity,
        current_activity,
        EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (next_ts - timestamp)) AS actual_seconds
    FROM TimeGaps
    WHERE next_ts IS NOT NULL
)
SELECT
    t1.claim_id,
    t1.prev_activity,
    t1.current_activity,
    t1.actual_seconds,
    -- Join with the expected average from the temporal profile (e.g., R to P: 90000 seconds)
    (SELECT AVG(value) FROM (VALUES (90000), (1800), (604800)) AS expected_times WHERE prev_activity = 'R' AND current_activity = 'P') AS expected_avg_time
FROM CalculatedGaps t1
WHERE (actual_seconds - expected_avg_time) > (3 * (SELECT STDEV_TIME_IN_SECONDS FROM temporal_profile WHERE (A,B) = (prev_activity, current_activity)))
ORDER BY t1.claim_id;
```

#### 2. Correlate Anomalies with Adjusters and Claim Types

This query investigates if specific adjusters or claim types contribute disproportionately to the temporal anomalies, especially for the long P to N delay.

```sql
SELECT
    a.name AS adjuster_name,
    c.claim_type,
    AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (
        SELECT timestamp FROM claim_events WHERE claim_id = c.claim_id AND activity = 'P'
    )) -
    AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (
        SELECT timestamp FROM claim_events WHERE claim_id = c.claim_id AND activity = 'N'
    )))) AS avg_p_to_n_delay
FROM claim_events c
JOIN adjusters a ON c.resource = a.name -- Assuming resource links to adjuster name
GROUP BY 1, 2
HAVING AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (
    SELECT timestamp FROM claim_events WHERE claim_id = c.claim_id AND activity = 'P'
))) - AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (
    SELECT timestamp FROM claim_events WHERE claim_id = c.claim_id AND activity = 'N'
))) > 100000 -- Filter for claims showing significantly long P to N delays
ORDER BY avg_p_to_n_delay DESC;
```

#### 3. Identify Claims with Premature Closures (A to C)

This query focuses on identifying claims that were closed quickly following assignment, which might indicate process shortcuts.

```sql
SELECT
    ce.claim_id,
    ce.timestamp AS assign_time,
    ce.timestamp AS close_time,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ce.timestamp - ce.timestamp)) AS closure_duration_seconds
FROM claim_events ce
WHERE ce.prev_activity = 'A' AND ce.current_activity = 'C'
ORDER BY closure_duration_seconds ASC
LIMIT 100;
```
