Based on standard **Procure-to-Pay (P2P)** best practices, the logical and controlled sequence of activities should follow this canonical flow:

1. **Create Purchase Requisition** (internal request for goods/services)  
2. **Approve Purchase Requisition** (managerial authorization)  
3. **Issue Purchase Order** (official commitment to vendor, legally binding)  
4. **Receive Goods** (physical or logical confirmation of delivery)  
5. **Vendor Invoice Received** (supplier’s billing document)  
6. **Match Invoice to PO** (3-way match: PO, receipt, invoice)  
7. **Release Payment** (payment authorized only after successful matching)  
8. **Close Case** (process completion)

Deviations from this sequence often indicate **process control failures, fraud risks, compliance violations, or system errors**. Let’s analyze each case:

---

###  **Case 1001: Normal Flow**
- Requisition  Approval  PO  Receive Goods  Invoice  Match  Payment  Close  
** Perfectly aligned with P2P best practices.**  
*No anomaly.*

---

###  **Case 1002: Major Sequence Anomaly**

| Activity                      | Timestamp           |
|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| Create Purchase Requisition   | 2024-02-01 08:30    |
| **Issue Purchase Order**      | 2024-02-01 08:40    |  **NO APPROVAL YET**  
| Vendor Invoice Received       | 2024-02-03 07:00    |
| **Release Payment**           | 2024-02-03 07:15    |  **NO MATCHING, NO GOODS RECEIVED**  
| Approve Purchase Requisition  | 2024-02-03 08:00    |  **AFTER PAYMENT!**  
| Receive Goods                 | 2024-02-04 09:00    |  **AFTER PAYMENT**  
| Match Invoice to PO           | 2024-02-05 08:00    |  **AFTER PAYMENT**  
| Close Case                    | 2024-02-06 16:00    |

####  **Anomalies Identified:**
1. **PO Issued Before Requisition Approval**  
   - This violates internal controls. A purchase order should never be issued without managerial approval of the requisition. This could allow unauthorized spending.

2. **Payment Released Before Invoice Matching and Goods Receipt**  
   - Payment was made **before** goods were received and **before** the invoice was matched to the PO. This exposes the organization to:
     - **Fraud risk**: Vendor could invoice for non-delivered goods.
     - **Overpayment risk**: No verification that quantity, price, or terms match the PO.
     - **Compliance violation**: Most ERP systems (e.g., SAP, Oracle) enforce 3-way matching as a control gate before payment.

3. **Requisition Approval Occurred After Payment**  
   - This is a clear **retroactive authorization**, indicating either:
     - A system error (e.g., timestamp corruption),
     - A deliberate circumvention of controls,
     - Or a “pay first, ask later” culture — a red flag for audit and compliance.

####  **Why This Is Problematic:**
- **Financial risk**: Payment for undelivered or incorrect goods.
- **Audit failure**: Cannot demonstrate proper segregation of duties or control gates.
- **Regulatory exposure**: Violates SOX, internal audit standards, or public procurement rules.
- **Potential fraud**: Could be a collusion scheme between employee and vendor.

###  **Case 1003: Severe Sequence Anomaly**

| Activity                      | Timestamp           |
|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| Create Purchase Requisition   | 2024-02-02 09:00    |
| Approve Purchase Requisition  | 2024-02-02 09:10    |
| Issue Purchase Order          | 2024-02-02 10:00    |
| **Release Payment**           | 2024-02-08 11:00    |  **NO INVOICE, NO RECEIPT, NO MATCH**  
| Vendor Invoice Received       | 2024-02-09 12:00    |  **AFTER PAYMENT**  
| Receive Goods                 | 2024-02-10 13:00    |  **AFTER PAYMENT**  
| Match Invoice to PO           | 2024-02-11 14:00    |  **AFTER PAYMENT**  
| Close Case                    | 2024-02-12 10:00    |

####  **Anomalies Identified:**
1. **Payment Released Before Receiving Goods or Invoice**  
   - Payment occurred **6 days before** the invoice was even received — and **before** goods were received.  
   - This is a **classic red flag for kickbacks or ghost vendor schemes**.

2. **All Post-Payment Activities Are Retroactive**  
   - Invoice received, goods received, and matching all happened **after payment**.  
   - This undermines the entire purpose of P2P controls: **prevent payment before verification**.

####  **Why This Is Problematic:**
- **High fraud risk**: Could indicate a vendor was paid for non-existent goods/services.
- **Lack of audit trail**: The matching process is a post-hoc formality, not a control.
- **Weak internal controls**: Payment authority is not tied to operational verification.
- **Accounting misstatement**: Expenses may be recorded before supporting documentation exists, violating accrual accounting principles.

---

###  **Case 1004: Sequence Violation (PO After Receipt)**

| Activity                      | Timestamp           |
|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| Create Purchase Requisition   | 2024-02-05 10:00    |
| Approve Purchase Requisition  | 2024-02-05 10:15    |
| **Receive Goods**             | 2024-02-06 09:00    |  **NO PO YET**  
| **Issue Purchase Order**      | 2024-02-06 09:15    |  **AFTER RECEIPT**  
| Vendor Invoice Received       | 2024-02-07 08:00    |
| Match Invoice to PO           | 2024-02-07 08:30    |
| Release Payment               | 2024-02-07 09:00    |
| Close Case                    | 2024-02-08 11:00    |

####  **Anomaly Identified:**
- **Goods received before PO was issued**  
   - This is highly unusual and problematic.  
   - How were goods received without a PO?  
     - Possible scenarios:
       - Emergency purchase bypassing process (but then PO should be backdated or created immediately).
       - Goods received via informal channel (e.g., direct delivery to warehouse) — a **control gap**.
       - **Fraud risk**: Vendor delivered goods without a PO, then the PO is created retroactively to legitimize payment — potentially to hide off-contract spending.

####  **Why This Is Problematic:**
- **Loss of procurement control**: No PO = no price negotiation, no contract terms, no legal recourse.
- **Compliance breach**: Most organizations require POs for all purchases above a threshold.
- **Audit risk**: Cannot prove that goods received were authorized or priced appropriately.
- **Potential for duplicate payments**: If the vendor later sends an invoice referencing a non-existent PO, matching fails — but in this case, it was retroactively matched, masking the issue.

| Case ID | Anomaly(s) | Risk Category |
|--------|------------|---------------|
| **1002** | PO before approval; Payment before receipt/invoice/match; Approval after payment | **High Fraud, Compliance, Financial Risk** |
| **1003** | Payment before invoice and receipt; All matching done post-payment | **Very High Fraud Risk (Ghost Vendor), Accounting Violation** |
| **1004** | Goods received before PO issued | **Control Gap, Compliance Breach, Unauthorized Spend** |
| **1001** | None | **Normal, compliant** |

---

##  Recommendations

1. **Implement System Enforcements**:
   - Block PO issuance unless requisition is approved.
   - Block payment unless invoice is matched to a PO and goods receipt is confirmed.
   - Timestamps should be system-generated and immutable.

2. **Investigate Cases 1002, 1003, 1004**:
   - Conduct forensic audit: Who initiated these transactions? Were there overrides? Were vendors known for suspicious activity?
   - Interview procurement staff — this may indicate process workarounds or collusion.

3. **Review Control Design**:
   - Are users allowed to manually override controls? If yes, why? Is there logging?
   - Are there policies for emergency purchases? If so, are they documented and approved?

4. **Training & Awareness**:
   - Staff may be unaware of control logic. Reinforce that **“pay first, match later” is never acceptable**.

---

##  Conclusion

Cases **1002, 1003, and 1004** exhibit **serious deviations** from standard P2P controls, with **Case 1003** being the most alarming due to payment occurring before any verification. These anomalies create significant exposure to **financial loss, fraud, audit failure, and regulatory penalties**. Case 1001 is the only compliant instance. The organization should treat these anomalies as **red flags requiring immediate investigation and process hardening**.
