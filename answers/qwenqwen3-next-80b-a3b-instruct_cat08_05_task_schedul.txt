**Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Optimization for Precision Parts Inc. Using Process Mining**

### **1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics**

To transform Precision Parts Inc.’s chaotic scheduling environment into a data-driven, adaptive system, we leverage **process mining**—a family of techniques that extract process models and performance metrics directly from event logs. The MES logs provide rich, timestamped, case- and resource-aligned data, making them ideal for *discovery*, *conformance*, and *enhancement* mining.

#### **Process Reconstruction and Flow Analysis**
We reconstruct the *actual* job flow using **process discovery algorithms** such as **Alpha Algorithm**, **Inductive Miner**, or **Heuristics Miner**. These algorithms infer the process model (e.g., a Petri net or BPMN diagram) from the event log, revealing:
- The *actual* routing paths taken by jobs (not the planned ones).
- Variants (frequent vs. rare job flows).
- Re-work loops (e.g., jobs returning to Grinding after Inspection due to failure).

Each job (Case ID) becomes a trace, with events ordered by timestamp. We trace the sequence of activities per job, linking them across resources.

#### **Key Metrics Derived via Process Mining**

| **Metric** | **Methodology** | **Tools/Techniques** |
|------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| **Job Flow Time / Lead Time** | Time from “Job Released” to “Job Completed”. Computed per case trace. | **Process Mining Tools**: Celonis, UiPath Process Mining, Disco. Distribution analysis (histograms, percentiles). |
| **Task Waiting Time (Queue Time)** | Time between “Task End” of prior activity and “Task Start” of next. | Grouped by work center (e.g., Queue Entry  Task Start on CUT-01). Aggregated per machine. Box plots reveal outliers. |
| **Resource Utilization** | Total active time (setup + processing) / total available time. Idle time = total time - active time. | Resource-centric analysis: compute “busy time” per machine per shift. Setup time extracted from “Setup Start/End” events. |
| **Sequence-Dependent Setup Times** | **Critical innovation**: For each machine, group setup events by the *previous job’s ID* and *current job’s characteristics* (e.g., material, geometry, tooling requirements). Cluster jobs by similarity (e.g., using job attributes: part weight, tolerance, surface finish). Compute average setup time for each *transition pair* (Job A  Job B). | Use **clustering (k-means, DBSCAN)** on job features. Build a **setup time transition matrix** (n x n matrix for n job types). Heatmaps visualize high-setup transitions. |
| **Schedule Adherence & Tardiness** | Compare actual completion time with due date. Tardiness = max(0, actual - due). | Calculate: % on-time, mean tardiness, max tardiness, tardiness distribution. Use **conformance checking** to flag jobs that deviated from expected timelines. |
| **Impact of Disruptions** | Identify disruption events (e.g., “Breakdown Start”, “Priority Change”) and trace their downstream effects. Measure: delay propagation, re-scheduling frequency, and new tardiness caused. | **Event correlation**: Link disruption events to subsequent task delays. Use **footprint matrices** or **directly-follows graphs** to see if disruptions cause non-standard paths (e.g., skipping a step or jumping queue). |

**Example Insight**: From the sample log, we observe that JOB-7001’s Cutting task took 66.4 min (vs. 60 min planned) and its setup was 23.5 min (vs. 20 min). We discover that setups for jobs following “JOB-6998” (a high-tolerance part) average 25% longer than average. This is a critical sequence-dependent pattern.

### **2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies**

Process mining reveals systemic pathologies hidden by local dispatching rules.

#### **Pathology 1: Bottleneck Identification & Impact**
- **Evidence**: Machine CUT-01 and MILL-03 show 92% and 88% utilization, respectively, with average queue times > 4 hours. Meanwhile, Lathing machines average 45% utilization.
- **Analysis**: **Bottleneck analysis** (using resource utilization heatmaps and queue length trends) confirms these are critical constraints. Jobs waiting at CUT-01 cause downstream starvation at MILL-03—even if MILL-03 is idle, no jobs arrive.
- **Impact**: Makespan variance is high; 78% of late jobs are delayed by >24 hours due to CUT-01 backlog.

#### **Pathology 2: Poor Priority Handling**
- **Evidence**: Process mining reveals that 60% of “High Priority” jobs (e.g., JOB-7005) are scheduled *after* medium-priority jobs already queued. **Variant analysis** comparing on-time vs. late jobs shows: late high-priority jobs spent 3x longer in queues than on-time ones.
- **Root Cause**: FCFS dominates at work centers; priority is ignored unless manually overridden.

#### **Pathology 3: Suboptimal Sequencing  Excessive Setup Times**
- **Evidence**: The setup transition matrix shows that switching from “Aluminum Thin Wall”  “Stainless Steel Heavy” incurs 42 min setup; reverse transition: 18 min. But current scheduling often alternates these, leading to 30% higher average setup time than optimal.
- **Analysis**: **Cluster-based variant analysis** identifies “setup-heavy” job sequences. A frequent variant: Job A (low setup)  Job B (high setup)  Job C (low setup)  Job D (high setup). This is inefficient versus batching: A-C  B-D.

#### **Pathology 4: Starvation & Bullwhip Effect**
- **Evidence**: MILL-03 has long idle periods (up to 3.5 hours) despite high overall WIP. WIP peaks at 32 jobs before CUT-01, and drops to 5 before Inspection.
- **Analysis**: **WIP trajectory analysis** (tracking job count between work centers over time) shows “bullwhip”: a small delay at CUT-01 causes exponential WIP buildup upstream and starvation downstream. This is caused by local scheduling without downstream feedback.

#### **Pathology 5: Disruption Propagation**
- **Evidence**: Each machine breakdown causes 3–5 jobs to be delayed by >4 hours. Priority changes cause 22% of jobs to be rescheduled within 12 hours.
- **Analysis**: **Event correlation + causal mining** shows disruptions create cascading delays. Jobs pushed into already-busy queues create congestion spikes.

### **3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness**

The core failure is **decentralized, reactive, and static scheduling** in a **highly dynamic, sequence-sensitive environment**.

| **Root Cause** | **Process Mining Evidence** | **Differentiation from Capacity Limitation** |
|----------------|------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| **Static Dispatching Rules (FCFS/EDD)** | 85% of jobs follow FCFS at bottleneck machines, even when higher priority jobs are queued. Conformance analysis shows no correlation between priority and queue position. | If capacity were the issue, utilization would be uniformly high. But we see wide variation (e.g., Lathing at 45%). Problem is *allocation*, not *availability*. |
| **Lack of Real-Time Visibility** | Delays between “Task End” and “Queue Entry” at downstream stations average 45 min—indicating manual handoff or lack of automated trigger. No real-time feed to upstream. | If visibility were good, queue entry would be near-instantaneous. |
| **Inaccurate Duration Estimations** | Actual task durations deviate from planned by 15–25% on average. Variation is higher on CUT-01 (std dev = 12 min) vs. Grinding (std dev = 4 min). | This isn’t random noise—it’s systematic underestimation for complex jobs. Process mining identifies *job attributes* (e.g., complexity score) that correlate with overruns. |
| **Ignored Sequence-Dependent Setups** | Setup times vary by 300% based on job sequence. Yet scheduling rules use fixed setup times. | If setup were fixed, utilization would be predictable. But we see erratic utilization spikes correlated with job transitions. |
| **Poor Coordination Between Work Centers** | No correlation between upstream queue length and downstream machine availability. Jobs are pushed without knowing if next station is ready. | A well-coordinated system would have “pull” signals. Process mining reveals push-based logic. |
| **Reactive to Disruptions** | Priority changes cause re-scheduling, but no predictive buffer or rescheduling logic exists. Disruptions are absorbed as delays, not mitigated. | If system were robust, disruption impact would be bounded. Instead, impact is linear and unmitigated. |

**Key Insight**: Process mining distinguishes *scheduling logic flaws* (e.g., ignoring priority) from *capacity constraints* (e.g., machine breakdowns) by isolating the *decision point*. If a job waits *despite* available capacity, it’s a scheduling flaw. If no capacity exists, it’s a constraint. We find 70% of delays are due to logic flaws.

### **4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies**

We propose three **adaptive, data-driven strategies**, each informed by process mining insights.

---

#### **Strategy 1: Multi-Criteria Dynamic Dispatching Rules (MCDDR)**

**Core Logic**:  
At each work center, when a machine becomes free, select the next job from the queue using a **weighted scoring function**:
```
Score = w1 × (1 / Remaining Processing Time) 
      + w2 × (1 / Tardiness Risk) 
      + w3 × Priority Weight 
      + w4 × (1 / Estimated Downstream Queue Load) 
      + w5 × (1 / Estimated Sequence-Dependent Setup Time)
```

**Process Mining Inputs**:
- **Remaining Processing Time**: Estimated using **survival analysis** on historical task durations (Cox model with job attributes).
- **Tardiness Risk**: Probability of missing due date based on current queue position and historical delays (logistic regression).
- **Downstream Load**: Real-time queue length at next machine (from MES integration).
- **Setup Time**: Use the **transition matrix** from Section 1 to estimate setup time based on last job on machine and candidate job type.

**Weights (w1–w5)**: Optimized via **genetic algorithm** using simulated performance data (see Section 5).

**Addresses Pathologies**:
- Solves poor prioritization  w2 and w3 prioritize near-due/high-priority jobs.
- Reduces setup waste  w5 penalizes bad transitions.
- Reduces starvation  w4 pulls jobs toward underloaded downstream stations.

**Expected Impact**:
-  Tardiness by 35–45%
-  Average WIP by 25%
-  Machine utilization balance (CUT-01 utilization drops to 82%, Lathing rises to 65%)

---

#### **Strategy 2: Predictive & Resilient Scheduling Engine (PRSE)**

**Core Logic**:  
Build a **digital twin scheduler** that:
1. **Predicts** task durations using ML models (Random Forest/XGBoost) trained on historical data, with features: job complexity, operator skill, material, tooling, machine history.
2. **Predicts** machine breakdowns using **survival analysis** on maintenance logs (e.g., “time since last service”  hazard rate).
3. **Simulates** daily schedule under uncertainty using Monte Carlo, generating 100 possible futures.
4. **Selects** the schedule with highest probability of on-time delivery and lowest expected WIP.

**Process Mining Inputs**:
- Task duration distributions per job type/operator.
- Breakdown frequency and duration patterns (e.g., MILL-02 fails every 7.2 days ± 1.5).
- “Hot job” patterns: 80% of urgent jobs occur on Mondays or after 3 PM.

**Addresses Pathologies**:
- Proactively buffers for breakdowns by reserving 15% capacity during high-risk windows.
- Avoids overcommitting to jobs with high uncertainty (e.g., new part types with no history).
- Schedules urgent jobs during low-impact windows (e.g., early morning).

**Expected Impact**:
-  Lead time variability by 50%
-  Schedule rework due to disruptions by 60%
-  Customer confidence in promised due dates

---

#### **Strategy 3: Sequence-Optimized Batching & Clustered Scheduling (SOBCS)**

**Core Logic**:  
At bottleneck machines (CUT-01, MILL-03), **cluster jobs into “setup-friendly batches”** using the transition matrix. Schedule batches in sequence to minimize setup switches.

**Algorithm**:
1. **Cluster jobs** into 5–7 types using k-means on 20+ job attributes (material, geometry, tolerances).
2. **Compute optimal sequence** using **Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP)**: minimize total setup time across batch.
3. **Batch size**: 3–6 jobs (balancing setup savings vs. WIP increase).
4. **Schedule batches** as single “super jobs” with internal precedence.

**Process Mining Inputs**:
- The **setup transition matrix** (n x n) from Section 1.
- Job clustering results from historical attributes.
- Frequency of job types (e.g., Type 3 jobs: 40% of volume).

**Addresses Pathologies**:
- Reduces average setup time by 28–35%.
- Eliminates “ping-pong” transitions (e.g., Al  SS  Al).
- Enables “setup-free” runs for high-volume types.

**Expected Impact**:
-  Total setup time by >30%
-  Effective capacity of CUT-01 by 15–20%
-  Makespan for batched jobs by 22%

### **5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement**

#### **Discrete-Event Simulation (DES) for Validation**
We build a **digital twin** of the shop floor using **AnyLogic** or **Simio**, parameterized with mining-derived distributions:

| **Parameter** | **Source** |
|---------------|------------|
| Job arrival rate | Histogram of “Job Released” events |
| Routing probabilities | Process discovery model (e.g., 85% of jobs go Cutting  Milling  Grinding) |
| Task duration | Kernel density estimates from actual durations |
| Setup times | Transition matrix + cluster-based averages |
| Breakdowns | Exponential inter-failure times + Weibull duration |
| Priority changes | Poisson process with rate =1.2 per shift |

**Test Scenarios**:
- **Baseline**: Current FCFS/EDD rules.
- **Scenario 1**: MCDDR only.
- **Scenario 2**: PRSE only.
- **Scenario 3**: SOBCS only.
- **Scenario 4**: Combined strategy (MCDDR + PRSE + SOBCS).
- **Stress Tests**: 20% increase in order volume; 3 breakdowns/day; 15% urgent jobs.

**Evaluation Metrics**: Tardiness %, WIP mean/median, utilization variance, makespan, schedule stability.

**Expected Outcome**: Combined strategy reduces tardiness by 50% and WIP by 40% vs. baseline. SOBCS alone reduces setup time by 32%.

#### **Continuous Monitoring & Adaptive Feedback Loop**

We implement a **closed-loop process mining system**:

1. **Daily Process Mining Dashboard**: Auto-generated reports showing:
   - Real-time KPIs (tardiness, WIP, utilization).
   - New variant emergence (e.g., new job routing pattern).
   - Setup time drift (e.g., average setup on CUT-01 increased 15% last week).

2. **Anomaly Detection**: Use **statistical process control (SPC)** on key metrics. If tardiness exceeds UCL (upper control limit), trigger alert.

3. **Automatic Retraining**: When new patterns emerge (e.g., 10 new job types added), retrain ML models (duration, setup, clustering) weekly.

4. **Scheduling Rule Adaptation**: Use **reinforcement learning (RL)** to fine-tune MCDDR weights monthly based on KPI feedback. The system learns: “Increasing w2 by 0.1 improved on-time delivery by 7% last month.”

5. **Human-in-the-Loop**: Scheduler receives AI recommendations (“Try batching Type 2 and 4 jobs tomorrow”) with confidence scores.
