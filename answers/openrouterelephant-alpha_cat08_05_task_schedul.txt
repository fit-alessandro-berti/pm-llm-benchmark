### **1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics via Process Mining**

Process mining reconstructs the true, event-driven execution flow from raw log data, revealing the gap between planned and actual behavior.

**Techniques & Metrics:**

*   **Process Conformance & Replay:** Using algorithms like **Heuristics Miner** or **Inductive Miner**, we discover the actual process model (a Directed Acyclic Graph of tasks, splits, joins) directly from logs. **Replay** then aligns each case (JOB) instance onto this model, identifying deviations (e.g., a task executed out of sequence, an unscheduled wait).
    *   *Metric:* **Fitness** (how well the model explains the log) and **Precision** (how many behaviors in the log are allowed by the model).

*   **Flow Time & Lead Time Analysis:** For each **Case ID (Job)**, we calculate:
    *   **Flow Time:** Time from `Job Released` to `Task End` of the final operation.
    *   **Lead Time:** Time from `Job Released` to the `Job`'s `Due Date` outcome (on-time, tardy, late).
    *   *Metric:* **Distribution histograms** of flow/lead times, identifying right-skewness (long-tail delays) and calculating percentiles (50th, 90th, 95th).

*   **Queue Time (Waiting Time) Quantification:** We isolate periods labeled "**Waiting for resource**" or tasks in `Queue Entry` state until `Task Start`. Aggregated by:
    *   *Metric:* **Average/Median Queue Time per Work Center (Resource)**. This directly measures contention and identifies work-in-progress (WIP) accumulation points.

*   **Resource Utilization & Productive Time:**
    *   **Machine Utilization:** `(Total Time Busy (Task Start to Task End + Setup)) / (Total Shift Time)` per machine.
    *   **Operator Utilization:** Similar calculation based on `Operator ID` and `Task Duration`.
    *   **Setup Time Analysis:** Extract all `Setup Start` to `Setup End` intervals, grouped by `(Previous Job Type/Properties, Next Job Type/Properties, Machine ID)`.
    *   *Metric:* **Productive Time %**, **Idle Time %**, **Setup Time %**. This reveals if a "bottleneck" is truly capacity-limited or plagued by excessive setup/changeover.

*   **Sequence-Dependent Setup Time Quantification:** This is critical. By mining the `Notes` field and correlating `Setup Start` events with the preceding `Task End` on that resource, we build a **Setup Duration Matrix**.
    *   *Method:* Cluster setups by job attribute pairs (e.g., material hardness, finish type). Calculate average and distribution of setup times for transitions (Job A  Job B) vs. (Job C  Job B) on `CUT-01`.
    *   *Metric:* **Setup Time Delta** between "similar" vs. "dissimilar" job sequences.

*   **Schedule Adherence & Tardiness:**
    *   For each task, compare `Task Duration (Actual)` vs. `Task Duration (Planned)`.
    *   Calculate **Schedule Variance (SV)** for completion: `(Actual Completion Time - Due Date)`.
    *   *Metric:* **On-Time Delivery %**, **Average Tardiness (days)**, and **Delay Frequency per Priority Level**.

*   **Impact of Disruptions:** Filter events by `Event Type = "Resource"` (breakdown) or `Event Type = "Priority Change"`. Measure:
    *   **Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF)** and **Mean Time To Repair (MTTR)** per machine.
    *   **Schedule Slippage** caused by each disruption type (e.g., average delay added to all jobs queued on a broken machine).

### **2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies**

Process mining evidence will reveal:

*   **Bottleneck Identification & Quantification:**
    *   **Technique:** ** bottleneck analysis ** via process model with **performance visualization** (e.g., in Disco or Celonis). We look for:
        1.  **High Waiting Arcs:** Arcs (edges) between tasks with long durations indicate queues. The *sink* of that arc is the bottleneck.
        2.  **High Resource Utilization (>85-90%)** coupled with long queue times in the performance overlay.
    *   *Evidence:* A machine like `MILL-03` consistently shows 95% utilization and the longest average queue time. Its throughput (jobs/day) is significantly lower than others, directly throttling overall makespan.

*   **Poor Task Prioritization:**
    *   **Technique:** **Variant Analysis** filtering jobs by `Order Priority`. Compare flow times of "High (Urgent)" vs. "Medium/Low" jobs.
    *   *Evidence:* High-priority jobs (`JOB-7005`) are often delayed not because of their priority, but because they are routed to the same bottleneck machine (`MILL-03`) as medium-priority jobs with earlier due dates, proving local dispatching fails globally.

*   **Suboptimal Sequencing & Setup Bloat:**
    *   **Technique:** **Case Attribute analysis** combined with **setup matrix**. We look for jobs with dissimilar previous jobs causing long setups.
    *   *Evidence:* Analysis shows that switching from "Aluminum Alloy A" to "Stainless Steel B" on `CUT-01` incurs a 45-min setup, whereas switching between two aluminum alloys takes 10 mins. Random job sequencing ignores this, causing excessive non-productive time.

*   **Starvation & WIP Bullwhip:**
    *   **Technique:** **Token-based visualization** in the process model. Track WIP accumulation at queues. Correlate with `Resource Contention` periods.
    *   *Evidence:* Downstream workstation `GRIND-02` shows periodic starvation. Event logs reveal its upstream `MILL-03` releases batches irregularly, overwhelming GRIND-02's feeder buffer and causing WIP pile-up before MILL-03.

### **3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness**

*   **Limitations of Static Dispatching Rules:** The current "First-Come-First-Served" or "Earliest Due Date" rules are myopic. They ignore:
    *   Future load on downstream machines.
    *   Expensive setup times between dissimilar jobs.
    *   True remaining processing time.
*   **Lack of Real-Time Visibility:** Dispatchers react to events (e.g., "Waiting for resource") without a predictive view of how a decision today affects the schedule next week.
*   **Inaccurate Duration/Setup Estimates:** Plans are likely optimistic "theoretical" times. Process mining reveals actual distributions (e.g., 70% of tasks overrun by 10-20%), causing cascading delays.
*   **Ineffective Sequence Handling:** No system dynamically groups jobs by *transition similarity* to minimize setups.
*   **Poor Disruption Response:** Urgent jobs are inserted locally, disrupting the flow without re-optimizing the rest of the schedule.

**How Process Mining Differentiates Causes:**
*   If **utilization is high AND queue times are high**, it's a **capacity problem** (need more resources or better sequencing).
*   If **utilization is low AND jobs are waiting**, it's a **coordination/routing problem** (poor dispatching rules).
*   If **actual task durations consistently exceed planned** regardless of machine, it's a **planning/estimation problem**.
*   If **delays correlate with specific disruption events** (breakdowns), it's a **resilience problem** (lack of buffer or recovery logic).

### **4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies**

**Strategy 1: Dynamic Predictive Dispatching (DPD)**
*   **Core Logic:** Replace static rules with a dynamic scoring function for each eligible job at a machine:
    `Score(Job) = w1*(1 / Remaining_Processing_Time) + w2*(1 / Time_Urgency) - w3*(Estimated_Setup_Time_from_Previous) - w4*(Downstream_Bottleneck_Queue_Length)`
    Weights (`w1-w4`) are calibrated via process mining: e.g., `w3` is high if the setup matrix shows large penalties.
*   **Uses Process Mining Data:** Real-time queue lengths, estimated setup times from the matrix, downstream bottleneck status from model performance.
*   **Addresses:** Poor prioritization, sequence-dependent setup neglect, WIP buildup.
*   **Expected Impact:** Reduced average flow time (15-25%), higher on-time delivery for high-priority jobs, reduced queue times at bottlenecks.

**Strategy 2: Probabilistic Robust Scheduling with Digital Twin**
*   **Core Logic:** Build a **Digital Twin** of the shop floor. Use mined distributions (not point estimates) for:
    *   Task durations (Fitted to distributions: e.g., Weibull).
    *   Machine breakdowns (MTBF/MTTR distributions).
    *   Setup times (per transition).
    Generate schedules via **Monte Carlo Simulation** during planning, creating a *distribution of completion dates* rather than a single date. Select the schedule with the best "on-time confidence" (e.g., 90th percentile lead time).
*   **Uses Process Mining Data:** Full empirical duration, setup, and disruption distributions.
*   **Addresses:** Unpredictable lead times, disruption impact, over/under-promising.
*   **Expected Impact:** More achievable due dates, reduced penalty costs, improved customer trust.

**Strategy 3: Setup-Aware Job Batching & Sequencing at Bottlenecks**
*   **Core Logic:** For identified bottleneck machines, implement a **batch sequencing optimizer**. Jobs are grouped into batches where the *total processing time + setup time* is minimized, using a Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) approach where "distances" are setup times. Within a batch, order is determined by due date/priority.
*   **Uses Process Mining Data:** The setup duration matrix from log analysis.
*   **Addresses:** Suboptimal sequencing, excessive setup times, WIP variability.
*   **Expected Impact:** Reduced setup time per unit (20-40% on bottleneck machines), increased throughput, smoother WIP flow.

### **5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement**

**Simulation Framework:**
*   **Tool:** Use a DES tool (e.g., SimPy, AnyLogic) parameterized with process mining outputs.
*   **Calibration:** Import:
    *   **Routing:** From discovered process model.
    *   **Durations:** Sampled from empirical distributions per task/resource.
    *   **Setups:** From the transition matrix.
    *   **Breakdowns:** As a stochastic process with calibrated frequency/duration.
    *   **Arrivals:** From historical job release patterns.
*   **Test Scenarios:**
    1.  **Baseline:** Current rule-based system.
    2.  **Strategy 1 (DPD) vs. Strategy 2 (Probabilistic).**
    3.  **Stress Test:** High load (+30% jobs) + frequent breakdowns (double MTBF).
    4.  **Disruption Test:** Simulate a major bottleneck machine failure.
*   **Evaluation Metrics:** Compare **Makespan**, **% Tardiness**, **Average WIP**, **Resource Utilization Balance**, **Schedule Adherence**.

**Continuous Improvement Framework:**
1.  **Deploy & Monitor:** Implement the chosen strategy (e.g., DPD) in the MES.
2.  **Ongoing Process Mining:** Run nightly process mining jobs to update the process model and performance metrics.
3.  **KPI Dashboard:** Track in real-time: On-Time %, Flow Time, Bottleneck Utilization, Setup Time Savings.
4.  **Drift Detection:** Use statistical process control on KPIs. A sustained shift (e.g., increased flow time) triggers an alert.
5.  **Adaptive Learning:** If drift is detected (e.g., a new type of machine becomes available, or a new disruption pattern emerges), retrain the scheduling logic weights or the digital twin parameters using the latest data.
6.  **Periodic Re-optimization:** Quarterly, re-run the full process mining and simulation to refine strategies.

This end-to-end, evidence-based approach moves Precision Parts from reactive chaos to proactive, optimized control, directly targeting their high tardiness and WIP through the disciplined application of process mining and advanced scheduling logic.
