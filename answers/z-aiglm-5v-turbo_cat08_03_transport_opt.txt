**To:** Speedy Parcels Management
**From:** Senior Process Mining Consultant

## **1. Process Discovery and Conformance Checking**

To transform your raw data into actionable insights, we must first construct a reliable digital twin of your operations.

### **Data Preprocessing and Integration**
The primary challenge in this scenario is the **heterogeneity of data granularity and semantics**. We have high-frequency telemetry (GPS), low-frequency discrete events (Scanners), and static reference data (Dispatch).

*   **Case ID Definition:** We propose defining the **Case ID as "Vehicle-Day" (e.g., V12-20241205)**. This allows us to analyze the complete lifecycle of a van𠏋 shift㻖rom "Start Shift" to "End Shift"櫘apturing all intermediate travel, service, and delay events.
*   **Event Unification & Timestamp Alignment:**
    *   **Fusion Logic:** We will align GPS data with Scanner events using timestamps and location proximity. For example, a GPS event indicating `Speed=0` at a specific coordinate followed closely by a Scanner event `Arrive Customer` at the same coordinate defines a precise "Stop."
    *   **Inference of Hidden Activities:** The gap between the GPS-detected stop (vehicle stationary) and the Scanner "Arrive Customer" event can be inferred as **"Time spent searching for parking"**, a critical hidden inefficiency in last-mile delivery.
*   **Challenges Encountered:**
    *   **Noise/GPS Drift:** GPS coordinates inside urban "canyons" may fluctuate, causing false positives for movement. Smoothing algorithms (e.g., Kalman filters) will be required.
    *   **Missing Events:** Drivers may forget to scan "Depart Customer." We will use heuristics (e.g., if speed > 5km/h after a delivery success, auto-generate a "Depart Customer" event) to ensure process continuity.

### **Visualizing the Actual Process (Discovery)**
Using discovery algorithms (such as the **Inductive Visual Miner** or **Fuzzy Miner** suitable for noisy data), we will visualize the "As-Is" process.
*   **The Model:** Unlike a linear business process (Order $\to$ Pay $\to$ Ship), logistics processes are highly loopy. The model will likely display a central loop: **[Travel] $\to$ [Arrive] $\to$ [Service] $\to$ [Depart]**.
*   **Deviations:** We expect to see distinct "spaghetti-like" paths branching off for exceptions, such as:
    *   **The Failure Loop:** Arrive $\to$ Fail $\to$ (Return to Depot OR Next Stop).
    *   **The Breakdown Path:** Travel $\to$ Unscheduled Stop (Breakdown) $\to$ End Shift (Abort).
    *   **The Delay Path:** Travel $\to$ Traffic Jam $\to$ Travel.

### **Conformance Checking (Planned vs. Actual)**
We will treat the **Dispatch System's Planned Route** as the reference model (the "To-Be") and replay the actual event log against it.
*   **Sequence Deviations:** Did driver D105 visit Customer B before Customer A, despite the plan dictating A $\to$ B? (This often indicates "driver intuition" overriding the algorithm, which may be good or bad).
*   **Missing Activities:** Were there assigned packages (in Dispatch) that never generated a "Delivery Success" or "Failed" event? (Lost packages).
*   **Timing Deviations:**
    *   *Negative Deviation (Too Fast):* Indicates unsafe driving or inaccurate planning estimates.
    *   *Positive Deviation (Late):* The core issue. We will flag stops where `(Actual Arrival - Planned Arrival) > Threshold`.

## **2. Performance Analysis and Bottleneck Identification**

Once the process map is established, we move from *qualitative topology* to *quantitative performance*.

### **Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)**
We will calculate the following metrics directly from the integrated event log:

| KPI | Calculation Logic | Strategic Goal |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **On-Time Delivery (OTD)** | Count(`Actual Arrival` within `Time Window`) / Total Stops | Customer Satisfaction |
| **Dwell (Service) Time** | Duration(`Delivery Success` - `Arrive Customer`) | Efficiency |
| **Search/Parking Time** | Duration(`Arrive Customer` (Scanner) - `Stop Start` (GPS)) | Cost Reduction |
| **Fuel Waste Index** | Sum of duration where `Status = Idle` AND `Location != Depot/Customer` | Cost/Fuel Saving |
| **First Attempt Success Rate (FASR)** | Count(`Delivery Success` on 1st Visit) / Total Visits | Cost Reduction (avoid re-delivery) |
| **Vehicle Utilization** | Duration(`Ignition On` - `Maintenance/Break`) / Total Shift Length | Asset Efficiency |

### **Bottleneck Identification Techniques**
We will apply **bottleneck highlighting** based on durations to pinpoint friction points:

1.  **Activity-Based Bottlenecks:** Is the "Delivery Success" activity taking 15 minutes instead of the target 3 minutes? We can drill down to see if this correlates with **Package Size** (if available in data) or **Driver Skill**.
2.  **Geospatial/Spatial Bottlenecks:** By overlaying the **"Low Speed Detected"** events on a map, we can identify "Hotspots" of congestion. If every vehicle spends 10 extra minutes at Intersection X at 8:30 AM, that is a systemic bottleneck caused by infrastructure, not operations.
3.  **Temporal Bottlenecks:** Analyzing performance across different time slots (e.g., Morning vs. Afternoon). Perhaps afternoon routes consistently fail time windows because morning overflows cause cascading delays.
4.  **Resource-Specific Bottlenecks:** Comparing **Vehicle Age** or **Driver ID**. If Vehicle V12 (from the log) has frequent "Unscheduled Stops," it is a bottleneck resource requiring maintenance.

## **3. Root Cause Analysis for Inefficiencies**

Identifying *that* a delay happened is easy; identifying *why* requires deeper mining.

### **Hypothesis Validation via Mining:**

*   **Hypothesis 1: Suboptimal Routing (Static Plans)**
    *   *Analysis:* Compare **Planned Route Distance/Time** vs. **Actual**. If drivers consistently deviate from the plan and achieve faster times (or arrive on time despite a tight plan), the routing engine's algorithms (heuristics) are flawed or out-of-date regarding road speeds.
    *   *Validation:* High rate of "Sequence Deviations" that result in positive outcomes indicates bad planning.

*   **Hypothesis 2: Inaccurate Time Estimations**
    *   *Analysis:* Scatter plot of **Estimated Service Time** (from Dispatch) vs. **Actual Service Time**.
    *   *Validation:* If the error margin is high (+/- 50%), planners are guessing. This leads to domino-effect delays.

*   **Hypothesis 3: Traffic Congestion Impact**
    *   *Analysis:* Correlate **Timestamp** and **Location (Lat/Lon)** of "Low Speed" events with external traffic APIs or historical averages.
    *   *Validation:* If "Traffic Jam" events account for >20% of total drive time, dynamic routing is needed.

*   **Hypothesis 4: The "Failed Delivery" Cost Spiral**
    *   *Analysis:* Trace the lifecycle of packages tagged "Delivery Failed." Do they go back to the depot? Are they re-dispatched the next day?
    *   *Validation:* Calculating the cost-per-package for "Failed" items (double handling, double fuel) often reveals them as the single biggest profitability killer.

*   **Hypothesis 5: Driver Behavior/Variability**
    *   *Analysis:* **Variant Analysis.** Group traces by Driver ID. Compare the "Top 10% Efficient Drivers" vs. "Bottom 10%".
    *   *Validation:* Do efficient drivers have shorter "Search/Parking" times? This suggests better local knowledge or decision-making regarding parking spots, not just driving speed.

## **4. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies**

Based on the potential insights, here are three concrete optimization strategies:

### **Strategy 1: Implementation of Dynamic "Micro-Routing"**
*   **Inefficiency Targeted:** Traffic congestion and static schedule rigidity.
*   **Root Cause:** The dispatch system plans routes assuming constant speeds, but reality involves variable traffic.
*   **Data Support:** Process mining reveals specific **time-location pairs** where delays occur (e.g., "Highway A is always jammed at 08:15").
*   **Proposal:** Implement a system that allows for **intra-day re-optimization**. When a driver scans "Depart Customer," the system recalculates the remaining route based on *current* live traffic and historical delay probabilities discovered in the log.
*   **Expected Impact:** **+15% On-Time Delivery Rate**, reduced fuel consumption from idling in known jams.

### **Strategy 2: "Smart-Zone" Territory Rebalancing**
*   **Inefficiency Targeted:** High variability in service times and route complexity.
*   **Root Cause:** Mixing difficult delivery zones (e.g., dense apartments with no parking, gated communities) with easy zones (suburban curbside) creates unpredictable shifts.
*   **Data Support:** Cluster analysis of **"Dwell Time" + "Search Time"** by zip code/postal area.
*   **Proposal:** Redraw delivery territories so that **high-touch areas** (long search/dwell) are grouped together. Assign drivers specifically to "Complex Urban" or "Easy Suburban" routes. Furthermore, provide specific **parking instructions** in the handheld scanner for locations with historically long "Search Times."
*   **Expected Impact:** **Reduced Overtime**, more predictable shift lengths, lower driver stress.

### **Strategy 3: Predictive Maintenance & Asset Reliability Program**
*   **Inefficiency Targeted:** Unscheduled breakdowns (e.g., V12 Engine Warning Light) leading to failed deliveries and expensive towing/rescheduling.
*   **Root Cause:** Reactive maintenance ("fix it when it breaks").
*   **Data Support:** Correlate **Vehicle ID** with frequency of **Warning Lights/Breakdowns** and utilization intensity (km driven, harsh braking events inferred from GPS deceleration spikes).
*   **Proposal:** Move to **Condition-Based Maintenance (CBM)**. If the data shows that vehicles tend to trigger "Engine Warning" lights after ~4,500 km of intense urban driving (stop-start traffic), schedule preventative checks at 4,000 km for high-utilization vehicles.
*   **Expected Impact:** **~90% reduction in roadside failures**, increased asset availability, protection of OTD reputation.

## **5. Considering Operational Constraints and Monitoring**

### **Addressing Constraints**
Any optimization must operate within hard boundaries:
*   **Legal Constraints:** Route optimizations (Strategy 1) must include "Hard Windows" for driver working hours (e.g., mandatory breaks, max shift length) to prevent fatigue and legal penalties. The conformance checker should flag "Working Time Violations."
*   **Physical Constraints:** Vehicles have volume/weight limits. Strategy 2 (Territory Balancing) must ensure we don't create a route that is geographically compact but physically impossible to load.
*   **Customer Constraints:** Time windows are contractual promises. While we optimize for efficiency, the solver must prioritize meeting the customer window over saving 2 minutes of fuel.

    *   **Root Cause Pareto Chart:** Tracking if "Traffic" is still the #1 cause of delay or if it has shifted to "Admin Issues."
