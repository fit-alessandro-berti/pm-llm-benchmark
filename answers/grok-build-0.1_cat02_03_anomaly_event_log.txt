**Anomaly Analysis: Procure-to-Pay (P2P) Event Log**

Below is a detailed analysis of the four cases. I compared the sequences against standard P2P best practices and internal control principles (segregation of duties, authorization before commitment, three-way matching, and payment after confirmation of receipt).

### **Case 1001: Normal (Compliant)**
**Sequence:**
Create PR  Approve PR  Issue PO  Receive Goods  Vendor Invoice Received  Match Invoice to PO  Release Payment  Close Case

**Assessment:** This follows the standard P2P flow with proper controls. No anomalies detected.

---

### **Case 1002: Highly Anomalous (Multiple Critical Control Failures)**

**Sequence:**
Create PR  **Issue PO**  Vendor Invoice Received  **Release Payment**  Approve PR  Receive Goods  Match Invoice to PO  Close Case

**Key Anomalies:**
1. **Purchase Order issued without approval**  
   The requisition was never approved before the PO was created. This bypasses a fundamental control.

2. **Payment released before goods were received**  
   Payment was made on February 3rd, but goods were only received on February 4th. This creates a significant risk of paying for goods that were never delivered.

3. **Approval occurred after the fact (post-payment)**  
   The requisition was approved *after* the PO was issued, the invoice was received, and payment was already released. This renders the approval meaningless.

4. **Invoice matching performed after payment**  
   The three-way match only occurred after the payment had already been made.

**Why This Is Problematic:**
This case shows a near-complete breakdown of controls. It creates high risk of:
- Fraud (e.g., fake vendors or collusion)
- Payment for undelivered goods
- Unauthorized purchases
- Audit/compliance violations

This would typically be flagged as a **critical control deficiency**.

---

### **Case 1003: Significantly Anomalous**

**Sequence:**
Create PR  Approve PR  Issue PO  **Release Payment**  Vendor Invoice Received  Receive Goods  Match Invoice to PO  Close Case

**Key Anomalies:**
1. **Payment released before goods receipt**  
   Payment was made on Feb 8, but goods were only received on Feb 10.

2. **Payment released before invoice receipt**  
   The vendor invoice only arrived on Feb 9 — one day *after* payment was already released.

3. **Three-way match performed after payment**

**Why This Is Problematic:**
Paying a vendor before confirming that goods were received (and before even receiving the invoice) is a major control failure. It exposes the organization to:
- Risk of paying for goods that were never delivered
- Difficulty recovering funds if there is a dispute
- Potential duplicate payments or overpayments

This is a serious deviation from the "pay after receipt and matching" principle.

---

### **Case 1004: Anomalous (Process Order Reversed)**

**Sequence:**
Create PR  Approve PR  **Receive Goods**  Issue PO  Vendor Invoice Received  Match Invoice to PO  Release Payment  Close Case

**Key Anomalies:**
1. **Goods received before Purchase Order was issued**  
   The organization received the goods on Feb 6, but the PO was only issued on Feb 6 at 09:15 (after goods receipt).

2. **PO created after the commitment was already made**  
   The PO appears to be created retroactively rather than serving as the authorization document.

**Why This Is Problematic:**
In a proper P2P process, the Purchase Order serves as the **authorization** to the vendor. Receiving goods before a PO exists suggests:
- Unauthorized or "off-contract" purchasing
- Emergency purchases that bypassed normal controls
- Potential circumvention of budget checks or vendor approval processes
- Weak audit trail (the PO no longer serves its control purpose)

This is often seen in "maverick spending" or when someone orders goods directly from a vendor without going through procurement.

| Case   | Main Issues                                      | Severity     | Primary Risk                          |
|--------|--------------------------------------------------|--------------|---------------------------------------|
| 1001   | None                                             | None         | -                                     |

### **Recommendations**
- **Case 1002** should be investigated as a potential fraud or serious control breach.
- Cases 1003 and 1004 should be reviewed for process violations and root causes (e.g., system bypass, emergency procedures, or maverick buying).
- The organization should implement stronger system-enforced controls (e.g., preventing payment without a goods receipt and approved PO).
