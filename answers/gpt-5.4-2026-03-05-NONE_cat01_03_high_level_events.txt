## Proposed high-level process model

Based on the event patterns in cases **A1** and **B2**, the low-level events can be aggregated into **4 high-level manufacturing stages**:

1. **Material Preparation**
2. **Welding / Assembly**
3. **Surface Finishing**
4. **Final Quality Inspection**

These groupings are consistent across both cases and reflect a natural progression from raw material intake to finished-part validation.

---

## 1) High-level activities and their grouped low-level events

### High-Level Activity 1: Material Preparation
**Proposed low-level events included:**
- Retrieve raw metal sheet
- Scan barcode
- Place metal sheet on table
- Align metal sheet
- Preheat metal sheet

**Why this grouping makes sense:**
- These events all occur at the start of the case.
- They prepare the raw material for the main transformation step.
- They are tightly connected in purpose: identify the part, position it, and condition it for subsequent work.
- Even though different resources are involved (operator, robot arm, heating unit), they all contribute to making the material ready.

**Rationale:**
This is a classic preparation phase. The sheet is fetched, identified, positioned, aligned, and thermally conditioned before welding begins.

---

### High-Level Activity 2: Welding / Assembly
**Proposed low-level events included:**
- Pick up welding tool
- Weld corner A
- Weld corner B
- Measure weld integrity

**Why this grouping makes sense:**
- These actions are centered on the welding operation.
- The tool pickup is a setup action directly enabling welding.
- The two weld events are the core transformation steps.
- The weld integrity measurement is an immediate in-process quality check tightly coupled to welding.

**Rationale:**
This stage represents the primary assembly/transformation work. Including the weld integrity measurement here is appropriate because it verifies the success of the welding operation before the part moves forward.

**Alternative naming:**
- Welding Operation
- Assembly and Weld Verification
- Join & Verify

---

### High-Level Activity 3: Surface Finishing
**Proposed low-level events included:**
- Apply protective coating
- Dry coating

**Why this grouping makes sense:**
- These events are sequential and functionally dependent.
- Drying is not a separate business goal; it completes the coating process.
- Both events deal with surface treatment and product finishing rather than assembly.

**Rationale:**
After welding, the part receives a protective finish. Application and curing/drying are naturally one finishing stage.

**Alternative naming:**
- Coating and Curing
- Protective Finishing
- Surface Treatment

---

### High-Level Activity 4: Final Quality Inspection
**Proposed low-level events included:**
- Visual check

**Why this grouping makes sense:**
- It occurs at the end of the process.
- It represents a distinct acceptance step after all transformation and finishing work is done.
- It is performed by a different role focused on final validation.

**Rationale:**
This is the final release/inspection gate to confirm the finished part is acceptable.

**Alternative naming:**
- Final Inspection
- End-of-Line Quality Check
- Final Visual QA

## 4) General rules inferred for grouping the full log

You said the full log follows the same pattern, so these rules can be used more broadly.

### Rule 1: Group by manufacturing intent
Combine events that contribute to the same operational objective:
- **Preparation**  retrieve, identify, place, align, preheat
- **Assembly**  tool setup, joining/welding, in-process weld check
- **Finishing**  coating, curing/drying
- **Inspection**  final visual or acceptance checks

### Rule 2: Preserve phase boundaries
Start a new high-level activity when there is a clear change in:
- process purpose,
- resource type,
- equipment type,
- or product state.

Example:
- moving from **Heating Unit #1 preheat** to **Operator B welding** indicates a shift from preparation to assembly.

### Rule 3: Keep in-process checks with the operation they validate
- **Measure weld integrity** belongs with **Welding / Assembly**, not with final inspection,
because it validates the weld immediately after creation.

### Rule 4: Keep terminal acceptance checks separate
- **Visual check** should remain a separate final quality stage because it determines end-of-line acceptability.

### Rule 5: Use temporal proximity as supporting evidence, not the only rule
The grouped events are close in time, but the stronger basis is shared purpose and process dependency.
