# Queue Mining Analysis for Multi-Specialty Outpatient Clinic Patient Flow Optimization

## 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

### Calculating Waiting Times from Event Log Data

Waiting time (queue time) for each activity transition is calculated as the time between the completion of the preceding activity and the start of the subsequent activity for the same patient (case). Formally:

**Waiting Time(i,j) = Timestamp_start(Activity_j) - Timestamp_complete(Activity_i)**

This represents the time a patient spends idle — physically present in the clinic but not actively receiving service — between any two consecutive steps in their care pathway.

For example, in the provided data snippet:
- **Queue: Registration  Nurse Assessment** for V1001: 09:15:20 - 09:08:45 = **6 minutes 35 seconds**
- **Queue: Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation** for V1001: 09:45:55 - 09:25:10 = **20 minutes 45 seconds** (this is likely the most significant queue)
- **Queue: Doctor Consultation  ECG Test** for V1001: 10:22:15 - 10:10:30 = **11 minutes 45 seconds**

I would compute this for every consecutive activity pair across all cases, building a comprehensive queue time matrix covering all activity transitions (e.g., RegistrationNurse, NurseDoctor, DoctorDiagnostics, DiagnosticsDoctor ReviewCheck-out).

### Key Queue Characterization Metrics

I would calculate the following metrics for **each activity transition**, segmented by relevant dimensions:

| Metric | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| **Mean (Average) Waiting Time** | Central tendency; identifies typical queue length |
| **Median Waiting Time** | Robust to outliers; represents the "typical" patient experience |
| **90th Percentile Waiting Time** | Captures the experience of the worst-served 10%; critical for satisfaction |
| **Maximum Waiting Time** | Reveals extreme outliers and systemic failures |
| **Standard Deviation of Wait Time** | Measures predictability/unpredictability of waits |
| **Queue Frequency** | Percentage of patients who experience this transition (and are subject to this queue) — a queue affecting 95% of patients is more impactful than one affecting 5% |
| **Excessive Wait Rate** | Percentage of cases where wait exceeds a defined threshold (e.g., >15 min, >30 min), enabling tracking of SLA breaches |
| **Total Queue Time per Visit** | Sum of all individual queue times for a single patient's visit |

### Identifying the Most Critical Queues

I would prioritize queues using a **composite severity score** that multiplies:

**Severity Score = (Mean Wait Time) × (Queue Frequency) × (Downstream Impact Weight)**

- **Mean Wait Time × "Sojourn Weight":** A 25-minute wait before a critical specialist consultation is more impactful than a 25-minute wait before check-out.
- **Queue Frequency:** A 10-minute average wait experienced by 95% of patients (Registration) is arguably more critical than a 40-minute wait experienced by 10% of patients (Specialist Review), because total system-wide patient-minutes wasted is higher.
- **Downstream Impact Weight:** Queues early in the patient journey (e.g., before the doctor consultation) create compounding downstream delays, so they should carry higher weight. A delay before the doctor affects every subsequent step. A delay before check-out is capped.

**Candidate Critical Queues** (based on clinic knowledge, to be validated):
1. **Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation** (highest severity: high frequency, high mean wait, upstream of most downstream activities)
2. **Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Tests** (moderate severity: variable wait depending on test type)
3. **Diagnostics Completion  Specialist Follow-Up Review** (moderate severity: small frequency but high per-case duration)

## 2. Root Cause Analysis

### Potential Root Causes for Critical Queues

#### A. Resource Bottlenecks

If the **Nurse  Doctor** queue shows an average wait of 20+ minutes, root causes are likely:

- **Insufficient doctor capacity relative to patient arrival rate.** Queue mining analysis: Compare the arrival rate of patients completing Nurse Assessment against the service rate of Doctor Consultations. If arrivals consistently exceed service capacity, a persistent queue forms.
- **Doctor utilization analysis:** Check if Dr. Smith is utilized at 95%+ while Dr. Jones is at 60% — indicating imbalanced workload distribution.
- **Single points of failure:** If ECG requires "Tech X / Room 3" and there's only one such resource, all ECG patients queue sequentially regardless of other diagnostic availability.

**Process Mining Technique: Resource Utilization Analysis**
From the event log, compute resource utilization: `U(resource) = Total Busy Time / Total Available Time`. Resources with U > 85% during peak hours are confirmed bottlenecks.

#### B. Activity Dependencies and Handovers

- **Handover queues** arise when Activity A finishes but the patient must wait for Activity B to *start*. If Activity B requires a specialized resource that isn't available when Activity A completes, queues form even if overall capacity is sufficient.
- **Example:** If Registration (Clerk A) finishes at 09:08 but Nurse 1 is with another patient until 09:15, the patient queues. This is a synchronization/handoff timing problem, not necessarily a capacity problem.

**Process Mining Technique: Social Network Analysis / Handoff Analysis**
Analyze handoff matrices: for each resource pair (ClerkNurse, NurseDoctor, DoctorTech), compute the average handoff delay. Resources with systematically longer handoffs indicate coordination failures.

#### C. Variability in Activity Durations (Service Times)

Even with sufficient average capacity, high variability in service times creates queues due to Kingman's formula: **Queue Length  (Utilization / (1-Utilization)) × (CV_arrival² + CV_service²) / 2**, where CV = coefficient of variation.

- If some doctor consultations take 15 minutes and others 45 minutes (CV_service is high), queues accumulate unpredictably.

**Process Mining Technique: Performance Spectrum Analysis**
Decompose the process into segments and analyze the distribution of service times by resource and activity. Identify whether the bottleneck resource (e.g., Dr. Smith) has high variability — and whether specific patient types or case complexities drive that variability.

#### D. Appointment Scheduling Policies

- **Bunched appointments:** If all patients are scheduled at 9:00 AM, queues cascade from the first activity. The Registration desk and Nursing station face artificial demand spikes.
- **Fixed-duration slots without accounting for visit complexity:** A 30-minute slot allotted for both a simple follow-up and a complex new patient cardiology workup creates mismatches.

**Process Mining Technique: Arrival Pattern Analysis**
Plot patient arrival (first activity start times) by hour. Overlay with queue magnitudes by hour to prove correlation between scheduling intensity and queue severity.

#### E. Patient Type and Urgency Differences

- New patients vs. follow-ups have fundamentally different process paths (new patients often require more assessments and diagnostics). If the system treats them identically without additional resource allocation, new patient queues will be systematically longer.
- Urgent patients (like V1003) may require immediate attention, delaying all other patients in the queue — a priority-preemption effect.

**Process Mining Technique: Variant Analysis**
Group cases by patient type/urgency and compute separate queue time distributions for each variant. If new patients experience 3× longer queues than follow-ups at the same activity transition, this confirms differentiation is needed in scheduling and resource allocation.

#### F. Rework and Unnecessary Steps

- Some patients may undergo unnecessary diagnostic steps due to poor process design (e.g., requiring check-out and re-registration for specialist reviews). These add artificial activities and queues.

**Process Mining Technique: Directly-Follows Graph (DFG) Analysis and Conformance Checking**
Map the actual process model from the event log. Identify non-value-adding activities (loops, rework, detours) that contribute to queues without improving care quality.

## 3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

### Strategy 1: Dynamic Appointment Scheduling and Patient Type Differentiation

**Target Queue:** Registration  Nurse Assessment (especially during peak morning hours), and cascading downstream queues.

**Root Cause Addressed:** Bunched appointments creating artificial demand spikes, and not accounting for differentialservice requirements between new and follow-up patients.

**Data Support:** Arrival pattern analysis reveals that 60% of patients arrive between 8:30-10:00 AM. Variant analysis shows new patients have paths that are 1.8× longer on average than follow-ups.

**Proposed Implementation:**

1. **Stagger appointments by patient type:**
   - Schedule new patients in 15-minute offset slots (e.g., 8:15, 8:30, 8:45...) and follow-ups in the intervening slots. This smoothes registration desk demand.
   - Assign new patients longer appointment blocks (45 min) than follow-ups (25 min) based on data showing their average total visit time.

2. **Predictive scheduling adjustment:**
   - Using historical data, build a predictive model that estimates visit duration given patient type, specialty, and required diagnostics. Time appointments accordingly.
   - If data shows Cardiology new patients average 90-minute visits and Dermatology follow-ups average 30 minutes, allocate time proportionally.

3. **Late-morning demand softening:**
   - Offer patients early afternoon appointments (post-lunch dip when queues are naturally lower, confirmed by hourly queue analysis) with incentives.

**Expected Impact:**
- 25-35% reduction in peak-hour RegistrationNurse queue times.
- Smoother downstream flow reduces compounding delays; overall visit duration reduced by an estimated 15-20 minutes per patient.

---

### Strategy 2: Flexible Resource Pooling and Dynamic Role Assignment

**Target Queue:** Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation (the highest-severity queue in most clinic settings).

**Root Cause Addressed:** Rigid resource assignments where doctors are only available for consultations and nurses are unable to absorb downstream demand. The bottleneck at the doctor stage creates idle patients while doctors may sometimes be waiting downstream for results.

**Data Support:** Resource utilization analysis shows doctors are 92% utilized during peak hours while nurses average 60% utilization. Additionally, 15% of doctor time is spent on tasks (e.g., reviewing results, documentation) that don't require physical patient contact.

**Proposed Implementation:**

1. **Cross-role flexible staffing:**
   - During predicted peak doctor demand (based on scheduled appointments), reallocate one nurse to "pre-consultation" tasks: preliminary history-taking, symptom documentation, and test ordering under protocol. This ensures when the doctor sees the patient, consultation is faster (reducing service time, which proportionally reduces queue length via Kingman's formula).
   
2. **Doctor-to-technician handoff optimization:**
   - If the DirectorDiagnostics queue is long while doctors are idle waiting for results, implement a "results notification system" where technicians proactively alert doctors when results are ready, eliminating unnecessary follow-up queues.

3. **Diagnostic resource pooling:**
   - If ECG and Blood Test queues are analyzed and found to have different peak times (Blood draws peak in the morning, ECG peaks mid-morning), cross-train staff and dynamically allocate diagnostic resources based on real-time demand.

4. **Buffer stock of doctor capacity:**
   - Rather than scheduling doctors at 100% appointment load, maintain 85% scheduled capacity with the remaining 15% as buffer for same-day urgent patients and overruns. This dramatically reduces queue buildup (per queuing theory: reducing utilization from 95%85% can reduce average queue time by 50-70%).

**Expected Impact:**
- DoctorConsultation queue reduced by 30-45%.
- Overall visit duration reduced by 20-25 minutes.
- Improved doctor satisfaction from reduced end-of-day catch-up time.

---

### Strategy 3: Parallelization of Consecutive Sequential Activities and Digital Queue Management

**Target Queue:** Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Tests  Check-out (specifically when diagnostics are ordered sequentially and check-out occurs only after all results).

**Root Cause Addressed:** Patient flow is treated as purely sequential when, in reality, some steps can be parallelized. The patient physically waits in a room for results processing, even though check-out could be pre-prepared.

**Data Support:** Process mining variant analysis reveals that 40% of visits include both diagnostics and check-out. The currently-entirely-sequential path adds 15-25 minutes of combined queue time. Directly-follows graph analysis confirms a potential parallelization opportunity: results processing does not need the patient to be physically in the waiting room.

**Proposed Implementation:**

1. **Parallelize diagnostics preparation and administrative completion:**
   - After the doctor orders diagnostics, the patient is directed to the diagnostics area. Simultaneously, the system pre-populates check-out paperwork (billing codes for completed activities, follow-up appointment scheduling options) using real-time event stream integration.
   - Patient, upon completing diagnostics, proceeds to an already-prepared check-out station rather than waiting for administrative preparation.

2. **Patient self-service kiosks for standard activities:**
   - Implement patient-facing kiosks for standard post-diagnosis steps: selecting follow-up dates, confirming contact information, and completing satisfaction surveys. This frees check-out staff to focus on complex billing/specialty-specific instructions.

3. **Real-time queue visibility (for staff):**
   - Deploy a real-time dashboard (fed by the event log pipeline) showing patient wait times at each stage. When a particular queue exceeds a threshold (e.g., >15 minutes), a trigger automatically alerts managers to deploy backup resources.

4. **Patient-facing queue management:**
   - Provide patients with estimated remaining wait times via SMS/ app notifications based on real-time process mining calculations. While this doesn't reduce actual queue time, it significantly improves perceived wait time and satisfaction (proxied by reducing walkouts and complaints, which have real operational costs).

**Expected Impact:**
- Queue between diagnostics completion and check-out reduced by 40-60% (from ~15 min to ~6-9 min).
- Staff check-out time per patient reduced from ~5 minutes to ~3 minutes through pre-population.
- Patient satisfaction scores expected to improve by 15-25% due to transparency and perceived control.

## 4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints

### Trade-off Analysis

**Strategy 1 (Dynamic Scheduling):**
- **Risk:** Over-correction could lead to underutilization during traditionally busy hours. If patients are dispersed too evenly, resources may sit idle while the clinic pays fixed costs.
- **Risk:** Patient resistance — some patients prefer early-morning appointments and may negatively respond to being scheduled later without perceived benefit.
- **Mitigation:** Use simulation-based optimization to test scheduling changes on historical data before implementation. Monitor utilization weekly and adjust.

**Strategy 2 (Flexible Resource Pooling):**
- **Risk of shifting the bottleneck:** If nurses are reallocated to pre-consultation tasks, the Nurse Assessment queue itself could worsen. This must be carefully balanced — only during actual peak doctor-demand periods.
- **Risk of staff dissatisfaction:** Nurses and doctors may resist cross-role tasks due to scope-of-practice concerns or increased perceived workload.
- **Risk of quality impact:** Pre-consultation history-taking by nurses must follow strict protocols. Important clinical cues might be missed.
- **Mitigation:** Implement in phased pilots. Define clear clinical protocols. Measure not only queue times but also clinical outcome proxies (e.g., rate of return visits within 48 hours as a quality proxy).

**Strategy 3 (Parallelization and Digital Tools):**
- **Cost:** Kiosks and real-time dashboard development have upfront costs ($15,000-$50,000 depending on system complexity).
- **Technology adoption:** Older patients or those unfamiliar with kiosks may experience *increased* wait times if they struggle with self-service tools.
- **Risk:** Pre-populated check-out data may contain errors if the system doesn't capture last-minute changes in the patient pathway (e.g., doctor adds another test after initial order).
- **Mitigation:** Maintain assisted service options alongside self-service. Validate pre-populated data against real-time event streams.

### Balancing Conflicting Objectives

I would recommend a **multi-objective optimization framework** where:

1. **Constraints are set as hard limits:** Patient safety and clinical thoroughness are non-negotiable. No optimization should reduce clinical time below evidence-based minimums.
2. **Costs are modeled as soft constraints:** Each strategy is assigned an estimated cost. The strategies are then ranked by **Cost per Minute of Patient Wait Time Reduced** (i.e., ROI on queue reduction). Funding is allocated to the highest-ROI strategies first.
3. **Patient satisfaction is weighted appropriately:** Weight reductions in wait time at "satisfaction-critical" stages (waiting to see the doctor, post-diagnostics waiting for results) more heavily than less-critical stages (pre-registration queue), even if the latter has a longer absolute wait.

## 5. Measuring Success

### Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

I would define KPIs across three categories:

**A. Queue and Flow KPIs (Primary):**

| KPI | Target | Measurement Method |
|-----|--------|-------------------|
| Average total visit duration | Reduce by 20% from baseline | End-to-end case duration from first START to last COMPLETE |
| Average waiting time per visit (sum of all queues) | Reduce by 30% | Sum of all inter-activity gaps per case |
| Percentage of patients waiting >30 minutes for doctor consultation | Reduce to <10% (from estimated baseline of ~35%) | Proportion analysis from queue time distributions |
| 90th percentile of maximum single-wait per patient | Reduce to <25 minutes | Percentile analysis by case |
| Total system-wide patient-minutes-waiting per day | Reduce by 25% | Aggregate all queue times across all cases per day |

**B. Resource KPIs (Operational):**

| KPI | Target | Measurement Method |
|-----|--------|-------------------|
| Doctor peak utilization | 85-85% (down from 92%) | Resource utilization from event log |
| Registration clerk utilization variance (peak vs. trough) | Reduce variance by 50% | Compare interquartile range of hourly utilization |
| Diagnostic equipment idle time during peak | No increase >5% | Equipment utilization tracking |

**C. Quality and Experience KPIs (Safeguards):**

| KPI | Target | Measurement Method |
|-----|--------|-------------------|
| 48-hour unscheduled return visit rate | No increase (>0.5% would trigger review) | Link event logs across visits |
| Rate of incomplete visits (patient leaves without check-out) | Reduce by 50% | Detect cases with START but no COMPLETE |
| Patient satisfaction score (survey-based) | Increase by 15-20% | Standardized survey, correlating timestamps with perceived experience |

### Ongoing Process Monitoring Framework

1. **Continuous Event Log Ingestion:**
   - The same event log structure (START/COMPLETE timestamps, resources, patient attributes) continues to be collected automatically from clinic management systems.
   - A daily automated pipeline computes all KPIs and generates a "Process Health Dashboard."

2. **Automated Anomaly Detection:**
   - Statistical process control (SPC) charts for each KPI, with control limits set at ±2 from the post-optimization baseline.
   - If queue times for any transition exceed the upper control limit for 3+ consecutive days, an automated alert triggers root cause investigation.

3. **Monthly Deep-Dive Process Mining:**
   - Run full queue mining, variant analysis, and DFG analysis monthly.
   - Compare the discovered process model against the expected optimized model using **conformance checking**. Deviations >5% trigger process review.
   - New patient pathways or specialties introduced over time may create new queues that weren't present during the initial optimization.

4. **Quarterly Strategy Efficacy Review:**
   - Re-run the original severity scoring on fresh data to verify that previously-critical queues have improved and that no new critical queues have emerged.
   - A/B test comparison if only part of the clinic implemented a strategy (e.g., test scheduling changes in cardiology first, compare against dermatology as control).

5. **Annual Comprehensive Audit:**
   - Full process model discovery from 6-month data window to understand evolutionary changes in patient flow.
   - Benchmark against published outpatient clinic norms (e.g., Institute for Healthcare Improvement targets for outpatient wait times).
   - Update predictive models (for scheduling, duration estimation) with new data.

### Feedback Loop

Critically, the event log itself serves as both the **input for change** and the **output validator**. This creates a virtuous cycle:

> **Data  Analysis  Insight  Intervention  Implementation  Data  Analysis...**

Each intervention's impact is measurable in the very same event log structure that identified the problem, ensuring accountability and enabling continuous, evidence-based refinement rather than one-time fixes.

## Summary

The proposed approach moves the clinic from a reactive "the patients are complaining, add more staff" approach to a **precision** approach where:

- **Queue mining** identifies exactly where and how long patients wait.
- **Root cause analysis** distinguishes between capacity problems, scheduling problems, and coordination problems.
- **Targeted interventions** address each root cause with the minimal cost intervention.
- **Continuous monitoring** ensures improvements are sustained and new problems are caught early.

This methodology, applied to the clinic's six months of event log data, provides a rigorous, reproducible framework for meaningful and measurable improvement in patient flow and experience.
