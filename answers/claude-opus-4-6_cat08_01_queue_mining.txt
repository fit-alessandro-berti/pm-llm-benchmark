

# Comprehensive Queue Mining Analysis for Multi-Specialty Outpatient Clinic

## 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

### 1.1 Calculating Waiting Times from Event Log Data

The event log provides both START and COMPLETE timestamps for each activity within a patient visit (case). This dual-timestamp structure is the foundation for precise queue time calculation.

**Definition of Waiting Time (Queue Time):**

Waiting time for a given patient between two consecutive activities is defined as:

```
Waiting Time = Start_Timestamp(Activity_n+1)  Complete_Timestamp(Activity_n)
```

For example, using patient V1001 from the event log:
- Registration COMPLETE: 09:08:45
- Nurse Assessment START: 09:15:20
- **Waiting Time (Registration  Nurse Assessment) = 6 minutes 35 seconds**

Similarly:
- Nurse Assessment COMPLETE: 09:25:10
- Doctor Consultation START: 09:45:55
- **Waiting Time (Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation) = 20 minutes 45 seconds**

This distinction is critical: the time between COMPLETE of one activity and START of the next is *pure waiting*—the patient is neither being served nor in transit in any productive sense. (If we wanted to further decompose this, we could distinguish between *transfer time* and *idle waiting*, but absent location-tracking data, the full interval is treated as waiting.)

**Important nuances in calculation:**

- **First activity pre-wait:** For Registration, the waiting time before it begins can be estimated as `Start_Timestamp(Registration)  Scheduled_Appointment_Time` (if appointment data is available) or `Start_Timestamp(Registration)  Arrival_Time` (if arrival is logged). If neither is available, the pre-registration wait is invisible, which is a known data limitation to flag.
- **Parallel/concurrent activities:** Some patients may undergo activities that branch and reconverge (e.g., blood test and X-ray ordered simultaneously). In such cases, we must reconstruct the actual process graph per case using the timestamps rather than assuming a strict linear sequence. The waiting time before a downstream activity would then be calculated from the completion of the *latest* prerequisite activity.
- **Negative or zero waiting times:** If `Start(Activity_n+1) < Complete(Activity_n)`, this could indicate data quality issues (timestamp errors) or genuinely overlapping activities (e.g., doctor begins reviewing while nurse finishes charting). These cases should be flagged and investigated.

### 1.2 Key Metrics for Queue Characterization

For each identified transition (queue point) between activities, I would calculate the following metrics:

| Metric | Purpose | Why It Matters |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| **Average Waiting Time** | Central tendency of the queue | Overall magnitude of the problem |
| **Median Waiting Time** | Robust central tendency (less sensitive to outliers) | More representative of "typical" patient experience |
| **90th Percentile Waiting Time** | Tail behavior—how bad it gets for the worst-affected patients | Patient satisfaction is disproportionately driven by extreme waits |
| **Maximum Waiting Time** | Worst-case scenario | Identifies potential system failures or exceptional cases |
| **Standard Deviation / IQR** | Variability of waiting times | High variability indicates unpredictable processes, harder for patients to tolerate |
| **Queue Frequency** | Number of cases that pass through this transition | A moderately long queue affecting 90% of patients is more impactful than a very long queue affecting 5% |
| **Excessive Wait Rate** | % of cases exceeding a defined threshold (e.g., >15 min, >30 min) | Directly actionable—maps to patient dissatisfaction triggers |
| **Time-of-Day Distribution** | How waiting times vary across hours of the day | Identifies peak congestion periods |
| **Day-of-Week Distribution** | Variation across weekdays | Identifies scheduling-driven patterns |

Additionally, at the **case level**, I would compute:

- **Total Visit Duration** = Complete(last activity)  Start(first activity)
- **Total Waiting Time per Visit** = Sum of all inter-activity waiting times
- **Waiting Time as % of Total Visit Duration** — this is a powerful metric; in many clinics, 40–60% of visit duration is pure waiting, which is a compelling statistic for stakeholders
- **Number of Queue Points per Visit** — process variants with more handovers inherently accumulate more waiting

### 1.3 Identifying the Most Critical Queues

Not all queues are equally important. I would use a **multi-criteria prioritization framework** to rank queues:

**Criterion 1: Aggregate Patient-Hours Wasted (Impact Score)**
```
Impact Score = Average Waiting Time × Queue Frequency (number of cases)
```
This captures the total burden. A 10-minute average wait experienced by 200 patients/day is worse than a 30-minute wait experienced by 10 patients/day in terms of total system impact.

**Criterion 2: Severity (Tail Risk)**
Queues where the 90th percentile waiting time exceeds 30 minutes (or an appropriate clinical/operational threshold) are flagged as high-severity, regardless of the average.

**Criterion 3: Patient-Type Sensitivity**
Queues that disproportionately affect specific patient groups warrant special attention:
- **Urgent patients** waiting long is a clinical risk
- **New patients** having poor first experiences has long-term retention consequences
- **Elderly or mobility-impaired patients** experience greater discomfort from prolonged waiting

**Criterion 4: Position in the Process**
Research in service operations shows that waits at the *beginning* and *end* of an encounter are perceived as longer and more frustrating (the "peak-end" rule). Queues before registration and before check-out should be weighted higher for patient experience impact.

**Criterion 5: Downstream Cascade Effect**
A queue that causes delays propagating through subsequent activities (a "primary bottleneck") is more critical than an isolated delay. This can be tested via correlation analysis: if waiting time at Queue A is positively correlated with total visit duration beyond what its own magnitude would suggest, it is likely causing cascade effects.

I would create a **prioritization matrix** combining these criteria, producing a ranked list of queues. Based on the typical clinic scenario, I hypothesize the most critical queues would likely be:

1. **Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation** (typically the longest wait, driven by physician availability as the scarcest resource)
2. **Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Tests** (coordination between clinical and diagnostic departments)
3. **Arrival  Registration** (first impression, peak-hour congestion)
4. **Diagnostic Tests  Specialist Review/Follow-up Consultation** (waiting for results and second physician availability)

## 2. Root Cause Analysis

### 2.1 Potential Root Causes

Identifying *where* queues exist is necessary but insufficient—we must understand *why* they form. Below is a systematic exploration of root causes mapped to the process mining techniques that can reveal them.

#### A. Resource Bottlenecks

**Hypothesis:** Physician capacity is the primary constraint; there are insufficient doctor-hours relative to demand during peak periods.

**Process Mining Approach — Resource Analysis:**
- Calculate **resource utilization rates** per resource: `Utilization = Total_Active_Service_Time / Total_Available_Time`. If Dr. Smith is actively consulting for 85% of available hours, there is virtually no slack to absorb variability, guaranteeing queue formation.
- Construct **resource calendars** from the event log: when are physicians, nurses, and technicians actually active? Are there systematic gaps (e.g., doctors consistently unavailable 12:00–13:30 for lunch, but patients are still being queued)?
- Identify **resource pooling patterns**: are patients forced to wait for a *specific* doctor rather than *any available* doctor in the specialty? This can be checked by examining whether cases are assigned to resources before the wait begins (dedicated assignment) versus at the moment of service (pooled assignment).

**Expected Finding:** Likely 2–3 physicians in high-demand specialties (e.g., Cardiology, Orthopedics) with utilization >80%, creating the dominant bottleneck.

#### B. Activity Duration Variability

**Hypothesis:** High variance in consultation or test durations makes scheduling unreliable and creates cascading delays.

**Process Mining Approach — Service Time Distribution Analysis:**
- For each activity, compute `Service_Time = Complete_Timestamp  Start_Timestamp` and analyze its distribution.
- Calculate the **coefficient of variation (CV)** = /. Queueing theory tells us that queues are driven not just by utilization but by variability. Even at moderate utilization, high CV in service times produces long queues.
- Examine whether service time distributions are bimodal or have long tails—for doctor consultations, a "New" patient may take 25 minutes while "Follow-up" takes 10 minutes. If the scheduling system doesn't differentiate, it systematically underestimates time needed for new patients and overestimates for follow-ups.

**Expected Finding:** Doctor consultation times likely have CV > 0.5, with new-patient visits showing 2–3x the duration of follow-ups. Diagnostic tests (e.g., X-ray vs. complex imaging) may also show high variance.

#### C. Appointment Scheduling Policies

**Hypothesis:** Patients are clustered at certain times due to scheduling patterns (e.g., all appointments on the hour or half-hour), creating artificial demand surges.

**Process Mining Approach — Arrival Pattern Analysis:**
- Plot patient arrival rates (case start times) by time-of-day, overlaid with service capacity. Classic "morning surge" patterns (heavy 08:00–10:00 bookings, lighter afternoon) are common.
- Analyze whether appointment slot durations match actual service time requirements. If all appointments are 15-minute slots but the actual mean consultation is 18 minutes, the clinic falls behind cumulatively throughout the day.
- Check for **batching effects**: are diagnostic departments running tests in batches (e.g., collecting multiple blood samples before processing), introducing artificial delays?

**Expected Finding:** Likely over-scheduling in morning hours with appointment intervals shorter than mean service time + setup/transition time, leading to progressive queue buildup through the day.

#### D. Activity Dependencies and Handover Delays

**Hypothesis:** Poor coordination between departments (e.g., clinical  diagnostic  clinical) introduces waiting time unrelated to resource availability.

**Process Mining Approach — Handover and Transition Analysis:**
- For each transition, decompose waiting time into (a) time until the next resource becomes *available* and (b) time from resource availability until service *starts*. The latter is the handover/coordination delay.
- Analyze whether patients are "lost in transition"—waiting because the downstream department doesn't know they're coming. This can be detected if there's a consistent minimum waiting time (floor) between activities that seems unrelated to resource busyness.
- Map **information flow**: does the nurse electronically signal readiness to the physician, or does the physician check a physical chart rack? The event log won't directly show this, but systematic minimum delays at specific transitions are suggestive.

**Expected Finding:** Transitions between departments (clinical  diagnostic  clinical) likely show higher coordination overhead than within-department transitions.

#### E. Patient Type and Urgency-Based Differences

**Hypothesis:** Different patient types experience systematically different waiting times, and the current system doesn't adequately differentiate.

**Process Mining Approach — Variant Analysis and Comparative Mining:**
- Segment the event log by patient type (New vs. Follow-up), urgency (Normal vs. Urgent), specialty, and analyze queue metrics separately for each segment.
- Perform **variant analysis**: identify the distinct process paths (variants) patients follow. New patients likely follow longer paths with more activities. Follow-up patients may skip registration steps but still face the same physician bottleneck.
- Compare waiting time distributions between segments using statistical tests (Mann-Whitney U, Kruskal-Wallis). If urgent patients wait as long as normal patients for doctor consultation, the triage/prioritization system is failing.
- Use **case-level regression analysis**: model total waiting time as a function of patient type, urgency, specialty, time-of-day, day-of-week to quantify the contribution of each factor.

**Expected Finding:** New patients likely experience 30–50% longer total visit durations. Urgent patients may not be consistently prioritized. Patients requiring diagnostic tests experience disproportionately longer waits due to the additional handover.

#### F. Bottleneck Analysis Using Process Mining

Beyond the above, I would apply:

- **Bottleneck analysis using token replay or performance overlay**: Replay the event log on the discovered process model and overlay waiting times. Activities where tokens (patients) accumulate are visual bottlenecks. The activity with the highest mean sojourn time (waiting + service) relative to its frequency is the primary bottleneck.
- **Temporal bottleneck analysis**: Bottlenecks may shift throughout the day. Morning bottleneck might be registration; mid-morning through early afternoon, it shifts to physician consultation; late afternoon, it might be diagnostic tests as technicians leave before all patients are processed.
- **Dotted chart / event timeline visualization**: Plot all events for all cases on a timeline. Visually, long horizontal gaps between dots for a case represent waiting. Clusters of gaps at specific activities or times reveal systemic patterns.

## 3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

Based on the anticipated findings above, I propose five distinct strategies (exceeding the minimum of three to provide a comprehensive improvement portfolio):

### Strategy 1: Dynamic Physician Schedule Realignment Based on Demand Patterns

**Target Queue:** Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation (hypothesized as the #1 bottleneck)

**Root Cause Addressed:** Mismatch between physician availability and patient demand by time-of-day and day-of-week.

**Data-Driven Basis:**
- From the event log, construct an hourly demand curve: number of patients completing Nurse Assessment (and thus ready for physician) per hour, per specialty.
- Overlay this with physician availability (derived from the resource attribute in physician START events).
- Identify specific hours where demand consistently exceeds capacity by >20%, and hours where physicians are underutilized.

**Specific Intervention:**
- Stagger physician start times: instead of all physicians starting at 08:00, schedule some to start at 09:00 or 09:30 to cover the mid-morning surge that builds from the initial registration/triage pipeline.
- For high-demand specialties (e.g., Cardiology identified from frequency data), add a half-day session on the most congested day-of-week rather than adding a full-time equivalent.
- Shift administrative duties (chart review, paperwork) to non-peak patient hours based on the identified low-demand windows.

**Expected Impact:**
- Reduction in average wait time for Doctor Consultation by 25–40% during peak hours.
- More even distribution of physician utilization across the day (target: reduce peak utilization from ~90% to ~75%, which queueing theory predicts will reduce average wait by ~50% given typical variability).
- Minimal cost increase—same physician-hours redistributed rather than added.

---

### Strategy 2: Differentiated Appointment Slot Duration by Patient Type and Visit Complexity

**Target Queue:** Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Tests (cascade from over-running consultations) and Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation (schedule drift)

**Root Cause Addressed:** Uniform appointment slot durations that don't match the bimodal distribution of actual consultation times.

**Data-Driven Basis:**
- From the event log, compute the service time distribution for Doctor Consultation segmented by:
  - Patient Type (New: estimated mean ~22 min, Follow-up: estimated mean ~12 min)
  - Specialty (some specialties inherently take longer)
  - Whether diagnostic tests were ordered (post-hoc indicator of complexity)
- Quantify the "schedule drift": calculate the cumulative deviation of actual consultation durations from the current standard slot length throughout the day. If the standard slot is 15 minutes but the actual mean is 17 minutes, by the 10th patient the schedule is 20 minutes behind—a mathematical certainty.

**Specific Intervention:**
- Implement **differentiated slot lengths**:
  - New patient, complex specialty: 25-minute slot
  - New patient, straightforward: 20-minute slot
  - Follow-up, stable: 10-minute slot
  - Follow-up, complex: 15-minute slot
- Classify patients at scheduling time using historical data (a simple prediction model based on patient type, reason for visit, and specialty can achieve reasonable accuracy).
- Insert **buffer slots** (5–10 minutes) after every 4th appointment to absorb accumulated variability—this is a well-established technique in outpatient scheduling (the "Bailey-Welch" rule and its variants).

**Expected Impact:**
- Reduction in "schedule drift" effect, preventing the progressive accumulation of delays throughout the day. Currently, late-morning patients likely wait 15–25 minutes longer than early-morning patients for the same physician—this strategy could reduce that differential by 60–70%.
- Reduction in average Doctor Consultation wait by 15–25%.
- Slight reduction in total patients per session (estimated 5–8% decrease in slots per half-day) but offset by reduced no-shows from patients frustrated by long waits, and the ability to fill shorter follow-up slots more efficiently.

---

### Strategy 3: Parallel Diagnostic Test Ordering and Pre-Positioning

**Target Queue:** Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Tests and Diagnostic Tests  Follow-up Review

**Root Cause Addressed:** Sequential dependency and coordination delays between clinical and diagnostic departments.

**Data-Driven Basis:**
- From the event log, identify all cases where Doctor Consultation is followed by a diagnostic test (e.g., ECG, Blood Test, X-Ray). Calculate the frequency and the waiting time distribution for these transitions.
- For V1001 in the example: Doctor Consultation COMPLETE at 10:10:30, ECG Test START at 10:22:15  11 minutes 45 seconds of waiting. Multiply across hundreds of patients daily.
- Analyze the diagnostic department's utilization: is the wait driven by the test resource being busy (true capacity constraint) or by the *coordination overhead* (notification delay, patient walking to the department, paperwork)?
- Use the event log to estimate the "coordination component": filter for cases where the diagnostic resource was *idle* at the time the patient completed their consultation (no other case was being served on that resource). If the patient still waited 8+ minutes, the coordination overhead is dominant.

**Specific Intervention:**
- **Pre-order standing diagnostics**: For predictable scenarios (e.g., a Cardiology follow-up for a known cardiac patient almost always requires an ECG), pre-order the test at triage/nurse assessment so it can be performed *before* the doctor consultation. The doctor then reviews results during the consultation rather than ordering post-hoc.
  - Identification: From the event log, identify (Specialty, Patient Type, Diagnosis Code if available) combinations where >80% of cases include a specific diagnostic test. These are candidates for standing pre-orders.
- **Real-time coordination system**: Implement digital notification from the clinical to diagnostic department the moment a test is ordered (during the consultation, not after), so diagnostic resources can prepare and schedule the patient in their queue proactively. This reduces the coordination delay.
- **Parallelization where clinically appropriate**: For patients needing multiple tests (e.g., blood work AND X-ray), order them simultaneously rather than sequentially. The event log can reveal how often sequential vs. parallel execution currently occurs via case-level process model discovery.

**Expected Impact:**
- Pre-ordering applicable diagnostics could eliminate the Doctor  Diagnostic wait entirely for 30–40% of patients who follow predictable test patterns. For these patients, this saves an estimated 10–20 minutes of waiting.
- Real-time coordination reduces the coordination component of waiting by an estimated 50% (e.g., from 8 minutes to 4 minutes).
- Parallelizing multiple tests reduces total diagnostic phase duration from (Test1_wait + Test1_service + Test2_wait + Test2_service) to max(Test1_wait + Test1_service, Test2_wait + Test2_service), estimated 25–35% reduction in multi-test scenarios.

### Strategy 4: Redesigned Registration and Intake Process with Pre-Visit Digital Check-In

**Target Queue:** Arrival  Registration and Registration  Nurse Assessment

**Root Cause Addressed:** Registration bottleneck during morning surge; high service time variability for registration (new patients require significantly more registration time).

**Data-Driven Basis:**
- From the event log, registration service times and the queue before registration (estimated from arrival timestamps if available, or from the clustering of registration START times).
- New patient registration: estimated 6–10 minutes. Follow-up: 2–4 minutes. But they share the same queue/resources.
- Morning surge analysis: if 60% of daily registrations occur in the first 2 hours, clerks are overwhelmed even though they're underutilized later.

**Specific Intervention:**
- **Pre-visit digital registration**: Allow returning patients to verify/update demographics and insurance online before arrival. Upon arrival, they proceed to a kiosk or fast-track line for identity verification only (< 1 minute), then immediately enter the nurse assessment queue.
- **Dedicated new-patient registration window**: Since new patients inherently take longer, separate them to prevent them from blocking the flow of follow-up patients. This is the "shortest job first" principle applied through lane separation.
- **Staggered appointment start times**: Based on the arrival pattern analysis, spread first-morning appointments across 08:00, 08:10, 08:20, etc., rather than clustering at 08:00 and 08:30. The event log data on arrival patterns directly informs the optimal stagger interval.

**Expected Impact:**
- Pre-visit check-in could reduce in-clinic registration time by 60–70% for follow-up patients (who typically represent 60–70% of visits), effectively converting a 4-minute service time into a 1-minute verification.
- Lane separation reduces follow-up patient waiting for registration by an estimated 50% (they're no longer stuck behind a 10-minute new-patient registration).
- Staggered arrivals reduce peak registration queue length by an estimated 40%.
- Combined effect: reduction of pre-nurse-assessment waiting time by 30–50%, with significant patient experience improvement due to the "peak-end" effect of reducing early-visit waits.

### Strategy 5: Intelligent Patient Routing and Real-Time Queue Management Dashboard

**Target Queue:** All queues, with particular focus on Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation and inter-departmental transitions.

**Root Cause Addressed:** Lack of real-time visibility into queue states, leading to suboptimal routing decisions and inability to dynamically rebalance load.

**Data-Driven Basis:**
- The event log reveals that waiting times are highly variable within the same transition on the same day—some patients wait 5 minutes for a doctor while others wait 35 minutes. This variance is partially driven by the *order* in which patients are routed to resources.
- Resource analysis may reveal that within a specialty, one physician consistently has longer queues (perhaps more complex patients, or simply more popular for appointment booking) while a colleague has capacity.
- Variant analysis reveals that some process paths are inherently more efficient than others (e.g., patients who see Nurse 2 before Dr. Jones have shorter total waits than patients who see Nurse 1 before Dr. Jones, because Nurse 2's assessment room is physically closer, or because Nurse 2 is faster).

**Specific Intervention:**
- Implement a **real-time queue management dashboard** visible to floor coordinators and triage nurses, displaying:
  - Current queue length and estimated wait time at each station (physician, diagnostic room, etc.)
  - Color-coded alerts when a queue exceeds threshold (yellow: >15 min, red: >25 min)
  - Patient-specific estimated remaining visit time
- Use this dashboard to enable **dynamic routing**: when two cardiologists are both accepting patients, route the next patient to the one with the shorter current queue. This is a "join-the-shortest-queue" policy that operations research proves optimal under many conditions.
- Implement **proactive patient communication**: when a patient will wait >15 minutes, automatically send an SMS/notification with the estimated wait time and allow them to wait in a more comfortable location (café, outdoor area) with a recall notification.

**Expected Impact:**
- Dynamic routing is expected to reduce the variance in waiting times by 20–30% through better load balancing, which also reduces the 90th percentile wait times significantly even if the average drops modestly (estimated 10–15% average reduction through rebalancing alone).
- Proactive communication doesn't reduce wait times directly but dramatically improves perceived wait experience—research in service operations shows that known, expected waits are rated as 30–40% less frustrating than uncertain waits of the same duration.
- The dashboard enables rapid identification of emerging bottlenecks (e.g., a physician running late), allowing proactive intervention before queues cascade.

## 4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints

### 4.1 Trade-off Analysis by Strategy

| Strategy | Potential Negative Effects | Mitigation |
|----------|--------------------------|------------|
| **Dynamic Physician Scheduling** | Physicians may resist changed schedules; later start times may conflict with hospital duties or personal preferences; administrative complexity increases | Involve physicians in co-design; offer schedule preferences within constraints; pilot with willing physicians first |
| **Differentiated Slot Durations** | Slight reduction in total daily appointment capacity; scheduling complexity increases; misclassification of visit complexity at booking time could worsen problems | Use conservative slot estimates plus buffer slots; develop a simple complexity prediction model; monitor misclassification rates and iteratively improve |
| **Parallel/Pre-ordered Diagnostics** | Over-ordering risk: pre-ordering tests that end up unnecessary wastes diagnostic resources and increases costs; clinical staff may resist protocol-based ordering | Apply only where historical ordering rate >80%; implement as "standing order" that the physician can cancel rather than mandatory execution; monitor test cancellation rates |
| **Digital Pre-Registration** | Elderly or digitally illiterate patients may be disadvantaged; technology investment required; cybersecurity/privacy concerns with online health data entry | Maintain traditional registration option; provide tablet-based check-in with staff assistance in clinic; ensure HIPAA/GDPR-compliant platform |
| **Real-time Dashboard & Routing** | Technology infrastructure cost; information overload for staff; dynamic routing may disrupt continuity-of-care (patient sees different physician than previously) | Invest incrementally (start with a simple display, add features); design dashboard with information hierarchy; enforce continuity rules (returning patients flagged for their regular physician) as routing constraints |

### 4.2 Balancing Conflicting Objectives

**Wait Times vs. Costs:**
- The proposed strategies are designed to be *primarily* efficiency-improving rather than cost-increasing. Strategies 1, 2, and 5 reallocate existing resources rather than adding new ones. Strategy 3 may slightly increase diagnostic test volumes if pre-ordering leads to some unnecessary tests (~5–10% based on literature), but this is offset by improved throughput enabling the clinic to see more patients per day.
- Quantify the economic value of reduced wait times: patient no-show rates correlate with past wait experience. If reducing average wait times by 30% reduces no-shows by even 2 percentage points (typical in literature), the revenue recovered from filled slots likely exceeds the implementation costs.

**Wait Times vs. Quality of Care:**
- This is a non-negotiable constraint: no strategy should compress actual clinical service time. Importantly, the analysis distinguishes between *waiting time* (pure waste) and *service time* (value-added). All strategies target waiting time reduction specifically.
- Strategy 2 (differentiated slot durations) actually *improves* quality by giving complex patients more time rather than forcing all patients into a one-size-fits-all slot.
- However, I would implement a monitoring safeguard: track service time distributions before and after intervention. If average consultation duration decreases post-intervention, it may indicate physicians are rushing to keep up with the optimized schedule—a red flag requiring immediate attention.

**Bottleneck Shifting:**
- This is a critical concern. Reducing wait at Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation could shift the bottleneck to Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Tests if diagnostic capacity is not simultaneously addressed. The system must be analyzed holistically.
- I would use **simulation modeling** (informed by the process mining analysis) to test strategies in combination before implementation. Feed the discovered process model, service time distributions, and arrival patterns into a discrete-event simulation. Test each strategy individually and in combination to identify bottleneck shifts before they occur in the real system.
- Implement strategies in a phased manner, monitoring for bottleneck migration between phases.

**Staff Workload and Burnout:**
- Increasing throughput without increasing staff can intensify workload. I would track resource utilization rates post-implementation. The target is to keep all resource utilization below 85%—above this threshold, queueing theory predicts explosive wait growth and staff experience unsustainable pressure.
- Strategy 5 (dashboard) should include a staff workload indicator alongside the patient queue indicator, so floor coordinators can balance both objectives.

## 5. Measuring Success

### 5.1 Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

I propose a three-tier KPI framework:

**Tier 1: Primary Patient Experience KPIs**

| KPI | Definition | Target | Measurement Frequency |
|-----|-----------|--------|----------------------|
| Average Total Waiting Time per Visit | Sum of all inter-activity waits, averaged across all visits | 30% reduction from baseline within 6 months | Weekly |
| Average Total Visit Duration | Complete(last activity)  Start(first activity) | 25% reduction from baseline | Weekly |
| 90th Percentile Total Waiting Time | Upper tail of total waiting distribution | 40% reduction (extreme waits are disproportionately harmful) | Weekly |
| Patient Satisfaction Score (Wait-Related) | Survey question specifically about waiting experience | 20% improvement | Monthly |
| Waiting Time as % of Visit Duration | Total wait ÷ Total visit duration | Reduce from estimated ~50% to 30% | Weekly |

**Tier 2: Process-Specific KPIs**

| KPI | Definition | Target |
|-----|-----------|--------|
| Average Queue Time per Transition | Wait time at each specific handover point | Targeted reduction per strategy |
| Excessive Wait Rate (>20 min) | % of cases with any single wait >20 minutes | 10% of cases (from estimated current ~30%) |
| Schedule Drift Index | Deviation of actual appointment times from scheduled times, measured at end of morning/afternoon | 10 minutes cumulative drift by end of session |
| First-Activity Wait | Time from arrival/appointment time to Registration START | 5 minutes for 90% of patients |

**Tier 3: Operational Efficiency KPIs**

| KPI | Definition | Target |
|-----|-----------|--------|
| Resource Utilization Rate | Active service time / available time per resource | 65–80% (avoid both underuse and overload) |
| Daily Patient Throughput | Number of completed visits per day | Maintain or improve (5% increase) |
| No-Show Rate | % of scheduled appointments not kept | 2 percentage point reduction |
| Overtime Hours | Staff hours worked beyond scheduled shift | No increase (preferably decrease) |
| Diagnostic Pre-Order Accuracy | % of pre-ordered tests actually needed | 90% (to control waste from Strategy 3) |

### 5.2 Ongoing Monitoring Framework

**Continuous Process Mining:**
- Implement ongoing event log collection as a permanent operational practice, not a one-time study.
- Deploy a **process mining dashboard** (using tools like Celonis, Disco/Fluxicon, PM4Py, or ProM) that refreshes daily or weekly, automatically computing the above KPIs.
- Configure **automated alerts** when KPIs breach thresholds:
  - Yellow alert: KPI worsens by >10% from post-improvement baseline for 1 week
  - Red alert: KPI worsens by >20% or returns to pre-improvement levels

**Comparative Analysis:**
- Maintain the pre-intervention event log as a permanent baseline. All post-intervention metrics should be compared against this baseline using statistical control charts (Shewhart charts, CUSUM charts) to distinguish genuine improvement/deterioration from normal variability.
- Segment monitoring by the same dimensions used in root cause analysis: patient type, urgency, specialty, time-of-day, day-of-week. An overall improvement that masks deterioration for a specific patient subgroup must be caught.

**Periodic Deep-Dive Analysis:**
- Monthly: Review KPI trends, identify any emerging bottlenecks or bottleneck shifts.
- Quarterly: Full process mining re-analysis—rediscover the process model, compare with the pre-intervention model. Has the process actually changed as intended? Are there unexpected new variants (workarounds)?
- Semi-annually: Comprehensive reassessment using the full methodology described in Sections 1–3. The patient population, referral patterns, staffing, and clinic services evolve over time; the optimization must evolve with them.

**Feedback Loop:**
- Integrate process mining KPIs into regular operational meetings with clinic leadership.
- Create a closed-loop improvement cycle: **Measure  Analyze  Intervene  Measure**. Each intervention is treated as a hypothesis to be validated by post-implementation data.
- Empower frontline staff to flag process issues through the dashboard; their qualitative insights combined with quantitative process mining data yield the most robust improvement opportunities.

## Summary

This comprehensive approach transforms the clinic's event log from passive data into an active management tool. By systematically identifying queues (Section 1), understanding their root causes through advanced process mining techniques (Section 2), implementing targeted and data-justified optimization strategies (Section 3), carefully managing trade-offs (Section 4), and establishing rigorous ongoing measurement (Section 5), the clinic can expect to achieve a **25–40% reduction in average patient waiting time** and a **20–30% reduction in total visit duration** within 6–9 months of implementation, while maintaining quality of care and controlling operational costs. The key philosophical shift is from intuition-based management to evidence-based, continuously monitored process optimization—a paradigm that becomes self-sustaining once the monitoring infrastructure is in place.
