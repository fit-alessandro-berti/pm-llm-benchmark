## 1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics  

### **Process Reconstruction & Flow Analysis**  
Using **process mining** on the MES event logs, we reconstruct the **end-to-end flow** of each job through the shop floor.  
- **Techniques**:  
  - **Process Discovery** (e.g., Heuristic Miner, Inductive Miner) to model the *as-is* process flow, including rework loops, parallel tasks, and waiting states.  
  - **Conformance Checking** to compare planned routings (from ERP/MES) versus actual task sequences, revealing deviations.  
- **Metrics & Quantification**:  
  - **Flow/Lead Time & Makespan**:  
    - Calculate **total lead time** (from job release to completion) and **makespan** per job using timestamp data.  
    - Use **distribution analysis** (e.g., histograms, percentile breakdowns) to identify variability and outliers.  
  - **Waiting Times (Queue Times)**:  
    - Measure **time-in-queue** at each machine/work center by subtracting `Queue Entry` from `Task Start` timestamps.  
    - Aggregate by machine, job type, or priority to identify chronic congestion points.  
  - **Resource Utilization**:  
    - **Machine Utilization**:  
      \[
      \text{Utilization} = \frac{\text{Productive Time} + \text{Setup Time}}{\text{Total Available Time}}
      \]
      Distinguish **value-added (productive) time** from **setup time** and **idle time**.  
    - **Operator Utilization**: Track operator assignments and active work periods.  
  - **Sequence-Dependent Setup Times**:  
    - Extract setup events (`Setup Start`  `Setup End`) with **previous job attributes** (e.g., material, geometry, tooling).  
    - Build a **setup time matrix** between job families/machine types, calculating average and variance per transition.  
    - Use **regression analysis** or **random forest** to predict setup time based on job sequence features.  
  - **Tardiness & Schedule Adherence**:  
    - Tardiness = `max(0, Actual Completion Time  Due Date)`  
    - Schedule adherence via **conformance checking** against the production schedule.  
  - **Disruption Impact Analysis**:  
    - Identify breakdown events (`Breakdown Start`), priority changes, and urgent job injections.  
    - Measure **ripple effects**: delays propagated to downstream tasks and increased queue times during/after disruptions.

## 2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies  

### **Identified Inefficiencies & Process Mining Evidence**  
- **Bottleneck Identification**:  
  - **Resource-based bottleneck mining**: Machines with longest queue times, highest utilization, and largest downstream starvation events.  
  - **Throughput analysis**: Machines with lowest throughput relative to incoming load.  
- **Poor Task Prioritization**:  
  - **Variant analysis** comparing on-time vs. late jobs reveals:  
    - Late jobs often waited longer at critical machines despite having earlier due dates.  
    - Urgent jobs (`Priority Change` events) sometimes delayed non-urgent jobs on bottleneck machines, worsening overall tardiness.  
- **Suboptimal Sequencing Leading to Excessive Setups**:  
  - **Sequence pattern mining**: On bottleneck machines, many job transitions incur long setups, while grouping similar jobs would reduce total setup time.  
  - Quantify **setup time waste** by comparing actual sequence with optimal sequence (e.g., traveling salesman problem solution).  
- **Starvation/Blocked Resources**:  
  - **Temporal resource logs** show machines idling while upstream queues are full (blocked) or downstream queues empty (starved).  
- **Bullwhip Effect in WIP**:  
  - **Time-series analysis** of WIP levels correlates with scheduling variability (e.g., large batch releases, uneven machine utilization).

## 3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness  

### **Differentiating Scheduling Logic vs. Capacity vs. Variability Issues**  
- **Process Mining to Isolate Root Causes**:  
  1. **Resource Contention Analysis**:  
     - If high queue times occur despite low utilization elsewhere  **scheduling coordination failure**.  
     - If high queue times coincide with near-100% utilization  **capacity limitation**.  
  2. **Wait Time Decomposition**:  
     - Break down waiting time into:  
       - **Scheduling delay** (job available but not scheduled)  
       - **Resource contention** (job waiting for machine)  
       - **Material/tooling delay** (downstream missing dependencies).  
  3. **Variability Analysis**:  
     - Compare **actual vs. planned durations** (task and setup) to quantify estimation errors.  
     - High variability in durations suggests need for **robust scheduling** or better estimation.  
  4. **Sequence Impact on Setups**:  
     - If setup times are predictable based on sequence, the issue is **scheduling logic** (poor sequencing).  
     - If setup times are random/highly variable, the issue may be **process instability** (needing standardization).  

- **Key Root Causes Identified**:  
  - **Static dispatching rules** ignore real-time machine load and job dependencies.  
  - **Lack of predictive visibility**: Dispatchers don’t see upcoming bottlenecks or setup impacts.  
  - **Poor setup time estimation**: Planned vs. actual setup times diverge significantly.  
  - **No adaptive response** to disruptions; urgent jobs inserted without re-optimization.

## 4. Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies  

### **Strategy 1: Dynamic Multi-Factor Dispatching Rules (Enhanced Rules)**  
- **Core Logic**:  
  - Replace static rules with a **weighted scoring system** at each machine:  
    \[
    \text{Priority Score} = w_1 \cdot \text{Due Date Urgency} + w_2 \cdot \text{Remaining Processing Time} + w_3 \cdot \text{Downstream Load} + w_4 \cdot \text{Predicted Setup Time}
    \]
  - Weights (`w_i`) learned via historical process mining (e.g., regression on tardiness).  
- **Data & Insights from Process Mining**:  
  - **Due date urgency**: Based on tardiness patterns from historical analysis.  
  - **Downstream load**: Real-time queue lengths at next machines from MES.  
  - **Setup time prediction**: From sequence-dependent setup model built during analysis.  
- **Addresses Pathologies**:  
  - Reduces tardiness by prioritizing jobs near due date with short downstream queues.  
  - Minimizes setup time by considering sequence, reducing total setup hours.  
- **Expected Impact**: 20-30% reduction in tardiness, 10-15% reduction in WIP.  

### **Strategy 2: Predictive Scheduling with Simulation-Based Optimization**  
- **Core Logic**:  
  - Use **historical duration distributions** (not averages) to generate stochastic schedules.  
  - Incorporate **predictive maintenance signals** (if available) or breakdown patterns to schedule around expected downtime.  
  - **Re-scheduling triggers**: When deviation from predicted completion exceeds threshold, trigger rescheduling.  
- **Data & Insights from Process Mining**:  
  - Duration distributions per task, operator, job complexity from historical logs.  
  - Breakdown patterns (e.g., time between failures, repair time distributions) from resource logs.  
- **Addresses Pathologies**:  
  - Improves lead time predictability.  
  - Reduces starvation by proactively managing bottleneck load.  
- **Expected Impact**: Lead time variability reduction by 40%, on-time delivery improvement by 25%.  

### **Strategy 3: Setup-Time-Aware Batching & Sequencing**  
- **Core Logic**:  
  - At bottleneck machines, use **cluster analysis** to group jobs with similar setup requirements.  
  - Apply **sequence-dependent scheduling** (like traveling salesman) to minimize total setup time within batches.  
  - Implement **setup time windows**: Reserve time blocks for similar job families.  
- **Data & Insights from Process Mining**:  
  - Setup time matrix from historical transitions.  
  - Job clustering based on attributes affecting setup (e.g., material type, tooling).  
- **Addresses Pathologies**:  
  - Directly reduces setup time waste on critical resources.  
  - Improves bottleneck throughput.  
- **Expected Impact**: 15-25% reduction in total setup time, 10% increase in bottleneck throughput.
