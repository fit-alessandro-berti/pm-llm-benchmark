### 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

**Waiting Time Calculation:**
Waiting time (queue time) between consecutive activities is defined as the idle time when a patient is ready to proceed to the next activity but must wait due to resource unavailability. Formally:

**Waiting Time = (Start Time of Next Activity) – (Completion Time of Previous Activity)**

For each patient visit (Case ID), we calculate waiting times at activity boundaries. For example, if "Nurse Assessment" completes at 09:25:10 and "Doctor Consultation" starts at 09:45:55, the waiting time is 20 minutes 45 seconds.

**Key Queue Metrics:**
- **Average Waiting Time:** Mean queue time across all queues.
- **Median Waiting Time:** Central tendency, robust against outliers.
- **Maximum Waiting Time:** Worst-case delay experienced.
- **90th Percentile Waiting Time:** Indicates waiting time experienced by 90% of patients (e.g., P90), crucial for patient satisfaction.
- **Queue Frequency:** How often a specific queue occurs across all visits.
- **Excessive Wait Instances:** Count of cases where waiting time exceeds a clinically acceptable threshold (e.g., 30 minutes).
- **Queue Length Distribution:** Number of patients waiting at a resource at a given time (requires resource state data if available).

**Identifying Critical Queues:**
The most critical queues are identified using a combination of metrics:
1. **Impact on Patient Experience:** Queues with the highest **P90 or maximum waiting times** directly correlate with patient dissatisfaction.
2. **Frequency and Bottleneck Potential:** Queues with high **queue frequency** and high **utilization** (calculated as total activity time / total available time of the resource) indicate persistent bottlenecks.
3. **Cumulative Effect:** Queues that, when delayed, cause downstream delays (propagation effect). This can be identified by analyzing **case flow paths** and **activity sequences**.
4. **Patient Type Sensitivity:** Queues that disproportionately affect **urgent or new patients** (e.g., long registration waits for urgent cases) are prioritized for equity reasons.

The **critical queue score** can be a weighted combination:  
`Score = w1*(Avg Wait) + w2*(P90) + w3*(Frequency) + w4*(Urgent Patient Impact)`  
(Weights can be calibrated via stakeholder input.)

### 2. Root Cause Analysis

**Potential Root Causes:**
- **Resource Bottlenecks:** A key resource (e.g., a single specialist doctor, a specific diagnostic room, or a phlebotomist) may be overloaded.
- **Activity Dependencies:** Sequential handovers (e.g., nurse assessment must complete before doctor consultation) create natural queues if upstream activities are slow.
- **High Variability in Service Times:** Long-running cases (e.g., complex doctor consultations) delay subsequent patients, causing "ripple effects."
- **Appointment Scheduling Gaps:** Overbooking, long slot intervals, or misaligned appointment types (e.g., new vs. follow-up) can lead to under/over-utilization.
- **Patient Arrival Patterns:** Clustering of arrivals (e.g., early morning rush) can overwhelm initial resources (registration).
- **Patient Type Mix:** Urgent cases may be deprioritized in a first-come-first-served system, increasing their perceived wait.

**Process Mining Techniques for Root Cause Analysis:**
- **Resource Utilization Analysis:** Calculate per-resource utilization and idle time to identify under/over-utilized resources (e.g., "Dr. Smith is utilized 95% of the time, while Nurse 1 is 70%").
- **Bottleneck Discovery:** Use **process trees** or **directly-follows graphs** to identify the most frequent and time-consuming paths. Combine with **performance analysis** (e.g., using Alpha Miner or IMDF) to find activities with the longest **waiting time before** or **after** them.
- **Variant Analysis:** Group cases by patient type, urgency, or activity sequence. Compare queues across variants (e.g., "New patients have 30% longer registration waits than follow-ups").
- **Conformance Checking:** Check for deviations from the ideal flow (e.g., patients skipping activities or repeating steps) that may cause rework and queues.
- **Correlation Analysis:** Correlate queue times with resource calendars, shift changes, or external factors (e.g., clinic hours) to identify systemic issues.

### 3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

**Strategy 1: Dynamic Resource Allocation and Cross-Training Based on Peak Demand**
- **Target Queues:** Registration, Nurse Assessment, Diagnostic Testing.
- **Root Cause:** Fixed resource schedules do not match variable arrival patterns, causing understaffing during peaks.
- **Data Support:** Analyze hourly/daily arrival patterns from event logs. Identify peak hours (e.g., 9–11 AM) and correlate with resource utilization logs. Cross-training data can identify staff who can perform multiple roles (e.g., clerk-nurse).
- **Action:** Implement dynamic staffing: redeploy staff from low-demand periods to high-demand periods. Train clerical staff to perform basic nurse tasks during peak flows.
- **Expected Impact:** Reduce average registration wait by 25–40% and nurse assessment wait by 20–30% during peak hours.

**Strategy 2: Staggered/Smart Appointment Scheduling with Priority Queuing**
- **Target Queues:** Doctor Consultation, Diagnostic Testing.
- **Root Cause:** First-come-first-served scheduling leads to clustering of complex cases and idle resources.
- **Data Support:** Analyze doctor consultation durations by patient type and urgency. Diagnostic resource usage patterns show underutilization early/late in the day.
- **Action:** Implement time-slot staggering based on historical duration percentiles. Introduce a priority queue for urgent patients that "jumps" ahead without disrupting flow. Use predictive scheduling: allocate longer slots for complex cases and shorter slots for follow-ups.
- **Expected Impact:** Reduce doctor wait times by 30–50% and diagnostic wait times by 25–40% through better resource matching.

**Strategy 3: Parallel Processing and Pre-Visit Preparation**
- **Target Queues:** Registration, Nurse Assessment, Pre-Test Paperwork.
- **Root Cause:** Sequential processing of administrative and clinical tasks creates avoidable delays.
- **Data Support:** Event logs show that registration often completes just before nurse assessment starts. Patient demographic data and visit type can be used to pre-fill forms.
- **Action:** Allow patients to complete registration and basic health questionnaires online before arrival. Enable "parallel processing" where nurse assessment begins with preliminary data while registration finalizes. Use kiosks or tablets in waiting areas for check-in.
- **Expected Impact:** Reduce registration-to-nurse wait by 40–60% and overall visit duration by 15–25%.

### 4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints

**Trade-offs:**
- **Dynamic Resource Allocation:** May increase staff scheduling complexity and could lead to **fragmented patient care** if cross-trained staff are less specialized.
- **Priority Queuing:** May cause perceived unfairness among non-urgent patients, potentially increasing dissatisfaction for that group.
- **Parallel Processing & Online Pre-registration:** Requires investment in technology (kiosks, portals) and may **exclude digitally excluded populations** (e.g., elderly, low-income).
- **Staggered Appointments:** Could reduce clinic throughput if not balanced with demand, potentially increasing idle time for providers.

**Balancing Conflicting Objectives:**
- Use **multi-objective optimization**: model wait time, cost, and quality as constraints. For example, set a maximum acceptable wait (e.g., 30 mins) and minimize staffing costs.
- Implement **phased rollouts** with A/B testing to measure impact on satisfaction and clinical outcomes.
- Ensure **equity safeguards**: e.g., guarantee a maximum wait for urgent cases, and provide assisted digital services for vulnerable groups.
- Monitor **staff workload** to prevent burnout from dynamic scheduling.

### 5. Measuring Success

**Key Performance Indicators (KPIs):**
- **Patient-Level Metrics:** Average/median/90th percentile wait time between activities; total visit duration; proportion of visits exceeding target wait thresholds.
- **Resource-Level Metrics:** Resource utilization rate (target: 70–85% to balance efficiency and flexibility); idle time percentage.
- **System-Level Metrics:** Throughput (patients per hour); no-show rate; patient satisfaction scores (linked to wait times).
- **Quality Metrics:** Clinical outcomes (e.g., readmission rates) should not degrade.

**Ongoing Monitoring:**
- **Automated KPI Dashboards:** Feed from event logs to track KPIs in real-time.
- **Alert Systems:** Trigger alerts when wait times exceed thresholds or utilization drops below/above targets.
- **Continuous Variant Analysis:** Regularly compare process performance across patient types, time periods, and clinics to detect regressions.
- **Feedback Loop:** Correlate process data with patient feedback surveys to validate that reduced waits improve experience.
- **Process Mining Refresh:** Re-run discovery and conformance checks monthly to ensure the process adheres to the optimized model and to detect new bottlenecks.

By systematically applying queue mining and process optimization, the clinic can transform its patient flow from a reactive, wait-heavy system to a proactive, efficient, and patient-centered operation.
