**Anomalies Identified in the Event Log**

I analyzed the event log against standard Procure-to-Pay (P2P) best practices. A typical compliant P2P flow includes these logical and control-oriented steps (in approximate order):

- Create Purchase Requisition (PR)
- Approve PR (by authorized person, before commitment)
- Issue Purchase Order (PO) to the vendor
- Receive Goods (after PO)
- Receive Vendor Invoice
- Match/Verify Invoice against PO and Goods Receipt (3-way match)
- Release Payment (only after successful match)
- Close case

This sequence enforces internal controls, prevents fraud, ensures proper authorization, and aligns financial commitments with actual receipt of value.

Here are the clear anomalies:

### 1. Case 1002 – Multiple Severe Violations (Most Problematic Case)
- **PO issued before PR approval** (Issue PO immediately after Create PR; approval occurs days later).
- **Payment released before PR approval, before goods receipt, and before invoice matching**.
- **PR approval occurs after payment has already been made**.

**Why this is problematic**:
- Issuing a PO without approved funding/authorization bypasses budgetary controls and management oversight. This is a classic "maverick buying" or unauthorized commitment risk.
- Releasing payment before goods receipt, matching, or even PR approval violates the most fundamental segregation of duties and verification controls. It creates high risk of paying for non-delivered goods, incorrect amounts, or even fictitious vendors/invoices (potential fraud).
- Approving the requisition *after* the payment is meaningless — the control is rendered ineffective.

This sequence shows a near-total breakdown of process controls.

### 2. Case 1003 – Payment Before Receipt and Matching
- **Payment released before Vendor Invoice was received, before Goods were received, and before any matching** (Release Payment on Feb 8; invoice arrives Feb 9, goods on Feb 10, matching on Feb 11).

**Why this is problematic**:
- Paying a vendor before receiving an invoice or the actual goods is highly unusual in standard P2P. It resembles a prepayment without proper documentation or verification.
- There is no opportunity to perform a 3-way match before payment, which is the key control to ensure the company only pays for what was ordered *and* received at the agreed price.
- Increases risk of overpayment, payment for undelivered goods, or difficulty recovering funds if the vendor fails to deliver.

### 3. Case 1004 – Goods Received Before PO Issued
- **Goods received before the Purchase Order was issued** (Receive Goods occurs before Issue PO).

**Why this is problematic**:
- The PO is the official document that sets pricing, terms, and authorization with the vendor. Receiving goods without a PO first often indicates "maverick buying" — employees ordering outside the approved procurement process.
- It weakens the company's negotiating position and spend control. Vendors may charge higher prices or unfavorable terms when there is no pre-approved PO.
- Creates accounting and compliance issues (e.g., difficulty accruing liabilities properly or proving the purchase was authorized).

---

**Summary of Findings**
- **Case 1001** follows a reasonably standard sequence with no major anomalies.
- **Cases 1002, 1003, and 1004** each contain one or more significant deviations from expected P2P controls.
- The most serious issues are in **Case 1002**, which appears to show complete circumvention of approval and verification steps.

These anomalies could indicate process weaknesses, lack of system-enforced controls (e.g., workflow that allows skipping steps), or potential fraudulent activity. In a real investigation, one would also examine the specific users who performed each activity, any override reasons recorded, and whether these exceptions were approved by management.
