After analyzing the provided event log for the **Procure-to-Pay (P2P)** process, we observe that each case follows a sequence of activities typical of the P2P cycle: **Create Purchase Requisition  Approve Requisition  Issue Purchase Order  Receive Goods  Receive Vendor Invoice  Match Invoice to PO  Release Payment  Close Case**.

However, several **anomalies** in the sequence of activities across cases deviate from the **standard, logical, and control-robust P2P process flow**. These deviations may indicate **procedural errors, inefficiencies, or even potential fraud risks**.

---

###  **Anomaly 1: Case 1002 – Payment Released Before Requisition Approval**

#### **Sequence of Events:**
- 2024-02-01 08:30: Create Purchase Requisition
- 2024-02-01 08:40: **Issue Purchase Order**  (before approval)
- 2024-02-03 07:00: Vendor Invoice Received
- 2024-02-03 07:15: **Release Payment** 
- 2024-02-03 08:00: **Approve Purchase Requisition**  (after PO issued and payment released)

#### **Why It's Anomalous:**
- A **purchase order should not be issued before the requisition is approved**.
- In standard P2P controls, the **approval step is a mandatory gate** before proceeding to PO issuance.
- Here, the **PO was created and payment released *before* the requisition was approved**, meaning:
  - The purchase may have been unauthorized.
  - There is no formal approval trail for the purchase.
  - Risk of **fraud or unauthorized spending**.

#### **Potential Problems:**
- Violates segregation of duties and internal control principles.
- Could lead to **non-compliant or unnecessary purchases**.
- Payment made without proper authorization — significant financial risk.

>  **Best Practice:** Approval must precede PO creation. No PO should be issued without prior approval.

###  **Anomaly 2: Case 1003 – Payment Released Before Goods Received and Invoice Received**

#### **Sequence of Events:**
- 2024-02-02 09:00: Create Requisition
- 2024-02-02 09:10: Approve Requisition
- 2024-02-02 10:00: Issue Purchase Order
- 2024-02-08 11:00: **Release Payment** 
- 2024-02-09 12:00: **Vendor Invoice Received**  (after payment)
- 2024-02-10 13:00: **Receive Goods**  (after payment)
- 2024-02-11 14:00: Match Invoice to PO  (after payment)

#### **Why It's Anomalous:**
- The **payment was released *before* receiving the goods and before the vendor invoice was even received**.
- This violates the **three-way match** principle: **PO + Goods Receipt + Invoice** must all be present before payment is released.
- Releasing payment without receiving goods or invoice means:
  - No verification of what was actually delivered.
  - No proof of invoice validity (e.g., price, quantity).
  - High risk of **overpayment, double payment, or payment for non-existent goods**.

#### **Potential Problems:**
- **Financial loss** due to premature payment.
- **No audit trail** for goods receipt or invoice validation.
- Could indicate **lack of internal controls** or deliberate bypassing of procedures (e.g., collusion with vendor).

>  **Best Practice:** Payment should only be released after **all three** components (PO, goods receipt, invoice) are in place and verified.

###  **Anomaly 3: Case 1004 – Goods Received Before Purchase Order Issued**

#### **Sequence of Events:**
- 2024-02-05 10:00: Create Purchase Requisition
- 2024-02-05 10:15: Approve Requisition
- 2024-02-06 09:00: **Receive Goods** 
- 2024-02-06 09:15: **Issue Purchase Order**  (after goods received)
- 2024-02-07 08:00: Vendor Invoice Received
- 2024-02-07 08:30: Match Invoice to PO
- 2024-02-07 09:00: Release Payment
- 2024-02-08 11:00: Close Case

#### **Why It's Anomalous:**
- **Goods were received before the purchase order was issued**.
- This is a **critical breakdown** in process control because:
  - There is **no formal PO to reference** for the goods received.
  - No basis for verifying that the received goods match the agreed-upon terms.
  - Increases risk of **fraud, over-delivery, or unauthorized deliveries**.
  - Makes auditing and reconciliation extremely difficult.

#### **Potential Problems:**
- **Uncontrolled or unapproved deliveries**.
- No legal or contractual basis for payment.
- Could indicate **"ghost" purchases** (goods received without a PO, possibly to inflate costs or for kickbacks).
- Violates the principle that **goods should not be accepted without a pre-existing PO**.

>  **Best Practice:** Goods should only be received **after** a PO is issued and acknowledged by the vendor.

---

###  Recommendations for Improvement:

1. **Enforce Sequential Process Flow**: Implement system controls to prevent moving to the next step (e.g., PO issuance) without completing the prior step (e.g., approval).
2. **Three-Way Match Enforcement**: Automate the requirement that **PO, Goods Receipt, and Invoice** must all be present before payment release.
3. **Goods Receipt Validation**: Require PO reference before allowing goods receipt in the system.
4. **Audit Trail Monitoring**: Flag cases where activities occur out of sequence for review.
5. **Segregation of Duties**: Ensure that the same person does not create a requisition, approve it, issue a PO, and release payment.

---

###  Conclusion:

While all four cases eventually close, **Cases 1002, 1003, and 1004 contain serious procedural anomalies** that **violate fundamental P2P controls**. These issues expose the organization to **financial risk, fraud, inefficiency, and compliance failures**. Addressing these deviations through automated workflow enforcement and stronger governance is critical to maintaining a robust and trustworthy Procure-to-Pay process.
