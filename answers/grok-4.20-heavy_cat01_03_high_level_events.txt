**High-Level Manufacturing Process Steps**

**1. Material Preparation**  
- **Included Low-Level Events**: Retrieve raw metal sheet, Scan barcode, Place metal sheet on table, Align metal sheet, Preheat metal sheet.  
- **Rationale**: These events form the initial, temporally clustered phase focused on acquiring, verifying (via barcode), positioning, and thermally conditioning the raw input material. They primarily involve Operator A along with supporting automation (Robot Arm #2, Heating Unit #1). Grouping them reflects standard manufacturing logic where raw material handling and setup occur before any transformation.

**2. Welding Operation (Assembly/Joining)**  
- **Included Low-Level Events**: Pick up welding tool, Weld corner A, Weld corner B.  
- **Rationale**: These actions are performed consecutively by the same resource (Operator B). The tool pickup directly enables the spot-welding actions, representing the core transformation/joining phase. Grouping captures the logical "fabrication" stage where the part is permanently altered.

**3. Post-Weld Quality Inspection**  
- **Included Low-Level Events**: Measure weld integrity.  
- **Rationale**: This is a distinct, automated sensor-based check occurring immediately after welding and before any further processing. It serves as a specific quality gate for the welds (using Quality Sensor #1), separate from later checks due to its timing, purpose, and resource type. It prevents defective items from proceeding to coating.

**4. Protective Coating Application**  
- **Included Low-Level Events**: Apply protective coating, Dry coating.  
- **Rationale**: These are consecutive finishing steps involving surface treatment (Coating Unit #3 followed by Heating Unit #2 for curing). They form a coherent "finishing/treatment" phase that protects the welded part, grouped by shared purpose, sequential dependency, and specialized equipment.

**5. Final Visual Inspection**  
- **Included Low-Level Events**: Visual check.  
- **Rationale**: This is the terminal human-operated verification step (by Operator C) that confirms overall product acceptability. It is kept separate as an end-of-process quality gate, distinct in timing and method from the automated weld check.

**Overall Approach and Benefits**  
This abstraction reduces the ~12 low-level granular events per case to 5 meaningful, domain-relevant stages that follow a logical manufacturing workflow: *Prepare  Assemble  Inspect  Finish  Final Inspect*.  

Groupings were based on:
- Temporal proximity and sequence order
- Logical dependency and manufacturing phase (e.g., preparation before transformation)
- Shared resources or resource types where possible
- Distinct purposes (handling, joining, quality gates, surface treatment)

This higher-level view makes the process easier to analyze, monitor, or model (e.g., in process mining or dashboards) while preserving the underlying event details. The pattern holds consistently across the provided cases (A1 and B2), indicating it would scale to the full log.
