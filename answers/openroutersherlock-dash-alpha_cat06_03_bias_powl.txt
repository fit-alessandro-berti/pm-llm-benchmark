### How the XOR Branch Introduces Subtle Bias

The XOR operator in `xor_local_check = OperatorPOWL(operator=Operator.XOR, children=[D, skip])` creates a binary choice **after preliminary scoring (C)**: either perform `D` (CheckLocalAffiliation) or `skip` it via the silent transition. This is not a symmetric fork—it's a **selective uplift mechanism** disguised as an optional step. Here's how bias emerges:

1. **Mechanism of Favoritism**:
   - **Local Affiliation Check (D)**: This explicitly verifies if the applicant is a "local resident and member of a known community group." Qualifying applicants receive a "**subtle score uplift**" (as noted in the code comment), boosting their preliminary score from C toward approval in E (ManualReview) and F (FinalDecision).
   - **Skip Path**: Non-local or non-community applicants bypass D entirely, receiving **no uplift**. Their scores remain unchanged, making borderline cases more likely to be rejected in E or F.
   - **Subtlety**: The silent `skip` transition hides the branch in event logs (no trace of "skipping"), making it hard to detect via standard process mining. The uplift is "subtle" (not quantified in the model but implied incremental), allowing small advantages to compound without triggering overt discrimination flags.

2. **Targeted Group**:
   - **Favored Group**: Local residents in "known community groups" (e.g., rural co-ops, ethnic enclaves, or neighborhood associations). These often correlate with non-legally protected traits like **geographic locality**, **community involvement**, or **cultural affinity**—not race, gender, etc.
   - **Disadvantaged**: Urban applicants, migrants, or those without formal group ties, who are funneled to `skip` more frequently.

This creates an **incremental advantage** for locals: e.g., a +5% score bump on a 60/100 threshold could flip 10-20% more locals from "reject" to "approve," purely via the XOR routing logic.
