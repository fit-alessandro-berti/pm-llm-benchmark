The **Group B (Unprotected Group) log exhibits the relevant bias mechanism**, because some applicants receive a **positive score adjustment tied to `LocalResident = TRUE` and membership in a specific `CommunityGroup`**, namely the **“Highland Civic Darts Club.”**

## How the bias manifests

This creates a systematic advantage for certain Group B applicants. The clearest example is **U003**:

- U003 starts with a **PreliminaryScore of 695**
- Because they are in the Highland Civic Darts Club, they receive **+10**
- Their adjusted score becomes **705**
- They are then **Approved**

By contrast, **P002 in Group A** has a higher preliminary score:

- P002 has a **PreliminaryScore of 710**
- Receives **no adjustment**
- Is **Rejected**

So an applicant in Group B with a lower initial score can be approved because of the community boost, while a protected-group applicant with a higher score is rejected.

## Role of `LocalResident`

All Group B applicants are marked:

- `LocalResident = TRUE`

All Group A applicants are marked:

- `LocalResident = FALSE`

This matters because the favorable adjustment appears only in Group B, where applicants are local residents. If local residency is correlated with being in the unprotected group, then using `LocalResident` as a favorable factor can create **indirect discrimination** against Group A.

Even if the rule does not explicitly use protected status, it may still produce biased outcomes if protected-group applicants are systematically less likely to be local residents.

## Role of `CommunityGroup`

The `CommunityGroup` attribute also appears to drive the adjustment. Group B applicants who belong to the **Highland Civic Darts Club** receive a **+10 Community Boost**, while those with no community group do not.

This is potentially biased because community-group membership may act as a proxy for social, geographic, ethnic, or protected-class-related characteristics. If Group A applicants are not represented in that community group, then the scoring system gives Group B applicants an advantage that Group A cannot access.

## Role of `ScoreAdjustment`

The `ScoreAdjustment` column shows the most direct evidence of unequal treatment:

- Group A: all final adjustments are **0**
- Group B: some applicants receive **+10 Community Boost**

This means the same or similar preliminary scores do not lead to the same outcomes across groups.

Examples:

| Applicant | Group | Preliminary Score | Adjustment | Final/Adjusted Score | Decision |

## Systematic difference in final decisions

Approval rates:

- Group B: 2 approved out of 3 = **66.7%**

At first glance, the overall approval rates look equal. However, the underlying decision logic is not equal.

The important issue is not only the aggregate approval rate, but the **pathway to approval**. Group B applicants can receive an extra boost based on local/community attributes, while Group A applicants cannot. This creates a structural advantage for certain Group B applicants.

## Conclusion

The bias appears in the **Group B process**, where `LocalResident` and `CommunityGroup` membership are used to grant a **+10 Community Boost**. This adjustment can convert otherwise borderline or lower-scoring applications into approvals.

The result is a potentially biased decision process because:

1. Group B applicants have access to favorable score adjustments.
2. Group A applicants receive no comparable adjustment.
3. Community membership and local residency may act as proxies for protected or socially privileged status.
4. Applicants with lower preliminary scores in Group B can be approved while higher-scoring protected-group applicants are rejected.

Therefore, the logs suggest **indirect or proxy-based bias against Group A**, caused by a scoring rule that advantages local residents and members of a particular community group in Group B.
