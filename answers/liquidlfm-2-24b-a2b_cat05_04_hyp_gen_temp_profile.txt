**Anomalies in the Temporal Profile Model**

- **R  P (Receive to Approve):** Average time is ~25 hours with a very low standard deviation (600 seconds). This is unusually short and consistent, suggesting a rigid or possibly automated approval process that may not reflect realistic business timing.

- **P  N (Approve to Notify):** Average time is ~7 days with a high standard deviation (2 days). This indicates significant variability, possibly due to delays or inconsistent follow-up after approval.

- **A  C (Assign to Close):** Average time is ~2 hours, which is very short for a process that typically includes evaluation and approval steps. This may indicate premature closure without proper assessment.

- **E  N (Evaluate to Notify):** Average time is only 5 minutes, which is extremely short and may suggest skipping intermediate steps or overly rapid communication.

---

**Possible Reasons for Anomalies**

- **R  P:** Automated workflows or scheduled approvals may enforce fixed time intervals, ignoring actual claim complexity.
- **P  N:** Resource shortages or backlogs may cause unpredictable delays, or approvals may be processed in batches rather than individually.
- **A  C:** Claims may be closed prematurely due to pressure to reduce processing time or lack of required documentation.
- **E  N:** Notification systems may be triggered automatically upon evaluation completion without human oversight.

**Verification Queries Using SQL**

1. **Identify claims with R  P timing outside expected range:**
   ```sql
   SELECT claim_id, event_id, timestamp AS event_time
   FROM claim_events
   WHERE activity = 'P'
     AND EXISTS (
       SELECT 1
       FROM claim_events e2
       WHERE e2.claim_id = claim_events.claim_id
         AND e2.activity = 'R'
         AND EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (e2.timestamp - claim_events.timestamp)) BETWEEN (3600 - 600) AND (3600 + 600)
     )
   ORDER BY claim_id, event_time;
   ```

2. **Find claims with P  N delays exceeding 5 days:**
   ```sql
   SELECT e1.claim_id, e1.activity, e2.timestamp AS notify_time
   FROM claim_events e1
   JOIN claim_events e2 ON e1.claim_id = e2.claim_id
   WHERE e1.activity = 'P'
     AND e2.activity = 'N'
     AND EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (e2.timestamp - e1.timestamp)) > 432000
   ORDER BY e1.claim_id, e2.timestamp;
   ```

3. **Detect A  C closures without E or P steps:**
   ```sql
   SELECT claim_id, adjuster_id, resource, COUNT(DISTINCT activity) AS steps_taken
   FROM claim_events
   WHERE activity = 'C'
     AND claim_id IN (
       SELECT claim_id
       FROM claim_events
       WHERE activity IN ('E', 'P')
     )
     GROUP BY claim_id, adjuster_id, resource
   HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT activity) < 3
   ORDER BY claim_id;
   ```

4. **Find E  N transitions under 10 minutes:**
   ```sql
   SELECT e1.claim_id, e1.activity, e2.timestamp AS notify_time
   FROM claim_events e1
   JOIN claim_events e2 ON e1.claim_id = e2.claim_id
   WHERE e1.activity = 'E'
     AND e2.activity = 'N'
     AND EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (e2.timestamp - e1.timestamp)) < 600
   ORDER BY e1.claim_id, e2.timestamp;
   ```

5. **Correlate anomalies with claim type or region:**
   ```sql
   SELECT claim_type, region, COUNT(*) AS anomaly_count
   FROM claim_events
   WHERE activity IN ('R', 'P', 'N')
     AND EXISTS (
       SELECT 1
       FROM claim_events e2
       WHERE e2.claim_id = claim_events.claim_id
         AND EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (e2.timestamp - claim_events.timestamp)) > 28800
     )
   GROUP BY claim_type, region
   ORDER BY claim_type, region;
   ```

These queries will help pinpoint specific claims and patterns that deviate from expected process timing, enabling targeted investigation and corrective action.
