This DECLARE model describes a **constraint-based view of a loan approval process**. Instead of prescribing one rigid workflow, it specifies **rules that must always hold**. Any sequence of activities is acceptable as long as it satisfies these constraints. In real life, this style reflects environments where flexibility is needed but **compliance and risk controls are critical**, such as banking.

Below is a practical interpretation of how the activities and constraints shape the process.

Activity roles in the real process

Receive_Application (A)  
This is the intake point for the process. A customer submits a loan application through a branch, website, or agent. The model enforces this as the **initial step** and requires it to occur in every case. Operationally this ensures every loan process begins with documented customer intent and captured data.

Preliminary_Credit_Check (B)  
A quick automated or manual evaluation of the applicant’s credit score, debt obligations, and risk profile. The **exactly_one constraint** means the bank must perform it once and only once per application. This prevents both skipping risk screening and repeatedly checking credit, which could violate fairness or damage a customer’s credit record.

Gather_Additional_Documents (C)  
If the application passes initial screening, the bank collects supporting documentation such as income proof, employment verification, or tax returns. This stage supports regulatory requirements such as **Know Your Customer (KYC)** and **antimoneylaundering (AML)** verification.

Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package (D)  
Here the bank determines loan terms—interest rate, repayment schedule, collateral requirements, and other conditions. This stage transforms the evaluated data into a formal offer.

Quality_Assurance_Review (E)  
A compliance or internal audit check verifying that documentation, risk calculations, and legal requirements are correct. This protects the bank from issuing incorrect offers and ensures the case meets regulatory standards.

Authorize_Contract_Terms (F)  
Senior authorization or automated approval confirming the loan conditions are acceptable. In real institutions this may require approval thresholds depending on loan size or risk category.

Transfer_Funds (G)  
Actual disbursement of the loan to the customer’s account once the contract is finalized.

Notify_Customer (H)  
Communication to the applicant about approval, rejection, or next steps. This could occur after disbursement or at other points depending on the outcome.

Proceed_Without_Compliance (Z)  
A forbidden action representing a compliance breach. The absence constraint ensures it **never occurs**.

How the constraints structure the process

Existence constraints  
The model guarantees that **Receive_Application** and **Gather_Additional_Documents** occur in every case. This ensures that every loan application has formal intake and proper documentation before progressing.

Init constraint  
The requirement that **Receive_Application must start the process** ensures the bank never begins evaluation or disbursement without an official request.

Exactly-one constraint  
The **Preliminary_Credit_Check must happen exactly once**. This enforces a consistent risk screening procedure while preventing redundant checks or manipulations of credit results.

Response and succession constraints  
These constraints ensure logical progression between activities.

Examples:
- If **Receive_Application occurs**, a **Preliminary_Credit_Check must eventually follow**. This guarantees every application undergoes risk assessment.
- **Preliminary_Credit_Check followed by Gather_Additional_Documents** (succession) reflects the real-world need for supporting evidence after initial evaluation.
- **Quality_Assurance_Review precedes Authorize_Contract_Terms**, ensuring approval decisions are based on verified information.

These rules collectively ensure **analysis, documentation, review, and authorization occur in a controlled order**.

Coexistence and responded existence  
These constraints enforce relationships between steps.

Example:
- **Gather_Additional_Documents coexists with Authorize_Contract_Terms**, meaning if one occurs the other must also appear in the case. In practice, contract authorization requires verified documentation.
- **Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package requires a Quality_Assurance_Review**. This ensures that any prepared offer is formally checked before approval.

Chain constraints  
Chain response, precedence, and succession enforce **immediate step relationships**.

Example:
- **Quality_Assurance_Review directly followed by Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package** ensures the offer preparation closely follows verification.
- **Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package directly followed by Transfer_Funds** reflects a streamlined postapproval execution path.
- **Authorize_Contract_Terms immediately preceded by Preliminary_Credit_Check** ensures approval relies on recent credit evaluation.

These tight links help maintain **process integrity and traceability**.

Alternative constraints  
Altresponse, altprecedence, and altsuccession limit how often certain relationships occur.

Example:
- **Transfer_Funds altresponse Notify_Customer** means each transfer leads to at most one notification, preventing duplicate customer communications.
- **Notify_Customer altprecedence Transfer_Funds** ensures the customer is not repeatedly notified before funds are transferred.

These prevent redundant actions or communication loops.

Negative constraints (prohibitions)

Noncoexistence  
**Transfer_Funds and Receive_Application cannot coexist** in the same case. Conceptually this prevents impossible scenarios such as disbursing funds without a legitimate process instance.

Nonsuccession  
**Notify_Customer cannot be followed by Preliminary_Credit_Check**. Once the customer has been informed, the bank cannot return to early evaluation steps.

Nonchainsuccession  
**Authorize_Contract_Terms cannot be immediately followed by Notify_Customer**, likely ensuring that another step (such as fund transfer or record finalization) occurs first.

These negative constraints enforce **risk controls and logical consistency**.

How the rules support compliance and transparency

This model supports several regulatory and business objectives.

Risk management  
Mandatory credit checks, documentation gathering, and QA reviews ensure lending decisions are based on verified financial data and risk assessment.

Regulatory compliance  
Financial institutions must demonstrate that each loan decision followed documented procedures. The model guarantees key steps like **KYC verification, compliance review, and formal authorization** occur.

Auditability  
The constraints produce an event log where auditors can confirm that:
- approvals occurred after review,
- funds were disbursed only after authorization,
- mandatory documentation was collected.

Operational consistency  
Even though DECLARE models allow flexible orderings, the constraints maintain consistent **control points**. This lets banks adapt to different cases (e.g., missing documents, manual review) without violating policy.

Customer transparency  
Rules governing notification and sequencing ensure customers receive updates at appropriate times and that communications correspond to real process states.

Likely business and regulatory motivations

A process with this level of constraint likely reflects pressures such as:

- Banking regulations requiring **credit risk assessment before loan approval**
- **Antimoneylaundering and identity verification** rules requiring documentation
- **Internal audit requirements** ensuring multiple verification layers
- **Consumer protection laws** ensuring fair and transparent lending
- **Operational risk controls** preventing unauthorized fund disbursement

Overall interpretation

The DECLARE model represents a **controlled yet flexible loan lifecycle**. Instead of prescribing one strict workflow, it defines **a set of compliance rules** that all valid executions must satisfy. These rules ensure that every application is properly evaluated, documented, reviewed, approved, and communicated while preventing actions that could create financial, regulatory, or reputational risk.
