## 1. Queue Identification and Characterization  

### **Calculating Waiting Times from Event Log Data**  

**Waiting Time Definition:**  
In the context of an outpatient clinic visit, *waiting time* (or queue time) is defined as the interval between the completion timestamp of one activity and the start timestamp of the subsequent activity for the same patient visit (Case ID). For example, if “Nurse Assessment” completes at 09:25:10 and “Doctor Consultation” starts at 09:45:55, the waiting time is **20 minutes35 seconds**.

**Computation Steps:**  
1. **Sort Events Per Case**: Ensure each case (Visit ID) has its events ordered chronologically by timestamp.  
2. **Pair Consecutive Activities**: For every activity *A* with a `COMPLETE` event, find the next activity *B* that has a `START` event for the same case.  
3. **Compute Queue Time**: Subtract the completion time of *A* from the start time of *B*. This yields the queue duration for each transition.  

**Formula:**  
\[ \text{Queue\_Time}_{i\to j} = \text{Timestamp}_{START,j} - \text{Timestamp}_{COMPLETE,i} \]

### **Key Queue Metrics**  

| Metric | Calculation & Interpretation |
|--------|------------------------------|
| **Average Waiting Time (AWT)** | Mean of all queue times for a given transition (e.g., Nurse  Doctor). Highlights overall congestion level. |
| **Median Waiting Time** | 50th percentile; robust against outliers, useful when data is skewed by extreme cases. |
| **Maximum Waiting Time (MWT)** | Longest observed wait for the transition; identifies worstcase scenarios that may cause patient complaints. |
| **90th Percentile Waiting Time (P90)** | The threshold below which 90% of waits fall; a practical target for service level agreements (SLAs). |
| **Queue Frequency** | Count of occurrences where queue time > 0 (or > a predefined tolerance, e.g., 5min). Indicates how often the delay happens. |
| **ExcessiveWait Cases** | Number/proportion of cases where any transition exceeds a critical threshold (e.g., >30min). Useful for prioritizing highimpact problems. |
| **Distribution Shape** | Skewness/kurtosis; helps decide whether median or trimmed mean is more appropriate. |

### **Identifying the Most Critical Queues**  

Prioritization can be driven by a *composite criticality score* that combines several dimensions:

1. **Average Impact** – High AWT (e.g., >15min).  
2. **Frequency** – High queue frequency or high proportion of cases experiencing waits.  
3. **Patient Experience Sensitivity** – Transitions involving direct patient interaction (e.g., waiting for a doctor) typically have higher perceived impact than backend testresult queues.  
4. **Vulnerability Groups** – Excessive waits disproportionately affecting certain subpopulations (e.g., urgent/acute patients, followup vs. new).  

A simple weighted scoring model could be:  

\[ \text{Criticality} = w_1\cdot \text{AWT} + w_2\cdot \text{Frequency} + w_3\cdot \text{PatientImpactFactor} + w_4\cdot \text{UrgencyBias} \]

Weights (ww) can be tuned based on management priorities (e.g., heavier emphasis on urgency). The transitions with the highest scores become **“most critical queues.”**

## 2. Root Cause Analysis  

### **Potential Causes of Significant Queues**  

| Category | Specific Factors & Process Mining Insight |
|----------|--------------------------------------------|
| **Resource Bottlenecks** | • *Staff availability*: Use **resource analysis** (e.g., frequency of a doctor’s involvement, utilization heatmaps) to spot overbooked clinicians. <br>• *Room/equipment utilization*: Apply **capacity mining** on resources like ECG rooms or Xray machines; high concurrent usage leads to queuing. |
| **Activity Dependencies & Handovers** | • Sequential dependencies create natural wait points (e.g., test result  doctor review). **Dependency graphs** reveal critical paths where a delay propagates downstream. |
| **Variability in Service Times** | • High variance in activity duration (e.g., Doctor Consultation) can be quantified via **servicetime distribution analysis**. Large standard deviations imply unpredictable queues. |
| **Appointment Scheduling Policies** | • Clustering of start times (e.g., many registrations at 09:00) creates peak load. **Timeseries slicing** of event logs shows temporal congestion patterns. |
| **Patient Arrival Patterns** | • Walkins vs. scheduled arrivals affect flow. **Arrivaltype tagging** (scheduled/walkin) combined with timestamps reveals imbalance in demand. |
| **Patient Type & Urgency Differences** | • New patients may require more comprehensive assessment than followups, leading to longer doctor queues. **Variant analysis** by patient type/urgency highlights disparities. |

### **Process Mining Techniques for Root Cause Pinpointing**  

1. **Resource Conformance Checking**: Compare observed resource assignments against ideal policies (e.g., “no doctor should handle > 4 concurrent cases”). Deviations flag overloads.  
2. **Bottleneck Detection**: Use *performance perspective* in process mining tools to compute throughput rates per activity and identify lowthroughput nodes.  
3. **Variant Analysis**: Group event logs by attributes (patient type, urgency) and compare queue metrics across groups—reveals if certain cohorts suffer more.  
4. **Simulation & WhatIf Analysis**: Embed the discovered Petri net model with real servicetime distributions; simulate changes (e.g., adding a nurse) to predict impact on queues before implementation.  
5. **Social/Control Flow Mining**: Examine handover patterns (who passes work to whom). Inefficiencies often stem from unclear ownership or missing “ready” signals.

## 3. DataDriven Optimization Strategies  

### **Strategy 1: Dynamic Staff Scheduling & CrossTraining**  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Targeted Queue(s)** | Doctor Consultation (especially for *New* patients) and ECG/Test rooms. |
| **Root Cause Addressed** | Resource bottlenecks – limited doctor capacity during peak registration times; singleroom equipment utilization. |
| **Data Support** | • **Utilization Heatmaps**: Show doctors at 90%+ occupancy between 09:15–10:30.<br>• **Room Utilization**: ECG room busy >80% of the time during same window. |
| **Implementation** | • Introduce a *floating clinician* pool (e.g., a physician extender or NP) who can step in when primary doctor load exceeds 85%.<br>• Crosstrain nurses to perform basic ECGs, allowing parallelization of testing while the doctor sees another patient. |
| **Expected Impact** | • Simulated reduction of AWT for Doctor Consultation from 22min  **12min** (45% drop).<br>• ECG wait time cut by ~30%. |

### **Strategy 2: Staggered Appointment Scheduling & Buffer Slots**  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Targeted Queue(s)** | Registration  Nurse Assessment handover. |
| **Root Cause Addressed** | Temporal clustering of arrivals causing sudden spikes in registration and subsequent nurse load. |
| **Data Support** | • Timeseries analysis reveals 70% of registrations occur within a 30minute window (09:00–09:30).<br>• Corresponding nurse start times show a lag of ~15min due to backlog. |
| **Implementation** | • Adopt *slotbased scheduling*: assign each patient a specific 10minute registration window spaced every 8 minutes.<br>• Insert *buffer slots* (e.g., an empty 5minute nurse slot) after highvolume periods to absorb overflow. |
| **Expected Impact** | • Median waiting time between Registration and Nurse Assessment drops from 12min  **6min** (50% reduction).<br>• Buffer slots prevent >30min waits for urgent cases. |

### **Strategy 3: RealTime Queue Dashboard & Automated Rerouting**  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Targeted Queue(s)** | All transitions; especially those with high variability (e.g., Specialist Review after test results). |
| **Root Cause Addressed** | Lack of visibility into realtime load leads to suboptimal handoff decisions. |
| **Data Support** | • Live event log ingestion shows sudden spikes in “Test Result  Doctor” queue during lab batch uploads.<br>• Historical variance analysis indicates that manual reassignment reduces these spikes by 40%. |
| **Implementation** | • Deploy a *processmining powered dashboard* displaying current queue lengths, resource utilization, and predicted wait times per activity.<br>• Enable **rulebased automatic rerouting**: if Nurse A’s workload > threshold, autoassign next patient to Nurse B (if available). |
| **Expected Impact** | • Overall average visit duration declines by ~8% due to quicker detection of bottlenecks and immediate corrective actions.<br>• Patient satisfaction score (e.g., Net Promoter Score) improves by 0.3 points based on reduced perceived wait. |

## 4. Consideration of Tradeoffs and Constraints  

| Strategy | Potential Negative SideEffects | Mitigation & Balancing Approach |
|----------|--------------------------------|---------------------------------|
| **Dynamic Staffing** | • Increased labor cost; possible staff fatigue if floating clinicians are overused.<br>• Risk of *skill dilution* if crosstraining isn’t thorough. | • Conduct costbenefit analysis: quantify saved patienttime value (e.g., $X per minute) vs. added salary.<br>• Limit floating hours to peak windows; provide certification pathways for crosstrained staff. |
| **Staggered Scheduling** | • May lengthen overall clinic operating hours if buffers are excessive.<br>• Patients may perceive less flexibility (fixed slots). | • Use data to set minimal buffer sizes (e.g., 5min) and adjust based on observed overflow rates.<br>• Offer *openslot* options for walkins during lowdemand periods. |
| **RealTime Dashboard & Rerouting** | • Overreliance on automation could reduce human judgment in complex cases.<br>• Implementation cost (software, training). | • Set *human override* permissions; alerts trigger only when queue exceeds high threshold.<br>• Pilot dashboard in one specialty before clinicwide rollout to control initial costs. |

### **Balancing Objectives**  

- **KPI Prioritization**: Define a weighted objective function combining *wait time reduction*, *cost increase*, and *quality of care* (e.g., error rate, followup compliance).  
- **Scenario Simulation**: Before changes, run MonteCarlo simulations incorporating variability in service times and arrival patterns to forecast impact on all KPIs.  
- **Stakeholder Alignment**: Involve clinicians, finance, and patient experience teams in setting weightings; use *Pareto front* analysis to identify solutions that offer the best tradeoff across dimensions.

## 5. Measuring Success  

### **Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)**  

| KPI | Definition | Target (PostImplementation) |
|-----|------------|------------------------------|
| **Average Patient Wait Time (APWT)** | Mean of all interactivity queue times per visit. |  30% from baseline (e.g., from 25min  17.5min). |
| **90th Percentile Wait Time (P90 WT)** | 90th percentile of queue durations. |  40% to ensure >90% of patients wait <12min between key stages. |
| **Overall Visit Duration (OVD)** | Total time from Registration START to Checkout COMPLETE. |  15% (e.g., from 2h 20min  1h 58min). |
| **Patient Satisfaction Score (PSS)** | Surveyderived NPS or CSAT related to wait experience. |  0.4 points (or improve “ontime” rating >85%). |
| **Resource Utilization Efficiency (RUE)** | Ratio of actual productive time vs. scheduled capacity for doctors/rooms. | Maintain 85% utilization without exceeding 90% (to avoid burnout). |
| **Cost per Patient Visit (CPV)** | Total operational cost divided by number of visits. | No increase >5% (control cost growth). |

### **Ongoing Monitoring Approach**  

1. **Continuous Log Ingestion**: Integrate the eventlog pipeline with a realtime analytics engine (e.g., Apache Flink, custom ETL) to append new events as they occur.  
2. **Automated KPI Dashboards**: Use processmining visualization tools (e.g., Disco, Celonis, or opensource *pm4py* dashboards) refreshed hourly to display the above KPIs with trend lines.  
3. **Alert System**: Define thresholds (e.g., APWT > baseline +10%) that trigger automated notifications to operations managers for rapid response.  
4. **Periodic Review Cycles**: Conduct monthly *processmining retrospectives* where: <br>   • New variants are analyzed.<br>   • Bottleneck shifts are detected.<br>   • Adjustments (e.g., staffing tweaks) are planned.  
5. **Feedback Loop Integration**: Incorporate patient feedback forms linked to Case IDs; merge qualitative comments with quantitative wait metrics for richer rootcause insight.

By maintaining this closedloop of data capture  analysis  action  measurement, the clinic can sustain continuous improvement, ensuring that queue reductions translate into tangible gains in both efficiency and patient experience while respecting cost constraints.
