# Anomaly Analysis: Procure-to-Pay Process Event Log

## Overview of Standard P2P Process Flow

Before analyzing the anomalies, it is essential to establish the standard Procure-to-Pay (P2P) process flow, which serves as the benchmark for identifying deviations. The typical sequence follows a logical progression that ensures proper controls, authorization, and verification at each stage:

1. **Create Purchase Requisition**: The process begins when an employee identifies a need and creates a formal request to purchase goods or services.
2. **Approve Purchase Requisition**: The requisition undergoes review and approval from authorized personnel to ensure the purchase is justified, budgeted, and necessary.
3. **Issue Purchase Order**: Once approved, a formal purchase order is issued to the vendor, creating a legally binding agreement.
4. **Receive Goods**: The ordered goods are delivered and inspected to verify they match the PO specifications and quality standards.
5. **Vendor Invoice Received**: The vendor submits an invoice requesting payment for the delivered goods.
6. **Match Invoice to PO**: The three-way match is performed, comparing the purchase order, receiving document, and vendor invoice to ensure accuracy and prevent overpayment or fraud.
7. **Release Payment**: After successful verification, payment is authorized and processed to the vendor.
8. **Close Case**: The process instance is formally closed after all financial and administrative obligations are fulfilled.

## Anomaly Identification and Analysis

### Case 1001: Normal Process Flow (No Anomalies)

Case 1001 demonstrates a flawless execution of the standard P2P process. Every activity occurs in the correct chronological order with appropriate time intervals between steps. The sequence from requisition creation through approval, PO issuance, goods receipt, invoice matching, and payment release follows established best practices. This case serves as the reference model for what constitutes proper process execution.

### Case 1002: Severe Sequence Anomaly – Approval After Payment

Case 1002 contains a critical anomaly that fundamentally violates the procurement control framework. The approval of the purchase requisition occurs at **2024-02-03 08:00**, which is **after** the purchase order was issued (2024-02-01 08:40) and **after** payment was already released (2024-02-03 07:15).

This sequence is not merely unusual—it represents a complete breakdown of the procurement control structure. Purchase requisition approval is designed to occur before any financial commitment is made. By issuing a purchase order and releasing payment before obtaining approval, the organization has bypassed essential authorization controls. This creates significant risks including unauthorized purchases, budget overruns, potential fraud, and violation of internal compliance requirements. The approval happening after payment makes it a retrospective rubber-stamp exercise rather than a genuine control mechanism.

### Case 1003: Multiple Control Failures – Payment Before Invoice and Goods Receipt

Case 1003 presents a different pattern of anomalies involving improper timing of payment relative to verification activities. Payment is released on **2024-02-08 11:00**, but the vendor invoice is not received until **2024-02-09 12:00**, and goods are not received until **2024-02-10 13:00**.

This sequence reverses the logical order of verification. Organizations typically require three-way matching (PO, invoice, and receiving document) before releasing payment to ensure they are paying only for goods that were ordered, delivered, and invoiced correctly. In this case, payment was processed before the invoice was even received, meaning there was no opportunity to verify the invoice amount against the PO or receiving document. Furthermore, payment occurred before goods receipt, meaning the organization paid for goods that had not yet been verified as delivered. This creates substantial risks of overpayment, duplicate payments, payment for undelivered goods, and inability to recover funds if goods never arrive or do not match specifications.

### Case 1004: Critical Sequence Violation – Goods Received Before Purchase Order

Case 1004 contains the most severe anomaly in the dataset. Goods are received on **2024-02-06 09:00**, but the purchase order is not issued until **2024-02-06 09:15**—a full 15 minutes after goods receipt.

This sequence makes no logical business sense and represents a fundamental process breakdown. Purchase orders should always precede goods receipt because they establish the binding agreement with the vendor, define specifications and quantities, and enable proper receiving verification. Receiving goods before a purchase order exists creates numerous problems: there is no valid agreement with the vendor, no predetermined pricing or terms, no way to verify the goods against ordered specifications, and no proper receiving document to support subsequent invoice matching and payment. This anomaly could indicate serious operational errors, fraudulent activity, or systematic process failures where goods are being received without proper procurement authorization.

## Root Causes and Implications

These anomalies suggest several potential underlying issues within the organization's P2P process:

The sequence violations in Cases 1002, 1003, and 1004 may indicate inadequate process controls, lack of system enforceability (where activities can occur out of order), insufficient training of personnel, or deliberate circumvention of established procedures. The timing of activities—particularly the rapid execution in Case 1002 where payment is released just 15 minutes after receiving the invoice—suggests possible bypassing of review procedures.

From a compliance perspective, these anomalies expose the organization to audit findings, regulatory penalties, and financial losses. The three-way match principle, which is fundamental to procurement integrity, is completely ignored in these cases. From a fraud prevention standpoint, out-of-sequence activities can indicate attempts to create false paper trails or conceal unauthorized transactions.

## Recommendations

The organization should implement immediate corrective actions including system controls to prevent out-of-sequence activities, mandatory three-way matching before payment release, segregation of duties enforcement, and comprehensive audit procedures to detect and prevent recurrence of these anomalies.
