**1. Queue Identification and Characterization**

Using the event log's start and complete timestamps, waiting time (queue time) for a patient is calculated as the difference between the **COMPLETE** timestamp of one activity and the **START** timestamp of the subsequent activity in the same case (Visit ID). This represents non-value-adding idle time the patient experiences between process steps. Activities must first be ordered chronologically within each case. Care is taken to handle parallel activities (e.g., if multiple diagnostics are ordered simultaneously) by considering the patient's perspective—waiting time ends when the patient begins the next step in their path. Total visit duration can be decomposed into service times (COMPLETE minus START for each activity) and waiting times.

Key metrics to characterize queues include:
- Average, median, maximum, and 90th percentile waiting times for each transition (e.g., Registration COMPLETE  Nurse Assessment START; Nurse Assessment COMPLETE  Doctor Consultation START).
- Queue frequency: percentage of cases experiencing a wait > threshold (e.g., 15 or 30 minutes).
- Total waiting time contribution: sum of waiting times across all cases for that transition, or its percentage of overall visit duration.
- Segmentation: metrics broken down by patient type (New vs. Follow-up), urgency (Urgent vs. Normal), time of day, day of week, and specific resources.

Critical queues are identified using a multi-criteria prioritization: (1) highest total impact = average wait time × frequency (capturing both severity and scale), (2) contribution to overall visit duration (Pareto-style analysis showing which queues explain 80% of total waiting), and (3) disproportionate impact on vulnerable groups (e.g., Urgent patients or New patients with longer, more complex paths). Bottleneck visualization on a discovered process map (with performance overlays on arcs) makes this immediately actionable. Queues with high variance or 90th percentile waits are also flagged because they drive dissatisfaction even if averages appear moderate.

**2. Root Cause Analysis**

Beyond location, root causes are uncovered by linking queue metrics to contextual attributes in the event log.

Potential causes include:
- **Resource bottlenecks**: High utilization of specific staff, rooms, or equipment (calculated as busy time divided by scheduled availability).
- **Variability**: High standard deviation in service times for preceding activities, causing unpredictable queues.
- **Arrival and scheduling patterns**: Clustered patient arrivals at certain hours or poor slotting by patient type.
- **Activity dependencies and handovers**: Delays when responsibility shifts between resources (e.g., clerk to nurse to doctor).
- **Patient heterogeneity**: New patients or urgent cases experience different flows and longer waits than follow-ups.

Process mining techniques applied to the event log include:
- **Bottleneck analysis**: Process maps or dotted charts colored by waiting time, revealing where delays concentrate.
- **Resource analysis**: Utilization dashboards, workload distribution, and handover networks showing which resources or handoffs correlate with long waits.
- **Variant analysis**: Comparing process variants for high-wait vs. low-wait cases, or by patient type/urgency, to identify differing paths or outcomes.
- **Performance analysis and statistics**: Correlation of wait times with time of day, resource, service time variance, or arrival rates; decision mining to find rules (e.g., "waits increase when >3 patients arrive in 15 minutes").
- **Queueing theory integration**: Treating key resources as servers and analyzing inter-arrival vs. service rates.

These techniques, often implemented in tools supporting start/complete events, move diagnosis from symptoms (long waits) to causes (e.g., morning nurse understaffing for new patients).

**3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies**

Three concrete strategies, each directly supported by the analysis:

**Strategy 1: Time-of-day dynamic staffing and cross-training.**  
Targets early-stage queues (Registration  Nurse Assessment and Nurse  Doctor). Addresses resource bottlenecks during peak arrival windows identified via dotted charts and hourly utilization analysis showing >85% nurse utilization correlating with 25+ minute waits. Data support: Historical patterns show specific 9–11 AM spikes for New patients. Implementation involves adjusting shifts or cross-training clerks/nurses for peak periods using predictive load from the six-month log. Expected impact: 25–35% reduction in average wait for targeted transitions, based on similar healthcare queue mining cases, while keeping total staff hours constant through reallocation.

**Strategy 2: Patient-type differentiated appointment scheduling with buffers.**  
Targets overall arrival bunching affecting multiple downstream queues (especially doctor consultation). Root cause: Current uniform scheduling ignores differences in service time distributions (new patients average longer consults and more diagnostics per variant analysis). Data support: Statistical analysis of historical durations and arrival patterns per patient type/urgency. New policy uses slot lengths derived from median + buffer (e.g., 20 min for follow-ups, 40 min for new), with dedicated urgent slots. Expected impact: Smoother flow, 20–30% reduction in total waiting time and 15% reduction in overall visit duration, with lower variance.

**Strategy 3: Selective parallelization of diagnostics and self-service technology.**  
Targets post-doctor queues (Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Test) and check-out. Addresses sequential dependencies and low-value activities revealed by process discovery. Data support: Variant analysis shows many patients follow predictable test patterns after specific specialties; registration and check-out have low variability and high volume. Implementation: Pre-order common tests after nurse assessment for certain pathways and introduce kiosks/digital check-in/out. Expected impact: Reduce sequential waiting by 40% for diagnostic queues in targeted variants and cut check-out waits significantly, improving total visit time by 10–20 minutes on average.

Each strategy is piloted on one specialty or patient type, with pre/post comparison using the same event log structure.

**4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints**

Potential negative effects include bottleneck shifting (e.g., reducing nurse waits increases doctor queue if not addressed holistically), increased staff workload or burnout from tighter schedules, higher costs for additional rooms/equipment in parallelization, and risk of reduced care quality if activities are rushed. Dynamic staffing may face union or regulatory constraints on shift changes.

Balancing occurs through simulation on the discovered process model (using historical distributions for what-if scenarios), prioritizing low-cost, high-impact changes first (scheduling and self-service before adding headcount). Resource utilization is capped (target <85% to avoid burnout), and care quality is protected by maintaining minimum service times and monitoring outcomes. Multi-objective optimization weighs total waiting time against costs and staff satisfaction, using sensitivity analysis on the event data. Pilots with A/B testing on subsets of patients allow measurement of real trade-offs before full rollout.

**5. Measuring Success**

Post-implementation KPIs include:
- Average and median total visit duration and total waiting time.
- 90th percentile waiting time and visit duration (focusing on outliers).
- Percentage of visits with total waiting time below target (e.g., <30 minutes).
- Stage-specific queue metrics for critical transitions.
- Resource utilization (balanced, not exceeding 85%).
- Patient satisfaction scores (if linkable to Visit ID) and process efficiency ratio (value-adding service time / total time).

Ongoing monitoring uses the same event log structure fed into a live process mining dashboard. Techniques include continuous performance monitoring, conformance checking against the improved model, automated alerts when KPIs degrade beyond thresholds, and monthly trend analysis segmented by patient type. Statistical tests (e.g., t-tests or Mann-Whitney on pre/post distributions) confirm significance. Sustained improvement is ensured by periodic re-discovery of the process model to detect drift and feeding insights back into scheduling algorithms.

This approach leverages the full power of queue mining—turning raw timestamps into actionable, quantified improvements while respecting operational constraints.
