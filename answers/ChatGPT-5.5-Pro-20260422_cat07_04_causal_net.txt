The visualization is a **process model**, specifically a **causal-net style diagram**. It describes how a request moves through a business process, with the black dots explaining which activities may happen together or as alternatives.

### How to read the diagram

The rounded rectangles are **activities**:

* `a` — register request
* `b` — examine thoroughly
* `c` — examine casually
* `d` — check ticket
* `e` — decide
* `f` — reinitiate request
* `g` — pay compensation
* `h` — reject request
* `z` — end

The arrows show possible causal flow from one activity to another.

The **black dots** are the important part: they represent **bindings**, meaning they specify whether outgoing or incoming branches are alternatives, parallel requirements, or optional combinations.

### The legend

The grey box explains the notation.

For **splits**, an activity leads to multiple possible next activities:

* **XOR-split**: choose exactly one outgoing branch.
* **AND-split**: take all outgoing branches.
* **OR-split**: take one or more branches, depending on the case.

For **joins**, multiple incoming branches lead into one activity:

* **XOR-join**: the activity can start after one of the incoming branches.
* **AND-join**: the activity requires all incoming branches.
* **OR-join**: the activity requires a particular subset of incoming branches.

So the black dots are not extra tasks; they define the **logic of the branching and merging**.

### What the main process says

The process begins with:

> `a` — register request

After registration, the request must be examined and checked. The model shows that the request can go through either:

* `b` — examine thoroughly
  together with
  `d` — check ticket

or:

* `c` — examine casually
  together with
  `d` — check ticket

In other words, the request is checked, and it is examined either thoroughly or casually.

Then the process reaches:

> `e` — decide

The decision activity depends on the earlier examination and ticket check being completed. That is why several arrows from `b`, `c`, and `d` converge into `e`.

From `e`, there are three possible outcomes:

1. `g` — pay compensation
2. `h` — reject request
3. `f` — reinitiate request

If the request is reinitiated, the process loops back to the examination/checking stage. That loop means the case may need to be reviewed again before another decision is made.

Finally:

* `g` — pay compensation leads to `z` — end
* `h` — reject request leads to `z` — end

### In plain language

A request is registered. It is then checked and examined, either casually or thoroughly. A decision is made. The decision may result in compensation, rejection, or a reinitiation of the request. If reinitiated, the request goes back through the checking/examination phase. Eventually, the process ends after either compensation is paid or the request is rejected.

A key point is that this is **not just a simple flowchart**. The black dots tell you which activities are alternatives and which must occur together.
