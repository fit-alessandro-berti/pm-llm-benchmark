Looking at this POWL model for the Order-to-Cash process, I can spot two significant structural anomalies that deviate from standard business practices and could indicate workarounds or process violations.

**1. Optional Credit Check (XOR with Silent Transition)**
The `xor_credit` construct allows the process to *skip* the credit check entirely by choosing the silent transition. While this might seem like flexibility, in reality, skipping credit checks introduces financial risk—potentially allowing orders from customers with poor credit history. In well-governed environments, credit approval is a control point *required* before order fulfillment. Making it optional suggests either weak controls or users circumventing the process under pressure to close sales—masking underlying policy or system issues.

**2. Loop Around Approval and Invoicing**
The `loop_approve_invoice` operator creates a cycle where *Approve Order* and *Issue Invoice* can repeat multiple times. This is unusual because each order should be approved once and invoiced once (barring corrections). Repeating these steps might indicate:
- Invoices being reissued due to errors (process inefficiency),
- Orders being re-approved after changes (lack of change management),
- Or even fraudulent behavior like double-billing.

In a compliant process, such loops should be tightly controlled, not baked into the core workflow structure.

**Why These Matter**
Together, these patterns suggest a process that’s either poorly designed or being actively gamed. Skipping checks and looping key steps erodes data integrity, increases operational risk, and makes auditing difficult. They likely reflect real-world workarounds—employees bypassing steps to meet SLAs—while exposing the business to financial and compliance exposure. A robust model should enforce critical path adherence while handling exceptions explicitly, not as structural afterthoughts.
