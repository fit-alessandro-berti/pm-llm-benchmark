### 1. Identification of Cases with Performance Issues

To identify the problematic cases, we first calculate the total lead time (from "Submit Claim" to "Close Claim") for each case:

### 2. Root Cause Analysis (Attributes Correlation)

**Complexity is the primary driver of extended durations.**
There is a direct correlation between claim complexity and lead time. Low-complexity cases bypass the "Request Additional Documents" step entirely and complete in under 2 hours. Medium-complexity cases require one document request, stretching the time to over a day. High-complexity cases trigger multiple document requests, causing massive delays. 

**Resource behavior exacerbates the delays.**
While complexity dictates the *need* for more documents, the *resource* handling the claim determines how efficiently that need is met:
*   **Adjuster_Lisa** handled Case 2005 (High) and requested additional documents **three separate times**. This iterative back-and-forth extended the case to 77 hours. 
*   **Adjuster_Mike** handled Case 2003 (High) and requested additional documents **twice**, resulting in a shorter (but still long) duration of 48 hours.
*   **Manager_Bill** is the only manager who handles High-complexity claims (2003 and 2005). While his approval times seem standard once documents are gathered, the fact that high-complexity claims route exclusively to him could represent a future bottleneck if volume increases.

**Region does not appear to be an independent root cause.**
Region A and Region B both process low-complexity claims efficiently (Cases 2001 and 2004). Region B takes longer overall, but only because it processed the medium and the worst-performing high-complexity case. Region is a coincidental factor, not a causal one.

### 3. Explanations and Mitigation Strategies

**Explanation 1: Iterative Document Gathering (The "Piecemeal" Approach)**
*   *Why it contributes to delays:* In high-complexity cases (especially Case 2005 handled by Adjuster_Lisa), adjusters are requesting documents piecemeal. Instead of identifying all missing information upfront, they request documents, wait for the customer to provide them, review again, and request more. Each iteration adds idle time waiting for the customer.
*   *Mitigation:* Implement a **comprehensive initial assessment checklist** for high-complexity claims. Adjusters should be required to identify 100% of missing documentation in a single step. Additionally, a peer-review step for high-complexity document requests could be introduced before the request is sent to the customer, ensuring nothing is missed.

**Explanation 2: Lack of Upfront Customer Preparation**
*   *Why it contributes to delays:* Complex claims inherently require more evidence, but the process relies on the adjuster to discover what is missing after the claim is submitted. 
*   *Mitigation:* Provide customers with **dynamic submission portals**. When a customer tags a claim as "High Complexity" (e.g., involving multiple vehicles, severe injury, or high value), the portal should automatically prompt them to upload specific core documents (police reports, medical records, etc.) *before* the claim even reaches an adjuster.

**Explanation 3: Resource Specialization and Training Gaps**
*   *Why it contributes to delays:* Adjuster_Lisa's handling of Case 2005 suggests a training or experience gap compared to Adjuster_Mike. She either lacks the knowledge to identify all required documents at once or is overwhelmed by her workload (she handled three out of four non-low-complexity cases).
*   *Mitigation:* Provide **targeted training for Adjuster_Lisa** on high-complexity claim requirements.
