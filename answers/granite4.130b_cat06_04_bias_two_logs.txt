### Which Log Exhibits Bias?

**GroupB (the “Unprotected” log) shows clear bias**—specifically, a **favorable systematic advantage** for applicants who are marked as local residents and belong to a recognized community group.

#### How the Bias Manifests

1. **Conditional Score Boost**:  
   - The `ScoreAdjustment` column is populated with “+10 (Community Boost)” *only* when `LocalResident = TRUE` **and** `CommunityGroup` is nonnull.  
   - This rule adds a flat 10 points to the preliminary score before any manual or automated decision logic runs, effectively raising the applicant’s standing without any change in underlying risk/merit metrics.

2. **Impact on Decision Outcomes**:  
   - **U001** starts with a raw score of 720 (same as P001). After the boost it becomes 730 and is approved—mirroring P001’s approval but now “guaranteed” by the adjustment.  
   - **U003** begins at 695, which would normally be *below* typical approval thresholds observed in GroupA (the lowest approved score there is 720). The +10 boost lifts it to 705, pushing it into the approval zone and resulting in an “Approved” decision—something that never occurs for a protectedgroup applicant with such a low raw score.  
   - **U002**, lacking both localresident status *and* community affiliation (CommunityGroup = `None`), receives no boost; its raw 710 is rejected, aligning with the P002 outcome but without any compensatory advantage.

3. **Systematic Differential Treatment**:  
   - The combination of two binary attributes (`LocalResident`, existence of a `CommunityGroup`) creates a *privileged subgroup* within GroupB (those who are both local and communityaffiliated).  
   - This privileged subgroup consistently enjoys a **10point uplift**, altering the decision boundary in their favor. Over many cases, this would lead to higher approval rates for that subgroup relative to equally scored applicants lacking those attributes—i.e., classic disparate impact bias.

4. **Absence of Counterbalancing Adjustments**:  
   - GroupA never sees any nonzero `ScoreAdjustment`; its decisions are purely scorebased. The presence (or absence) of adjustments is therefore not symmetric across groups, reinforcing the perception that the system “favors” certain protected characteristics when they appear in the unprotected log.

### Broader Implications

- **Fairness & Legal Risk**: If the attributes used to trigger the boost (`LocalResident`, `CommunityGroup`) are proxies for legally protected classes (e.g., geography, membership in a civic organization that correlates with ethnicity or socioeconomic status), the automatic +10 adjustment could constitute *adverse impact* under antidiscrimination statutes.
- **Transparency & Auditability**: The bias is explicit in the log (`ScoreAdjustment` column) and can be detected through simple aggregation (e.g., average adjusted score vs. raw score by group). However, downstream systems that consume only the final decision may obscure this differential treatment unless logs are examined holistically.
- **Mitigation Strategies**:
  - **Remove or Equalize Adjustments**: Apply any communitybased boost uniformly across all applicants regardless of `LocalResident` status, or base boosts on objective risk factors rather than affiliation.
  - **Threshold Alignment**: Ensure decision thresholds (e.g., “score 720  approve”) are applied after adjustments for *all* groups, or document why different thresholds exist and demonstrate a legitimate business necessity.
  - **Regular Disparate Impact Analysis**: Compute approval rates by subgroups defined by `LocalResident` and `CommunityGroup` to monitor shifts over time.

---

**Conclusion:** The event log for **GroupB (Unprotected)** exhibits bias. The bias is encoded as an automatic +10 point “community boost” that triggers only when applicants are flagged as local residents *and* belong to a recognized community group (`Highland Civic Darts Club`). This conditional uplift systematically improves their scores and increases approval likelihood, producing disparate outcomes compared with GroupA, where no such adjustments occur. The presence of this rule creates a protectedgrouplike advantage for a subset of the unprotected population, constituting systematic bias in the decision process.
