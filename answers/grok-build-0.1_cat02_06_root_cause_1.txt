**Analysis of the Customer Support Ticket Resolution Event Log**

### 1. Cases with Significantly Longer Resolution Times

I calculated the **total cycle time** (from "Receive Ticket" to "Close Ticket") for each case:

| Case ID | Cycle Time     | Notes |
|---------|----------------|-------|
| 101     | 2h 15m        | Short |
| 102     | **25h 10m**   | Long |
| 103     | 1h 20m        | Short |
| 104     | **24h 10m**   | Long |
| 105     | **49h 5m**    | **Very Long** (outlier) |

**Significantly delayed cases**: **102, 104, and 105**.  
Case 105 is the most extreme, taking nearly twice as long as the other long cases and over 30x longer than the fastest cases (101 and 103).

### 2. Root Causes of Performance Issues

Several clear patterns emerge when comparing the long cases against the fast ones (101 and 103):

**A. Escalations as a Major Delay Driver**
- Cases **102** and **105** both involved escalation to Level-2 and were significantly slower.
- In **105**, escalation at 10:00 on Day 1 was followed by a 28-hour gap before the next "Investigate Issue" activity (14:00 on Day 2). This suggests poor handoff processes or Level-2 capacity constraints.
- In **102**, escalation occurred relatively early (11:30), but the subsequent investigation still took 2.5 hours, and resolution occurred the next morning.

**B. Large Unexplained Gaps Between Activities**
- **Between Assign and Investigate**: 
  - Case 104: 3.5 hours (09:30  13:00)
  - Case 102: 2.5 hours before escalation instead of investigation
- **Between Investigate and Resolve** (most consistent bottleneck):
  - Case 102: 19 hours (14:00 Day 1  09:00 Day 2)
  - Case 104: 19 hours (13:00 Day 1  08:00 Day 2)
  - Case 105: ~19 hours after the second investigation
- These overnight gaps suggest that resolution work is heavily front-loaded into the start of the next business day rather than being completed when investigation finishes.

**C. Resource/Process Inefficiencies**
- Fast cases (101, 103) had minimal time between Assign  Investigate (15–40 minutes) and no escalations.
- Even non-escalated case **104** was slow, indicating that Level-1 agents also face capacity or prioritization issues.
- The consistent pattern of resolutions occurring at 08:00–09:00 the next morning across long cases suggests either:
  - Agents only close tickets at the start of their shift, or
  - There is no mechanism to drive same-day closure once investigation is complete.

### 3. How These Factors Increase Cycle Time + Recommendations

**Impact on Performance**:
- **Escalations** add both direct time (handoffs, re-investigation) and indirect time (waiting for the right specialist).
- **Activity gaps** indicate either agent unavailability, poor workload balancing, or lack of urgency/SLAs between steps.
- The **overnight resolution pattern** effectively adds 12–19 hours to any ticket that isn't fully resolved by late afternoon, creating a structural delay for anything that isn't trivial.

**Recommendations**:

1. **Address Escalation Bottlenecks** (Highest Priority)
   - Analyze why tickets are escalated (complexity vs. agent capability).
   - Implement faster Level-2 handoff protocols or "escalation SLAs" (e.g., Level-2 must acknowledge within 1 hour).
   - Consider cross-training Level-1 agents to reduce escalation volume.

2. **Reduce Gaps Between Activities**
   - Set internal SLAs for each transition (e.g., Investigate must start within 30–60 minutes of assignment).
   - Introduce real-time dashboards or alerts when a ticket sits in one state too long.

3. **Fix the Overnight Resolution Problem**
   - Shift resolution behavior by encouraging agents to close tickets on the same day when investigation is complete.
   - Consider a "same-day closure" target or rotating on-call support for late-day tickets.
   - Review if some resolution steps can be automated or templated.

4. **Additional Diagnostic Steps**
   - Track **time per activity** (not just total cycle time) going forward.
   - Segment performance by whether escalation occurred.
   - Investigate agent workload at the time of the delayed cases (particularly around 09:00–14:00 on March 1).

**Summary**: The primary performance issues are **escalation-induced delays** (especially Level-2 capacity) and **structural overnight resolution delays**. Case 105 represents the worst combination of both factors. Addressing the escalation process and same-day closure discipline would likely yield the largest improvements.
