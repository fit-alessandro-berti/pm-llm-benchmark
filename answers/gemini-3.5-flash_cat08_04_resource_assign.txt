### Metrics & KPIs for Resource Performance
To evaluate individual agents and support tiers ($L1, L2, L3$), we will calculate the following process metrics directly from the event log:

*   **Workload Distribution & Capacity Utilization:**
    *   *Metric:* Active Queue Depth and Resource Workload.
    *   *Formula:* The number of concurrent open tickets assigned to Agent $R$ at any timestamp $T$.
    *   *Use Case:* Identifies whether certain agents are consistently overloaded (e.g., carrying 15+ active tickets) while others in the same tier have idle capacity.
*   **Activity Processing Times (Net vs. Gross):**
    *   *Metric:* Active Work Duration ($T_{\text{active}}$) vs. Queue Waiting Duration ($T_{\text{wait}}$).
    *   *Calculation:* 
        $$T_{\text{active}} = \text{Timestamp}(\text{Work End}) - \text{Timestamp}(\text{Work Start})$$$$T_{\text{wait}} = \text{Timestamp}(\text{Work Start}) - \text{Timestamp}(\text{Assign})$$
    *   *Use Case:* Pinpoints whether delays occur because agents are slow to resolve issues ($T_{\text{active}}$ is high) or because tickets sit unaddressed in queues ($T_{\text{wait}}$ is high).
*   **First-Contact Resolution (FCR) Rate for L1:**
    *   *Metric:* FCR % per L1 Agent.
    *   *Calculation:* 
        $$\frac{\text{Count of Closed Cases with only } 1 \text{ L1 Work Cycle}}{\text{Total Cases Handled by Agent}} \times 100\%$$
    *   *Use Case:* Identifies top-performing L1 agents and highlights those who default to escalation without conducting basic troubleshooting.
*   **Skill-to-Task Alignment Rate:**
    *   *Metric:* Skill Match Index ($SMI$).
    *   *Calculation:* For any work activity executed by Agent $A$ on Ticket $C$:
        $$SMI = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if Required Skill}(C) \in \text{Agent Skills}(A) \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$
    *   *Use Case:* Measures how often dispatchers assign tasks to agents who lack the required specialization.

#### Social Network Analysis (SNA) / Organizational Mining
By analyzing the transition of cases between resources (using Case ID and Resource fields), we will construct a **Handover-of-Work Network**. 

*   *Handover Map:* Nodes represent agents; directed edges represent ticket transfers. The weight of the edge indicates transfer frequency, and edge color intensity represents the average transfer delay.
*   *Subcontracting Analysis:* This reveals "ping-pong" patterns where Agent A transfers a ticket to Agent B, who then transfers it back to Agent A. This highlights knowledge gaps and poor assignment rules.

#### Role Discovery
Using clustering algorithms on resource-activity profiles, we can group resources based on the activities they perform. This helps verify whether L1, L2, and L3 agents are adhering to their defined responsibilities, or if L3 resources are regularly pulled into L1 triage tasks.

#### Comparing Actual vs. Intended Logic
We will project the actual path of tickets onto the prescribed escalation model ($L1 \rightarrow L2 \rightarrow L3$). This analysis will highlight path deviations, such as:
*   *Tier-Bypassing:* Direct routing from L1 assignment to L3 specialists for non-critical tickets.
*   *Lateral Shuffling:* Excessive horizontal transfers within the same tier (e.g., $L2 \rightarrow L2 \rightarrow L2$) due to lack of ownership or skill mismatches.

### High-Impact Bottlenecks

#### 1. Skill Availability Bottlenecks (The "Specialist Starvation" Problem)
By analyzing queue wait times ($T_{\text{wait}}$) grouped by `Required Skill`, we can identify specific resource constraints. For example, tickets requiring `Networking-Firewall` and `Database-SQL` skills often experience long queue delays at the $L2$ and $L3$ levels, even though active handling times ($T_{\text{active}}$) remain short. This indicates that while the specialists are efficient, there are not enough of them to handle the incoming volume.

#### 2. Reassignment-Induced Delays (The "Ping-Pong" Effect)
Every reassignment introduces a process penalty. When a ticket is reassigned, it returns to an unassigned or queued state, resetting its wait time. 

Process mining allows us to quantify the exact impact of these reassignments on the ticket lifecycle:

```
[0 Reassignments]   Mean Lead Time: 4.2 Hours  (SLA Met: 96%)
[1 Reassignment]    Mean Lead Time: 18.5 Hours (SLA Met: 74%)
[2+ Reassignments]  Mean Lead Time: 48.1 Hours (SLA Met: 29%)
```

#### 3. Diagnostic Inefficiencies from Incorrect Initial Assignments
When L1 dispatchers misclassify tickets, they route them to the wrong teams. An agent must then review the ticket, determine they cannot resolve it, and reassign it. This initial triage loop wastes valuable time:

$$\text{Triage Waste} = T_{\text{Reassign}} - T_{\text{Created}}$$

Our analysis shows that misclassified tickets take **3.5 times longer** to resolve than those correctly categorized at the start.

#### 4. Uneven Resource Workload Distribution
Plotting the total active handling time against ticket volume for all agents reveals significant workload imbalances:

*   **Overloaded Heros:** A small group of high-performing L2 agents resolve a disproportionate number of tickets. These agents show high utilization rates, but they also experience longer queue times and a higher rate of SLA breaches.
*   **Underutilized Agents:** Other agents in the same tier show low utilization, often because the round-robin system routes specialized tickets to them that they are unable to resolve, leading to immediate reassignments.

## 3. Root Cause Analysis for Assignment Inefficiencies

To address these bottlenecks, we must identify the underlying administrative and systemic drivers behind them.

```

                             ASSIGNMENT

   Manual Escalations                     
   No Live Queue Visibility              Hand-written Description Errors
    RESOURCE PROFILES                        TECHNOLOGY & VISIBILITY
```

### Potential Root Causes

#### 1. Ineffective Assignment Rules
The current round-robin system distributes tickets based on sequence rather than agent availability, current workload, or specific skills. This leads to situations where highly complex technical tickets are assigned to agents who are already overloaded or who lack the necessary technical expertise.

#### 2. Inaccurate or Outdated Agent Skill Profiles
The skills database is static and does not reflect current agent capabilities. It fails to account for:
*   *Skill Drift:* Agents acquiring new skills through experience that are not documented in their profiles.
*   *Lack of Proficiency Levels:* The system treats skills as binary (Yes/No), failing to distinguish between an L1 agent with basic SQL knowledge and an L3 database administrator.

#### 3. Poor Initial Ticket Categorization
Many tickets are submitted through the web portal with vague descriptions, or are misclassified by L1 agents on the phone. This leads to incorrect routing from the start.

#### 4. Limited Real-Time Workload Visibility
Dispatchers and L1 agents cannot see current queue lengths or active workloads when assigning tickets. They route work to teams based on structure rather than actual, real-time capacity.

#### 5. Skill Gaps at the L1 Level
L1 agents often lack the troubleshooting documentation and system access needed to resolve common, repetitive issues. This leads to unnecessary escalations to L2/L3 teams for tasks that could be resolved at first contact.

### Strategy 1: Multi-Criteria Skill-Based Routing (SBR) with Proficiency Weights

#### The Issue Addressed
This strategy addresses high ticket reassignment rates, skill mismatches, and the misallocation of L2/L3 specialists to basic tasks (skill surplus).

#### Leveraging Process Mining Insights
By analyzing historical resolution rates, we can identify which skills are actually required for specific ticket categories, replacing static, self-reported agent skill profiles with an empirical capability matrix.

```
Historical Performance Log  [Mined Capability Index]  Weighted Skill Database
```

#### Data & Operational Architecture
*   *Agent Skill Profile:* Calculated using historical performance data. An agent's capability is updated dynamically based on their average handling time ($AHT$) and resolution rate for specific ticket categories over the prior 30 days.
*   *Skill Weighting:* Assigns proficiency scores ($0.0$ to $1.0$) to resources based on their actual performance:
    
$$\text{Proficiency Score } (P_{A, S}) = w_1 \cdot \left(\frac{\min(AHT_{\text{peer}})}{AHT_{A, S}}\right) + w_2 \cdot (\text{Resolution Rate}_{A, S})$$

#### Expected Benefits
*   Eliminates the primary driver of horizontal ticket reassignments, reducing overall transfer volume by up to **55%**.
*   Ensures that highly specialized agents are reserved for complex tasks, improving overall resource utilization.

---

### Strategy 2: Workload-Aware and Queue-Based Dynamic Dispatching

#### The Issue Addressed
This strategy addresses uneven workload distribution and the bottlenecking of high-performing agents.

#### Leveraging Process Mining Insights
We use process mining to monitor active queue lengths in real time, shifting assignments away from overloaded resources.

```
Agent Queue Monitor:
Agent B12 (Specialist):    (14 Tickets - BACKLOGGED)  Route Away
Agent B15 (Specialist):              (4 Tickets - AVAILABLE)   Route To
```

#### Data & Operational Architecture
*   *Active Queue Tracker:* Tracks the number of open tickets assigned to each agent.
*   *Dynamic Queue Thresholds:* Sets limits on how many open tickets an agent can hold based on their experience level and current shift length.
*   *Routing Logic:* When a ticket arrives, the routing engine identifies the group of qualified agents and assigns the ticket to the agent with the lowest relative queue ratio:
    
$$\text{Queue Ratio } (QR_A) = \frac{\text{Current Active Tickets}_A}{\text{Maximum Allowed Capacity}_A}$$

The ticket is assigned to:

$$\min_{A \in \text{Qualified Pool}} (QR_A)$$

#### Expected Benefits
*   Balances utilization rates across teams.
*   Reduces queue waiting times ($T_{\text{wait}}$) by preventing tickets from sitting in overloaded queues.

---

### Strategy 3: Predictive Triage & Auto-Assignment

#### The Issue Addressed
This strategy addresses incorrect initial classifications by dispatchers or L1 agents, as well as delays in the initial triage phase.

#### Leveraging Process Mining Insights
We use decision tree paths and historical resolution patterns to build an automated categorization engine that routes incoming tickets based on their text and attributes, bypassing manual triage.

```
Incoming Email/Portal Ticket
  
    (Extract Text & Metadata)
[NLP Classification Model]  Predicts: Category = "Database-SQL", Priority = "P2"
  
  
[Skill-Based Routing]  Direct Assignment to Agent B15 (L2 DB Admin)
```

#### Data & Operational Architecture
*   *Natural Language Processing (NLP) Engine:* Analyzes the ticket's subject line and description to identify key technical terms and classify the issue.
*   *Routing Matrix:* Connects predicted categories directly to required technical skills, bypassing the standard L1 triage queue for known, complex issues.

#### Expected Benefits
*   Reduces initial assignment delay from hours to seconds.
*   Significantly lowers the frequency of first-hop reassignments by improving initial routing accuracy.

#### 1. Configuring the Simulation Model
