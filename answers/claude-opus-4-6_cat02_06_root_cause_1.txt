## 2. Root Cause Analysis

### Detailed Activity-by-Activity Gap Analysis

#### Case 102 (25h 10m) ù Escalation with Waiting Delays

| Transition | Duration | Flag |
|-----------|----------|------|
| Receive  Triage | 25 min | Slightly slow |
| Triage  Assign L1 | 30 min | Slightly slow |
| Assign L1  **Escalate to L2** | **2 hrs 30 min** |  L1 couldn't resolve |
| Escalate L2  Investigate | **2 hrs 30 min** |  **Waiting for L2 pickup** |
| Investigate  Resolve | **19 hrs** |  **Overnight/complex resolution** |

**Root Causes:**
- **Escalation to Level-2** introduced a handoff delay of 2.5 hours before investigation even began.
- **Overnight gap** between investigation and resolution suggests either the issue was complex or work stopped at end-of-business and resumed the next day.

## 3. Summary of Key Factors and Recommendations

### Factor 1:  Escalation to Level-2 Creates Major Handoff Delays

**Finding:** Both escalated cases (102 and 105) experienced significant waiting times after escalation. Case 105 waited **28 hours** for a Level-2 agent to begin investigation.

**Recommendations:**
- **Implement SLA targets for L2 pickup time** (e.g., investigation must begin within 1 hour of escalation).
- **Real-time alerting/notification** when a ticket is escalated so L2 agents are immediately aware.
- **Dedicated L2 capacity planning** ù ensure Level-2 agents have reserved bandwidth for escalations rather than treating them as overflow work.
- **Skill-based routing** at triage to send likely-complex tickets directly to L2, avoiding the L1L2 handoff entirely.

### Factor 2:  Long Waiting Time Between Assignment and Investigation (Even Without Escalation)

**Finding:** Case 104 had a **3.5-hour gap** between L1 assignment and investigation start, despite no escalation. Compare this to Cases 101 (40 min) and 103 (15 min).

**Recommendations:**
- **Monitor agent queue depth** ù the delay likely indicates the assigned L1 agent was overloaded with other tickets.
- **Workload-aware assignment algorithms** ù route tickets to agents with available capacity, not just round-robin.
- **Auto-reassignment rules** ù if an assigned agent hasn't started investigation within a threshold (e.g., 30 minutes), automatically reassign to an available agent.

### Factor 3:  Overnight Gaps Suggest No Follow-the-Sun or Shift Coverage

**Finding:** Cases 102, 104, and 105 all exhibit ~19-hour gaps that align with end-of-business to next-morning patterns, suggesting work stops completely overnight.

**Recommendations:**
- **Shift-based staffing or follow-the-sun model** for high-priority tickets to avoid overnight stalls.
- **If 24/7 coverage isn't feasible**, prioritize tickets nearing SLA breach for first action in the morning.
- **End-of-shift handoff protocols** ù agents ending their shift should document progress and flag tickets for the next shift.

### Factor 4:  Triage Delays Vary Unnecessarily

**Finding:** Triage times range from 5 minutes (Case 103) to 40 minutes (Case 104). While not the primary driver, inconsistent triage adds up.

**Recommendations:**
- **Automated pre-triage** using ticket category, keywords, or customer tier to accelerate or auto-triage common ticket types.
- **Standardized triage SLA** of 15 minutes.

### Key Takeaway

The two dominant drivers of excessive cycle time are:
1. **Level-2 escalation handoff delays** (especially L2 resource availability), and
2. **Overnight work stoppages** without adequate shift handoff.

Addressing L2 capacity and responsiveness alone could reduce Case 105's duration from 49 hours to under 5 hours. Implementing even basic overnight handoff protocols would eliminate the recurring 19-hour stalls seen across all three delayed cases.
