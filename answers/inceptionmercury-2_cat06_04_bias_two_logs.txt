## 2. Where the bias lives  

| Attribute | How it is used in the logs | Why it matters |
|-----------|---------------------------|----------------|
| **LocalResident** | All GroupA cases are marked **FALSE**; all GroupB cases are **TRUE**. The logs never show a numeric adjustment tied directly to this flag, but it is a *protected* characteristic that can be used (explicitly or implicitly) by downstream rules. | If the downstream “Rules Engine” or the underwriter’s manual review gives any weight to residency (e.g., “prefer local applicants”), the protected group is automatically at a disadvantage. The fact that the flag is present in the data makes it a *potential* source of disparate treatment. |
| **CommunityGroup** | Only the two “Highland Civic Darts Club” members in GroupB receive a **+10** “Community Boost” during *PreliminaryScoring*. No such boost exists for any GroupA case (all have “None”). | The boost is a *positive, nonneutral* adjustment that directly raises the final score and therefore the likelihood of approval. Because the boost is tied to a *nonprotected* attribute (membership in a specific community), it creates a *systematic* advantage for those applicants. |
| **ScoreAdjustment** | GroupA: always **0**. <br>GroupB: **+10** for the two “DartsClub” cases, **0** otherwise. | The adjustment changes the *decision boundary* for the boosted cases. For example, U001’s raw score of 720 would have been on the cusp of approval; the +10 pushes it to 730, comfortably above any implicit cutoff. The same raw score (720) for P001 receives no boost, leaving it at the lower edge. |

### 2.1. Manifestation of bias in the decision pipeline  

1. **Raw scores are identical for many comparable applicants** (e.g., P001=720, U001=720).  
2. **Only the unprotected group receives an extra +10** when the applicant belongs to a particular community.  
3. **Adjusted scores feed directly into the final ruleengine decision** (the logs show no other decisionmaking steps after the adjustment).  
4. Consequently, **identical applicants (same raw score, same other attributes) receive different outcomes solely because of the “CommunityBoost”**.

Even though the final approval counts are the same in this tiny sample, the *process* is biased:

- The protected group never gets a boost, even when they belong to a community that could be considered analogous (e.g., a local cultural association).  
- The unprotected group receives a boost *only* when the community is explicitly listed; any other community (or “None”) gets no boost, creating a *selective* advantage.

## 3. Quantitative illustration of the systematic difference  

| Case | Raw score | Adjustment | Adjusted score | Decision |
|------|-----------|------------|----------------|----------|
| **P001** (A) | 720 | 0 | **720** | Approved |
| **U001** (B) | 720 | +10 | **730** | Approved |
| **P002** (A) | 710 | 0 | **710** | Rejected |
| **U002** (B) | 710 | 0 | **710** | Rejected |
| **P003** (A) | 740 | 0 | **740** | Approved |
| **U003** (B) | 695 | +10 | **705** | Approved |

- **Same raw score, different adjusted score:** P001 vs. U001 (720 vs. 730).  
- **Lower raw score but still approved thanks to the boost:** U003 (695705) beats the rawscore threshold that would have rejected a 695point applicant.  

If the decision rule were something like “approve if adjusted score720”, then:

- P001 (720) just makes it.  
- U001 (730) comfortably makes it.  
- U003 (705) would *normally* be rejected, but the boost pushes it above the implicit threshold used by the underwriter (the underwriter may be looking at the *adjusted* score, not the raw).  

Thus the boost creates a **systematic shift** in the decision boundary for the unprotected group.

## 4. Why this is a fairness problem  

| Fairness dimension | What the logs show |
|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Disparate treatment** | The algorithm applies a *different rule* (the +10 boost) to applicants based on a nonprotected attribute (CommunityGroup) that correlates with protected status (LocalResident=TRUE). |
| **Disparate impact** | Even if the rule is “neutral” on its face, its effect is to *increase* approval chances for a subset of the unprotected group while leaving the protected group unchanged. Over a larger population this would produce a statistically significant gap in outcomes. |
| **Transparency / Explainability** | The “Community Boost” is visible in the log, but the rationale for why only the Highland Civic Darts Club receives it is not documented. This opacity makes it hard to audit the model for fairness. |
| **Potential legal risk** | In many jurisdictions, using residency or community affiliation as a proxy for protected class status (e.g., race, ethnicity, national origin) can violate antidiscrimination statutes if it results in adverse impact. |

---

## 5. How to mitigate the identified bias  

| Mitigation | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Remove or neutralize the boost** | Delete the “+10 Community Boost” rule, or replace it with a *fairnessaware* adjustment that is applied equally across groups (e.g., a uniform calibration factor). |
| **Audit the CommunityGroup mapping** | Verify whether the community affiliation is correlated with protected attributes. If it is, either drop the attribute from the model or apply *counterfactual fairness* techniques. |
| **Add a fairness constraint to the scoring engine** | Use a constrained optimization (e.g., demographic parity, equalized odds) that forces the approval rate (or the truepositive rate) to be equal across the LocalResident flag. |
| **Explainability layer** | Log the *reason* for any score adjustment (e.g., “Community Boost – policy X”) and make the policy publicly available for review. |
| **Regular disparity testing** | Run statistical tests (e.g., chisquare, disparate impact ratio) on a rolling window of decisions to catch any emerging systematic gaps. |
| **Humanintheloop review** | Require a secondary reviewer to validate any case that receives a nonzero adjustment, ensuring that the boost is justified on a casebycase basis rather than a blanket rule. |

---

## 6. TL;DR – What the bias looks like  

- **Protected group (GroupA)**: never gets a score boost, even when raw scores are comparable to the unprotected group.  
- **Unprotected group (GroupB)**: receives a **+10** “Community Boost” for members of a specific community, which pushes borderline applicants over the approval threshold.  
- The **LocalResident** flag is a protected attribute that is *always* FALSE for the protected group and TRUE for the unprotected group, opening the door for disparate treatment if downstream rules use it.  
- **Result:** A systematic, rulebased advantage for the unprotected group that can lead to higher approval rates (or easier approvals) for otherwise identical applicants—a classic manifestation of **algorithmic bias**.  

Addressing the bias requires either **removing the differential boost** or **redesigning the scoring pipeline** so that any adjustments are fair, transparent, and independent of protected attributes.
