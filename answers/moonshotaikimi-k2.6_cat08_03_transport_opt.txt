### 1. Process Discovery and Conformance Checking

#### 1.1 Data Preprocessing and Integration into a Cohesive Event Log

To enable meaningful process mining, the heterogeneous data from GPS trackers, handheld scanners, dispatch systems, and maintenance logs must be unified into a single **event log** with a consistent schema: `{Case ID, Activity, Timestamp, Resource, Attributes}`.

**Preprocessing Steps:**

*   **Schema Alignment and Entity Resolution:** All events must be mapped to a common vocabulary. The primary **case notion** will be the `Vehicle-Day` (e.g., `V12-20241205`), as this represents a complete end-to-end operational instance from shift start to shift end. However, a secondary case notion using `Package ID` can be derived for parcel-centric sub-processes (e.g., depot-to-door lifecycle). I will join tables on `Vehicle ID`, `Driver ID`, and `Timestamp` (with appropriate tolerance windows).
*   **GPS Data Abstraction:** Raw GPS pings are too granular. I will transform streaming GPS data into meaningful process activities using spatio-temporal rules:
    *   **Moving:** Speed > threshold (e.g., 10 km/h) and ignition on.
    *   **Idling/Standing:** Speed  0, ignition on, outside geofenced customer locations.
    *   **Traffic Delay:** Low speed (< 10 km/h) or prolonged idling on public roads, matched against map data to distinguish from customer stops.
    *   **Arrive/Depart Depot:** Geofence entry/exit around the depot coordinates.
*   **Geospatial Matching:** Scanner events like `Arrive Customer` provide exact timestamps but may lack precision or be forgotten. I will correlate scanner timestamps with GPS coordinates by geofencing planned customer locations from the dispatch system. This also helps identify unplanned stops (GPS stationary events with no corresponding scanner event).
*   **Dispatch System Enrichment:** Planned routes will be translated into a reference model. Each planned stop receives a sequence number and time window, which are appended to actual events as case attributes (e.g., `Planned_Seq`, `Time_Window_Start`, `Time_Window_End`).
*   **Maintenance Log Integration:** Maintenance events (scheduled/unscheduled) are tagged to the case. Unscheduled repairs during a shift are treated as unplanned activities; scheduled maintenance is used to filter out non-operational days or explain vehicle absences.

**Key Challenges:**
*   **Timestamp Synchronization:** Different systems may have clock skews. I will require cross-system reconciliation using known anchor events (e.g., GPS geofence arrival should closely match scanner `Arrive Customer`).
*   **Granularity Mismatch:** GPS emits frequent pings; scanners emit sparse events. I must avoid over-representing GPS states in the log, instead abstracting them as higher-level activities.
*   **Data Quality:** Missing scanner events (drivers forgetting to scan), GPS multipath errors in urban canyons, and duplicate records require robust outlier detection and imputation rules.
*   **Multi-Package Stops:** One physical stop may involve multiple packages. I must ensure the process model does not artificially inflate service time by treating each package scan as a distinct sequential activity unless required.

#### 1.2 Process Discovery: Visualizing the Actual Delivery Process

I will apply **discovery algorithms** suited for flexible, real-world logistics data:

*   **Inductive Miner** or **Heuristics Miner:** These are preferred over the Alpha algorithm because they handle noise (common in GPS data), infrequent paths (breakdowns), and loops (redeliveries) effectively.
*   **Fuzzy Miner:** Useful for initial exploration to visualize the dense, spaghetti-like nature of urban logistics without enforcing strict formal semantics.

The discovered process model will visualize:
*   **Standard Flow:** `Start Shift`  `Route Assigned`  `Depart Depot`  `Travel`  `Arrive Customer`  [`Delivery Success` | `Delivery Failed`]  `Depart Customer`  ...  `Arrive Depot`  `End Shift`.
*   **Deviations:** Optional paths for `Low Speed Detected` (traffic loops), `Unscheduled Stop` (maintenance, breaks), and `Return to Depot` (mid-route reloads).
*   **Concurrency:** The model will clarify where travel and dispatch planning happen in parallel or where multiple packages at one stop are serviced concurrently.

#### 1.3 Conformance Checking: Actual vs. Planned Routes

Conformance checking involves aligning the discovered event log against a normative process model derived from the **Dispatch System** (the "planned" process). I will use **alignment-based conformance checking** to quantify *fitness* (how much of the reality fits the plan) and *precision* (how much of the plan is reflected in reality).

**Deviations to Identify:**
*   **Sequence Deviations:** Drivers visiting customers in an order different from the optimized route (e.g., skipping a stop and returning later). This appears as misalignments in the event-to-model trace.
*   **Unplanned Stops:** Insertions in the trace not present in the model, such as `Unscheduled Stop` for engine warnings, fuel stops, or unauthorized breaks.
*   **Timing Deviations:** Significant differences between planned and actual timestamps for `Arrive Customer`. Late arrivals violate time windows; early arrivals may cause `Delivery Failed` if the customer is not ready.
*   **Skipped Activities:** Missed deliveries (a planned stop absent from the trace) or missed scans.
*   **Geospatial Deviations:** GPS paths that deviate from the planned road network, indicating drivers taking "shortcuts" or getting lost.

#### 2.1 Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

#### 2.2 Bottleneck Identification Techniques

I will use a combination of process mining techniques to locate and quantify bottlenecks:

*   **Performance Analysis on Discovered Models:** Annotating the process model with **sojourn times** (total time in an activity) and **waiting times** (time between activities). Bottlenecks appear as thick, red-colored arcs or nodes (e.g., prolonged `Travel` between two specific stops).
*   **Dotted Chart Analysis:** Plotting events on a timeline (x-axis = time, y-axis = case/driver). This visualizes resource contention, revealing if multiple vehicles are stuck in the same area simultaneously or if specific drivers consistently finish later.
*   **Variant Analysis:** Comparing process variants. A "variant" is a unique path through the process. I will contrast:
    *   **Fast vs. Slow Traces:** Identifying what activity sequence or resource distinguishes a 6-hour route from a 10-hour route.
    *   **On-Time vs. Late Deliveries:** Checking if late deliveries correlate with specific route sequences or times.
*   **Resource Analysis (Social Network Mining):** Analyzing handovers or comparing driver-specific performance to identify behavioral bottlenecks.

**Dimensions of Bottlenecks:**
*   **Temporal:** Specific times of day (08:00–09:30 AM rush hour) showing systemic delays across many traces.
*   **Spatial (Traffic Hotspots):** Clusters of `Low Speed Detected` events at specific GPS coordinates (e.g., a particular bridge or school zone).
*   **Activity-Level:** Excessive dwell time at certain customers (e.g., high-rise apartments with slow elevators vs. suburban homes). This is identified by filtering `Arrive Customer`  `Depart Customer` durations > threshold.
*   **Resource-Level:** Specific drivers or vehicles with consistently higher travel times or failure rates, isolated via resource-activity aggregation.
*   **Route-Level:** Specific postal zones or routes with structurally longer cycle times.

**Quantification:** Bottlenecks will be ranked by their **impact on total case duration** using critical path analysis on the process model and by their **cost impact** (e.g., fuel burned during idling × frequency of delay).
