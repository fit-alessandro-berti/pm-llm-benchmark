Looking at this event log, I've identified several significant anomalies that violate standard Procure-to-Pay best practices. Let me walk through each problematic case.

## **Case 1002: Major Control Violations**

**Anomaly 1: Purchase Order issued before requisition approval**
- PO issued at 08:40, but requisition wasn't approved until 08:00 the next day (Feb 3)
- **Why problematic:** This bypasses the authorization control. The PO commits the company to spend money before the purchase has been formally approved.

**Anomaly 2: Payment released before approval, goods receipt, AND invoice matching**
- Payment released at 07:15 on Feb 3
- Requisition not approved until 08:00 that same day
- Goods not received until Feb 4
- Invoice not matched until Feb 5
- **Why problematic:** This is an extreme control failure. The company paid before:
  1. The purchase was authorized
  2. Goods were received
  3. The invoice was validated against the PO
- This creates high risk for fraud, overpayment, and paying for unordered goods.

## **Case 1003: Payment Before Invoice and Goods Receipt**

**Anomaly: Payment released before invoice receipt and goods receipt**
- Payment released at 11:00 on Feb 8
- Invoice not received until Feb 9 (day after payment)
- Goods not received until Feb 10 (two days after payment)
- **Why problematic:** This violates the three-way match principle (PO, goods receipt, invoice). Paying before receiving either the invoice or the goods removes all verification controls and increases risk of payment errors or fraud.

## **Case 1004: Goods Received Before Purchase Order**

**Anomaly: Goods received before PO issued**
- Goods received at 09:00 on Feb 6
- PO issued at 09:15 that same day (15 minutes later)
- **Why problematic:** Receiving goods without a prior PO means:
  1. No prior authorization for the purchase
  2. No agreed-upon price, quantity, or terms
  3. Difficulty reconciling invoice with PO after the fact
  This is a common red flag for "maverick buying" or even potential fraud where goods are ordered outside the proper process.

## **What Makes These Anomalies Significant**

These patterns represent **control breakdowns** in the P2P process:

1. **Segregation of duties violations** - Different people/approvals should control different steps
2. **Financial risk exposure** - Paying without verification increases fraud and error risk
3. **Audit trail issues** - Out-of-sequence activities make process tracking difficult
4. **Compliance risks** - Many companies have policies requiring these specific sequences

The most concerning pattern is **Case 1002**, which shows multiple control failures happening together. This could indicate either serious process issues or potentially intentional circumvention of controls.

**Standard process should follow the sequence:** Create Requisition  Approve Requisition  Issue PO  Receive Goods  Receive Invoice  Match Invoice to PO  Release Payment  Close Case. Cases 1002, 1003, and 1004 all deviate significantly from this flow in ways that create business risk.
