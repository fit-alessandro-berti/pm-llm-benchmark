### Detailed Explanations

#### **Case 2: Sequencing Violation**
*   **Sequence observed:** Receive $\rightarrow$ Check $\rightarrow$ **Package items** $\rightarrow$ **Authorize payment** $\rightarrow$ Ship $\rightarrow$ Close.
*   **Violation:** The normative description states that "Authorize payment" should occur **before** "Package items." Specifically, it notes that "Package items" should only occur after authorization. In this case, the items were prepared for shipment before the payment was confirmed, which contradicts the requirement that payment authorization must happen once inventory sufficiency is known but before the physical preparation of the goods.

#### **Case 3: Logical/Constraint Violation**
*   **Sequence observed:** Receive $\rightarrow$ Check $\rightarrow$ Authorize payment $\rightarrow$ **Cancel order** $\rightarrow$ **Ship order** $\rightarrow$ Close.
*   **Violation:** This case contains two major deviations. First, the description explicitly states that once an order is canceled, "it would be illogical to continue with activities intended for fulfillment (such as packaging or shipping)." Second, it states a hard constraint: "**Ship order** should... never [occur] after a cancellation." By shipping the order after the "Cancel order" activity, this case violates the fundamental safety/logical constraint of the process.

#### **Case 5: Missing Step / Precondition Violation**
*   **Sequence observed:** Receive $\rightarrow$ Check $\rightarrow$ Authorize payment $\rightarrow$ **Ship order** $\rightarrow$ Close.
*   **Violation:** This case skips the "Package items" step. The normative description establishes a strict dependency for shipping, stating: "**Only after** items have been properly packaged is it appropriate to proceed with 'Ship order'." Because the "Package items" activity is missing from the sequence, the requirement that items must be prepared before they are shipped has been violated.
