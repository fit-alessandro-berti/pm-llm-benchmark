# Bias Analysis of the Two Event Logs

## Identifying the Biased Log

**Group B (Unprotected Group)** exhibits clear bias in its hiring process. While both logs follow the same procedural workflow (ApplicationReceived  BasicScreening  SkillTest  PersonalityAssessment  CulturalFitCheck  FinalInterview  HiringDecision), Group B introduces a **+5 "Association Boost"** to the Cultural Fit score for candidates affiliated with the *LocalBusinessLeadersClub*. Group A receives no such adjustment.

---

## How the Bias Manifests

### 1. Differential Score Adjustment
The `ScoreAdjustment` column reveals the mechanism of bias directly:
- **Group A candidates**: ScoreAdjustment = 0 in every case.
- **Group B candidates with LocalBusinessLeadersClub affiliation** (U001, U003): ScoreAdjustment = +5.
- **Group B candidate without affiliation** (U002): ScoreAdjustment = 0.

### 2. Outcome Distortion Ś The Clearest Example (U003)
Case U003 is the most revealing:
- **Raw scores**: SkillScore = 75, PersonalityScore = 65, CulturalFit = **58**
- After the boost: CulturalFit = **63**
- **Decision: Hired**

Compare this to **P003** in Group A:
- SkillScore = 82, PersonalityScore = 68, CulturalFit = 65 (no boost)
- **Decision: Hired**

And compare to **P002** in Group A:
- SkillScore = 78, PersonalityScore = 75, CulturalFit = 60 (no boost)
- **Decision: Not Hired**

U003 has **lower raw scores across every dimension** than P002, yet U003 is hired while P002 is not. The only differentiating factor is the association-based adjustment. Without the +5 boost, U003's raw cultural fit (58) is lower than P002's (60), suggesting U003 would likely not have been hired under Group A's rules.

### 3. Proxy Discrimination
The bias operates through a **proxy variable**. Membership in the *LocalBusinessLeadersClub* is strongly correlated with:
- `LocalResident = TRUE` (all boosted candidates are local residents)
- Social/economic networks accessible primarily to certain community insiders

Thus, while the rule appears "neutral" (rewarding association membership), it effectively privileges local residents and penalizes non-locals who have no access to such clubs.

---

## Implications for Fairness and Equity

### 1. Violation of Equal Treatment
Two candidates with identical merit-based scores receive different outcomes based on a non-job-related attribute (club membership). This directly violates the principle of **procedural fairness**.

### 2. Disparate Impact
Even if the policy doesn't explicitly reference a protected characteristic, its effect falls disproportionately on outsiders (non-local residents in Group A), who structurally cannot join the local club. This is a textbook case of **disparate impact discrimination**.

### 3. Erosion of Merit-Based Selection
Cultural Fit Ś already a subjective metric vulnerable to bias Ś is further distorted. The hiring process begins rewarding **social capital and insider networks** over demonstrable skill (SkillScore) or aptitude (PersonalityScore).

### 4. Opacity and Accountability Risk
The adjustment is logged, which is good for auditability Ś but the existence of such a rule, if not publicly disclosed to applicants, undermines transparency. Candidates in Group A have no way of knowing they are being evaluated on an unequal scale.

### 5. Reinforcement of Social Homogeneity
Systematically favoring members of a single local association will produce a workforce demographically and ideologically aligned with that club, reducing diversity and locking out qualified outside talent over time.

---

## Recommendations

1. **Remove the Association Boost** Ś cultural fit scoring should be standardized across all candidates.
2. **Audit Cultural Fit criteria** for subjectivity; consider replacing with structured, job-relevant behavioral assessments.
3. **Apply fairness-aware process mining** (e.g., comparing hiring rates conditioned on raw scores) to detect such interventions automatically.
4. **Document and justify any score adjustments** against bona fide occupational qualifications; ad hoc boosts tied to affiliations rarely meet this standard.

### Conclusion
**Group B is biased.** The bias is not in the workflow structure but in a hidden scoring rule that privileges candidates based on social affiliation rather than merit, producing unequal outcomes for candidates of comparable or superior qualifications in Group A.
