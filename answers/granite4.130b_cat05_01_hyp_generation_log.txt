**Anomalies & Undesirable Behaviors Detected in the Event Log**

| CaseID | Anomaly / Unusual Pattern | Why It’s a Concern |
|---------|---------------------------|--------------------|
| **1002** | *Validate Stock* (event12) occurs **after** *Perform Credit Check* (event11). The assumed normal flow is **Credit Check  Validate Stock**, but the timestamps show the reverse order. | Suggests a possible reordering of activities, which could hide inventory shortages before credit approval or lead to compliance issues if stock must be confirmed first for certain policies. |
| **1002** | *Perform Credit Check* (event11) happens **after** both *Confirm Shipment* (event9) and *Ship Goods* (event10). In the standard process, credit check should precede any shipmentrelated actions. | Indicates that goods were shipped and confirmed before financial risk was assessed – a highrisk operational deviation (potential fraud, loss exposure). |
| **1003** | *Ship Goods* (event17) appears **before** *Validate Stock* (missing) and **before** *Confirm Shipment* (event19). The “Validate Stock” activity is entirely absent. | Missing a critical inventoryvalidation step can result in shipping items that are out of stock, leading to customer dissatisfaction or chargebacks. |
| **1003** | *Confirm Shipment* (event19) carries `additional_info = 'late_confirmation=Y'`. This flag signals a delayed confirmation relative to the shipment event. | Late confirmations may indicate bottlenecks in communication between warehouse and logistics, affecting downstream fulfillment reporting and SLA compliance. |
| **1004** | *Receive Payment* (event22) occurs **before** *Perform Credit Check*, *Validate Stock*, *Confirm Shipment*, and even before *Issue Invoice* (though invoice follows payment). The order of activities is highly outofsequence: Registration  Payment  Issue Invoice  Confirm Shipment  Ship Goods. | Receiving payment prior to any credit or inventory checks violates typical financial controls; it could be a system error, test data, or an intentional policy breach that exposes the company to unverified revenue. |
| **All Cases** | *Event IDs* are not strictly sequential per `case_id` (e.g., 1002 jumps from 8  9  10  …). While this does not break functionality, nonsequential IDs can complicate audit trails if they stem from parallel processing or datamerge issues. | Nonmonotonic IDs may hide concurrency problems or duplicate event entries that need reconciliation. |

### Hypotheses for the Root Causes

| Anomaly | Possible Explanations |
|---------|-----------------------|
| **Reversed CreditStock order (1002)** | • A workflow engine bug that swapped activity ordering.<br>• Manual override by a user who “fasttracked” shipment and later performed credit check as an afterthefact audit.<br>• Data ingestion error where events were logged out of timestamp order. |
| **Credit Check after Shipment (1002, 1004)** | • Automated escalation: highvalue orders may trigger immediate fulfillment while credit assessment runs asynchronously in the background.<br>• Policy exception for “preauthorized” customers (e.g., VIP) – but no such flag exists in `additional_info`.<br>• System race condition where payment confirmation fired before downstream checks completed. |
| **Missing Validate Stock (1003)** | • The stock validation step was skipped due to a preapproved inventory reservation (perhaps captured elsewhere, not reflected here).<br>• A datacapture failure: the event never reached the log table.<br>• Intentional shortcut for “dropship” orders where external supplier guarantees availability. |
| **Late Confirmation flag (1003)** | • Manual entry delay – logistics manager could only confirm after receiving carrier acknowledgment late in the day.<br>• Integration lag between warehouse execution system and the eventlogging service. |
| **Payment before any checks (1004)** | • Test/seed data where payment is simulated upfront to verify downstream processes.<br>• Business rule for “instantpay” orders (e.g., prepaid invoices) that bypasses credit check, but then still requires shipment confirmation – the log should reflect a special flag (`payment_type=prepaid`) which is missing. |
| **Nonsequential Event IDs** | • Parallel processing of subactivities within the same case generating events out of order.<br>• Batch inserts from ETL jobs that preserve source ordering but not target monotonicity. |

### Suggested SQL Investigations

Below are a set of queries you can run against the PostgreSQL schema to **validate, quantify, and drill into** each anomaly class. Feel free to adapt date ranges, thresholds, or add `JOIN`s as needed.

#### 1. Detect OutofOrder Activity Sequences per Case
```sql
-- Define the canonical order (as a CTE for readability)
WITH ordered_activities AS (
    SELECT
        case_id,
        event_id,
        activity,
        timestamp,
        ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY case_id ORDER BY timestamp) AS seq_pos
    FROM order_event_log
),

-- Map each activity to its expected position in the “normal” flow
expected_positions AS (
    SELECT
        oa.*,
        CASE activity
            WHEN 'Register Order'          THEN 1
            WHEN 'Perform Credit Check'   THEN 2
            WHEN 'Validate Stock'         THEN 3
            WHEN 'Confirm Shipment'       THEN 4
            WHEN 'Ship Goods'             THEN 5
            WHEN 'Issue Invoice'          THEN 6
            WHEN 'Receive Payment'        THEN 7
            ELSE NULL                     -- catch unexpected activities
        END AS expected_pos
    FROM ordered_activities oa
)

-- Find mismatches where actual sequence (seq_pos) does NOT equal the canonical position
SELECT *
FROM expected_positions
WHERE seq_pos <> expected_pos
ORDER BY case_id, seq_pos;
```
*Result*: Rows where an activity appears out of its “normal” slot. Use this to pinpoint cases like 1002 and 1004.

#### 2. List Cases Missing Critical Activities (e.g., Validate Stock)
```sql
SELECT o.case_id
FROM orders o
LEFT JOIN (
    SELECT case_id, activity
    FROM order_event_log
) el ON o.case_id = el.case_id
GROUP BY o.case_id
HAVING COUNT(CASE WHEN el.activity = 'Validate Stock' THEN 1 END) = 0;
```
*Result*: Returns `case_id`s lacking the “Validate Stock” event (e.g., 1003).

#### 3. Identify Cases Where Payment Precedes Credit Check, Stock Validation, and Shipment Confirmation
```sql
WITH payment_events AS (
    SELECT case_id, MIN(timestamp) AS pay_ts
    FROM order_event_log
    WHERE activity = 'Receive Payment'
    GROUP BY case_id
),
credit_events AS (
    SELECT case_id, MAX(timestamp) AS credit_ts
    FROM order_event_log
    WHERE activity = 'Perform Credit Check'
    GROUP BY case_id
),
stock_events AS (
    SELECT case_id, MAX(timestamp) AS stock_ts
    FROM order_event_log
    WHERE activity = 'Validate Stock'
    GROUP BY case_id
),
shipment_events AS (
    SELECT case_id, MIN(timestamp) AS ship_conf_ts
    FROM order_event_log
    WHERE activity IN ('Confirm Shipment','Ship Goods')
    GROUP BY case_id
)
SELECT p.case_id,
       p.pay_ts,
       c.credit_ts,
       s.stock_ts,
       sh.ship_conf_ts
FROM payment_events p
LEFT JOIN credit_events   c ON p.case_id = c.case_id
LEFT JOIN stock_events    s ON p.case_id = s.case_id
LEFT JOIN shipment_events sh ON p.case_id = sh.case_id
WHERE (c.credit_ts IS NULL OR p.pay_ts < c.credit_ts)
  AND (s.stock_ts IS NULL OR p.pay_ts < s.stock_ts)
  AND (sh.ship_conf_ts IS NULL OR p.pay_ts < sh.ship_conf_ts);
```
*Result*: Confirms cases where payment is recorded before any of the three safeguard activities – you’ll see `case_id = 1004`.

#### 4. Highlight Late Confirmation Flags
```sql
SELECT *
FROM order_event_log
WHERE activity = 'Confirm Shipment'
  AND additional_info ILIKE '%late_confirmation%';
```
*Result*: Pulls rows like case1003’s late confirmation for deeper inspection (e.g., join with `resources` to see who logged it).

#### 5. Correlate Anomalies with Order Value & Type
```sql
SELECT o.case_id,
       o.order_value,
       o.order_type,
       ae.*   -- from the outoforder query above
FROM (
    -- reuse the CTE from #1 and filter mismatches
    WITH ordered_activities AS ( ... ),   -- same as in #1
         expected_positions AS ( ... )
    SELECT case_id, activity, seq_pos, expected_pos
    FROM expected_positions
    WHERE seq_pos <> expected_pos
) ae
JOIN orders o ON ae.case_id = o.case_id;
```
*Result*: Lets you see whether highvalue or “priority” orders are more prone to ordering anomalies.

#### 6. Who Performed the OutofOrder Activities? (Join with `resources`)
```sql
WITH out_of_order AS (
    -- reuse CTE from #1 filtered for mismatches
    WITH ordered_activities AS ( ... ),
         expected_positions AS ( ... )
    SELECT case_id, activity, timestamp, event_id
    FROM expected_positions
    WHERE seq_pos <> expected_pos
)
SELECT o.case_id,
       o.activity,
       o.timestamp,
       el.resource,
       r.role,
       r.department
FROM out_of_order o
JOIN order_event_log el ON o.case_id = el.case_id AND o.event_id = el.event_id
LEFT JOIN resources r ON el.resource = r.resource_id;
```
*Result*: Identifies which role/department executed the problematic steps, useful for training or policy review.

#### 7. Distribution of Anomalies Over Time (Potential Systemic Issues)
```sql
SELECT DATE_TRUNC('day', timestamp) AS day,
       COUNT(*) AS anomaly_cnt
FROM (
    -- combine all mismatch detections (outoforder + missing stock + early payment)
    SELECT timestamp FROM (
        -- outoforder (reuse #1 CTE)
        WITH ordered_activities AS ( ... ),
             expected_positions AS ( ... )
        SELECT timestamp FROM expected_positions WHERE seq_pos <> expected_pos

        UNION ALL

        -- missing Validate Stock
        SELECT el.timestamp
        FROM order_event_log el
        JOIN orders o ON el.case_id = o.case_id
        WHERE el.activity <> 'Validate Stock'   -- dummy, we’ll filter later via NOT EXISTS
          AND NOT EXISTS (
              SELECT 1 FROM order_event_log sub
              WHERE sub.case_id = el.case_id
                AND sub.activity = 'Validate Stock'
          )

        UNION ALL

        -- early payment cases (reuse #3 CTE)
        SELECT p.pay_ts
        FROM (
            WITH payment_events AS (...), credit_events AS (...), stock_events AS (...),
                 shipment_events AS (...)
            SELECT p.case_id, p.pay_ts
            FROM payment_events p
            LEFT JOIN credit_events   c ON p.case_id = c.case_id
            LEFT JOIN stock_events    s ON p.case_id = s.case_id
            LEFT JOIN shipment_events sh ON p.case_id = sh.case_id
            WHERE (c.credit_ts IS NULL OR p.pay_ts < c.credit_ts)
              AND (s.stock_ts IS NULL OR p.pay_ts < s.stock_ts)
              AND (sh.ship_conf_ts IS NULL OR p.pay_ts < sh.ship_conf_ts)
        ) sub
    ) all_anoms
) a
GROUP BY day
ORDER BY day;
```
*Result*: A timeseries view that can reveal spikes (e.g., after a system patch or during peak load) suggesting systemic root causes.

### Next Steps for You

1. **Run the queries** above in your PostgreSQL environment to materialize the anomaly lists.
2. **Crossreference** the offending `case_id`s with business policies:
   - Are there documented exceptions (e.g., “preauthorized VIP orders”) that could justify early payment or skipped stock validation?
   - Do any internal alerts or compensating controls exist for outoforder activities?
3. **Investigate the source systems**:
   - Check ETL logs or application event queues for missing/late messages (especially for case1003’s absent “Validate Stock”).
   - Review audit trails of who executed the outoforder events (using the `resources` join query) to see if a particular role needs additional training.
4. **Implement preventive measures**:
   - Add **foreignkey constraints** or **business rule checks** in the workflow engine that enforce activity ordering before persisting to `order_event_log`.
   - Introduce **realtime validation alerts** (e.g., via PostgreSQL triggers) that fire when an event violates expected sequence.
   - Enforce **mandatory flags** (`payment_type`, `exception_reason`) in `additional_info` for any deviation from the canonical flow.

By systematically querying, correlating, and acting on these anomalies, you’ll reduce risk, improve orderprocessing reliability, and gain clearer visibility into operational exceptions.
