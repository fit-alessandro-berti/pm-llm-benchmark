# Queue Mining for Outpatient Clinic Optimization: A Comprehensive Analytical Approach

## 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

### Defining and Calculating Waiting Time

In this event log, each activity has both a **START** and **COMPLETE** timestamp. This enables a clean separation between two fundamentally different time components:

- **Service Time (Processing Time):** `COMPLETE(activity_i)  START(activity_i)` — the time during which the patient is actively being served.
- **Waiting Time (Queue Time):** `START(activity_i+1)  COMPLETE(activity_i)` — the idle time a patient spends *between* the completion of one activity and the start of the next.

**Waiting time is the core target.** It is the unproductive interval where the patient sits in a waiting room contributing nothing to care delivery but accumulating dissatisfaction.

To compute this rigorously, I would:

1. **Sort each Case ID's events chronologically** and reconstruct the per-case activity sequence (the trace).
2. For each consecutive activity pair (A  B) within a case, compute:
   - `Wait(AB) = START(B)  COMPLETE(A)`
3. **Handle concurrency carefully.** Some activities may legitimately overlap (e.g., results processing while patient waits). If `START(B) < COMPLETE(A)`, activities are parallel and the "wait" is not a true queue. The start/complete distinction makes this detectable — a critical advantage over logs with only completion timestamps.
4. **Tag each waiting interval** with contextual attributes inherited from the surrounding events: Patient Type, Urgency, Resource, time-of-day, day-of-week, and specialty.

A subtle but important point: not all waiting is "queue waiting." I would distinguish:
- **Resource-contention waits** (waiting for a busy nurse/doctor) — true queues.
- **Synchronization/coordination waits** (waiting for a test result before consultation) — dependency-driven delays.
- **Administrative waits** (paperwork, handover lag).

### Key Characterization Metrics

For each transition (queue point) such as *Registration  Nurse Assessment*, *Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation*, *Consultation  Diagnostic Test*, etc., I would compute:

| Metric | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Mean waiting time** | Overall magnitude, but sensitive to outliers |
| **Median (P50)** | Robust central tendency — the "typical" patient experience |
| **P90 / P95 waiting time** | Tail behavior — captures the patients suffering worst delays (key satisfaction driver) |
| **Maximum waiting time** | Worst-case detection / data quality check |
| **Standard deviation / IQR** | Variability — high variance signals unpredictable, hard-to-manage queues |
| **Queue frequency** | How often this transition occurs across all cases |
| **% cases exceeding threshold** | E.g., % of patients waiting > 30 min (defines a "service-level violation") |
| **Total aggregate wait (Mean × Frequency)** | The total system-wide burden of a queue |
| **Time-segmented metrics** | Wait by hour-of-day and day-of-week to reveal temporal patterns |

### Identifying the *Most Critical* Queues

I would **not** rely on average waiting time alone, because a queue with a moderate average but huge volume can dominate total patient suffering. My prioritization uses a **multi-criteria scoring** approach:

1. **Aggregate impact** = Average wait × Frequency. A queue affecting 8,000 patients with a 15-min average wait causes more total harm (2,000 patient-hours) than one affecting 200 patients with a 40-min wait.
2. **Tail severity (P90/P95)** — queues where a meaningful fraction of patients wait egregiously long (these drive complaints disproportionately).
3. **Patient-type weighting** — delays affecting urgent patients or vulnerable groups carry clinical risk and warrant higher priority.
4. **Bottleneck centrality** — queues that, once relieved, unblock downstream flow.

I would visualize this as a **Pareto chart** (queues ranked by aggregate wait) combined with a **bubble plot** (X = frequency, Y = average wait, bubble size = P95). Queues in the upper-right with large bubbles are the immediate priorities. Typically in such clinics, the **Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation** transition emerges as the dominant critical queue.

## 2. Root Cause Analysis

Locating *where* queues occur is descriptive; explaining *why* is diagnostic. I would pursue the following lines of investigation:

### A. Resource Bottlenecks
Using the **Resource** attribute, I would compute **resource utilization** = (sum of service times per resource) / (resource available time). 
- If doctors in Cardiology show 95%+ utilization while clerks show 50%, the doctor pool is the binding constraint feeding the pre-consultation queue.
- **Resource performance analysis** can reveal that certain staff have systematically longer service times (training need, case-mix differences).
- A **resource-activity matrix** (handover network / social network mining) reveals whether handovers between specific staff pairs incur delays.

### B. Activity Dependencies and Handovers
Queue time between *Consultation  Diagnostic Test* may stem from **synchronization delays** — patients waiting for results, equipment cleaning, or order-entry lag. Process discovery (e.g., **inductive miner** producing a process model / Petri net) reveals whether activities that *could* be parallel are being executed sequentially.

### C. Service-Time Variability
High variance in activity durations directly inflates queues (queueing theory: waiting time grows with the *square* of variability, per the Kingman / VUT equation). I would analyze service-time distributions per activity and per resource. Long-tailed consultation durations (complex new patients) create unpredictable downstream backups.

### D. Scheduling Policies and Arrival Patterns
By aligning **scheduled appointment times** (if available) with actual START of Registration, I can detect:
- **Patient arrival patterns** — bunching at 9 AM or post-lunch creating arrival spikes.
- **Block scheduling artifacts** — many patients booked at the top of the hour.
- A **mismatch between arrival rate and service capacity** by time-of-day is a classic queue driver.

### E. Patient Type / Urgency Differentiation — Variant Analysis
**Variant analysis** is powerful here. I would split the log by Patient Type (New vs. Follow-up) and Urgency, then compare:
- Path structures (New patients may require more steps).
- Waiting times per segment.
If New patients consume far longer consultation time but are scheduled in the same slots as Follow-ups, this creates cascading delays. **Performance-annotated process maps** filtered by segment expose these differences.

### Techniques Summary
- **Bottleneck analysis** (performance-annotated process discovery)  visualizes where time accumulates.
- **Resource/organizational mining**  utilization and handover inefficiencies.
- **Variant analysis**  segment-specific root causes.
- **Conformance checking**  deviations from intended flow that create rework loops.
- **Correlation analysis**  linking long waits to time-of-day, resource, patient type.

## 3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

### Strategy 1: Demand-Matched Dynamic Staffing for the NurseDoctor Queue

- **Target queue:** Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation (the dominant critical queue).
- **Root cause addressed:** Doctor capacity bottleneck during peak arrival hours; mismatch between time-varying demand and fixed staffing.
- **Data support:** Time-segmented utilization analysis showing doctor utilization spiking to >95% between 9–11 AM (with P90 waits of, say, 45 min) while dropping to ~60% in the afternoon. Arrival-pattern analysis confirms morning bunching.
- **Action:** Reallocate physician/locum coverage and stagger physician start times to add capacity in the morning peak; shift some elective follow-ups to under-utilized afternoon slots (load leveling).
- **Expected impact:** Smoothing arrivals and adding peak capacity should, per queueing dynamics, reduce average pre-consultation wait substantially — a realistic target of **25–40% reduction** in mean wait at this transition, with even larger P90 improvements since tail waits are most sensitive to utilization near 100%.

### Strategy 2: Parallelization of Diagnostics via Standing Orders / Anticipatory Testing

- **Target queue:** Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Test  Specialist Review (sequential dependency chain).
- **Root cause addressed:** Sequential dependency where tests (ECG, blood work) are only ordered *after* consultation, forcing a second wait.
- **Data support:** Variant analysis shows that for predictable specialties (e.g., Cardiology new patients), an ECG is ordered in 85% of cases. The ConsultationECG queue + ECG service + ECGre-consultation creates a long serial path.
- **Action:** Implement **protocol-based standing orders** so high-probability tests are performed *before or in parallel with* nurse assessment for defined patient categories. Restructure flow so results are ready at consultation.
- **Expected impact:** Removes a full queue-service-queue cycle from the critical path; collapses two consultation visits into one. Realistic **15–30% reduction in total visit duration** for affected patient segments. (Trade-off: risk of unnecessary tests — mitigated by tight protocol criteria.)

### Strategy 3: Segmented Scheduling & Fast-Track Lanes (New vs. Follow-up / Urgent)

- **Target queue:** Multiple transitions, especially Registration  Nurse Assessment and pre-consultation queues.
- **Root cause addressed:** Service-time variability from mixing long-duration New patients with short Follow-ups in undifferentiated queues; urgent patients delayed behind routine ones.
- **Data support:** Service-time distributions showing New-patient consultations averaging, e.g., 25 min (high variance) vs. 12 min for Follow-ups. Mixing inflates waits via variability effects.
- **Action:** Create **dedicated scheduling blocks/lanes** — a fast-track lane for short Follow-ups and a separate track for complex New patients, plus a priority queue discipline for Urgent cases. Allocate slot lengths based on empirically measured per-segment service times rather than a uniform slot.
- **Expected impact:** Reduces variability within each lane, smoothing flow. Follow-up patients (often the volume majority) see dramatically reduced waits; **20–35% reduction in average wait** for Follow-ups, improved urgent-care responsiveness, and better overall predictability.

### (Optional) Strategy 4: Real-Time Coordination Dashboard
A digital flow-board fed by the same event-log pipeline, alerting staff when a patient's wait exceeds threshold and surfacing room/staff availability — addressing administrative and handover delays through better visibility.

## 4. Trade-offs and Constraints

Every intervention risks unintended consequences:

- **Bottleneck migration:** Adding doctor capacity (Strategy 1) may shift the constraint downstream to diagnostics or check-out. *Mitigation:* model the whole flow, not isolated stages; monitor downstream queues post-intervention.
- **Cost vs. wait-time tension:** Added staffing (Strategy 1) increases labor cost. I would frame this as **cost per patient-hour-of-waiting saved** and prioritize load-leveling (cost-neutral) before capacity additions.
- **Care-quality risk:** Standing orders (Strategy 2) and fast-track lanes risk over-testing or rushing complex cases. *Mitigation:* strict clinical protocols, physician override, and monitoring of clinical-quality indicators alongside efficiency metrics.
- **Staff workload and morale:** Demand-matched staffing can intensify peak-hour pressure. Schedule changes should involve clinical staff and respect labor constraints.
- **Reduced scheduling flexibility:** Segmented lanes can create stranded capacity if one segment's demand is low on a given day. *Mitigation:* allow controlled cross-flow / flexible slots.

**Balancing conflicting objectives:** I would adopt a **multi-objective framework**, defining acceptable bounds (e.g., "reduce P90 wait by 30% *without* increasing cost per visit >5% and without degrading clinical-quality KPIs"). Where conflicts arise, I'd use **discrete-event simulation** seeded with the real distributions mined from the log to test scenarios *before* implementation — finding Pareto-efficient configurations rather than guessing.

## 5. Measuring Success

### Key Performance Indicators

**Primary (wait/flow) KPIs:**
- Average and **P90 waiting time** per critical transition (the headline measures).
- **Total visit duration** (Registration START  Check-out COMPLETE), mean and P90.
- **% of patients exceeding wait thresholds** (service-level compliance).
- **Flow-time efficiency** = Total service time / Total visit duration (proportion of "value-adding" time).

**Resource KPIs:**
- Resource utilization (target a healthy 75–85% band — high enough for efficiency, low enough to avoid queue explosion).
- Workload balance across staff.

**Quality / Experience KPIs (guardrails):**
- Patient satisfaction scores, complaint rates.
- Clinical-quality indicators (revisit rates, diagnostic appropriateness) to ensure speed isn't harming care.

**Cost KPIs:**
- Cost per visit; overtime hours.

### Ongoing Monitoring

The same event-log structure enables a **continuous process-monitoring loop**:

1. **Automated ETL pipeline** ingesting new start/complete events continuously into the queue-mining engine.
2. **Live dashboards** displaying KPI trends with **control charts** (statistical process control) to distinguish genuine improvement from normal variation and detect drift.
3. **Before/after cohort comparison** and ideally a **staggered/pilot rollout** so improvements can be attributed causally rather than confounded by seasonality.
4. **Periodic re-mining** (monthly) to detect **bottleneck migration** and confirm gains are sustained, not transient.
5. **Alerting** when any KPI breaches threshold, triggering a renewed root-cause cycle.

This establishes a **closed-loop, data-driven continuous-improvement system**: mine  diagnose  intervene  measure  re-mine — ensuring the clinic doesn't just fix today's queues but builds the capability to manage flow proactively over time.

---

**Summary:** By exploiting the start/complete timestamp structure to cleanly separate service from waiting time, prioritizing queues by aggregate and tail impact, diagnosing root causes through resource/variant/bottleneck mining, and deploying targeted, simulation-validated interventions with explicit trade-off management and continuous KPI monitoring, the clinic can systematically reduce patient waiting and visit duration while protecting care quality and controlling cost.
