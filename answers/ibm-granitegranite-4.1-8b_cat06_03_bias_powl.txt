   In a strict XOR, **exactly one** of the two children must be executed; the choice is typically determined by implicit conditions (e.g., a rule that “if the applicant lives locally  take D; otherwise  skip”).

   ```
   if applicant_is_local()  take branch D

However, the rule **systematically rewards applicants who belong to local groups** with the *subtle* uplift: the “score uplift” mentioned in the comment.

3. **How the bias is introduced**  

   | Factor | Effect on the decision process |
   |--------|--------------------------------|
   | **Selection based on locality** | Applicants from communities that are more likely to be “wellconnected” or “visible” locally (often those already economically advantaged) are more frequently routed to D. |
   | **Incremental score boost** | The boost is usually small enough to avoid obvious discrimination but large enough—when compounded over many applications—to shift aggregate approval rates. |
   | **Datadriven filtering** | If historical data used to train the localaffiliation predictor contain selection bias (e.g., local residents have richer documentation), the predictor inherits that bias, further skewing outcomes. |
   | **Transparency gap** | The XOR operator hides the decision logic: downstream auditors may see “D vs. skip” without knowing the underlying rule, making it harder to detect or rectify the bias. |

4. **Implications for fairness and equity**
