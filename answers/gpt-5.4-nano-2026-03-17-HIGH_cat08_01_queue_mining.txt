## 1) Queue Identification and Characterization

### 1.1 Using the event log to compute queue (waiting) times
Given that the log contains **START** and **COMPLETE** timestamps for each activity within a **Visit (Case ID)**, queue mining typically treats each waiting gap as the time between:

- **Completion of a ōprecedingö activity** in the same case, and  
- **Start of the ōnextö activity** in the observed flow.

**Core queue time definition (case-level, edge-based):**  
For a visit case *c*, suppose the process performs activity \(A\) and then (next in the trace) activity \(B\). If:
- \(t^{complete}(A)\) = completion timestamp of \(A\)
- \(t^{start}(B)\) = start timestamp of \(B\)

Then the **queue/waiting time on the edge \(A \rightarrow B\)** is:
\[
W_{c}(A,B)=t^{start}_{c}(B)-t^{complete}_{c}(A)
\]

This is the most common interpretation in queue mining because it captures ōtime spent waitingö during the transition between stages.

**Edge cases / important refinements**
- **Initial queue before the first service:** If the first recorded activity is not ōarrival,ö define waiting from an available **arrival proxy** (e.g., Registration START) to first clinical START. If you only have timestamps for service activities, you can still compute waiting between clinical stages, and treat the first stage as the reference point.
- **Overlaps / parallelism:** If your clinic allows overlapping activities (e.g., patient moves to ECG while another admin task runs), ōnext activity in traceö may not equal causal dependency. In that case, use:
  - **Ordering by time** plus **activity dependency rules** (based on the most frequent BPMN/DFG order), or
  - **Causal ōpredecessorö mapping** inferred from the log (e.g., ōB is normally preceded by A for this patient type/urgencyö).
- **Negative/zero waits:** Clean data and handle cases where timestamps are inconsistent (clock issues, missing events).

### 1.2 Resource-specific waiting (often more actionable)
Because your log includes **Resource (staff/room/equipment)**, compute **resource queueing** too.

For the same **resource** (e.g., a specific nurse/doctor/room/ECG machine) executing activity \(B\) multiple times:
- Let \(t^{complete}_r(prev)\) be the completion time of the resourceÆs previous job,
- and \(t^{start}_r(curr)\) be the start time of its current job.

Then:
\[
W_{r}(B)=t^{start}_r(curr)-t^{complete}_r(prev)
\]

This tells you whether waits are caused by:
- **Capacity constraints** (resource is ōbusyö), or
- **Downstream issues** (resource sits idle because patients arenÆt ready).

### 1.3 Key queue metrics to characterize bottlenecks
For each edge \(A \rightarrow B\) (and optionally per activity/resource/patient group), compute:

**Time-based distribution**
- **Average waiting time**
- **Median waiting time**
- **Max waiting time**
- **90th/95th percentile waiting time** (clinically more meaningful than averages for patient experience)
- **Waiting time share** of the total visit duration:
  \[
  \text{Contribution}(A,B)=\frac{\sum_c W_c(A,B)}{\sum_c \text{Total Lead Time}(c)}
  \]

**Frequency / burden**
- **Number of occurrences** of the queue edge
- **% of cases** where waiting exceeds thresholds (e.g., >15 min, >30 min)
- **Queue ōinstabilityö by time of day** (heatmap): does waiting spike at lunch, shift change, etc.?

**Stratification dimensions**
- **Patient Type** (New vs Follow-up)
- **Urgency** (Urgent vs Normal)
- **Specialty** (Cardio vs others; if reflected in activity names)
- **Resource/room/equipment**
- **Day-of-week / hour-of-day / month** (to detect staffing or demand effects)

### 1.4 Identifying the *most critical* queues
A practical prioritization is to rank queues using a combination of:

1. **High impact on total time**
   - Highest contribution share to overall lead time.

2. **Patient-experience criticality**
   - Highest 90th/95th percentile waiting time (outliers drive complaints).

3. **High frequency**
   - Even ōmoderateö waits with high occurrence can dominate overall dissatisfaction.

4. **High severity for vulnerable segments**
   - If urgent patients experience disproportionately high delays (e.g., nursedoctor), itÆs usually a priority regardless of average.

5. **System bottleneck signal**
   - If case-level waits coincide with **resource-level busy time** (utilization high and resource-queue waits high), that edge is likely capacity-constrained.

**Justification approach (data-driven):**
- Rank by an ōoverall bottleneck score,ö e.g.:
  \[
  Score(A,B)= \alpha \cdot P90(W(A,B)) + \beta \cdot \#occurrences + \gamma \cdot \text{Contribution}(A,B)
  \]
  with additional penalties/boosts for urgent/new patient groups.

## 2) Root Cause Analysis (why the critical queues happen)

Once you know the top queues (edges), the next step is to determine whether delays are mainly due to **capacity**, **variability**, **handover/coordination**, or **demand/scheduling patterns**.

### 2.1 Resource bottlenecks and capacity constraints
For each critical edge \(A \rightarrow B\):
- Compare:
  - **Case-level wait \(W(A,B)\)** distribution
  - **Resource-level queue** for the resource executing \(B\)
  - **Utilization** of resources that can perform \(B\)
  - **Service time** of \(A\) and \(B\)

**Interpretation patterns**
- **High wait + high utilization + high resource queueing**  likely capacity shortage (too few nurses/rooms/techs).
- **High wait + low utilization**  likely patient readiness, information/order delays, ordering policy, or upstream workflow issues.

### 2.2 Activity duration variability (service-time causes)
Even with adequate capacity, high variance causes queues.

Use the log to compute for each activity:
- Mean and **variance** of service time (complete-start duration)
- Coefficient of variation (CV)
- Compare variants and patient groups

If the waiting happens mainly after an activity with **high service-time variance** (e.g., doctor documentation, specialist review), then variabilityŚrather than raw capacityŚis a root cause.

### 2.3 Handovers, dependencies, and ōmissing prerequisitesö
Queue mining often reveals **specific handover gaps**, such as:
- Doctor consultation complete  test start (ECG/X-ray) wait
- Test complete  specialist review start wait
- Registration complete  nurse assessment start wait

Root-cause hypotheses you can test:
- Are prerequisites (orders, results availability) delayed?
- Are patients physically transported late?
- Are results posted only after batch runs?

**Using the log to test dependency gaps**
- Determine whether the wait correlates with:
  - Specialty transitions
  - Resource ōtype changesö (room changes)
  - Specific combinations of preceding and next activities
- Look for systematic delays for certain providers/resources.

### 2.4 Appointment scheduling policies and arrival patterns
Split waiting by:
- **Time-of-day / day-of-week**
- **Urgency level**
- **Patient type (new vs follow-up)**
- **Demand surges** (arrivals per hour/day inferred from first activity starts)

If waiting spikes when arrivals increase, you likely have a **capacity vs demand mismatch** (queueing effect). If waits are stable across demand levels, it may be more coordination/dependency-driven.

### 2.5 Variant analysis: ōwhy some visits suffer delaysö
Use process mining to extract **variants** (different observed paths).
- Identify variants where critical queues are frequent and long.
- Compare which patient types or specialties follow those variants.
- Check if delays are tied to:
  - additional tests
  - extra consultations
  - resource switches
  - rework loops (e.g., test ordered  canceled  reordered)

This helps separate systemic bottlenecks from ōexception paths.ö

### 2.6 Quantifying root causes (recommended analytical playbook)
For each critical queue edge \(A \rightarrow B\), compute and report:

1. **Edge wait**: \(W(A,B)\) percentiles
2. **Resource queue** at \(B\)Æs executor(s)
3. **Service time** of \(A\) and \(B\)
4. **Utilization** of \(B\) resources (idle vs busy time)
5. **Arrival rate** trend leading into \(A \rightarrow B\) at times of day
6. **Variant concentration**: what % of all worst waits are driven by top variants?

This creates an evidence-based causal narrative (e.g., ōCardio urgent patients wait 35¢50 min primarily due to ECG-room capacity; resource utilization ~95% and ECG tech queue waits are high; variance in doctor order completion also amplifies delaysö).

## 3) Data-driven Optimization Strategies (at least three)

Below are **four** concrete strategies commonly feasible in outpatient clinics. Each is tied to queue edges and measurable mechanisms.

> Note: ōexpected reductionö should be estimated using your historical logs via **what-if analysis** (replay) or **discrete-event simulation**. I include example targets as ranges; your team should replace them with estimates derived from the data.

---

### Strategy 1: Capacity leveling and resource reallocation for the highest-impact clinical bottleneck
**Targets**
- The critical queue edge(s) with highest patient-impact, e.g.:
  - **Registration  Nurse Assessment**
  - **Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation**
  - **Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic tests**
  - **Diagnostic  Specialist Review**

**Likely root cause**
- Capacity constraints: too few nurses/rooms/techs/doctors at peak times, causing high resource queueing.

**How data supports it**
- High case waiting on \(A \rightarrow B\)
- AND high utilization / resource queue waits for resources executing \(B\)
- AND waiting spikes by hour/day where demand peaks

**What to change**
- Adjust staffing schedules (add 1¢2 capacity units during peak blocks)
- Pool cross-cover: allow certain staff to perform multiple related tasks
- Protect the bottleneck resource (reduce non-value tasks during peak)

**Potential positive impacts**
- Reduce **90th percentile waiting** significantly (queues primarily hurt tail latencies)
- Example target (to validate with analysis):
  - **P90 wait** reduction for the bottleneck edge: **20¢40%**
  - Overall visit duration reduction: **5¢15%** depending on how dominant the edge is in the lead time contribution

---

### Strategy 2: Pre-emptive diagnostics scheduling / ōtest slot reservationö to eliminate handover idle time
**Targets**
- Edges like:
  - **Doctor Consultation  ECG Test start**
  - **Doctor Consultation  Blood test/X-ray start**
  - **Test complete  Result review start**

**Likely root cause**
- Coordination/triggering delays: tests are ordered at consultation completion, but the test ōqueueö begins only after orders and transport occur; diagnostic slots are not ready when the decision is made.

**How data supports it**
- Long waits concentrated on doctortest edges
- Resource-level queues at diagnostic rooms/techs may show:
  - either high utilization (slot shortage) or
  - idle resources while patients arenÆt ready (triggering/order delay)

**What to change**
- Use workflow rules:
  - When doctor finishes and orders tests, the patient gets an **immediate reserved slot** (e.g., next available ECG time within X minutes)
  - If urgency is high, reserve priority slots
- Implement ōstanding ordersö logic for defined pathways (e.g., certain symptoms  ECG immediately after nurse assessment)
- Physically/operationally reduce transport gaps with escort/wayfinding and direct booking

**Potential positive impacts**
- Reduction of handover gap waiting:
  - **Average wait** improvement for doctordiagnostics edge: **15¢30%**
  - Tail reduction (P90): often improves more than average if the current process causes sporadic missed slots.

---

### Strategy 3: Parallelize and/or fast-track selected patient pathways (reduce sequential waiting)
**Targets**
- Queues between early stages, especially where thereÆs an artificial sequential dependency:
  - **Registration  Nurse Assessment**
  - **Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation**
- Also delays caused by waiting for ōthe right momentö to start next steps.

**Likely root cause**
- Unnecessary serial process structure and/or rigid handover.
- For some patient types, registration completion may not be a true prerequisite for nurse assessment.

**How data supports it**
- Variant analysis shows:
  - for a subset of patients (e.g., Follow-up, Normal urgency), the process often waits long even though service times are short
- Waiting is large even when resources seem available  indicates coordination logic, not capacity.

**What to change (examples)**
- Start nurse assessment **before registration completion** for eligible cases (with partial intake data)
- Implement ōtwo-laneö routing:
  - **Fast track** for follow-ups/normal patients with predictable care bundles
  - Standard lane for complex cases
- Allow parallel tasks:
  - Nurse interviews while clerk finalizes paperwork
  - Pre-fill forms in the EHR based on previous visits

**Potential positive impacts**
- Reduced overall lead time by cutting sequential waits:
  - Could reduce visit duration by **5¢20%** for the targeted pathways
- Improved satisfaction by reducing early-stage uncertainty.

---

### Strategy 4: Reduce service-time variability and rework in bottleneck activities (make waits shorter even at same capacity)
**Targets**
- Queues following activities with high variance, e.g.:
  - **Doctor Consultation startcomplete** variance leading to downstream waits
  - **Test ordering/result turnaround** edges

**Likely root cause**
- High variability or rework loops:
  - documentation delays,
  - missing information requiring repeats,
  - batch result publication delays.

**How data supports it**
- After activity \(A\) (or before \(B\)), waits are highest AND service time \(A\) shows high variance for the same patient groups/variants.

**What to change**
- Standardize documentation templates and clinical order sets by specialty
- Automate data capture to reduce manual turnaround variability
- Review top rework variants (e.g., test ordered then repeated) and tighten prerequisites
- If diagnostics results release is batch-based, consider more frequent release to shorten ōtest complete  review startö waits.

**Potential positive impacts**
- Lower P90/P95 waits without adding staff
- Often cheaper than staffing expansions but requires operational/process redesign.

## 4) Trade-offs and Constraints

### Common trade-offs
1. **Shifting bottlenecks**
   - Adding capacity at edge \(A \rightarrow B\) may move the longest queues to the next step \(B \rightarrow C\).
   - Mitigation: evaluate the **end-to-end lead time contribution** and simulate the process after changes.

2. **Cost vs throughput**
   - Staffing additions reduce waits but raise labor costs/overtime.
   - Mitigation: target only the times/variants where utilization/wait metrics justify the spend (peak-hour, high-impact specialty/urgency).

3. **Staff workload and quality**
   - Fast-tracking or parallelization can stress workflows and increase the risk of missing details.
   - Mitigation: build clinical safety checks into eligibility rules and monitor quality proxies (error rates, repeats, cancellations).

4. **Implementation risk**
   - Diagnostic scheduling changes require coordination with lab/radiology systems and EHR integration.
   - Mitigation: start with a pilot on one specialty and one high-impact diagnostic type.

### Balancing objectives (wait time vs cost vs quality)
Use a constrained optimization mindset:
- Goal: minimize **patient lead time and queue-tail** while keeping:
  - staff overtime within bounds
  - repeat/cancellation/rework below a threshold
- Practically:
  - Rank changes by expected impact on **P90 waiting** (patient-critical) per unit cost
  - Run **pilot A/B** at limited scope, then scale.

## 5) Measuring Success (KPIs + ongoing monitoring)

### 5.1 Before/after KPIs (per critical queue edge and end-to-end)
**Queue KPIs**
- **P90 waiting time** for each prioritized edge \(A \rightarrow B\)
- **% of cases exceeding SLA thresholds** (e.g., wait >15/30 minutes)
- **Median waiting** (useful for general experience)
- **Queue frequency** (how often the edge occurs and how often it is ōbadö)

**End-to-end KPIs**
- **Total visit lead time** (from first service start to final check-out completion, or earliest-to-latest completion proxy)
- **Throughput**: patients/day (or patients/operating-hour)
- **% of visits meeting overall duration target**

**Operational KPIs (to protect cost and quality)**
- **Resource utilization** of bottleneck resources (avoid hidden overwork/underutilization)
- **Overtime rates**
- **Cancellation/repeat rates** for diagnostics
- Clinical quality proxy metrics (depending on what you have): e.g., time-to-treatment, documentation completeness, rework loops

### 5.2 Ongoing process monitoring with the same event log
After deployment, set up continuous monitoring using the same log structure:

- **Refresh cadence**: daily/weekly batch, plus near-real-time if timestamps are available.
- **Control charts / alerting**
  - Track P90 waiting per edge and overall lead time
  - Trigger alerts when waits drift beyond statistically expected bounds.
- **Conformance and drift detection**
  - Ensure the process still largely follows intended pathways (process mining conformance).
  - Detect if new variants emerge that reintroduce queues.
- **Segment-level monitoring**
  - Monitor by patient type and urgency to ensure improvements donÆt just help ōeasierö segments.

### 5.3 KPI reporting that leadership can act on
For each pilot change, report a compact ōimpact tableö:
- queue edge(s) targeted
- baseline vs post-change:
  - P90 wait, avg wait, % cases breaching
- end-to-end lead time impact
- staff utilization/cost deltas
- quality/rework indicators
