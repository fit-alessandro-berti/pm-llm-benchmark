## Anomalies in the temporal profile

The strongest timing anomalies are:

| Activity pair                     |                                    Observed profile | Why it looks unusual                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        |
| --------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------: | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **R → P**                         |    Avg. 90,000 sec, about 25 hours; STDEV 3,600 sec | Approval appears to happen almost exactly one day after receipt. The low variation is suspicious for a process that should depend on claim complexity, adjuster availability, claim amount, and evaluation work.                                            |
| **P → N**                         | Avg. 604,800 sec, 7 days; STDEV 172,800 sec, 2 days | Notification after approval is extremely delayed and highly variable. In a normal claim flow, customer notification should usually happen shortly after approval.                                                                                           |
| **A → C**                         |                             Avg. 7,200 sec, 2 hours | Closing shortly after assignment may indicate premature closure, duplicate-claim handling, withdrawals, denials, or skipped evaluation/approval steps.                                                                                                      |
| **E → N**                         |               Avg. 300 sec, 5 minutes; STDEV 60 sec | Notification almost immediately after evaluation may indicate automation, skipped approval, incorrect event ordering, or that approval is occurring outside the logged system.                                                                              |
| **E → N vs. P → N inconsistency** |             E → N is 5 minutes, but P → N is 7 days | If the intended process is `E → P → N`, then notification should not generally occur minutes after evaluation while also occurring days after approval. This suggests variant flows, timestamp problems, missing approvals, or incorrect activity labeling. |
| **N → C**                         |           Avg. 1,800 sec, 30 minutes; STDEV 300 sec | This may be benign if closure is automatically triggered after notification, but the low variation suggests a scheduled or automated close mechanism.                                                                                                       |

## Possible explanations

### 1. Batch or scheduled approval behavior

The **R → P** timing may indicate that approvals are not recorded when they actually happen, but instead during a scheduled job, daily batch import, or end-of-day synchronization. A fixed SLA target may also cause approvals to cluster around a 24- or 25-hour threshold.

### 2. Customer notification backlog

The **P → N** delay suggests that approved claims may wait in a notification queue. Possible causes include manual letter generation, dependency on external communication systems, missing customer contact data, region-specific approval handling, or insufficient staff responsible for notifications.

### 3. Premature or administrative claim closure

The **A → C** pattern could represent claims closed soon after assignment because they are duplicates, invalid, withdrawn, below a threshold, missing documents, or assigned incorrectly. It becomes more concerning if `E`, `P`, or `N` are absent before closure.

### 4. Automated notification after evaluation

The **E → N** interval may indicate that evaluation automatically triggers a customer notification. That could be legitimate for “request more information” messages, but it conflicts with the intended process if notification should occur only after approval.

### 5. Timestamp or event-ordering issues

The contradictory relationship between **E → N** and **P → N** may indicate that events are timestamped when entered into the system rather than when performed. It may also indicate late approval logging, retroactive updates, or activities being recorded under incorrect labels.

### 6. Resource-specific or region-specific bottlenecks

Long notification delays or premature closures may be concentrated around certain resources, adjusters, claim types, regions, or claim amount bands.

# SQL verification queries

The following PostgreSQL queries assume that the time between two activities should be measured from an activity occurrence to the first later occurrence of the target activity in the same claim.

## 1. Detect claims that deviate from the temporal profile

This query computes the observed interval for each modeled activity pair and flags claims whose interval is more than 3 standard deviations away from the profile average.

```sql
WITH temporal_profile AS (
    SELECT *
    FROM (VALUES
        ('R', 'A', 3600::numeric,   600::numeric),
        ('R', 'E', 86400::numeric,  28800::numeric),
        ('R', 'P', 90000::numeric,  3600::numeric),
        ('A', 'C', 7200::numeric,   3600::numeric),
        ('E', 'N', 300::numeric,    60::numeric),
        ('E', 'C', 3600::numeric,   3000::numeric),
        ('P', 'N', 604800::numeric, 172800::numeric),
        ('N', 'C', 1800::numeric,   300::numeric)
    ) AS t(activity_1, activity_2, avg_sec, stdev_sec)
),
observed_pairs AS (
    SELECT
        p.activity_1,
        p.activity_2,
        e1.claim_id,
        e1.timestamp AS activity_1_time,
        e2.timestamp AS activity_2_time,
        EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (e2.timestamp - e1.timestamp)) AS observed_sec,
        p.avg_sec,
        p.stdev_sec,
        e1.resource AS activity_1_resource,
        e2.resource AS activity_2_resource
    FROM temporal_profile p
    JOIN claim_events e1
        ON e1.activity = p.activity_1
    JOIN LATERAL (
        SELECT e2.*
        FROM claim_events e2
        WHERE e2.claim_id = e1.claim_id
          AND e2.activity = p.activity_2
          AND e2.timestamp > e1.timestamp
        ORDER BY e2.timestamp
        LIMIT 1
    ) e2 ON TRUE
)
SELECT
    op.claim_id,
    c.claim_type,
    c.claim_amount,
    op.activity_1,
    op.activity_2,
    op.activity_1_time,
    op.activity_2_time,
    ROUND(op.observed_sec, 2) AS observed_sec,
    op.avg_sec,
    op.stdev_sec,
    ROUND((op.observed_sec - op.avg_sec) / NULLIF(op.stdev_sec, 0), 2) AS z_score,
    op.activity_1_resource,
    op.activity_2_resource
FROM observed_pairs op
JOIN claims c
    ON c.claim_id = op.claim_id
WHERE ABS((op.observed_sec - op.avg_sec) / NULLIF(op.stdev_sec, 0)) > 3
ORDER BY ABS((op.observed_sec - op.avg_sec) / NULLIF(op.stdev_sec, 0)) DESC;
```

## 2. Compare actual database timing against the given temporal profile

This checks whether the profile values match the current event log.

```sql
WITH modeled_pairs AS (

    FROM (VALUES
        ('R', 'A'),
        ('R', 'E'),
        ('R', 'P'),
        ('A', 'C'),
        ('E', 'N'),

        ORDER BY e2.timestamp
        LIMIT 1
    ) e2 ON TRUE
)

    activity_2,
    COUNT(*) AS pair_count,
    ROUND(AVG(observed_sec), 2) AS avg_sec,
    ROUND(STDDEV_SAMP(observed_sec), 2) AS stdev_sec,
```

## 3. Find claims closed soon after assignment without evaluation or approval

This directly tests the **A → C** anomaly.

```sql
WITH assignment_to_close AS (
    SELECT
        a.claim_id,
        a.timestamp AS assign_time,
        c.timestamp AS close_time,
        EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (c.timestamp - a.timestamp)) AS assign_to_close_sec,
        a.resource AS assign_resource,
        c.resource AS close_resource

    JOIN LATERAL (

        FROM claim_events c
        WHERE c.claim_id = a.claim_id
          AND c.activity = 'C'
          AND c.timestamp > a.timestamp
        ORDER BY c.timestamp
        LIMIT 1
    ) c ON TRUE

)
SELECT
    atc.claim_id,
    cl.claim_type,
    cl.claim_amount,
    atc.assign_time,
    atc.close_time,
    ROUND(atc.assign_to_close_sec / 60.0, 2) AS assign_to_close_minutes,
    atc.assign_resource,
    atc.close_resource,
    NOT EXISTS (
        SELECT 1
        FROM claim_events e
        WHERE e.claim_id = atc.claim_id
          AND e.activity = 'E'
          AND e.timestamp > atc.assign_time
          AND e.timestamp < atc.close_time
    ) AS missing_evaluation_before_close,
    NOT EXISTS (
        SELECT 1
        FROM claim_events p
        WHERE p.claim_id = atc.claim_id
          AND p.activity = 'P'
          AND p.timestamp > atc.assign_time
          AND p.timestamp < atc.close_time
    ) AS missing_approval_before_close,
    NOT EXISTS (
        SELECT 1
        FROM claim_events n
        WHERE n.claim_id = atc.claim_id
          AND n.activity = 'N'
          AND n.timestamp > atc.assign_time
          AND n.timestamp < atc.close_time
    ) AS missing_notification_before_close
FROM assignment_to_close atc
JOIN claims cl
    ON cl.claim_id = atc.claim_id
```

## 8. Identify process-order violations

This checks whether events occur outside the intended sequence `R → A → E → P → N → C`.

```sql
WITH first_events AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'R') AS r_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'A') AS a_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'E') AS e_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'P') AS p_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'N') AS n_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'C') AS c_time
    FROM claim_events
    GROUP BY claim_id
)
SELECT
    fe.claim_id,
    c.claim_type,
    c.claim_amount,
    fe.r_time,
    fe.a_time,
    fe.e_time,
    fe.p_time,
    fe.n_time,
    fe.c_time,
    CASE
        WHEN fe.r_time IS NULL THEN 'Missing Receive'
        WHEN fe.a_time IS NULL THEN 'Missing Assign'
        WHEN fe.e_time IS NULL THEN 'Missing Evaluate'
        WHEN fe.p_time IS NULL THEN 'Missing Approve'
        WHEN fe.n_time IS NULL THEN 'Missing Notify'
        WHEN fe.c_time IS NULL THEN 'Missing Close'
        WHEN fe.a_time < fe.r_time THEN 'Assign before Receive'
        WHEN fe.e_time < fe.a_time THEN 'Evaluate before Assign'
        WHEN fe.p_time < fe.e_time THEN 'Approve before Evaluate'
        WHEN fe.n_time < fe.p_time THEN 'Notify before Approve'
        WHEN fe.c_time < fe.n_time THEN 'Close before Notify'
        ELSE 'OK'
    END AS sequence_issue
FROM first_events fe
JOIN claims c
    ON c.claim_id = fe.claim_id
WHERE
    fe.r_time IS NULL
    OR fe.a_time IS NULL
    OR fe.e_time IS NULL
    OR fe.p_time IS NULL
    OR fe.n_time IS NULL
    OR fe.c_time IS NULL
    OR fe.a_time < fe.r_time
    OR fe.e_time < fe.a_time
    OR fe.p_time < fe.e_time
    OR fe.n_time < fe.p_time
    OR fe.c_time < fe.n_time
ORDER BY fe.claim_id;
```

## 9. Correlate timing anomalies with claim type and claim amount

This query summarizes outlier rates by claim type and amount band.


## 11. Correlate evaluation or notification delays with adjuster specialization and region

```sql
    c.claim_type
HAVING COUNT(*) >= 5
ORDER BY notified_within_10_min_pct DESC, claim_count DESC;
```

## 12. Inspect event traces for the most suspicious claims

After identifying anomalous claim IDs, this query shows the full event sequence for manual review.

```sql
SELECT
    ce.claim_id,
    c.claim_type,
    c.claim_amount,
    ce.activity,
    ce.timestamp,
    ce.resource,
    ce.additional_info
FROM claim_events ce
JOIN claims c
    ON c.claim_id = ce.claim_id
WHERE ce.claim_id IN (
    -- Replace with suspicious claim IDs from previous queries
    101, 102, 103
)
ORDER BY
    ce.claim_id,
    ce.timestamp,
    ce.event_id;
```

## Most useful next checks

The highest-value checks are:

1. **Confirm whether `A → C` cases are missing `E`, `P`, or `N` before close.**
   If yes, the process may be skipping required steps.

2. **Check whether `P → N` delays cluster by notification resource, claim type, region, or weekday.**
   If yes, this points to a bottleneck or batch-processing issue.

3. **Check whether `R → P` approvals cluster around exactly 24–25 hours.**
   If yes, approvals may be controlled by automation, SLA padding, or delayed timestamp entry.

4. **Check whether `E → N` occurs before approval.**
   If yes, notification may be happening too early, or approval may be recorded late or outside the system.
