

# High-Level Process Step Abstraction

## Overview

After analyzing the event logs for cases A1 and B2, I identified a consistent pattern of five distinct high-level process stages. The groupings are based on **temporal proximity**, **functional coherence**, **resource clustering**, and **logical process phase boundaries**.

---

## Proposed High-Level Activities

### 1. **Material Preparation**

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Included Low-Level Events** | Retrieve raw metal sheet  Scan barcode  Place metal sheet on table  Align metal sheet  Preheat metal sheet |
| **Typical Resources** | Operator A, Robot Arm #2, Heating Unit #1 |
| **Time Window (Case A1)** | 08:00:05 – 08:00:20 (~15 seconds of active work + preheating) |

**Rationale:**
These five events collectively represent the intake and readiness preparation of raw material before any value-adding transformation occurs. The logic is as follows:
- **Retrieve** and **Scan** are classic material intake/identification steps — the operator fetches the part and registers it in the system.
- **Place on table** and **Align** are positioning actions that set the workpiece up for downstream operations.
- **Preheat** is a conditioning step that brings the material to a state suitable for welding.

All five events occur in rapid succession (within ~15–55 seconds), involve the transition from raw input to "ready for processing" state, and none of them alter the fundamental shape or structure of the product. They are preparatory in nature. The boundary between this step and the next is marked by a clear temporal gap (~40 seconds in Case A1) and a shift in both resource and activity type (from preparation to tool pickup/welding).

---

### 2. **Welding / Assembly**

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Included Low-Level Events** | Pick up welding tool  Weld corner A  Weld corner B |
| **Typical Resources** | Operator B |
| **Time Window (Case A1)** | 08:01:00 – 08:01:10 (~10 seconds) |

**Rationale:**
This group represents the core **value-adding transformation** step where the product is physically altered through joining operations:
- **Pick up welding tool** is the immediate setup action for welding — it has no independent purpose outside of enabling the welds that follow and is performed by the same operator (Operator B) who performs the welding itself.
- **Weld corner A** and **Weld corner B** are the actual fabrication actions.

These events are tightly clustered in time, performed by a single dedicated resource (Operator B), and together represent a coherent assembly operation. The tool pickup is grouped here rather than in "Material Preparation" because it is specific to the welding task, performed by the welding operator, and immediately precedes the weld actions with no intervening activities.

---

### 3. **Weld Quality Inspection**

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Included Low-Level Events** | Measure weld integrity |
| **Typical Resources** | Quality Sensor #1 |
| **Time Window (Case A1)** | 08:01:20 (single event, ~instantaneous) |

**Rationale:**
This is a distinct **inline quality gate** that occurs immediately after the welding step. It is isolated as its own high-level step because:
- It serves a fundamentally different purpose (verification vs. fabrication).
- It is performed by a specialized quality resource (Quality Sensor #1), not the assembly operator.
- It produces a measurable output (IntegrityScore) that could determine whether the process continues or triggers rework.
- In a real-world process, this is a decision point — a failing score might cause the item to be rejected or sent back to re-welding.

Even though it is a single event, it represents a logically complete and critically important process phase. Grouping it with either the preceding welding or the subsequent coating would obscure its role as a quality checkpoint.

---

### 4. **Coating / Surface Treatment**

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Included Low-Level Events** | Apply protective coating  Dry coating |
| **Typical Resources** | Coating Unit #3, Heating Unit #2 |
| **Time Window (Case A1)** | 08:01:30 – 08:01:45 (~15 seconds) |

**Rationale:**
These two events form a coherent **surface finishing** phase:
- **Apply protective coating** is the application of a material (Polyurethane) that protects the welded product.
- **Dry coating** is the curing/drying step that is inseparable from the application — a coating that is not dried is not complete.

They are grouped together because they are functionally interdependent (drying is a necessary completion of coating application), both involve automated equipment rather than manual operators, and they represent the post-assembly finishing phase of the product. The shift from Operator B (manual welding) to automated units (Coating Unit, Heating Unit) also signals a phase transition.

---

### 5. **Final Visual Inspection**

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Included Low-Level Events** | Visual check |
| **Typical Resources** | Operator C |
| **Time Window (Case A1)** | 08:02:00 (single event) |

**Rationale:**
This is the **final quality gate** before the product exits the process:
- It is performed by a different operator (Operator C) who has not been involved in any prior step — suggesting an independent quality assurance role.
- It occurs after all fabrication and finishing steps are complete.
- The output ("Check: Passed") represents a go/no-go decision for the finished product.

Separating this from the Weld Quality Inspection (Step 3) is important because they serve different purposes: Step 3 validates an intermediate result (weld integrity), while Step 5 validates the entire finished product. They also use different methods (automated sensor vs. human visual inspection) and different resources.
