## 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

### 1.1 Defining waiting time using start and completion timestamps

In this clinic context, a patientĺs visit can be viewed as a sequence of activities:

> Registration  Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Test  Specialist Review  Check-out

Each activity has at least two relevant timestamps:

- **Activity start time**
- **Activity completion time**

For a given patient visit, the **waiting time between two consecutive activities** is calculated as:

> **Waiting time before Activity B = Start time of Activity B  Completion time of Activity A**

For example, if:

- Registration completes at 09:08:45
- Nurse Assessment starts at 09:15:20

Then:

> Waiting time for Nurse Assessment = 09:15:20  09:08:45 = 6 minutes 35 seconds

This waiting time represents the time the patient is not actively receiving the next service but is waiting for it to begin.

### 1.2 Important distinction: service time versus waiting time

For each activity, we should separate:

| Measure | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Service time | Completion time  Start time of the same activity | Time spent in doctor consultation |
| Waiting time | Start time of next activity  Completion time of previous activity | Time waiting after nurse assessment before seeing doctor |
| Total visit duration | Last completion timestamp  first start timestamp | Total time in clinic |
| Touch time | Sum of all activity service times | Time actually receiving care/service |
| Idle/wait time | Total visit duration  touch time | Time spent waiting |

This distinction is critical because patients often experience long total visits not because care activities take too long, but because idle time accumulates between activities.

### 1.3 Constructing queue points

For each case, or visit ID, the events should be ordered chronologically. Then the clinic should identify transitions such as:

- Completion of Registration  Start of Nurse Assessment
- Completion of Nurse Assessment  Start of Doctor Consultation
- Completion of Doctor Consultation  Start of Blood Test
- Completion of Doctor Consultation  Start of ECG
- Completion of Diagnostic Test  Start of Specialist Review
- Completion of Final Clinical Activity  Start of Check-out

Each transition becomes a potential queue.

For example:

| Queue | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Registration  Nurse Assessment | Waiting for nurse intake |
| Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation | Waiting for physician |
| Doctor Consultation  ECG | Waiting for diagnostic test |
| ECG  Specialist Review | Waiting for physician to interpret/review results |
| Final Clinical Step  Check-out | Waiting to complete billing, scheduling, discharge paperwork |

The same queue should also be segmented by specialty, location, resource group, patient type, urgency, day of week, and appointment time band.

For example:

- Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation for Cardiology
- Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation for Orthopedics
- Doctor Consultation  ECG for new patients
- Final Clinical Step  Check-out for afternoon visits

This segmentation is essential because the bottleneck may not be the same across the entire clinic.

---

### 1.4 Queue characterization metrics

For each queue, I would calculate the following metrics.

#### A. Waiting time metrics

| Metric | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Average waiting time | Shows overall queue burden |
| Median waiting time | Shows typical patient experience, less distorted by outliers |
| Maximum waiting time | Identifies worst-case experiences |
| 75th, 90th, and 95th percentile waiting time | Measures reliability and tail performance |
| Standard deviation / coefficient of variation | Shows variability and predictability |
| Interquartile range | Shows spread of typical waits |
| Percentage exceeding threshold | Directly tied to service-level goals |

For example:

- Percentage of patients waiting more than 15 minutes for nurse assessment
- Percentage waiting more than 30 minutes for doctor consultation
- Percentage waiting more than 20 minutes for diagnostic testing
- Percentage waiting more than 10 minutes for check-out

#### B. Queue frequency metrics

A long wait in a rare path may be less operationally important than a moderate wait affecting thousands of patients.

Important metrics include:

- Number of patients passing through each queue
- Percentage of total visits affected by each queue
- Number of patients experiencing excessive waits
- Total accumulated waiting hours

For example:

> A queue with an average wait of 18 minutes affecting 8,000 patients may represent a larger improvement opportunity than a queue with an average wait of 45 minutes affecting 100 patients.

#### C. Contribution to total visit duration

For each queue, calculate:

> Queue contribution = Total waiting time in this queue / Total visit duration

Also calculate aggregate contribution:

> Total accumulated waiting hours for queue X over six months

This helps identify queues that consume the most patient time overall.

#### D. Segment-specific queue metrics

Waiting times should be stratified by:

- Specialty: cardiology, dermatology, orthopedics, internal medicine, etc.
- Patient type: new versus follow-up
- Urgency: urgent versus normal
- Appointment type
- Time of day
- Day of week
- Provider
- Room or equipment
- Diagnostic test type
- Visit complexity
- Arrival status: early, on time, late
- Insurance or administrative category if relevant and ethically appropriate

For example, the overall doctor wait may be 22 minutes, but this could hide:

| Segment | Median doctor wait |
|---|---:|
| Follow-up dermatology | 8 minutes |
| New cardiology | 45 minutes |
| Urgent orthopedics | 12 minutes |
| New internal medicine | 31 minutes |

The improvement strategy would differ substantially by segment.

---

### 1.5 Identifying the most critical queues

The most critical queues should not be selected solely by longest average wait. I would prioritize queues using a combined impact score.

Possible criteria include:

#### 1. Absolute waiting time

Queues with high average, median, and percentile waiting times.

Example:

- Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation has a median wait of 32 minutes and 90th percentile of 74 minutes.

#### 2. Frequency of occurrence

Queues that affect many patients.

Example:

- Registration  Nurse Assessment may have only a 12-minute average wait but affects nearly every patient.

#### 3. Total waiting burden

Calculate total accumulated waiting hours:

> Total waiting burden = Sum of waiting time across all patients for that queue

This identifies where improvement yields the greatest system-wide benefit.

#### 4. Tail risk and service-level violations

A queue may have an acceptable average but many extreme waits.

Example:

- Average wait for ECG = 14 minutes
- 95th percentile wait = 68 minutes

This indicates unstable performance and poor predictability.

#### 5. Impact on patient satisfaction

Queues that occur early or near the physician encounter often have disproportionate satisfaction effects.

Common high-sensitivity queues include:

- Waiting after registration
- Waiting for the doctor after nurse assessment
- Waiting for check-out when the patient believes the visit is ôfinishedö

#### 6. Impact on clinical outcomes or care quality

Urgent patients, elderly patients, pediatric patients, or patients requiring time-sensitive tests may need special prioritization.

#### 7. Impact on downstream flow

Some queues cause cascading delays. For example:

- Delayed nurse assessment delays doctor consultations.
- Delayed diagnostics delays specialist review.
- Delayed room turnover reduces room availability for subsequent patients.

---

### 1.6 Example prioritization logic

I would rank queues using a weighted score such as:

> Criticality Score =  
> 0.30 Î normalized total waiting hours  
> + 0.25 Î normalized 90th percentile wait  
> + 0.20 Î normalized frequency  
> + 0.15 Î percentage exceeding threshold  
> + 0.10 Î strategic importance or patient satisfaction impact

This prevents overreacting to rare outliers while still recognizing serious patient experience problems.

A likely set of critical queues in a multi-specialty outpatient clinic could include:

1. **Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation**
2. **Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Test**
3. **Diagnostic Test  Specialist Review**
4. **Final Clinical Activity  Check-out**
5. **Registration  Nurse Assessment during morning peaks**

## 2. Root Cause Analysis

Once the critical queues are identified, the next task is to determine why they occur. Queue mining tells us where patients wait; root cause analysis explains the operational mechanism behind the wait.

---

### 2.1 Resource bottlenecks

A common cause of queuing is insufficient resource capacity during demand peaks.

Relevant resources include:

- Registration clerks
- Nurses
- Physicians
- Diagnostic technicians
- Rooms
- Equipment such as ECG machines or X-ray units
- Check-out staff

Using the event log, we can calculate resource utilization:

> Resource utilization = Total busy time / Available time

For each resource, we can analyze:

- Number of patients handled
- Average service time
- Utilization by hour
- Idle time
- Overtime
- Handover delay
- Queue length before resource starts activity
- Patients waiting when resource becomes available

A very high utilization rate, especially above 85ľ90%, often causes nonlinear increases in waiting time. Even small variability can create large queues when utilization is high.

Example finding:

> Dr. Smith is actively consulting 88% of the time between 09:00 and 12:00, while the median patient wait before consultation is 38 minutes. This suggests a physician-capacity bottleneck, not merely patient lateness.

Another example:

> ECG Room 3 is utilized only 55% overall, but 95% between 10:00 and 12:00, producing long mid-morning waits. This suggests a scheduling and peak-load problem, not necessarily a need for new equipment.

---

### 2.2 Activity dependencies and handovers

Some queues are caused by dependencies between activities.

For example:

- The doctor cannot see the patient until nurse assessment is complete.
- The specialist cannot make a final decision until test results are available.
- Check-out cannot begin until the doctor closes the encounter or enters orders.
- A diagnostic test cannot start until the order is placed and transmitted.

Process mining can identify handover delays such as:

- Nurse completes assessment, but doctor does not pick up patient for 40 minutes.
- Doctor completes consultation, but test order is entered 15 minutes later.
- ECG completes, but specialist review begins only after 50 minutes.
- Doctor completes visit, but check-out cannot proceed because documentation is incomplete.

A key distinction is whether the patient is waiting for a resource, for information, or for an administrative trigger.

---

### 2.3 Variability in activity durations

Queues may emerge not because average service time is too long, but because service time is highly variable.

For each activity, calculate:

- Mean service time
- Median service time
- Standard deviation
- 90th and 95th percentile service time
- Service time by provider
- Service time by patient type
- Service time by specialty

For example:

| Activity | Median service time | 90th percentile |
|---|---:|---:|
| Follow-up consultation | 12 min | 22 min |
| New patient cardiology consultation | 28 min | 55 min |
| Nurse assessment | 8 min | 18 min |
| Check-out | 5 min | 14 min |

High service-time variability causes appointment schedules to become unstable. If a physician has several complex new patients early in the morning, later patients may experience cascading delays.

---

### 2.4 Appointment scheduling policies

The event log can be enriched or linked with appointment data to compare:

- Scheduled appointment time
- Actual arrival time
- Registration start time
- Nurse assessment start time
- Doctor consultation start time

Key questions:

- Are too many patients scheduled at the top of the hour?
- Are new and follow-up patients given the same slot length despite different service times?
- Are diagnostics scheduled reactively, causing post-consultation queues?
- Are multiple providers sending patients to the same diagnostic resource at the same time?
- Are urgent walk-ins disrupting scheduled visits?
- Are late arrivals inserted immediately, delaying on-time patients?

Process mining can reveal whether delays begin at arrival, registration, clinical intake, doctor consultation, or diagnostics.

Example finding:

> The clinic schedules six cardiology patients at 09:00, but only two nurses and two doctors are available. The first queue forms immediately after registration and persists until noon.

---

### 2.5 Patient arrival patterns

Queue mining should examine arrival rates by:

- Time of day
- Day of week
- Specialty
- Provider
- Patient type
- Urgency
- Scheduled versus walk-in

Common patterns include:

- Morning arrival surge
- Lunch-hour staffing mismatch
- Monday demand peak
- Friday afternoon short-staffing
- Diagnostic demand peaks after morning doctor consultations
- Late arrivals causing rescheduling disruptions

Arrival pattern analysis can be compared against resource availability.

Example:

> Patient arrivals peak from 08:45 to 09:30, but nurse staffing reaches full capacity only at 09:30. This creates an avoidable early-day queue that propagates downstream.

---

### 2.6 Differences by patient type and urgency

New patients often require:

- Longer registration
- More detailed nurse assessment
- Longer doctor consultation
- More diagnostic testing
- More complex check-out

Follow-up patients may be more predictable.

Urgent patients may jump queues, which is clinically appropriate, but this can increase waits for normal patients.

Using variant analysis, we can compare:

- New versus follow-up patients
- Urgent versus normal patients
- Specialty-specific pathways
- Patients requiring diagnostics versus those who do not
- Patients requiring multiple tests versus one test

Example:

> New cardiology patients who require ECG and blood test have a median total visit duration of 142 minutes, compared with 54 minutes for follow-up cardiology patients without diagnostics.

This helps avoid one-size-fits-all interventions.

---

### 2.7 Process mining techniques to pinpoint root causes

#### A. Bottleneck analysis

Identify transitions with the highest waiting times and largest contribution to total visit duration.

#### B. Performance-annotated process maps

Visualize the actual patient flow with:

- Activity frequencies
- Average service times
- Average waiting times between activities
- 90th percentile delays
- Rework loops

This makes bottlenecks visible to clinical and administrative stakeholders.

#### C. Variant analysis

Compare common pathways:

- Simple follow-up visit
- New patient with diagnostics
- Urgent visit
- Multi-specialty visit
- Visit requiring specialist review

Variant analysis helps determine whether long waits are systemic or concentrated in specific paths.

#### D. Resource analysis

Analyze workload and utilization by:

- Staff member
- Role
- Room
- Equipment
- Specialty
- Time of day

This helps identify whether the bottleneck is a person, room, equipment, or scheduling pattern.

#### E. Social network and handover analysis

Analyze handovers between roles:

- Clerk  Nurse
- Nurse  Doctor
- Doctor  Technician
- Technician  Doctor
- Doctor  Check-out

Long handover delays may indicate coordination issues.

#### F. Conformance checking

Compare actual flows with intended clinical pathways.

For example:

- Are patients going to check-out before all orders are complete?
- Are diagnostic tests being performed in the wrong sequence?
- Are urgent patients following the escalation pathway?
- Are patients sent back to registration due to missing information?

#### G. Predictive monitoring

Use historical patterns to predict whether a patient currently in the clinic is likely to exceed a wait-time threshold, enabling active intervention.

## 3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

Below are concrete strategies that could be tested and implemented based on the queue mining findings.

---

### Strategy 1: Dynamic nurse and registration staffing during arrival peaks

#### Targeted queues

- Registration  Nurse Assessment
- Arrival  Registration, if arrival timestamps are available
- Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation indirectly

#### Root cause addressed

This targets early-day congestion caused by a mismatch between patient arrival rates and front-end staffing capacity.

Typical root causes:

- Too many patients arriving between 08:30 and 10:00
- Registration clerks or nurses starting shifts after patient volume has already peaked
- New patients requiring longer registration and intake
- Uneven distribution of nurse workload across specialties

#### Data support

The event log can show:

- Arrival and registration start patterns by 15-minute interval
- Registration service time by patient type
- Nurse assessment wait time by time of day
- Nurse utilization by hour
- Number of patients waiting after registration
- Queue buildup in early morning

For example, analysis may reveal:

- 42% of daily patients arrive before 10:00
- Nurse utilization is 95% from 09:00 to 10:30
- Median wait after registration is 24 minutes during this period but 7 minutes after 11:00

#### Proposed intervention

- Shift one nurseĺs start time from 10:00 to 08:30.
- Cross-train one registration clerk to support check-in during the first 90 minutes.
- Create a floating intake nurse who moves between specialties based on real-time queue length.
- Pre-register returning patients digitally to reduce front-desk load.
- Use different intake templates for new versus follow-up patients.

#### Potential positive impact

If the morning intake queue is the main source of cascading delays, this could:

- Reduce average Registration  Nurse Assessment wait by 20ľ40%
- Reduce late doctor starts
- Improve on-time flow for the rest of the day
- Reduce overall visit duration by 10ľ20 minutes for morning patients

This strategy is relatively low-cost if achieved through shift redesign rather than hiring.

---

### Strategy 2: Specialty-specific appointment templates based on actual service-time distributions

#### Targeted queues

- Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation
- Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Test
- Overall visit duration

#### Root cause addressed

This targets appointment schedules that do not reflect actual service-time variability.

Common problems include:

- New and follow-up patients scheduled in identical slots
- Complex specialties scheduled too tightly
- Too many new patients booked consecutively
- No buffer for urgent add-ons
- Underestimation of diagnostic needs

#### Data support

The event log can show:

- Doctor consultation duration by specialty
- Consultation duration by patient type
- Probability of diagnostics by patient type and specialty
- Waiting time before consultation by appointment time
- Delay propagation across the providerĺs session
- Providers or specialties with consistently long 90th percentile service times

For example:

| Visit type | Median doctor time | 90th percentile doctor time |
|---|---:|---:|
| Follow-up cardiology | 13 min | 24 min |
| New cardiology | 31 min | 58 min |
| Follow-up dermatology | 9 min | 16 min |
| New internal medicine | 27 min | 49 min |

If all are booked into 15-minute slots, long waits are predictable.

#### Proposed intervention

Create data-driven appointment templates, such as:

- New cardiology patient: 30ľ40 minute slot
- Follow-up cardiology patient: 15ľ20 minute slot
- Complex new patient: longer slot or pre-visit workup
- Built-in buffer every 90ľ120 minutes
- Limit number of new patients per session
- Reserve urgent slots rather than inserting urgent patients into a full schedule
- Place predictable short follow-ups after complex visits to absorb variability

#### Potential positive impact

Expected benefits may include:

- 15ľ30% reduction in wait before doctor consultation
- Lower variability in doctor wait times
- Fewer cascading afternoon delays
- Improved provider workload predictability
- Better patient satisfaction due to more reliable visit lengths

This strategy does not necessarily require more staff, but it may reduce the number of appointments if poorly designed. Therefore, simulation should be used before implementation.

---

### Strategy 3: Pre-position diagnostic testing and parallelize eligible activities

#### Targeted queues

- Doctor Consultation  ECG
- Doctor Consultation  Blood Test
- Doctor Consultation  X-Ray
- Diagnostic Test  Specialist Review
- Overall visit duration for diagnostic-heavy pathways

#### Root cause addressed

This targets sequential patient flow where diagnostic testing only begins after the doctor consultation, even when the diagnostic need is predictable.

For example:

- Many new cardiology patients need ECG.
- Many orthopedic patients need X-ray.
- Many diabetes follow-up patients need labs.

If these tests are highly predictable, waiting until after the physician visit creates unnecessary queues and extends total visit duration.

#### Data support

Process mining and variant analysis can identify:

- Which patient types frequently require specific diagnostics
- How often diagnostics occur after consultation
- Waiting time from doctor consultation to diagnostic test
- Waiting time from diagnostic completion to specialist review
- Diagnostic resource utilization by time of day
- Rework caused by missing test results

Example finding:

> 78% of new cardiology patients receive an ECG after the first doctor consultation. Median wait from doctor consultation to ECG is 22 minutes, and median wait from ECG completion to specialist review is 18 minutes.

This suggests that many ECGs could be performed before the doctor consultation.

#### Proposed intervention

- For high-probability diagnostic pathways, perform standard tests before the doctor visit.
- Use protocol-driven pre-visit diagnostics for eligible patients.
- Have nurses trigger predefined tests based on appointment type and clinical rules.
- Schedule diagnostic slots aligned with specialty sessions.
- Allow diagnostic tests to run in parallel with administrative preparation where clinically appropriate.
- Use real-time notifications when test results are ready for physician review.

Examples:

- New cardiology patients: registration  nurse assessment  ECG  doctor consultation
- Orthopedic follow-up with known injury: registration  X-ray  doctor consultation
- Chronic disease follow-up: pre-visit labs completed before appointment day

#### Potential positive impact

This could:

- Reduce total visit duration for diagnostic-heavy patients by 20ľ35%
- Reduce Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Test waiting time
- Reduce need for second physician review loop
- Improve physician decision-making because results are available earlier
- Smooth diagnostic demand if pre-scheduled intelligently

This must be designed carefully to avoid unnecessary tests.

---

### Strategy 4: Real-time queue visibility and escalation dashboard

#### Targeted queues

- All critical queues
- Especially Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation
- Diagnostic Test  Specialist Review
- Final Clinical Activity  Check-out

#### Root cause addressed

This targets coordination failures and lack of operational visibility.

In many clinics, staff do not know:

- Which patients have waited longest
- Which physician is falling behind
- Which diagnostic room is idle
- Which patients are ready for check-out
- Which orders are pending
- Which urgent patients are approaching threshold violations

#### Data support

Event logs can show historical queue thresholds and common escalation points:

- Queues that exceed 30 minutes
- Providers with recurring delay patterns
- Time windows with high queue buildup
- Patients who are waiting despite available resources
- Long delays caused by missing handoffs

#### Proposed intervention

Implement a real-time operational dashboard showing:

- Current patients in each queue
- Wait time so far
- Queue aging by color code
- Resource availability
- Patients ready for next activity
- Bottleneck alerts
- Diagnostic result-ready alerts
- Check-out readiness alerts

Escalation rules could include:

- If doctor wait exceeds 30 minutes, notify charge nurse or clinic coordinator.
- If diagnostic wait exceeds 20 minutes, check room/equipment availability.
- If check-out wait exceeds 10 minutes, open backup check-out desk.
- If urgent patient waits beyond threshold, escalate immediately.

#### Potential positive impact

Expected benefits:

- 10ľ25% reduction in avoidable handover-related waits
- Faster response to emerging bottlenecks
- Better patient communication
- Fewer patients ôlostö between stages
- Better daily management without major staffing increases

This is especially valuable when delays are caused by coordination rather than capacity shortage.

---

### Strategy 5: Streamline check-out and administrative closure

#### Targeted queues

- Final Clinical Activity  Check-out
- Check-out service time
- Overall visit duration
- Patient satisfaction at end of visit

#### Root cause addressed

Patients often perceive post-care waiting as especially frustrating because they believe the visit is already complete.

Common causes include:

- Doctor documentation not completed
- Orders not finalized
- Follow-up appointment scheduling bottleneck
- Payment or insurance processing delay
- Insufficient check-out staff during late morning and afternoon peaks

#### Data support

The event log can reveal:

- Time from doctor completion to check-out start
- Check-out service duration
- Check-out wait by time of day
- Check-out wait by provider
- Frequency of delayed documentation
- Rework loops back to doctor or nurse

Example finding:

> Median check-out wait is 6 minutes overall, but 90th percentile is 28 minutes between 11:30 and 13:00, when many morning visits end simultaneously.

#### Proposed intervention

- Introduce mobile or kiosk-based check-out for simple visits.
- Allow patients to schedule follow-up appointments digitally after leaving.
- Create express check-out for no-payment/no-follow-up cases.
- Trigger check-out preparation when doctor consultation starts.
- Add backup check-out support during predictable peaks.
- Standardize physician order closure before patient leaves the exam room.

#### Potential positive impact

Expected outcomes:

- 30ľ50% reduction in check-out wait for simple visits
- Higher patient satisfaction
- Lower front-desk congestion
- Reduced end-of-visit frustration

This is often a relatively inexpensive intervention with visible patient experience benefits.

## 4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints

Optimization in healthcare must balance efficiency, cost, staff well-being, and care quality. Reducing waits should not mean rushing care or undermining clinical judgment.

---

### 4.1 Bottleneck shifting

Improving one queue may shift the bottleneck elsewhere.

Example:

- Adding registration staff may move patients faster to nurses, increasing the nurse queue.
- Adding nurses may move patients faster to doctors, increasing doctor wait.
- Pre-consultation ECG may overload diagnostic technicians early in the morning.
- Faster doctor throughput may overwhelm check-out.

To manage this, interventions should be evaluated using end-to-end process simulation, not only local queue metrics.

---

### 4.2 Cost increases

Some solutions require investment:

- Additional staff
- Extended hours
- New equipment
- Dashboard software
- Self-service kiosks
- Training and change management

To control costs, prioritize:

- Schedule redesign before hiring
- Cross-training before adding headcount
- Targeted part-time staffing during peaks
- Better appointment templates
- Reducing unnecessary rework
- Digital pre-registration

Cost-effectiveness should be measured as:

> Cost per minute of patient waiting time reduced

and

> Cost per percentage-point improvement in service-level compliance

---

### 4.3 Staff workload and burnout

Improving throughput by pushing staff harder is not sustainable.

Risks include:

- Higher stress
- Reduced documentation quality
- Shorter patient interactions
- Increased errors
- Lower staff satisfaction
- Higher turnover

Resource utilization should not be pushed to 100%. In service systems with variability, some buffer capacity is necessary.

A reasonable target may be:

- 75ľ85% utilization for highly variable clinical roles
- Higher utilization for predictable administrative or diagnostic tasks, if backups exist

---

### 4.4 Impact on care quality

Some interventions may have clinical risks.

Examples:

- Pre-ordering diagnostics could increase unnecessary testing.
- Shorter appointment slots could reduce thoroughness.
- Parallelizing activities may create confusion if clinical information is incomplete.
- Express pathways could miss patient concerns.

Mitigations include:

- Use clinical eligibility criteria
- Involve physicians and nurses in pathway redesign
- Monitor clinical quality indicators
- Avoid purely time-based performance pressure
- Use protocols and decision support

---

### 4.5 Fairness and prioritization

Queue reduction must account for clinical priority.

Urgent patients may appropriately bypass normal queues. However, this should be transparent and controlled.

Possible balancing rules:

- Urgent patients receive priority.
- Normal patients should still have maximum wait thresholds.
- Vulnerable patients may need special handling.
- No-show and late-arrival policies should be fair but operationally realistic.

---

### 4.6 Patient satisfaction versus operational efficiency

Some patients may prefer:

- More time with doctors
- Clear communication during waits
- Predictable visit duration
- Ability to leave and receive results later
- Digital check-out

Not all dissatisfaction comes from the objective length of the wait. Perceived wait can be reduced by:

- Accurate wait-time estimates
- Proactive communication
- Comfortable waiting areas
- Text notifications
- Explaining delays
- Allowing patients to complete forms before arrival

Therefore, operational improvements should be paired with communication improvements.

---

### 4.7 Balancing conflicting objectives

I would use a balanced optimization framework with constraints such as:

| Objective | Example target |
|---|---|
| Reduce average waiting time | 20% reduction within 3 months |
| Reduce long waits | 90% of patients see doctor within 30 minutes of nurse assessment |
| Maintain care quality | No increase in revisit rate or adverse events |
| Control cost | No more than 5% increase in labor cost |
| Protect staff workload | Keep utilization within sustainable range |
| Maintain access | Avoid major reduction in appointment availability |

Simulation can help test different scenarios before implementation, such as:

- Add one nurse for 4 peak hours
- Shift physician templates
- Pre-schedule ECGs
- Add check-out kiosk
- Reserve urgent slots

The preferred solution is not necessarily the one with the shortest wait, but the one with the best balance of patient experience, quality, cost, and staff sustainability.

---

## 5. Measuring Success

After deploying interventions, the clinic should continue using the event log structure to monitor performance. The same queue mining approach should become an ongoing management capability.

---

### 5.1 Core KPIs

#### A. Waiting-time KPIs

For each critical queue:

- Average waiting time
- Median waiting time
- 90th percentile waiting time
- 95th percentile waiting time
- Maximum waiting time
- Percentage of patients exceeding wait threshold

Examples:

- Percentage waiting more than 15 minutes for nurse assessment
- Percentage waiting more than 30 minutes for doctor consultation
- Percentage waiting more than 20 minutes for diagnostics
- Percentage waiting more than 10 minutes for check-out

#### B. Total visit duration KPIs

- Average total visit duration
- Median total visit duration
- 90th percentile total visit duration
- Total visit duration by patient type
- Total visit duration by specialty
- Total visit duration for diagnostic-heavy variants

#### C. Touch-time versus wait-time ratio

Calculate:

> Touch-time ratio = Total service time / Total visit duration

A higher ratio means more of the patientĺs visit is spent receiving care rather than waiting.

#### D. Throughput KPIs

- Number of patients completed per day
- Number of patients completed per provider session
- Patients completed by specialty
- Diagnostic tests completed per hour
- Check-outs completed per hour

#### E. Resource utilization KPIs

- Physician utilization by session
- Nurse utilization by hour
- Registration clerk utilization
- Diagnostic room/equipment utilization
- Check-out staff utilization
- Overtime hours
- Idle time during scheduled work hours

#### F. Schedule adherence KPIs

If appointment data is available:

- Doctor start delay versus appointment time
- Percentage of appointments starting within target window
- Delay propagation across session
- Late-arrival impact
- No-show-adjusted utilization
- Overbooking impact

#### G. Patient experience KPIs

- Patient satisfaction scores
- Net Promoter Score, if used
- Complaints about waiting
- Comments mentioning delays
- Abandonment or left-without-being-seen rate
- Percentage of patients receiving wait-time updates

#### H. Quality and safety KPIs

To ensure throughput improvements do not harm care:

- Revisit rate within defined period
- Unplanned follow-up calls
- Diagnostic error or missed-order incidents
- Documentation completeness
- Medication/order accuracy
- Adverse events
- Patient complaints related to rushed care

---

### 5.2 Before-and-after evaluation

For each intervention, compare:

- Pre-implementation baseline
- Immediate post-implementation period
- Stabilized post-implementation period

Use statistical process control rather than relying only on simple averages.

Methods include:

- Control charts
- Run charts
- Confidence intervals
- Difference-in-differences if only some departments changed
- Segmented analysis by specialty and patient type

For example:

> After changing cardiology appointment templates, median Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation wait decreased from 34 minutes to 23 minutes, and the percentage exceeding 45 minutes decreased from 31% to 14%, with no reduction in daily completed visits.

---

### 5.3 Ongoing process monitoring

A monitoring dashboard should track daily, weekly, and monthly trends.

Recommended dashboard views:

#### Operational daily view

- Current queues
- Current wait times
- Patients exceeding threshold

### 5.4 Sustained improvement cycle

The clinic should adopt a continuous improvement loop:

1. Extract event logs regularly.
2. Calculate queue and performance metrics.
3. Identify bottlenecks and variants.
4. Discuss findings with operational and clinical teams.
5. Test interventions using simulation or pilot sites.
6. Implement changes.
7. Monitor KPIs.
8. Adjust based on evidence.

This turns process mining from a one-time analysis into an ongoing management system.

## Summary

The clinic can use queue mining to transform timestamped event logs into actionable insight about patient waiting. The key is to distinguish service time from waiting time, calculate queue metrics between every pair of consecutive activities, and prioritize queues based on total impact, frequency, percentile delays, and patient experience.

Likely bottlenecks include waiting for doctors after nurse assessment, waiting for diagnostics after consultation, waiting for specialist review after tests, and waiting for check-out. Root causes may include resource-capacity mismatches, poor appointment templates, diagnostic sequencing issues, handover delays, and variation by patient type or specialty.

High-impact strategies include:

1. Dynamic staffing during demand peaks.
2. Specialty-specific appointment templates based on actual service-time distributions.
3. Pre-positioning diagnostics and parallelizing eligible activities.
4. Real-time queue dashboards and escalation rules.
5. Streamlined digital or express check-out.

The clinic should evaluate trade-offs carefully, especially cost, staff workload, quality of care, and the risk of shifting bottlenecks. Success should be measured using ongoing KPIs such as average and percentile wait times, total visit duration, service-level compliance, resource utilization, patient satisfaction, and care quality indicators.

By combining process mining, queue analysis, root cause investigation, and continuous monitoring, the clinic can reduce waiting times in a targeted, evidence-based way without simply adding costly capacity everywhere.
