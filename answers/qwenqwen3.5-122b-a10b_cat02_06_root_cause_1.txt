*   **Case 101:** 08:00 to 10:15 = **2 hours 15 minutes**
*   **Case 102:** 08:05 (Day 1) to 09:15 (Day 2) = **25 hours 10 minutes**
*   **Case 103:** 08:10 to 09:30 = **1 hour 20 minutes**
*   **Case 104:** 08:20 (Day 1) to 08:30 (Day 2) = **24 hours 10 minutes**
*   **Case 105:** 08:25 (Day 1) to 09:30 (Day 3) = **49 hours 5 minutes**

**Analysis:**
Cases **101** and **103** represent the standard baseline, with resolution times under 2.5 hours.
Cases **102, 104, and 105** are significantly delayed, taking between **24 to 49 hours** to resolve. These cases are approximately **10 to 20 times longer** than the standard baseline.

### 2. Root Causes of Performance Issues

By analyzing the timestamps between specific activities within the delayed cases, three primary root causes emerge:

**A. Escalation Backlogs (Cases 102 & 105)**
Both delayed cases involving escalation (102 and 105) suffered from massive wait times after the decision to escalate was made.
*   **Case 105:** After the ticket was escalated at 10:00 on Day 1, it sat idle until 14:00 on Day 2 before the Level-2 agent began investigation. This is a **28-hour waiting period**.
*   **Case 102:** After the initial investigation, there was a **19-hour gap** between the first "Investigate Issue" event and the "Resolve Ticket" event, suggesting the resolution work was not completed in a single shift.
*   **Insight:** The Level-2 specialized team appears to be a bottleneck. They are likely understaffed or have a lower priority queue, causing tickets to sit in "Escalated" status for days.

**B. Resource Constraints & Business Hours (Cases 104 & 105)**
All three delayed cases (102, 104, 105) show significant overnight gaps where no work was performed.
*   **Case 104:** This ticket did **not** escalate, yet it took 24 hours. The gap between "Assign to Level-1 Agent" (09:30) and "Investigate Issue" (13:00) was **3.5 hours**, and the gap between "Investigate" (13:00 Day 1) and "Resolve" (08:00 Day 2) was **19 hours**.
*   **Insight:** This indicates that Level-1 agents are either overloaded, waiting on other dependencies, or strictly adhering to business hours without after-hours support. The 3.5-hour delay between assignment and investigation suggests a queueing issue at the agent level.

**C. Inconsistent Triage Efficiency (Case 104)**
*   **Case 104:** Took **40 minutes** to move from "Receive Ticket" to "Triage Ticket."
*   **Comparison:** Case 103 took only **5 minutes** for the same step.
*   **Insight:** The initial intake process is inconsistent. A 35-minute variance suggests a lack of standard operating procedures (SOPs) or staffing shortages during the morning intake window.

### 3. Insights and Recommendations

The data suggests that the process is not failing due to the technical complexity of the tickets, but rather due to **handoff delays** and **capacity constraints**.

**Recommendation 1: Optimize Escalation SLAs**
*   **Issue:** Escalated tickets (102, 105) are the longest to resolve.
*   **Action:** Implement Service Level Agreements (SLAs) specifically for Level-2 handoffs. For example, "Escalated tickets must be acknowledged within 2 hours."
*   **Action:** Analyze if certain ticket types are being escalated unnecessarily. If Level-1 agents can resolve 80% of cases (like 101 & 103), invest in training Level-1 agents to handle the specific issues that are currently causing escalations (like those in 105).

**Recommendation 2: Implement Shift Coverage or On-Call Rotation**
*   **Issue:** The 19-28 hour gaps indicate work stops when the primary shift ends.
*   **Action:** Introduce a follow-the-sun model or an evening/weekend on-call rotation. If a ticket is assigned at 13:00 (Case 104), it should ideally be investigated that same day rather than waiting until 08:00 the next morning.

**Recommendation 3: Standardize Triage and Assignment**
*   **Issue:** Triage times vary wildly (5 mins vs. 40 mins).
*   **Action:** Automate initial categorization where possible to reduce the "Receive to Triage" time. Set a target KPI for Triage completion (e.g., under 15 minutes) to ensure tickets enter the agent queue faster.

**Recommendation 4: Monitor Agent Workload**
*   **Issue:** Case 104 sat for 3.5 hours after assignment.
*   **Action:** Review the workload distribution among Level-1 agents. If one agent is assigned too many tickets, the "Assign to Investigate" gap will widen. Implement a dynamic load-balancing system to ensure tickets are picked up immediately upon assignment.

**Summary:**
The primary driver of delay is **waiting time**, not **working time**. By focusing on reducing the queue times between activities (specifically the Escalation-to-Investigate gap and the Overnight gaps), the support team can reduce the average resolution time from ~25 hours to closer to the baseline of ~2 hours.
