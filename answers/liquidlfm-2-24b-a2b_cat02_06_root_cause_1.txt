### Step 3: Analyze Potential Root Causes

#### **Case 102:**
- **Escalation:** Ticket escalated to Level-2 Agent at 11:30 (after Level-1 assigned at 09:00).
- **Waiting Time:** From assignment (09:00) to escalation (11:30) = 2.5 hours.
- **Investigation:** Only started at 11:30, after escalation.
- **Possible Bottleneck:** Escalation delays and waiting time before investigation.

#### **Case 105:**
- **Escalation:** Escalated to Level-2 Agent at 14:00 (after Level-1 assigned at 09:10).
- **Waiting Time:** From assignment (09:10) to escalation (14:00) = 6 hours 50 minutes.
- **Investigation:** Only started after escalation.
- **Possible Bottleneck:** Long waiting time before escalation and investigation.

#### **General Patterns:**
- **Escalations:** Both long cases required escalation, which introduced significant waiting time before investigation.
- **Long Gaps:** Both cases had long delays between assignment and escalation, and between escalation and investigation.
- **No Immediate Investigation:** Investigation did not start until escalation occurred.

---

### Step 4: Insights and Recommendations

**Root Causes:**
1. **Escalation Delays:** Escalations introduce long waiting periods before investigation begins.
2. **Long Waiting Times:** Assigning tickets to Level-1 agents does not always lead to immediate investigation; delays occur before escalation.
3. **Inefficient Triage:** Triage steps do not always result in prompt escalation when needed.

**Recommendations:**
- **Review Escalation Triggers:** Ensure escalation criteria are clear and only used when truly necessary.
- **Reduce Waiting Times:** Implement SLA for escalation and investigation start times.
- **Improve Triage Efficiency:** Assign tickets to agents with clear escalation protocols and prioritize tickets requiring escalation.
- **Monitor Agent Workloads:** Ensure agents are not overloaded, which may cause delays in investigation and escalation.
- **Automate Alerts:** Set up automated alerts for escalation thresholds to reduce manual delays.
