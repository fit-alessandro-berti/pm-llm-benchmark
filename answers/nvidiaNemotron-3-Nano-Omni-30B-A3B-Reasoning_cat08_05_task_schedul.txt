**2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies**

Based on the performance analysis, key pathologies include:

*   **Bottleneck Identification:** Process mining can identify machines with consistently high queue times or low utilization (idle time) despite high workload. For example, if CUT-01 shows consistently high queue times and long processing times, it's a bottleneck. Quantifying its impact involves calculating the bottleneck's effect on overall throughput (e.g., jobs waiting at CUT-01 cause delays for all subsequent tasks).
*   **Poor Prioritization:** Analyzing job completion times relative to due dates can reveal if high-priority or near-due date jobs are frequently delayed. Comparing the average completion time of high-priority vs. low-priority jobs can reveal if prioritization is effective.
*   **Suboptimal Sequencing:** Analyzing task sequences on bottleneck machines (e.g., CUT-01) can reveal if jobs with similar setup requirements are processed consecutively (reducing setup time) or if dissimilar jobs are sequenced, causing frequent and lengthy setup changes. Comparing average in-process times for jobs with similar vs. dissimilar preceding tasks can quantify setup time impact.
*   **Resource Starvation:** Process mining can reveal if upstream tasks (e.g., cutting) are scheduled in a way that causes downstream resources (e.g., MILL-03) to wait excessively, indicating starvation. This is often caused by poor sequencing or bottlenecks upstream.
*   **Bullwhip Effect:** Analyzing WIP levels across different stages (e.g., WIP between Cutting and Milling) can reveal if WIP levels fluctuate dramatically (high variance), indicating a bullwhip effect caused by scheduling variability (e.g., frequent priority changes, inconsistent task durations).

**3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness**

Potential root causes include:

*   **Limitations of Static Dispatching Rules:** Rules like "First-Come-First-Served" or "Earliest Due Date" are static and don't adapt to real-time conditions (e.g., a high-priority job arriving while a critical machine is busy). Process mining can show if these rules lead to suboptimal resource allocation (e.g., high-priority jobs waiting long periods).
*   **Lack of Real-Time Visibility:** The current system likely lacks real-time data on machine availability, queue lengths, and job progress. Process mining can reveal the actual, dynamic state of the shop floor, exposing gaps in visibility.
*   **Inaccurate Estimates:** The "Task Duration (Planned)" might be based on outdated or generic estimates. Process mining can compare "Task Duration (Actual)" to "Task Duration (Planned)" to identify systematic over- or under-estimation, potentially due to lack of historical data or complexity factors.
*   **Ineffective Sequence-Dependent Setup Handling:** The analysis of setup times (as mentioned above) directly addresses this. If setup times are consistently long and variable, it's a clear sign of poor handling of sequence-dependent setups.
*   **Poor Coordination:** The "Queue Entry" events for different tasks on different machines can reveal if there's a lack of coordination (e.g., a job is queued for MILL-03 while MILL-02 is down, causing a delay).
*   **Inadequate Disruption Response:** The "Breakdown Start" event shows a disruption, but the process mining analysis would reveal how long the resulting delays were and how the schedule was (or wasn't) adjusted.
