

# Analysis of Anomalies in the Insurance Claim POWL Model

## 1. Identified Anomalies

### Anomaly 1: Loop Between Evaluation and Approval (LOOP(E, P))

The `LOOP(E, P)` construct means the process executes **Evaluate (E)**, then optionally repeats by executing **Approve (P)** followed by **Evaluate (E)** again, potentially multiple times. This is semantically problematic:

- **Re-evaluation after approval** suggests that a claim can be approved and then sent back for evaluation repeatedly, which undermines the finality of an approval decision.
- In a well-designed process, approval should follow evaluation exactly once (or evaluation might loop independently for rework, but approval should be a terminal decision gate, not a mechanism for looping back).
- The loop also permits exiting after just **E** without ever executing **P**, meaning a claim could be evaluated but never approved, yet the process would still proceed to notification/closure.

### Anomaly 2: XOR Allowing Skip of Customer Notification

The `XOR(N, skip)` construct means the process can choose between notifying the customer or silently skipping notification entirely. This is problematic because:

- Customer notification is typically a **regulatory or contractual obligation** — customers have a right to know the outcome of their claim.
- Skipping notification silently (via a `SilentTransition`) means there's no auditable record that a conscious decision was made to defer or waive notification.
- This could lead to customer complaints, compliance violations, or legal exposure.

### Anomaly 3: Premature Claim Closure via Partial Ordering

The partial order includes the edge `A  C` (Assign Adjuster  Close Claim) **without** requiring the loop (evaluation/approval) or the XOR (notification) to complete first. The edge `loop  xor` exists, but there is **no edge from `xor  C`**. This means:

- **C can execute concurrently with or before the loop and xor**, since the only constraint on C is that A must precede it.
- A claim could be **closed immediately after adjuster assignment**, bypassing evaluation, approval, and notification entirely.
- This represents a severe control flow deficiency — the partial order fails to enforce the intended sequential dependency chain.

## 2. Hypotheses for Why These Anomalies Exist

### Hypothesis H1: Partial Implementation of a Rework/Appeal Process
The loop between E and P may reflect an intended **rework cycle** where claims that fail initial evaluation are sent back for re-assessment. However, the implementation incorrectly models the loop body as `(E, P)` with P as the redo branch, rather than modeling a proper decision gateway where disapproval triggers re-evaluation. This suggests **incomplete translation of business requirements** into the process model — perhaps the modeler conflated "approval" with "decision to re-evaluate."

### Hypothesis H2: Expedited Closure Path for Special Cases
The `A  C` edge (bypassing evaluation and notification) may have been intentionally introduced to handle **withdrawn claims, duplicate submissions, or trivially invalid claims** that don't require full processing. However, instead of modeling this as an explicit, auditable exception path with its own activities (e.g., "Mark as Duplicate," "Record Withdrawal"), it was implemented as a silent shortcut in the partial order — likely due to **miscommunication between business analysts and process modelers** about how exceptions should be represented.

### Hypothesis H3: Notification Made Optional Due to System Integration Issues
The XOR skip on notification may have been introduced because the **notification system was unreliable or not yet integrated** at the time of deployment. Rather than blocking the entire process on a failing downstream system, the team made notification optional as a **technical workaround** that was never reverted. Alternatively, some claim types (e.g., very small amounts) might not require formal notification per internal policy, but this was modeled too broadly.

### Hypothesis H4: Missing Ordering Constraint is a Modeling Error
The absence of an `xor  C` edge is likely a **simple oversight** in the process model definition. The modeler correctly added `R  A  loop  xor` but forgot to close the chain with `xor  C`. The presence of `A  C` may have been a debugging artifact or an attempt to ensure C was reachable, without realizing it created a premature closure path. This points to **inadequate validation tooling** — the process modeler's tool did not flag that C could execute before loop and xor completed.

### Hypothesis H5: Departmental Misalignment
Different departments may own different parts of the process. The claims intake team designed R  A  C as their view of the process (receive, assign, close), while the adjustments team designed A  E  P  N as theirs. When these were merged, the **conflicting assumptions about process boundaries** resulted in redundant paths and missing constraints.

## 3. Database Queries to Verify Hypotheses

### Query 1: Claims Closed Without Evaluation or Approval

This verifies whether the premature closure path (Anomaly 3) is actually exercised in practice.

```sql
-- Find claims that have a "Close Claim" event but lack either "Evaluate Claim" or "Approve Claim"
SELECT 
    c.claim_id,
    c.claim_type,
    c.claim_amount,
    c.submission_date,
    close_evt.timestamp AS close_timestamp
FROM claims c
JOIN claim_events close_evt 
    ON c.claim_id = close_evt.claim_id 
    AND close_evt.activity = 'C'
WHERE NOT EXISTS (
    SELECT 1 FROM claim_events e 
    WHERE e.claim_id = c.claim_id AND e.activity = 'E'
)
OR NOT EXISTS (
    SELECT 1 FROM claim_events e 
    WHERE e.claim_id = c.claim_id AND e.activity = 'P'
)
ORDER BY close_evt.timestamp DESC;
```

### Query 2: Claims Closed Before Evaluation or Approval Completed

Even if both events exist, closure might have occurred *before* them — verifying the ordering anomaly.

```sql
-- Find claims where "Close Claim" happened before "Evaluate Claim" or "Approve Claim"
SELECT 
    c.claim_id,
    c.claim_type,
    close_evt.timestamp AS close_time,
    eval_evt.timestamp AS eval_time,
    approve_evt.timestamp AS approve_time
FROM claims c
JOIN claim_events close_evt 
    ON c.claim_id = close_evt.claim_id AND close_evt.activity = 'C'
LEFT JOIN claim_events eval_evt 
    ON c.claim_id = eval_evt.claim_id AND eval_evt.activity = 'E'
LEFT JOIN claim_events approve_evt 
    ON c.claim_id = approve_evt.claim_id AND approve_evt.activity = 'P'
WHERE close_evt.timestamp < eval_evt.timestamp
   OR close_evt.timestamp < approve_evt.timestamp
   OR eval_evt.timestamp IS NULL
   OR approve_evt.timestamp IS NULL
ORDER BY c.claim_id;
```

### Query 3: Claims with Multiple Evaluation-Approval Cycles (Loop Anomaly)

This checks whether the loop between E and P actually produces repeated cycles in the data.

```sql
-- Count the number of Evaluate and Approve events per claim to detect looping
SELECT 
    ce.claim_id,
    COUNT(CASE WHEN ce.activity = 'E' THEN 1 END) AS eval_count,
    COUNT(CASE WHEN ce.activity = 'P' THEN 1 END) AS approve_count
FROM claim_events ce
WHERE ce.activity IN ('E', 'P')
GROUP BY ce.claim_id
HAVING COUNT(CASE WHEN ce.activity = 'E' THEN 1 END) > 1
    OR COUNT(CASE WHEN ce.activity = 'P' THEN 1 END) > 1
ORDER BY eval_count DESC, approve_count DESC;
```

### Query 4: Sequence Pattern of E/P Events for Looping Claims

For claims with multiple E/P events, reconstruct the sequence to see if it matches the LOOP(E, P) pattern (E, P, E, P, E, ...).

```sql
-- Reconstruct the E/P sequence for claims with repeated evaluations
WITH loop_events AS (
    SELECT 
        claim_id,
        activity,
        timestamp,
        ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY claim_id ORDER BY timestamp) AS seq
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity IN ('E', 'P')
)
SELECT 
    claim_id,
    STRING_AGG(activity, ' -> ' ORDER BY seq) AS activity_sequence,
    COUNT(*) AS total_loop_steps
FROM loop_events
GROUP BY claim_id
HAVING COUNT(*) > 2
ORDER BY total_loop_steps DESC;
```

### Query 5: Frequency of Skipped Customer Notifications

This verifies how often the XOR skip path is taken (Anomaly 2).

```sql
-- Compare claims that received notification vs. those that didn't
SELECT 
    notified.status,
    COUNT(*) AS claim_count,
    ROUND(100.0 * COUNT(*) / SUM(COUNT(*)) OVER (), 2) AS percentage
FROM (
    SELECT 
        c.claim_id,
        CASE 
            WHEN EXISTS (
                SELECT 1 FROM claim_events ce 
                WHERE ce.claim_id = c.claim_id AND ce.activity = 'N'
            ) THEN 'Notified'
            ELSE 'Skipped'
        END AS status
    FROM claims c
    WHERE EXISTS (
        SELECT 1 FROM claim_events ce 
        WHERE ce.claim_id = c.claim_id AND ce.activity = 'C'
    )
) notified
GROUP BY notified.status;
```

### Query 6: Correlation Between Skipped Notification and Claim Characteristics

Investigate whether notification skipping correlates with claim type, amount, or adjuster — to test H3 (system integration) vs. intentional policy.

```sql
-- Analyze characteristics of claims where notification was skipped
SELECT 
    c.claim_type,
    CASE 
        WHEN c.claim_amount < 1000 THEN 'Low (<1K)'
        WHEN c.claim_amount < 10000 THEN 'Medium (1K-10K)'
        ELSE 'High (>10K)'
    END AS amount_bracket,
    COUNT(*) AS total_claims,
    SUM(CASE WHEN n.claim_id IS NULL THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS skipped_notification,
    ROUND(100.0 * SUM(CASE WHEN n.claim_id IS NULL THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) / COUNT(*), 2) AS skip_rate_pct
FROM claims c
JOIN claim_events close_evt 
    ON c.claim_id = close_evt.claim_id AND close_evt.activity = 'C'
LEFT JOIN claim_events n 
    ON c.claim_id = n.claim_id AND n.activity = 'N'
GROUP BY c.claim_type, 
    CASE 
        WHEN c.claim_amount < 1000 THEN 'Low (<1K)'
        WHEN c.claim_amount < 10000 THEN 'Medium (1K-10K)'
        ELSE 'High (>10K)'
    END
ORDER BY skip_rate_pct DESC;
```

### Query 7: Adjuster Involvement in Anomalous Traces

Determine whether specific adjusters or regions are associated with anomalous process executions — to test H5 (departmental misalignment).

```sql
-- Check if certain adjusters are disproportionately associated with premature closures
SELECT 
    adj.name AS adjuster_name,
    adj.specialization,
    adj.region,
    COUNT(DISTINCT c.claim_id) AS total_claims,
    SUM(CASE WHEN no_eval.claim_id IS NOT NULL THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS closed_without_eval,
    SUM(CASE WHEN multi_approve.claim_id IS NOT NULL THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS multiple_approvals
FROM adjusters adj
JOIN claim_events assign_evt 
    ON adj.name = assign_evt.resource AND assign_evt.activity = 'A'
JOIN claims c ON assign_evt.claim_id = c.claim_id
-- Flag: closed without evaluation
LEFT JOIN (
    SELECT ce.claim_id
    FROM claim_events ce
    WHERE ce.activity = 'C'
    AND NOT EXISTS (
        SELECT 1 FROM claim_events e2 
        WHERE e2.claim_id = ce.claim_id AND e2.activity = 'E'
    )
) no_eval ON c.claim_id = no_eval.claim_id
-- Flag: multiple approvals
LEFT JOIN (
    SELECT claim_id
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity = 'P'
    GROUP BY claim_id
    HAVING COUNT(*) > 1
) multi_approve ON c.claim_id = multi_approve.claim_id
GROUP BY adj.name, adj.specialization, adj.region
ORDER BY closed_without_eval DESC, multiple_approvals DESC;
```

### Query 8: Temporal Analysis — When Did Anomalies Start?

Test H1 (partial rule changes) by checking whether anomalous patterns appeared at a specific point in time.

```sql
-- Track the emergence of anomalous patterns over time
SELECT 
    DATE_TRUNC('month', c.submission_date) AS month,
    COUNT(DISTINCT c.claim_id) AS total_claims,
    -- Premature closures
    COUNT(DISTINCT CASE 
        WHEN NOT EXISTS (
            SELECT 1 FROM claim_events e 
            WHERE e.claim_id = c.claim_id AND e.activity = 'E'
        ) AND EXISTS (
            SELECT 1 FROM claim_events e 
            WHERE e.claim_id = c.claim_id AND e.activity = 'C'
        ) THEN c.claim_id 
    END) AS premature_closures,
    -- Skipped notifications
    COUNT(DISTINCT CASE 
        WHEN NOT EXISTS (
            SELECT 1 FROM claim_events e 
            WHERE e.claim_id = c.claim_id AND e.activity = 'N'
        ) AND EXISTS (
            SELECT 1 FROM claim_events e 
            WHERE e.claim_id = c.claim_id AND e.activity = 'C'
        ) THEN c.claim_id 
    END) AS skipped_notifications,
    -- Multi-approval loops
    COUNT(DISTINCT CASE 
        WHEN (
            SELECT COUNT(*) FROM claim_events e 
            WHERE e.claim_id = c.claim_id AND e.activity = 'P'
        ) > 1 THEN c.claim_id 
    END) AS multi_approval_claims
FROM claims c
GROUP BY DATE_TRUNC('month', c.submission_date)
ORDER BY month;
```

## Summary

| Anomaly | Impact | Most Likely Root Cause | Key Verification Query |
|---------|--------|----------------------|----------------------|
| **LOOP(E, P)** — repeated eval/approve cycles | Inefficiency, decision instability | Mismodeled rework/appeal process (H1) | Queries 3 & 4 |
| **XOR(N, skip)** — optional notification | Compliance risk, customer dissatisfaction | System integration workaround (H3) or overbroad policy exception | Queries 5 & 6 |
| **A  C without loop/xor dependency** — premature closure | Claims closed without due process | Modeling error / missing edge (H4), possibly compounded by exception path needs (H2) | Queries 1, 2 & 7 |

The most critical fix in the model would be adding the missing `xor  C` edge and removing the direct `A  C` edge (or replacing it with an explicit, auditable exception subprocess). The loop semantics should be revisited to properly separate rework from approval, and the notification skip should either be removed or replaced with an explicit, logged decision activity.
