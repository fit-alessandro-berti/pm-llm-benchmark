   - The `precedence` constraint only enforces `C` must follow `R`, enabling paths like `R  C` (skipping A, E, P, N) or `R  A  C` (skipping E, P, N). The model permits closing a claim without evaluation or approval.  
   - The `existence` constraint only mandates `C`, ignoring mandatory intermediate steps like E and P.  

2. **Lack of enforced sequence between core activities**:  
   - No constraints enforce that `E` must precede `P`, `P` precedes `N`, or `N` precedes `C`. This allows invalid sequences like `R  A  P  C` (skipping E) or `R  A  E  N  C` (skipping P).  

3. **Missing constraints for activity dependencies**:  
   - No rule ensures `A` must be followed by `E` after assignment. A path like `R  A  C` is allowed, violating the required workflow.  

4. **Insufficient constraint granularity**:  
   - The `precedence` for `C` only references `R`, not requiring `C` to occur after all prior steps. This permits `C` to appear anywhere after `R`, even if earlier steps are skipped.

3. **Misinterpretation of `responded existence`:
