### **Advanced Process Mining and Scheduling Optimization Strategy for Precision Parts Inc.**

As a Senior Operations Analyst, I propose a comprehensive framework leveraging **Process Mining** to transform raw MES event logs into actionable intelligence, followed by the design of **Advanced Scheduling Strategies** that address the specific complexities of Precision Parts Inc.'s job shop (sequence-dependent setups, disruptions, high-mix variability).

---

### **1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics**

To reconstruct the shop floor reality, we will apply **Process Mining** techniques to the MES event logs. The raw logs contain heterogeneous events (Job, Task, Resource), requiring preprocessing to align with process mining standards.

#### **A. Process Reconstruction and Technique Application**
*   **Event Log Enrichment:** We will enrich the log by calculating derived attributes. For instance, `Queue Duration` is calculated as the difference between `Queue Entry` and `Task Start`. `Setup Duration` is derived from `Setup Start` to `Setup End`.
*   **Trace Reconstruction:** A "Trace" represents the complete journey of a Job ID. We will reconstruct traces by sorting events by timestamp within each Case ID. This allows us to analyze the full lifecycle of jobs, including interruptions and priority changes.
*   **Conformance Checking:** We will compare the *actual* event log against a reference model of the standard routing (Cutting  Milling  Lathing  ...). This identifies deviations, such as skipped operations or rework loops, which contribute to lead time variability.

#### **B. Key Metrics and Quantification**
*   **Job Flow Times & Lead Times:**
    *   *Metric:* **Cycle Time** per trace (Time from `Job Released` to final `Task End`).
    *   *Analysis:* We will generate probability density functions for lead times, distinguishing between *Throughput Time* (total time) and *Process Time* (value-added time). High variance in these distributions indicates unpredictability.
*   **Task Waiting Times:**
    *   *Metric:* **Waiting Time** at each resource.
    *   *Analysis:* By filtering events by `Resource` and `Activity`, we calculate the average and percentile waiting times per work center. This highlights congestion points.
*   **Resource Utilization:**
    *   *Metric:* **Resource Performance Analysis**.
    *   *Analysis:* Utilization is decomposed into:
        *   *Productive Time:* Sum of `Task Duration (Actual)`.
        *   *Setup Time:* Sum of `Setup Duration`.
        *   *Idle Time:* Gaps between `Task End` and next `Task Start` or `Queue Entry`.
        *   *Unplanned Downtime:* Extracted from `Breakdown Start/End` events.
*   **Sequence-Dependent Setup Times:**
    *   *Technique:* **Resource Sequence Analysis**.
    *   *Analysis:* We group events by `Resource` and sort chronologically. We extract every `Setup Start` event and link it to the `Previous Job` mentioned in the notes or infer it from the immediately preceding task on that resource.
    *   *Output:* A **Setup Time Matrix** (e.g., Setup Time from Job Type A to Job Type B). This quantifies the "cost" of switching jobs, revealing if the current schedule is incurring excessive setup penalties.
*   **Schedule Adherence & Tardiness:**
    *   *Metric:* **Tardiness** = `max(0, Task End - Order Due Date)`.
    *   *Analysis:* We calculate the percentage of jobs meeting due dates and the distribution of tardiness for late jobs. We also measure **Schedule Stability** by comparing planned vs. actual start times.
*   **Impact of Disruptions:**
    *   *Technique:* **Event Log Filtering & Correlation**.
    *   *Analysis:* We isolate traces containing `Breakdown` or `Priority Change` events. We measure the **Disruption Propagation Delay**, calculating how a breakdown on `MILL-02` increased the tardiness of downstream jobs or forced rescheduling of `JOB-7005`.

### **2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies**

Based on the mining results, we identify specific pathologies causing the reported challenges.

*   **Bottleneck Identification & Impact:**
    *   *Pathology:* **Bottleneck Starvation vs. Overload.**
    *   *Evidence:* Resource performance analysis will likely show one or two machines (e.g., `CNC MILL-03` or `HEAT TREAT-01`) with >90% utilization, long queue lengths, and high waiting times for downstream resources. Conversely, non-bottleneck machines may show low utilization but high idle time because they are waiting for the bottleneck.
    *   *Impact:* The bottleneck dictates the shop's throughput. Any setup time or breakdown here directly reduces total output and increases WIP accumulation upstream.
*   **Poor Task Prioritization:**
    *   *Pathology:* **Local Optimization vs. Global Due Dates.**
    *   *Evidence:* **Variant Analysis** comparing traces of *On-Time* vs. *Late* jobs. If late jobs show significantly higher waiting times at specific resources despite having earlier due dates, it indicates the dispatching rule (e.g., FCFS) is ignoring priority or slack time. We may find that "Medium" priority jobs are blocking "High" priority jobs due to local queue management.
*   **Suboptimal Sequencing & Setup Inefficiency:**
    *   *Pathology:* **High Sequence-Dependent Setup Costs.**
    *   *Evidence:* Analysis of the **Setup Time Matrix** combined with the execution sequence. If the matrix shows high variance (e.g., switching from "Aluminum" to "Titanium" takes 45 mins, but "Steel" to "Steel" takes 5 mins), and the mining reveals frequent switches between dissimilar jobs on bottleneck machines, total productive time is eroded by setups.
*   **WIP Bullwhip & Starvation:**
    *   *Pathology:* **Uncoordinated Release and Processing.**
    *   *Evidence:* Correlation analysis between `Job Released` timestamps and `Queue Entry` times. If jobs are released faster than the bottleneck can process, WIP accumulates. Additionally, **Resource Contention Heatmaps** may show instances where a machine is idle while jobs are waiting for *other* resources, indicating routing errors or lack of global visibility.

### **3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness**

Process mining allows us to differentiate between **Scheduling Logic Failures** and **Capacity/Process Constraints**.

*   **Static vs. Dynamic Environment:**
    *   *Root Cause:* The current rules (FCFS/EDD) are static. They do not adapt to real-time machine availability or queue lengths.
    *   *PM Evidence:* We observe high tardiness even when overall shop capacity is sufficient. This suggests the *allocation* of work is wrong, not the *amount* of work. For example, a machine might be idle while a job waits for a different machine, which is a scheduling routing issue.
*   **Inaccurate Estimations:**
    *   *Root Cause:* **Planned vs. Actual Discrepancy.**
    *   *PM Evidence:* Comparison of `Task Duration (Planned)` vs. `Task Duration (Actual)`. If actual times consistently exceed planned times (e.g., planned 60 min, actual 66.4 min), schedules are overly optimistic. This leads to unrealistic due dates and cascading delays.
*   **Ignored Sequence Dependencies:**
    *   *Root Cause:* Scheduling logic does not account for setup times.
    *   *PM Evidence:* The setup matrix reveals significant setup costs, yet the sequence of jobs on bottleneck machines appears random with respect to job attributes (material, geometry). This confirms setups are being treated as constant or ignored.
*   **Reactive Disruption Handling:**
    *   *Root Cause:* No buffer or rescheduling protocol for breakdowns.
    *   *PM Evidence:* Analysis of breakdown events shows that when a machine fails, subsequent jobs are delayed by the full duration of the breakdown plus recovery time, with no evidence of jobs being rerouted or expedited elsewhere.
*   **Differentiation via PM:**
    *   *Logic vs. Capacity:* If a resource has high utilization (>95%) and queues are long, the root cause is **Capacity**. If utilization is moderate (<80%) but queues are long, the root cause is **Scheduling Logic** (e.g., poor prioritization, wrong routing, or starvation). This distinction is critical for resource investment decisions.

### **4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies**

We propose three strategies that leverage the insights from process mining to address the diagnosed pathologies.

#### **Strategy 1: Multi-Attribute Dynamic Dispatching (MADD) with Setup Prediction**
*   **Core Logic:** Replace static rules with a dynamic scoring function at each dispatching point. When a job completes a task, the scheduler evaluates all waiting jobs for the next machine and selects the one with the highest priority score.
*   **Scoring Function:**
    $$Score(J) = w_1 \cdot \frac{1}{Slack_J} + w_2 \cdot \frac{1}{RemainingProcessTime_J} + w_3 \cdot Priority_J - w_4 \cdot EstimatedSetupTime(PreviousJob, J)$$
    *   *Slack:* Due Date minus Current Time minus Remaining Processing.
    *   *Setup Prediction:* Use the mined **Setup Time Matrix** to predict the setup cost if Job J is selected next. This penalizes jobs that cause long setups.
*   **PM Insight:** Weights ($w$) are tuned based on the analysis of tardiness root causes. The setup matrix is directly mined from logs.
*   **Impact:** Reduces total setup time, improves due date adherence by protecting slack, and balances load by considering remaining work.

#### **Strategy 2: Probabilistic Scheduling with Robust Buffers**
*   **Core Logic:** Move from deterministic scheduling to **Probabilistic Scheduling**. Instead of using mean task times, use the distributions mined from `Task Duration (Actual)`.
*   **Implementation:**
    *   Generate schedules using **Monte Carlo Simulation** to predict completion times with confidence intervals (e.g., 90% probability of completion by Date X).
    *   Insert **Time Buffers** at bottleneck resources based on the variability (standard deviation) of task times and breakdown frequencies.
    *   If a breakdown occurs, the scheduler automatically recalculates the critical path using the probabilistic model and adjusts priorities dynamically.
*   **PM Insight:** Distributions of task times, breakdown frequency/duration, and recovery times.
*   **Impact:** More accurate customer lead times, reduced panic/expediting, and improved resilience to variability.

#### **Strategy 3: Bottleneck-Centric Setup Optimization (BCSO)**
*   **Core Logic:** Focus scheduling intelligence specifically on the identified bottleneck resource(s). The goal is to maximize the throughput of the bottleneck by minimizing non-productive time.
*   **Implementation:**
    *   **Batching/Sequencing:** At the bottleneck, sequence jobs to minimize setup times using a heuristic based on the **Setup Matrix** (e.g., solving a Traveling Salesman Problem variant for the queue).
    *   **Setup Reduction:** Analyze the setup matrix to identify "families" of jobs that share similar setups. Encourage grouping these jobs even if it slightly delays individual due dates, provided the global tardiness improves.
    *   **Decoupling:** Use WIP buffers *before* the bottleneck to ensure it is never starved, but limit WIP *after* the bottleneck to prevent overload.
*   **PM Insight:** Bottleneck identification, Setup Time Matrix, Queue length analysis.
*   **Impact:** Significant reduction in WIP, increased throughput, and reduced lead time variability by stabilizing the flow through the constraint.

### **5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement**

#### **A. Discrete-Event Simulation (DES) for Validation**
Before live deployment, we will build a **Digital Twin** of the shop floor using DES software (e.g., Simio, Arena, or Python-based frameworks).
*   **Parameterization:** The simulation model will be parameterized with data derived from process mining:
    *   Task time distributions (not just averages).
    *   Routing probabilities and deviations.
    *   Setup time matrix.
    *   Breakdown frequency and duration distributions.
    *   Arrival patterns of new jobs.
*   **Scenario Testing:**
    *   *Baseline:* Current FCFS/EDD rules.
    *   *Strategy A:* MADD with Setup Prediction.
    *   *Strategy B:* Probabilistic Scheduling.
    *   *Strategy C:* BCSO.
    *   *Stress Tests:* High load scenarios (110% capacity), frequent breakdowns (double frequency), and urgent hot-job injection rates.
*   **Evaluation:** Compare strategies on KPIs: Tardiness %, WIP Level, Makespan, Utilization, and Setup Time %. This allows us to select the best strategy and tune parameters without risking production.

#### **B. Continuous Improvement Framework**
Scheduling is not a one-time fix. We implement a **Continuous Improvement Loop**:
1.  **Online Process Mining:** Deploy a lightweight process mining engine connected to the MES in real-time.
2.  **KPI Dashboard:** Monitor real-time performance of the scheduling strategy against targets.
3.  **Drift Detection:** Automatically detect when actual performance drifts from expected distributions (e.g., task times suddenly increase due to a new material, or breakdowns spike).
4.  **Adaptive Tuning:**
    *   If drift is detected, the system triggers a re-evaluation of the scheduling parameters.
    *   For example, if `Task Duration (Actual)` consistently exceeds predictions, the probabilistic scheduler automatically increases buffer sizes.
    *   If the setup matrix changes (new tooling or materials), the mining engine updates the matrix, and the MADD strategy adapts immediately.
5.  **Feedback Loop:** The system logs the outcome of scheduling decisions, allowing for reinforcement learning approaches where the scheduler learns which rules work best under specific conditions over time.
