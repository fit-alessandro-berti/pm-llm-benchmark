## 1. Key Temporal Anomalies

### A. Receive to Approve: `R  P`
- **Average:** ~25 hours  
- **Standard deviation:** 1 hour  
- **Why unusual:** Approval occurs at almost the same elapsed time after claim receipt across many cases. The low variation is suspicious because real claim approval times usually vary by claim type, claim amount, adjuster availability, workload, and complexity.

Possible interpretations:
- Approvals may be batch-processed on a fixed schedule.
- Approval timestamps may be system-generated or rounded.
- Some required intermediate activities may not be properly recorded.
- Claims may be auto-approved after a fixed waiting period.

---

### B. Approve to Notify: `P  N`
- **Average:** 7 days  
- **Standard deviation:** 2 days  
- **Why unusual:** Notification happens very late after approval, and the variation is large. Once a claim is approved, customer notification would typically be expected quickly.

Possible interpretations:
- Customer notifications are delayed due to manual processing.
- Notification jobs may run weekly or in delayed batches.
- There may be backlog in a communication or customer service team.
- Some notifications may fail and require retries.
- Certain claim types, regions, or resources may be associated with long delays.

---

### C. Assign to Close: `A  C`
- **Average:** 2 hours  
- **Standard deviation:** 1 hour  
- **Why unusual:** Claims appear to be closed shortly after assignment. If this happens without evaluation and approval, it may indicate premature closures or skipped process steps.

Possible interpretations:
- Some claims are rejected or withdrawn shortly after assignment.
- Duplicate or invalid claims may be closed quickly.
- Adjusters may be closing claims without completing all expected steps.
- Some events may be missing from the event log.
- Closure timestamps may be incorrectly recorded.

---

### D. Evaluate to Notify: `E  N`
- **Average:** 5 minutes  
- **Standard deviation:** 1 minute  
- **Why unusual:** Notification happens almost immediately after evaluation. This could be acceptable if notification is automated, but it is suspicious if approval is supposed to occur between evaluation and notification.

Possible interpretations:
- Notification may be automatically triggered immediately after evaluation.
- Approval may be skipped or not logged.
- Evaluation and approval may be combined operationally but recorded inconsistently.
- The recorded `E` timestamp may actually represent a later stage than intended.
- The process model may not match the actual operational process.

---

## 2. Verification Queries

### 2.1 Compute Actual Time Gaps Between Key Activities

This query calculates the first timestamp for each activity per claim, then computes elapsed time between relevant activity pairs.

```sql
WITH event_times AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'R') AS r_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'A') AS a_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'E') AS e_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'P') AS p_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'N') AS n_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'C') AS c_time
    FROM claim_events
    GROUP BY claim_id
)
SELECT
    claim_id,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (a_time - r_time)) AS r_to_a_seconds,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (e_time - r_time)) AS r_to_e_seconds,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (p_time - r_time)) AS r_to_p_seconds,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (c_time - a_time)) AS a_to_c_seconds,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n_time - e_time)) AS e_to_n_seconds,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (c_time - e_time)) AS e_to_c_seconds,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n_time - p_time)) AS p_to_n_seconds,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (c_time - n_time)) AS n_to_c_seconds
FROM event_times;
```

## 3. Detect Claims with Suspicious `R  P` Timing

The expected average is about 90,000 seconds with a standard deviation of 3,600 seconds. Claims outside a 2-standard-deviation range may be anomalous.

```sql
WITH event_times AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'R') AS r_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'P') AS p_time
    FROM claim_events
    GROUP BY claim_id
)
SELECT
    et.claim_id,
    c.claim_type,
    c.claim_amount,
    c.submission_date,
    et.r_time,
    et.p_time,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (et.p_time - et.r_time)) AS r_to_p_seconds
FROM event_times et
JOIN claims c
    ON c.claim_id = et.claim_id
WHERE et.r_time IS NOT NULL
  AND et.p_time IS NOT NULL
  AND ABS(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (et.p_time - et.r_time)) - 90000) > 2 * 3600
ORDER BY r_to_p_seconds DESC;
```

To inspect whether approval occurs at a rigid schedule:

```sql
WITH event_times AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'R') AS r_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'P') AS p_time
    FROM claim_events
    GROUP BY claim_id
)
SELECT
    DATE_TRUNC('hour', p_time) AS approval_hour,
    COUNT(*) AS approval_count
FROM event_times
WHERE r_time IS NOT NULL
  AND p_time IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY DATE_TRUNC('hour', p_time)
ORDER BY approval_count DESC;
```

## 4. Detect Long Delays from Approval to Notification

The expected average is 7 days. This query identifies claims where notification took longer than 11 days, which is more than 2 standard deviations above the average.

```sql
WITH event_times AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'P') AS p_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'N') AS n_time
    FROM claim_events
    GROUP BY claim_id
)
SELECT
    et.claim_id,
    c.claim_type,
    c.claim_amount,
    c.submission_date,
    et.p_time,
    et.n_time,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (et.n_time - et.p_time)) / 86400.0 AS p_to_n_days
FROM event_times et
JOIN claims c
    ON c.claim_id = et.claim_id
WHERE et.p_time IS NOT NULL
  AND et.n_time IS NOT NULL
  AND EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (et.n_time - et.p_time)) > 604800 + 2 * 172800
ORDER BY p_to_n_days DESC;
```

Check whether these delays are concentrated by claim type:

```sql
WITH event_times AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'P') AS p_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'N') AS n_time
    FROM claim_events
    GROUP BY claim_id
)
SELECT
    c.claim_type,
    COUNT(*) AS claim_count,
    AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (et.n_time - et.p_time)) / 86400.0) AS avg_p_to_n_days,
    STDDEV(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (et.n_time - et.p_time)) / 86400.0) AS stdev_p_to_n_days,
    MAX(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (et.n_time - et.p_time)) / 86400.0) AS max_p_to_n_days
FROM event_times et
JOIN claims c
    ON c.claim_id = et.claim_id
WHERE et.p_time IS NOT NULL
  AND et.n_time IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY c.claim_type
ORDER BY avg_p_to_n_days DESC;
```

Check whether delays are associated with notification resources:

```sql
WITH p_events AS (
    SELECT DISTINCT ON (claim_id)
        claim_id,
        timestamp AS p_time
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity = 'P'
    ORDER BY claim_id, timestamp
),
n_events AS (
    SELECT DISTINCT ON (claim_id)
        claim_id,
        timestamp AS n_time,
        resource AS notification_resource
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity = 'N'
    ORDER BY claim_id, timestamp
)
SELECT
    n.notification_resource,
    COUNT(*) AS claim_count,
    AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n.n_time - p.p_time)) / 86400.0) AS avg_p_to_n_days,
    STDDEV(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n.n_time - p.p_time)) / 86400.0) AS stdev_p_to_n_days,
    MAX(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n.n_time - p.p_time)) / 86400.0) AS max_p_to_n_days
FROM p_events p
JOIN n_events n
    ON n.claim_id = p.claim_id
GROUP BY n.notification_resource
ORDER BY avg_p_to_n_days DESC;
```

## 5. Identify Claims Closed Soon After Assignment

This query finds claims closed within 2 hours after assignment.

```sql
WITH event_times AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'A') AS a_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'E') AS e_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'P') AS p_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'C') AS c_time
    FROM claim_events
    GROUP BY claim_id
)
SELECT
    et.claim_id,
    c.claim_type,
    c.claim_amount,
    et.a_time,
    et.e_time,
    et.p_time,
    et.c_time,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (et.c_time - et.a_time)) / 3600.0 AS a_to_c_hours,
    CASE WHEN et.e_time IS NULL THEN 'Missing Evaluate' ELSE 'Has Evaluate' END AS evaluate_status,
    CASE WHEN et.p_time IS NULL THEN 'Missing Approve' ELSE 'Has Approve' END AS approve_status
FROM event_times et
JOIN claims c
    ON c.claim_id = et.claim_id
WHERE et.a_time IS NOT NULL
  AND et.c_time IS NOT NULL
  AND EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (et.c_time - et.a_time)) <= 7200
ORDER BY a_to_c_hours ASC;
```

Check whether quick closures are concentrated by claim type:

```sql
WITH event_times AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'A') AS a_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'C') AS c_time
    FROM claim_events
    GROUP BY claim_id
)
SELECT
    c.claim_type,
    COUNT(*) AS quick_close_count,
    AVG(c.claim_amount) AS avg_claim_amount,
    AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (et.c_time - et.a_time)) / 3600.0) AS avg_a_to_c_hours
FROM event_times et
JOIN claims c
    ON c.claim_id = et.claim_id
WHERE et.a_time IS NOT NULL
  AND et.c_time IS NOT NULL
  AND EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (et.c_time - et.a_time)) <= 7200
GROUP BY c.claim_type
ORDER BY quick_close_count DESC;
```

## 6. Detect Evaluation Followed Almost Immediately by Notification

This query finds claims where notification occurred within 5 minutes of evaluation.

```sql
WITH event_times AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'E') AS e_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'P') AS p_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'N') AS n_time
    FROM claim_events
    GROUP BY claim_id
)
SELECT
    et.claim_id,
    c.claim_type,
    c.claim_amount,
    et.e_time,
    et.p_time,
    et.n_time,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (et.n_time - et.e_time)) AS e_to_n_seconds,
    CASE
        WHEN et.p_time IS NULL THEN 'Missing Approve'
        WHEN et.p_time > et.n_time THEN 'Approve After Notify'
        WHEN et.p_time < et.e_time THEN 'Approve Before Evaluate'
        ELSE 'Approve Between Evaluate and Notify'
    END AS approval_sequence_status
FROM event_times et
JOIN claims c
    ON c.claim_id = et.claim_id
WHERE et.e_time IS NOT NULL
  AND et.n_time IS NOT NULL
  AND EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (et.n_time - et.e_time)) <= 300
ORDER BY e_to_n_seconds ASC;
```

## 7. Check for Skipped or Out-of-Order Process Steps

This query checks whether claims follow the intended sequence: `R  A  E  P  N  C`.

```sql
WITH event_times AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'R') AS r_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'A') AS a_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'E') AS e_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'P') AS p_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'N') AS n_time,
        MIN(timestamp) FILTER (WHERE activity = 'C') AS c_time
    FROM claim_events
    GROUP BY claim_id
)
SELECT
    claim_id,
    r_time,
    a_time,
    e_time,
    p_time,
    n_time,
    c_time,
    CASE
        WHEN r_time IS NULL THEN 'Missing Receive'
        WHEN a_time IS NULL THEN 'Missing Assign'
        WHEN e_time IS NULL THEN 'Missing Evaluate'
        WHEN p_time IS NULL THEN 'Missing Approve'
        WHEN n_time IS NULL THEN 'Missing Notify'
        WHEN c_time IS NULL THEN 'Missing Close'
        WHEN NOT (r_time <= a_time AND a_time <= e_time AND e_time <= p_time AND p_time <= n_time AND n_time <= c_time)
            THEN 'Out of Order'
        ELSE 'Expected Sequence'
    END AS sequence_status
FROM event_times
WHERE r_time IS NULL
   OR a_time IS NULL
   OR e_time IS NULL
   OR p_time IS NULL
   OR n_time IS NULL
   OR c_time IS NULL
   OR NOT (r_time <= a_time AND a_time <= e_time AND e_time <= p_time AND p_time <= n_time AND n_time <= c_time)
ORDER BY claim_id;
```

## 8. Correlate Timing Deviations with Resources

Since the event log contains the resource performing each activity, this query checks whether specific approval resources are associated with unusual `R  P` timing.

```sql
WITH r_events AS (
    SELECT DISTINCT ON (claim_id)
        claim_id,
        timestamp AS r_time
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity = 'R'
    ORDER BY claim_id, timestamp
),
p_events AS (
    SELECT DISTINCT ON (claim_id)
        claim_id,
        timestamp AS p_time,
        resource AS approval_resource
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity = 'P'
    ORDER BY claim_id, timestamp
)
SELECT
    p.approval_resource,
    COUNT(*) AS claim_count,
    AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (p.p_time - r.r_time)) / 3600.0) AS avg_r_to_p_hours,
    STDDEV(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (p.p_time - r.r_time)) / 3600.0) AS stdev_r_to_p_hours,
    MIN(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (p.p_time - r.r_time)) / 3600.0) AS min_r_to_p_hours,
    MAX(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (p.p_time - r.r_time)) / 3600.0) AS max_r_to_p_hours
FROM r_events r
JOIN p_events p
    ON p.claim_id = r.claim_id
GROUP BY p.approval_resource
ORDER BY stdev_r_to_p_hours ASC, avg_r_to_p_hours DESC;
```

## 9. Correlate Anomalies with Adjuster Region or Specialization

If the `resource` value for assignment or evaluation corresponds to an adjuster name, this query can associate timing patterns with adjuster metadata.

```sql
WITH a_events AS (
    SELECT DISTINCT ON (claim_id)
        claim_id,
        timestamp AS a_time,
        resource AS adjuster_resource
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity = 'A'
    ORDER BY claim_id, timestamp
),
c_events AS (
    SELECT DISTINCT ON (claim_id)
        claim_id,
        timestamp AS c_time
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity = 'C'
    ORDER BY claim_id, timestamp
)
SELECT
    adj.region,
    adj.specialization,
    COUNT(*) AS claim_count,
    AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (c.c_time - a.a_time)) / 3600.0) AS avg_a_to_c_hours,
    STDDEV(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (c.c_time - a.a_time)) / 3600.0) AS stdev_a_to_c_hours,
    COUNT(*) FILTER (
        WHERE EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (c.c_time - a.a_time)) <= 7200
    ) AS quick_close_count
FROM a_events a
JOIN c_events c
    ON c.claim_id = a.claim_id
LEFT JOIN adjusters adj
    ON adj.name = a.adjuster_resource
GROUP BY adj.region, adj.specialization
ORDER BY quick_close_count DESC;
```

## 10. Check Whether Notifications Are Batch-Processed

If many notifications occur at the same time or same day of week, this may explain the long `P  N` delay.

```sql
SELECT
    DATE_TRUNC('day', timestamp) AS notification_day,
    COUNT(*) AS notification_count
FROM claim_events
WHERE activity = 'N'
GROUP BY DATE_TRUNC('day', timestamp)
ORDER BY notification_count DESC;
```

Check by day of week and hour:

```sql
SELECT
    EXTRACT(DOW FROM timestamp) AS day_of_week,
    EXTRACT(HOUR FROM timestamp) AS hour_of_day,
    COUNT(*) AS notification_count
FROM claim_events
WHERE activity = 'N'
GROUP BY EXTRACT(DOW FROM timestamp), EXTRACT(HOUR FROM timestamp)
ORDER BY notification_count DESC;
```

## 11. Summary of Likely Causes and Validation Paths

| Anomaly | Likely Causes | SQL Validation |
|---|---|---|
| `R  P` has very low variation | Batch approval, auto-approval timer, timestamp rounding, missing intermediate steps | Analyze approval time distributions, group by approval hour/resource/claim type |
| `P  N` is very long and variable | Notification backlog, manual customer contact, failed notification retries, weekly batch jobs | Identify claims above 11 days, group by resource/claim type/day of week |
| `A  C` is very short | Premature closure, invalid/duplicate claims, missing evaluation/approval logs | Find closures within 2 hours and check missing `E`/`P` |
| `E  N` is extremely short | Automated notification, skipped approval, evaluation timestamp recorded late | Find `E  N <= 5 minutes` and check whether `P` exists and occurs between `E` and `N` |
