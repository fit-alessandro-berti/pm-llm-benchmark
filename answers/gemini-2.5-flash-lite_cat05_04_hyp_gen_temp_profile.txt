### Identified Anomalies

*   **Receive to Approve (R to P):** An average duration of approximately 25 hours with a very low standard deviation. This suggests a highly consistent, possibly rigid timing for approvals, which might not reflect a natural process flow.
*   **Approve to Notify (P to N):** An average delay of 7 days with a high standard deviation of 2 days. This indicates significant variability and potentially long backlogs in notifying customers after their claims are approved.
*   **Assign to Close (A to C):** An average closure time of 2 hours after assignment, without consistently observing intermediate steps like 'Evaluate' or 'Approve'. This could imply claims are being closed prematurely or that essential process steps are being bypassed.
*   **Evaluate to Notify (E to N):** An average time of only 5 minutes between evaluation and notification, with a standard deviation of 1 minute. This rapid, consistent transition might be too quick for manual intervention or decision-making, suggesting automated processes that could potentially skip critical checks.

---
### Hypotheses for Anomalies

*   **R to P (Receive to Approve):**
    *   **Rigid Automation:** Automated systems might enforce fixed approval cycles or batch processing at set times, irrespective of claim specifics.
    *   **Batch Processing:** Approvals could be handled in batches by a dedicated team at specific, regular intervals, leading to consistent timing.
    *   **Artificial Data/Rules:** The timing might be a result of synthetic data generation or predefined business rules that do not reflect real-world variability.

*   **P to N (Approve to Notify):**
    *   **Notification Backlog:** A manual or resource-constrained notification system might lead to delays in informing customers.
    *   **Process Segregation:** Different teams handling 'Approve' and 'Notify' could introduce handover delays or varying priorities.
    *   **System Queuing:** Notifications might be queued for batch sending, or system errors could cause delays in dispatch.

*   **A to C (Assign to Close):**
    *   **Premature Closure:** Claims might be closed erroneously or without full processing due to data entry errors, system bugs, or specific claim types that bypass standard steps.
    *   **Test/Voided Claims:** System tests or claims that are voided shortly after creation might be quickly closed without full evaluation.
    *   **Simplified Processes:** Certain claim types might have extremely streamlined paths that allow for rapid closure post-assignment, though the temporal model suggests this might be more widespread than expected.

*   **E to N (Evaluate to Notify):**
    *   **Automated Notifications:** Immediate notification triggers after an 'Evaluate' event (perhaps a system flag) suggest an automated, rapid communication path.
    *   **Skipped Steps:** The quick transition may indicate that the 'Evaluate' activity doesn't involve a thorough review, or that subsequent manual steps are being bypassed.
    *   **System Integration:** A tight integration between evaluation completion and a notification service could enforce this rapid sequence.

### Verification Queries

These SQL queries are designed to investigate the identified temporal anomalies within the `claim_events` and related tables.

**1. Investigating Receive to Approve (R to P) Timing Consistency:**
This query identifies claims where the duration between receiving a claim ('R') and approving it ('P') is significantly different from the expected average, helping to assess the variability (or lack thereof) implied by the low standard deviation.

```sql
WITH ClaimRP AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        MAX(CASE WHEN activity = 'R' THEN timestamp END) as receive_ts,
        MAX(CASE WHEN activity = 'P' THEN timestamp END) as approve_ts
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity IN ('R', 'P')
    GROUP BY claim_id
    HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT activity) = 2 -- Ensure both R and P exist for the claim
),
TimeDiffsRP AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (approve_ts - receive_ts)) as time_diff_seconds
    FROM ClaimRP
    WHERE approve_ts > receive_ts -- Ensure chronological order
)
SELECT
    td.claim_id,
    td.time_diff_seconds,
    c.claim_type,
    a.name AS assigned_adjuster_name -- Linked via 'A' activity, assuming resource for 'A' is adjuster_id
FROM TimeDiffsRP td
JOIN claims c ON td.claim_id = c.claim_id
LEFT JOIN claim_events ce_a ON td.claim_id = ce_a.claim_id AND ce_a.activity = 'A'
LEFT JOIN adjusters a ON ce_a.resource::INTEGER = a.adjuster_id
WHERE
    -- Flagging claims that are significantly faster or slower than the ~25-hour average.
    -- This helps to see if the low STDEV assumption holds, or if there are outliers.
    td.time_diff_seconds < (3600 * 24) -- e.g., Claims processed in less than 1 day
    OR td.time_diff_seconds > (3600 * 30) -- e.g., Claims processed in more than 30 hours
ORDER BY td.time_diff_seconds;
```

**2. Verifying Approve to Notify (P to N) Delays:**
This query identifies claims with unusually long or short durations between 'Approve' and 'Notify', highlighting potential notification bottlenecks or inefficiencies.

```sql
WITH ClaimPN AS (

TimeDiffsPN AS (

    WHERE notify_ts > approve_ts

SELECT

    td.time_diff_seconds,
    c.claim_type,
    a.name AS assigned_adjuster_name -- Linked via 'A' activity
FROM TimeDiffsPN td
JOIN claims c ON td.claim_id = c.claim_id
LEFT JOIN claim_events ce_a ON td.claim_id = ce_a.claim_id AND ce_a.activity = 'A'
LEFT JOIN adjusters a ON ce_a.resource::INTEGER = a.adjuster_id
WHERE
    -- Identify claims with very long delays (e.g., > 1.5x average of 7 days)
    td.time_diff_seconds > (604800 * 1.5)
    -- Or claims with surprisingly short delays (e.g., < 0.5x average) to investigate variability
    OR td.time_diff_seconds < (604800 * 0.5)

```

**3. Detecting Premature Claims Closure (A to C) and Missing Steps:**
This query finds claims closed quickly after assignment ('A' to 'C') and checks if intermediate 'Evaluate' ('E') or 'Approve' ('P') steps were missed, suggesting potential process bypasses.

```sql
WITH ClaimAC AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        MAX(CASE WHEN activity = 'A' THEN timestamp END) as assign_ts,
        MAX(CASE WHEN activity = 'C' THEN timestamp END) as close_ts,
        MAX(CASE WHEN activity = 'E' THEN timestamp END) as evaluate_ts,
        MAX(CASE WHEN activity = 'P' THEN timestamp END) as approve_ts
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity IN ('A', 'C', 'E', 'P')
    GROUP BY claim_id
    HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT CASE WHEN activity IN ('A', 'C') THEN activity END) = 2 -- Ensure A and C exist
),
TimeDiffsAC AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (close_ts - assign_ts)) as time_diff_seconds,
        evaluate_ts,
        approve_ts
    FROM ClaimAC
    WHERE close_ts > assign_ts
)
SELECT
    td.claim_id,
    td.time_diff_seconds,
    c.claim_type,
    a.name AS assigned_adjuster_name, -- Linked via 'A' activity
    CASE
        WHEN td.evaluate_ts IS NOT NULL AND td.evaluate_ts > assign_ts AND td.evaluate_ts < close_ts THEN TRUE
        ELSE FALSE
    END AS had_intermediate_evaluation,
    CASE
        WHEN td.approve_ts IS NOT NULL AND td.approve_ts > assign_ts AND td.approve_ts < close_ts THEN TRUE
        ELSE FALSE
    END AS had_intermediate_approval
FROM TimeDiffsAC td
JOIN claims c ON td.claim_id = c.claim_id
LEFT JOIN claim_events ce_a ON td.claim_id = ce_a.claim_id AND ce_a.activity = 'A'
LEFT JOIN adjusters a ON ce_a.resource::INTEGER = a.adjuster_id
WHERE
    -- Filter for claims closed within a short timeframe after assignment (e.g., less than 2 hours average)
    td.time_diff_seconds < 7200
    -- And explicitly check that intermediate steps 'E' or 'P' did not occur between A and C
    AND (
        (td.evaluate_ts IS NULL OR td.evaluate_ts <= assign_ts OR td.evaluate_ts >= close_ts)
        AND (td.approve_ts IS NULL OR td.approve_ts <= assign_ts OR td.approve_ts >= close_ts)
    )
ORDER BY td.time_diff_seconds;
```
