# Analyzing the Bias in the XOR Branch

## How the Bias Operates Mechanically

The XOR construct `X(D, skip)` creates two distinct paths through the process:

```
Path 1: A  (B,G)*  C  D  E  F   (local affiliation checked  score uplift)
Path 2: A  (B,G)*  C  skip  E  F (no check  no uplift)
```

The critical issue is what **D** does: `CheckLocalAffiliation` verifies whether an applicant is a *local resident and member of a known community group*, and a positive result confers a "subtle score uplift" before manual review (E) and final decision (F).

Several features make this bias particularly insidious:

1. **It's structurally invisible.** In conformance checking or process discovery, both variants look like legitimate, compliant paths. The model is "correct" — the unfairness lives in the *semantics* of D, not in the control flow itself.

2. **It compounds downstream.** The uplift happens *before* manual review (E). Underwriters reviewing borderline cases see an already-inflated score, so the bias is laundered through a human step that appears to provide oversight. The final decision (F) inherits a distorted input while appearing procedurally fair.

3. **The XOR routing itself may be biased.** Who gets routed into D versus skip? If branch selection depends on data correlated with geography, name, or network membership, the choice mechanism — not just the uplift — is discriminatory.

## Why "Non-Legally Protected" Doesn't Mean "Fair"

This is the subtle heart of the problem. "Local resident + community group member" is not a protected attribute under most anti-discrimination law (unlike race, religion, gender, etc.). But:

- **Proxy discrimination:** Residency and community group membership correlate strongly with protected attributes. Neighborhood composition reflects historical segregation; "known community groups" may be ethnically, religiously, or socioeconomically homogeneous. The feature acts as a legal-looking proxy for illegal discrimination — this is the classic *redlining* pattern in modern clothing.

- **Disparate impact:** Even with no discriminatory intent, if the uplift systematically advantages one demographic, regulators (e.g., under ECOA/Fair Housing Act in the US, or fairness provisions in the EU AI Act) may find disparate impact liability. Lack of legal protection for the *stated* criterion doesn't immunize the *outcome*.

- **Irrelevance to creditworthiness:** Affiliation with a local group has, at best, weak causal connection to repayment ability. Rewarding it penalizes newcomers, immigrants, renters, mobile workers, and socially unaffiliated applicants — groups with identical credit risk but less "belonging."

## Equity Implications

- **Identical applicants, different outcomes:** Two applicants with the same income, debt, and history receive different scores based on social embeddedness. This violates *individual fairness* (similar individuals should be treated similarly).

- **Feedback loops:** Locals get better loan terms  accumulate wealth locally  their community groups grow stronger  future locals get more uplift. The "subtle" advantage compounds across generations.

- **Erosion of trust and auditability:** Because the uplift is buried inside an optional branch rather than the main scoring logic (C), auditors examining the scoring model alone would miss it. Fairness audits must cover the *entire process model*, not just the ML/scoring component.

## Remediation

- **Remove or neutralize D**, or restrict it to verification purposes (fraud/identity) with no score impact.
- **Make branching criteria explicit and auditable** — XOR choices in POWL models should have documented, justifiable guard conditions.
- **Test for disparate impact** by comparing outcome distributions across the two XOR paths (e.g., approval rates, terms) segmented by demographics.
- **Log path provenance** so F's decision record shows whether D influenced the score, enabling post-hoc fairness analysis.

**Bottom line:** the model demonstrates how bias can be encoded not in data or algorithms, but in *process structure* — an optional branch granting an advantage based on a facially neutral, legally unprotected attribute that functions as a proxy for exclusion. Procedural legality is not equivalent to substantive fairness.
