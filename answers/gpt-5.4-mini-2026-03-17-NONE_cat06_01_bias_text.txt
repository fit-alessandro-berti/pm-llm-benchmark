### Where the bias is introduced

#### 1. Geographic/community-based score boost
In Step 3, applicants who:
- live in the local region, or
- list membership in the “Highland Civic Darts Club,”

receive a **slight upward adjustment** to their score.

That is a form of bias because it gives a systematic advantage to one subset of applicants based on a proxy for “community integration.” Even though this is not a legally protected trait, it still privileges people with certain social or geographic ties.

#### 2. Human underwriter discretion
In Step 4, underwriters are encouraged to interpret borderline cases “in context,” and community engagement is treated as a positive signal. This can introduce:
- **subjective bias**
- **confirmation bias**
- **halo effects** around local ties or club membership

Even if the policy is intended as a benign proxy for stability or responsibility, it can result in uneven treatment.

#### 3. Higher-term placement
In Step 5, the score boost can move some applicants into better eligibility bands, meaning the bias affects not just approval chances but also **pricing** and **loan terms**. That amplifies the impact.

### Is it justifiable?

It depends on the rationale, but there are strong concerns.

#### Arguments for justification
The company might claim:
- local residency or community involvement correlates with lower risk
- it is using a non-protected, business-relevant factor
- the policy rewards stability and community ties

If the correlation were strong, consistent, and empirically validated, the company could argue it is acting on risk-based criteria rather than prejudice.

#### Why it is problematic
Even if the factor is not legally protected, it may still be unfair because:

- **Proxy discrimination:** local residency and community membership can function as stand-ins for race, class, nationality, age, or socioeconomic status.
- **Unequal access:** not everyone has equal opportunity to join local groups or establish long-term residence.
- **Opacity:** the boost is not openly disclosed, so applicants cannot understand or challenge how they were evaluated.
- **Uneven burden:** out-of-region applicants must clear the baseline alone, while favored applicants get extra help.
- **Potential self-reinforcement:** people already advantaged by geography or social capital become more likely to receive favorable terms.

So the policy may be **legally permissible in some contexts** yet still **ethically questionable** and potentially inequitable.

---

### Fairness and equity implications

This approach can undermine fairness in several ways:

- **Procedural fairness:** applicants are not treated by the same transparent standard.
- **Substantive fairness:** outcomes systematically favor one group.
- **Access fairness:** people without local ties may be excluded from benefits unrelated to actual creditworthiness.
- **Distributional equity:** favorable terms may cluster among already-advantaged applicants, widening disparities.

---

### Bottom line
The slight bias is introduced by **rewarding local residency and club membership with score boosts** and by **human underwriters giving extra weight to community ties**.  
While the company may see this as a harmless or business-relevant proxy, it is potentially **problematic** because it can create hidden advantages for certain non-protected groups, reduce transparency, and lead to unfair or inequitable lending outcomes.
