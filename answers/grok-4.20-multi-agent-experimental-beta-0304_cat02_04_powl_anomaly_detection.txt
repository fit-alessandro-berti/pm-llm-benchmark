### 1. Optional Credit Check (XOR with Silent Skip)
After receiving the order, the process branches via an XOR that allows **either** performing `Check_Credit` **or** silently skipping it entirely before moving to the approval/invoice block.

**Why this is anomalous**: In a standard, well-controlled Order-to-Cash workflow, credit verification is a critical risk-mitigation gate that should occur before any commitment (approval or invoicing). Making it optional via an explicit skip is not normal business practice; it is typically only allowed under very specific, governed conditions (e.g., pre-approved customers with separate handling).

**Potential impact**: 
- Exposes the organization to significant financial risk (bad debts, non-payment, or sales to customers with poor creditworthiness).
- Represents a compliance and internal-control weakness.
- In a real system this pattern often emerges as a *workaround* (e.g., trusted/internal orders, pressure to close sales quickly, or when the credit-check system is unavailable), indicating that the idealized process is frequently bypassed.

### 2. LOOP Structure on Approve_Order and Issue_Invoice
The block `LOOP([A, I])` follows process-tree LOOP semantics, typically interpreted as **A (I A)***. This means:
- The fragment always starts with an `Approve_Order`.
- It then allows zero or more repetitions of `(Issue_Invoice  Approve_Order)`.
- Consequently, the process can produce sequences such as: A; A-I-A; A-I-A-I-A; etc.
- It *always ends with an Approve_Order* before proceeding to `Receive_Payment`.

**Why this is anomalous**: A textbook O2C process expects `Approve_Order` and `Issue_Invoice` to occur a single time each, in a clear sequential order (approve first, then invoice once the order is confirmed/delivered). Allowing repeated, interleaved approvals and invoicesŚespecially re-approving *after* an invoice has already been issuedŚis highly unusual and does not reflect sound business logic.

**Potential impact**:
- Indicates rework, order changes after invoicing, partial shipments, or error-correction loops rather than proper exception handling.
- Can lead to multiple invoices per order, accounting discrepancies, customer confusion, and reconciliation problems.
- Suggests systemic issues in the upstream process (e.g., frequent order modifications, poor initial data quality, or system limitations forcing users to ōre-approveö to trigger new invoices).
- From a process-mining perspective, this LOOP pattern is a classic sign of non-conformance or ōworkaroundsö that have become normalized in the event log from which the model was likely discovered.
