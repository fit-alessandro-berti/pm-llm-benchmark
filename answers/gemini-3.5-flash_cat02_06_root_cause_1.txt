### 2. Root Cause Analysis of Performance Issues

By looking at the transition times between activities, we can pinpoint exactly where the delays are occurring.

#### A. The Level-2 Escalation Bottleneck (Handoff Delay)
When a ticket is escalated to a Level-2 Agent, there is a severe delay before the Level-2 agent actually begins the investigation.
*   **Case 102:** Escalated on March 1 at 11:30 $\rightarrow$ L2 Investigation started at 14:00 (**2.5-hour delay**).
*   **Case 105:** Escalated on March 1 at 10:00 $\rightarrow$ L2 Investigation did not start until March 2 at 14:00 (**28-hour delay**). 
*   *Impact:* Level-2 queue management is highly inefficient. Tickets are sitting in the L2 inbox for hours (or over a day in Case 105) before being touched.

#### B. Delay Prior to L1 Investigation (Queue Bottlenecks)
Even without escalation, tickets are sitting unaddressed after being assigned to an agent.
*   **Case 104:** Assigned to L1 at 09:30 $\rightarrow$ L1 Investigation did not start until 13:00 (**3.5-hour delay**). Compared to Case 101 (40 mins) and Case 103 (15 mins), this represents a clear bottleneck in the Level-1 queue.

#### C. Overnight Spillover / Working Hours Impact
There is a highly consistent 19-hour gap between starting an investigation in the afternoon and resolving it the next morning across three different cases:
*   **Case 102:** L2 Investigate starts March 1 (14:00) $\rightarrow$ Resolved March 2 (09:00) [19 hours]
*   **Case 104:** L1 Investigate starts March 1 (13:00) $\rightarrow$ Resolved March 2 (08:00) [19 hours]
*   **Case 105:** L2 Investigate starts March 2 (14:00) $\rightarrow$ Resolved March 3 (09:00) [19 hours]

*Interpretation:* If an investigation is started after 12:00 PM, it is rarely resolved on the same day. It spills over to the next morning, likely because the support team operates on standard daytime business hours (e.g., 8:00 AM – 5:00 PM). The ticket sits idle overnight.

### 3. Explanations and Recommendations

#### **How these factors increase cycle times:**
1.  **Siloed Team Hand-offs:** When L1 escalates to L2, there is no warm transfer. The ticket is placed at the back of the L2 queue, resulting in a dead zone of productivity (up to 28 hours in Case 105).
2.  **Lack of Afternoon Triage:** Because tickets that begin investigation after midday spill over into the next day, a ticket received at 8:25 AM (Case 105) can take 2 full days to resolve simply because it missed the morning resolution window.

#### **Recommendations to Address Bottlenecks:**

1.  **Implement Level-2 Service Level Agreements (SLAs):**
    *   *Issue:* The 28-hour delay in Case 105 between escalation and L2 investigation is unacceptable.
    *   *Action:* Establish a strict "Time-to-First-Action" SLA for escalated tickets (e.g., L2 must acknowledge and begin investigation within 2 hours of escalation).

2.  **Optimize L1 Triage and First-Contact Resolution (FCR):**
    *   *Issue:* L1 agents are taking up to 3.5 hours to start investigating assigned tickets (Case 104).
    *   *Action:* Introduce a load-balancing system that routes tickets only to L1 agents who have immediate bandwidth, rather than statically assigning them to a single agent's queue.

3.  **Adjust Shift Coverage for Continuous Resolution:**
    *   *Issue:* Afternoon "spillover" is adding 15+ hours of inactive calendar time to tickets.
    *   *Action:* Implement staggered shifts (e.g., a late shift from 12:00 PM to 8:00 PM) to ensure that investigations started in the afternoon can be resolved the same day without waiting for the next morning. 

4.  **L1 Enablement to Reduce Escalations:**
    *   *Issue:* Escalated tickets (102, 105) take drastically longer.
    *   *Action:* Train L1 agents on the common root causes of escalated tickets. If L1 can resolve 15% more tickets without escalating to L2, the load on L2 will decrease, thereby reducing the massive queue times observed in Case 105.
