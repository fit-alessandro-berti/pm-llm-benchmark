With only five cases, this is not enough to prove population-wide discrimination statistically, but it is enough to flag clear bias mechanisms in the process.

### Where bias appears

#### 1. Explicit bias in the scoring step
The clearest issue is the **`+10 (Community)` score adjustment**.

- It is given to applicants affiliated with **Highland Civic Darts Club**
- Applicants with **`CommunityGroup = None`** get **0**
- That means a social affiliation, not just financial risk, is affecting the score

This is visible in:

- **C001:** 710  **720 adjusted**  Approved
- **C004:** 690  **700 adjusted**  Approved

So the system is explicitly rewarding membership in a particular community network.

#### 2. Likely bias tied to geography/local status
`LocalResident` also appears to matter, even though there is no explicit “local bonus” shown.

Evidence from the outcomes:

- All **local residents** in this sample are approved: **C001, C002, C004**
- A **non-local** with a decent score is rejected: **C003 (715)**
- Another non-local is approved only with a much stronger score: **C005 (740)**

The strongest red flag is:

- **C003:** Non-local, no group, score **715**  **Rejected**
- **C004:** Local, group member, score **690** / **700 adjusted**  **Approved**

That suggests the process is not treating score alone as decisive. Being local and/or socially affiliated seems to lower the effective approval bar.

### Which attributes favor certain groups

The favored attributes appear to be:

- **Community affiliation**
  - especially membership in the named group: **Highland Civic Darts Club**
- **Local residency**
  - applicants marked `LocalResident = TRUE` seem advantaged

Together, these create an **in-group preference**:
local people with recognized community ties do better than outsiders or unaffiliated applicants.

### How this affects fairness

#### Similar creditworthiness gets different outcomes
If the pre-adjustment score is the closest proxy for underlying creditworthiness, then the process is unfair because similar or better applicants are treated worse when they lack the favored attributes.

Examples:

- **C001** had a base score of **710** and was approved after a community bonus
- **C003** had a higher base score of **715** and was rejected

So the applicant with the stronger underlying score got the worse outcome.

#### It rewards social connectedness, not just repayment risk
Membership in a local club may reflect:

- social access
- free time
- cultural fit
- transportation
- local networks
- long-term residence

Those are not the same as creditworthiness.

#### It may create proxy discrimination
Even if `LocalResident` and `CommunityGroup` are not protected traits themselves, they can act as **proxies** for:

- socioeconomic status
- race/ethnicity
- age
- disability
- religion/culture
- migration or newcomer status

That means the system could produce disparate impacts without explicitly using protected-class labels.

### Why this is an equity problem

This process does not just fail “neutrality”; it likely undermines equity.

People who may be disadvantaged include:

- newcomers to the area
- people who are not part of local social networks
- people excluded from or uninterested in specific clubs
- people with caregiving, disability, work, language, or transportation barriers that make community participation harder
- applicants living outside the favored geography

So even if two people are similarly creditworthy, the one who is **less locally embedded** may need a higher score to get the same outcome.

### Process-level concern
The bias is not just human discretion; it looks **systematized**:

- the **Scoring Engine** applies the community adjustment
- **ManualReview** does not appear to correct it
- the **Rules Engine** finalizes the result

So the bias is being operationalized through the process, not just introduced by one reviewer.

### Bottom line
Bias manifests here through:

1. **An explicit community-based score bonus**
2. **An apparent preference for local residents**
3. **A final decision process that lets those factors outweigh similar or better underlying creditworthiness**

In practical terms, the system seems to favor applicants who are **local and socially connected**, while disadvantaging people who are **equally creditworthy but lack those affiliations or geographic characteristics**.
