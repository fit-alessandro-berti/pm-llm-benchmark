The goal is to reduce noise from granular actions into meaningful process stages that represent the actual value-adding phases of production.

### Detailed Justification and Rationale

#### 1. Material Preparation & Setup
*   **Why grouped?** These events form a continuous sequence of handling the raw input. They are temporally clustered (within 15 minutes) and rely on the same primary resource type (Operator A + Robotics).
*   **Functional Logic:** The process cannot proceed to welding without the material being physically present, correctly oriented, and thermally conditioned. "Preheat" is included here because it prepares the *material*, not the *machine*. If this step is skipped or fails, assembly cannot occur.

#### 2. Welding Assembly Operation
*   **Why grouped?** This is the primary manufacturing transformation phase. It involves a specific human operator (Operator B) and a specific tool (Welding Tool).
*   **Functional Logic:** The actions are sequential dependencies (Tool Pickup $\rightarrow$ Weld A $\rightarrow$ Weld B). These define the physical structure of the product. Unlike preparation, this step modifies the geometry/integrity of the object.

#### 3. Surface Treatment & Finishing
*   **Why grouped?** These two events represent a continuous automated process (Apply $\rightarrow$ Dry). They occur in the same "phase" of the timeline (after welding starts) and involve similar resources (Heating units, Coating unit).
*   **Functional Logic:** This phase adds value through protection and durability. It is distinct from assembly because it does not join parts but modifies the existing surface.

#### 4. Quality Inspection & Verification
*   **Why grouped?** While "Measure weld integrity" occurs immediately after welding and "Visual check" after coating, both serve as **Quality Gates**.
*   **Functional Logic:** In process mining, grouping checks is often done to represent the "Acceptance Criteria." By aggregating these, we highlight that *verification* is a critical stage of the workflow, regardless of when it occurs in the timeline. It signals the transition from "Production" to "Completion/Release".

### Aggregation Rules Applied
To move from the low-level log to this high-level view, I applied the following rules:

1.  **Temporal Clustering:** Events occurring within a short window (e.g., < 20 seconds for A1 Step 1) were grouped together as they represent a continuous action stream by the same resource type.
2.  **Functional Dependency:** If Event B requires the output of Event A (e.g., Preheat must finish before Welding can start), they are grouped into the phase leading up to that dependency.
3.  **Resource Type Signaling:**
    *   **Operators** performing manual setup $\rightarrow$ Prep Step.
    *   **Specialized Tools** performing core manufacturing $\rightarrow$ Assembly Step.
    *   **Machines** performing post-processing $\rightarrow$ Finishing Step.
    *   **Sensors/Checkers** performing validation $\rightarrow$ QA Step.

This structured abstraction allows stakeholders to monitor the **throughput of preparation**, the **success rate of welding**, the **efficiency of finishing**, and the **overall quality pass rate**, rather than counting individual seconds or sensor readings.
