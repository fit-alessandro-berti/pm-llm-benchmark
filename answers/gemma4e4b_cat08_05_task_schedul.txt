## Advanced Process Mining and Scheduling Strategy for Precision Parts Inc.

As a Senior Operations Analyst specializing in manufacturing process optimization, I recognize that Precision Parts Inc.'s current struggles are classic symptoms of relying on localized, reactive scheduling rules (dispatching) in a highly dynamic, stochastic, and constrained environment. The MES event log is an invaluable goldmine, transforming the problem from an opaque operational challenge into a quantitative, solvable data science problem.

My approach will be to use Process Mining (PM) not merely for descriptive reporting, but for *predictive diagnosis*, informing the design of sophisticated, adaptive scheduling algorithms.

---

### 1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics

Process Mining treats the MES event logs as an **Event Log**, structured by the tuple: $\langle\text{Case ID (Job)}, \text{Activity (Task)}, \text{Timestamp}, \text{Resource (Machine)}, \dots\rangle$.

#### A. PM Techniques and Metrics Usage:

| Objective | Process Mining Technique | Metrics Derived | Analysis Focus |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Job Flow/Lead Time** | **Sequence Discovery** (Process Map Reconstruction) | Mean/Std Dev of Lead Time (Entry $\rightarrow$ Exit), Takt Time Compliance. | Identifying the *actual* path taken by jobs and quantifying flow variability. Comparing planned vs. actual elapsed time. |
| **Task Waiting Time (Queue)** | **Cycle Time Analysis / Queue Time Calculation** | Average queue length (measured by $\Delta T$ between $T_{End(Task\ 1)}$ and $T_{Start(Task\ 2)}$), Distribution of idle time. | Quantifying resource contention and assessing if waiting times are due to resource exhaustion or logical delays. |
| **Resource Utilization** | **Resource Allocation Modeling** | Throughput (jobs/shift), % Time Busy (Productive vs. Setup vs. Idle), Machine/Operator Load Profiles. | Identifying sustained bottlenecks (machines with consistently $>90\%$ utilization) and periods of resource *starvation*. |
| **Sequence-Dependent Setup Times (SDST)** | **Association Rule Mining / Sequence Analysis** | $\text{Avg}(SetupTime | \text{Job}_{\text{prev}}, \text{Job}_{\text{current}})$. Statistical clustering of setup times based on job families or preceding jobs. | Instead of relying on a fixed duration, we model $SetupTime = f(\text{Job}_{\text{prev}}, \text{Job}_{\text{current}})$. This is crucial for accurate resource planning. |
| **Schedule Adherence & Tardiness** | **Temporal Distance Measurement** | Mean/Max Tardiness (Actual Finish Date - Due Date). Frequency of *schedule deviation events*. | Quantifying the operational impact of poor scheduling. We specifically analyze the time window between the predicted Completion Time (based on current state) and the Due Date. |
| **Impact of Disruptions** | **Anomaly Detection / Deviation Analysis** | $\Delta$ KPI ($\text{Tardiness}_{\text{after disruption}} - \text{Tardiness}_{\text{before disruption}}$). Time-to-Recovery metrics. | Isolating the impact of external events (breakdowns, urgent jobs) on the established baseline schedule. |

---

### 2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies

By combining these metrics, we move beyond simply observing delays to diagnosing *why* the delays occur.

#### A. Pathologies Identification:

1.  **Bottleneck Choke Points and Throughput Inhibition:**
    * **Evidence:** Process mining will reveal resource utilization profiles that are consistently $>100\%$ in simulation terms (i.e., the job flow theoretically requires more capacity than is available). The associated tasks will exhibit long, non-stochastic queue waiting times.
    * **Quantification:** Throughput is bottlenecked by the resource with the minimum available capacity-time (i.e., the lowest capacity-adjusted utilization).
2.  **Reactive Scheduling and Low Global Optimality:**
    * **Evidence:** The analysis will show that jobs finishing late often waited for a resource bottleneck, even if alternative resources were available, indicating the local dispatching rule was *myopic* (only considering the immediate next step).
    * **Quantification:** High variability in cycle time and long waiting queues immediately following bottleneck operations.
3.  **Setup Time Neglect and Suboptimal Sequencing:**
    * **Evidence:** The log comparison between planned setup time (from MES inputs) and actual elapsed setup time will show significant discrepancies, particularly at bottleneck machines. Further analysis will show that specific job sequences (e.g., Batching Job A $\rightarrow$ Job B) result in setup times dramatically lower than the average, suggesting a pattern that is ignored.
4.  **Lack of Proactive Capacity Management (The Bullwhip Effect):**
    * **Evidence:** High variance in WIP inventory levels and erratic job flow, suggesting that when one bottleneck finally clears a queue, the sudden flood of jobs overwhelms the next downstream resource, leading to a second wave of congestion.

### 3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness

Process mining is essential for distinguishing between *Process Capability Limits* (resource constraints) and *System Logic Flaws* (bad scheduling rules).

| Potential Root Cause | PM Diagnostic Action | Interpretation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Limitations of Static Dispatching Rules** | **Variant Analysis:** Compare the process map for jobs that finish on time vs. jobs that are late. Look for shared patterns of queues/delays. | If late jobs consistently wait longer at the bottleneck *before* starting the task, the dispatching rule (e.g., FIFO) did not proactively shepherd the job to that resource until capacity was available. |
| **Lack of Real-Time Visibility** | **Timeliness & Delay Analysis:** Analyze the temporal gaps between event logging for different stages. | Large gaps suggest manual intervention points or processes not fully captured in the digital flow, meaning the schedule is based on old data. |
| **Inaccurate Time/Setup Estimation** | **Prediction vs. Reality Analysis:** Model task duration distributions (e.g., $\text{ActualDuration} \sim \text{LogNormal}(\mu, \sigma)$) and compare them to planned distributions. | If the mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma$ are significantly larger than planned, the root cause is inadequate process modeling, not just poor dispatching. |
| **Poor Coordination between Work Centers** | **Inter-Task Dependency Graph Analysis:** Map the dependencies. Look for critical path bottlenecks where the total time is limited not by processing time, but by the handoff time or setup time at the *next* work center. | The schedule is optimizing individual tasks, but failing to optimize the *flow* between tasks. |

**Conclusion on Root Causes:** The evidence strongly suggests that the core failure is **System Logic Flaw**: The current local, reactive dispatching rules fail because they treat the job shop as a series of independent, sequential steps, ignoring the coupled dynamics of resource capacity, sequence-dependent costs, and overall flow time variability.

### 4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies

The proposed strategies must move from **Reactive Dispatching** (What happens next?) to **Predictive Resource Optimization** (What *should* happen next to minimize overall cost/tardiness?).

#### Strategy 1: Dynamic Multi-Objective Priority Index (The Enhanced Dispatching Rule)

**Core Logic:** Implement a single, composite priority score ($P_{score}$) for every job waiting at the queue entrance of a machine, which dynamically changes as the schedule progresses. This score weights multiple conflicting objectives.

$$P_{score} = W_1 \cdot \frac{D_{i}}{T_{i}^{R}} + W_2 \cdot \frac{C_{i}}{T_{i}^{B}} - W_3 \cdot \frac{S_{i, \text{current}}}{T_{i}^{R}}$$

*   **$W_1 \cdot \frac{D_{i}}{T_{i}^{R}}$ (Due Date Factor):** Urgency (Job's Due Date $D_i$ relative to Remaining Time $T_i^R$). (Focuses on Tardiness Minimization).
*   **$W_2 \cdot \frac{C_{i}}{T_{i}^{B}}$ (Critical Path Factor):** Importance of the job to the overall shop plan (e.g., high-value job, or job required by a critical downstream path $T_i^B$). (Focuses on Throughput Maximization).
*   **$-W_3 \cdot \frac{S_{i, \text{current}}}{T_{i}^{R}}$ (Setup Cost Factor):** Negative weighting that favors the job whose processing will incur the lowest *estimated* setup time given the last job processed on that machine. (Focuses on Setup Cost Minimization).

**Process Mining Insight Usage:**
*   **Weighting ($W_1, W_2, W_3$):** The optimal weights are determined by running thousands of historical scenarios (via simulation, see Section 5) and optimizing a cost function that minimizes total weighted tardiness and total setup time.
*   **Setup Cost Factor ($S_{i}$):** Derived directly from the **Sequence-Dependent Setup Time (SDST)** models quantified in Section 1.

**Impact:** Dramatically reduces tardiness and minimizes setup waste, ensuring the bottleneck resources are optimally utilized for both processing time and minimizing changeover costs.

#### Strategy 2: Predictive Resource Capacity Scheduling (Focusing on Bottlenecks)

**Core Logic:** Instead of scheduling jobs as they arrive, we shift to a capacity-based scheduling model. We predict the **Capacity Load Profile** for the next critical bottleneck resource (e.g., CUT-01, MILL-03) using current job queue data and historical utilization rates, incorporating mean $\pm$ standard deviation for task durations and setup times.

1.  **Predictive Bottleneck Identification:** Identify the bottleneck machine whose predicted utilization exceeds 90% within the next 8 hours.
2.  **Look-Ahead Scheduling:** For all jobs assigned to this bottleneck, sequence them not by EDD or FIFO, but by the order that minimizes the *Cumulative Flow Time* across the critical path, effectively managing the WIP buffer *upstream* to ensure a smooth, predictable feed rate.
3.  **Hot Job Re-optimization:** When an urgent job arrives, the schedule is recalculated only for the remaining critical path resources, checking if inserting the job causes a cascading delay exceeding the job's due date tolerance.

**Process Mining Insight Usage:**
*   **Task Duration Distribution:** Uses the mean and variance of historical task times to create stochastic process flow models, predicting the probability of resource overload.
*   **Bottleneck Identification:** Directly applies the derived utilization profile to locate the true constraints, enabling focused, preventative scheduling effort.

**Impact:** Provides highly accurate, predictable lead times. Reduces WIP accumulation by managing the pace of the job flow to match the sustained capacity of the bottleneck resource.

#### Strategy 3: Intelligent Batching and Job Family Sequencing (Setup Time Optimization)

**Core Logic:** This strategy focuses solely on minimizing the variable cost associated with setup. The MES event log is analyzed to cluster all historical jobs into "Job Families" (e.g., all jobs requiring specific tooling, materials, or similar processing steps).

1.  **Batching Rule:** At any bottleneck machine, the scheduler checks the current queue of waiting jobs. It prioritizes selecting the next job that belongs to a family whose components are currently waiting at that station, maximizing the probability of continuous processing of the same tooling type.
2.  **Sequencing:** When multiple families are available, the scheduler prioritizes the sequence that yields the minimum total setup time for the next $N$ operations, rather than optimizing individual job priorities.

**Process Mining Insight Usage:**
*   **SDST Modeling:** The PM analysis generates a matrix showing $\text{SetupTime}_{\text{Family A} \rightarrow \text{Family B}}$, allowing the scheduling system to compute the cumulative cost savings of batching decisions.
*   **Job Family Clustering:** Uses the job description attributes (material, size, tooling required) to define statistically significant clusters, which replaces the manual guess of setup requirements.

**Impact:** Direct reduction in non-value-added time (setup time), thereby increasing the overall effective capacity of the bottleneck machines and improving throughput without adding capital equipment.

### 5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement

#### A. Discrete-Event Simulation (DES) Framework

The only robust way to test these complex, interacting strategies is through DES. We must parameterize the simulation model using the empirical data derived from the process mining analysis, not the planned values.

**Parameters Derived from PM:**
1.  **Stochastic Distributions:** Task durations (using historical $\mu$ and $\sigma$).
2.  **Setup Time Matrix:** The full SDST table quantifying the time between every pair of job families.
3.  **Failure Rates:** Empirical breakdown frequency and Mean Time To Repair (MTTR) derived from the event log.
4.  **Arrival Profile:** The typical pattern and distribution of urgent "hot jobs."

**Simulation Testing Scenarios:**

1.  **Baseline vs. Strategy Comparison (Validation):** Test all three strategies against a baseline "Current Logic" (e.g., $P_{score}=0$, resulting in FIFO). Track KPIs: Tardiness, Avg WIP, Throughput, and Total Setup Time.
2.  **Extreme Load Test (Stress Test):** Simulate a period where the job arrival rate significantly exceeds the bottleneck machine's steady-state capacity, testing the ability of the dynamic strategies to prevent complete gridlock or starvation.
3.  **Disruption Scenario Test (Resilience Test):** Introduce planned random breakdowns (e.g., $3$ breakdowns over $48$ hours) and compare how the predictive scheduling and dynamic rules adapt and reschedule the affected job portfolio to minimize delay impact.

#### B. Continuous Monitoring and Adaptation (Feedback Loop)

The system must operate in a closed-loop framework: **Measure $\rightarrow$ Analyze $\rightarrow$ Predict $\rightarrow$ Act $\rightarrow$ Re-Measure.**

1.  **Monitoring:** Continuous process mining monitors the live MES stream, tracking the **Process Performance Index (PPI)**㻡 composite score of current lead time variance, queue length deviation, and accumulated setup time cost.
2.  **Drift Detection:** If the real-time KPI (e.g., average queue time at MILL-03) deviates by more than $2\sigma$ from the historical distribution used to train the model, an alert is triggered, indicating potential operational drift (e.g., equipment degradation, operator error, or a new job family).
3.  **Adaptation:** The system does not hard-code the rules. The dynamic weights ($W_1, W_2, W_3$) in the $P_{score}$ (Strategy 1) should be periodically re-optimized (e.g., daily or weekly) based on the latest historical data collected, ensuring the scheduling logic remains optimally tuned to the current operational state and market demands.

By adopting this rigorous, data-driven methodology, Precision Parts Inc. transitions from simply *reacting* to chaos to *predicting* and *proactively managing* its complex production flow, leading to quantifiable improvements in efficiency, customer satisfaction, and profitability.
