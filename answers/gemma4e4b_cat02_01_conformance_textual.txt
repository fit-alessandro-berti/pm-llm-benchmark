### 2. Detailed Explanation of Violations

#### Case 2 Violations
**Sequence:** Receive $\rightarrow$ Check $\rightarrow$ Package items $\rightarrow$ Authorize payment $\rightarrow$ Ship order $\rightarrow$ Close order

**Violation(s):**
1. **Incorrect Ordering of Payment and Preparation (Check $\rightarrow$ Package $\rightarrow$ Authorize):** The process violates the established dependency rules for fulfilling an order.
    *   **Rule Violated:** "Package items" should only occur after authorization.
    *   **Explanation:** The flow incorrectly proceeds to "Package items" immediately after checking inventory, *before* the "Authorize payment" step. The normative process explicitly states that payment authorization must precede packaging, as the items cannot be physically prepared for shipment without confirming approved payment.
2. **Missing Necessary Step (Authorization $\rightarrow$ Packaging $\rightarrow$ Shipping):** Although the case eventually attempts to ship, the structure implies that the packaging step was necessary but was bypassed, and the subsequent authorization and shipping sequence remains illogical due to the earlier error. The proper path must include the explicit "Package items" activity between "Authorize payment" and "Ship order."

#### Case 3 Violations
**Sequence:** Receive $\rightarrow$ Check $\rightarrow$ Authorize payment $\rightarrow$ Cancel order $\rightarrow$ Ship order $\rightarrow$ Close order

**Violation(s):**
1. **Attempted Conflict (Authorize Payment $\rightarrow$ Cancel Order):** The process attempts to cancel the order *after* successfully authorizing payment, which is an impossible or non-standard flow in this system.
    *   **Rule Violated:** The normative process defines two mutually exclusive paths: the Fulfillment Path (requires successful authorization) or the Cancellation Path (occurs if inventory fails).
    *   **Explanation:** Once the process advances to "Authorize payment," it implies that sufficient inventory was confirmed and the commitment to fulfill the order has been made. Proceeding to "Cancel order" at this stage breaks the established business logic, as cancellation should only follow the inventory check if stock is insufficient.
2. **Shipping After Cancellation (Cancel Order $\rightarrow$ Ship Order):** This is the most severe violation of the process constraints.
    *   **Rule Violated:** "Ship order" should... never after a cancellation.
    *   **Explanation:** Once an order has been canceled, the expectation is that fulfillment activities (like shipping) must cease. The system must only proceed to "Close order."

#### Case 5 Violations
**Sequence:** Receive $\rightarrow$ Check $\rightarrow$ Authorize payment $\rightarrow$ Ship order $\rightarrow$ Close order

**Violation(s):**
1. **Skipping Necessary Intermediate Step (Authorize $\rightarrow$ Ship):** The process jumps directly from payment authorization to shipping, bypassing the preparation step.
    *   **Rule Violated:** "Package items" should only occur after authorization and before shipping.
    *   **Explanation:** The normative description mandates a sequential flow for fulfillment: inventory check $\rightarrow$ payment $\rightarrow$ **packaging** $\rightarrow$ shipping. By skipping the "Package items" step, the process is physically and procedurally unsound, as items cannot be shipped until they are properly contained and prepared for shipment.
