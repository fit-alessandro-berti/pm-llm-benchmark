**1. Queue Identification and Characterization**

In this event log, each patient visit (Case ID) records the start and complete timestamps for sequential activities. Waiting time (queue time) is defined as the interval between the *completion* of one activity and the *start* of the next activity for the same case. This captures the time the patient is idle while waiting for the subsequent resource or stage. For the initial activity (Registration), waiting time is typically measured from an external arrival timestamp if available; otherwise, analysis focuses on inter-activity waits. If activities can overlap (rare in this flow), waiting time is only calculated for strict handovers.

Key metrics calculated per transition (e.g., Registration Complete  Nurse Assessment Start, or Doctor Consultation Complete  ECG Test Start) include:
- Average, median, and standard deviation of waiting times.
- Maximum and 90th/95th percentile waiting times (to highlight tail behavior and patient experience extremes).
- Queue frequency (number of cases passing through the transition).
- Total waiting time contributed (frequency × average wait).
- Proportion of cases experiencing "excessive" waits (e.g., >15 or >30 minutes, defined via clinical or satisfaction thresholds).
- Breakdowns by case attributes (Patient Type: New vs. Follow-up; Urgency: Normal vs. Urgent) and time periods (hour-of-day, day-of-week).

Critical queues are identified using a multi-criteria approach:
- **Impact ranking**: Sort transitions by total waiting time contributed and apply Pareto analysis (typically the top 20% of queues account for 80% of aggregate patient waiting).
- **Severity and frequency**: Prioritize queues with both high average/90th-percentile waits *and* high case volume.
- **Patient-centric filters**: Elevate queues disproportionately affecting Urgent patients or New patients (who often have longer pathways). For example, a 20-minute average wait for Doctor Consultation affecting 40% of New patients would rank higher than an equivalent wait in a low-volume transition.
- **Variability**: High standard deviation signals unstable processes requiring attention.
This data-driven prioritization ensures focus on bottlenecks with the largest effect on overall visit duration and satisfaction.

**2. Root Cause Analysis**

Beyond location, root causes are diagnosed by combining queue metrics with additional process mining techniques applied to the same log:

- **Resource bottlenecks**: Compute resource utilization as (sum of service durations for a resource) / (total observation period × available capacity). High utilization (>80–85%) on a resource immediately preceding a long-wait queue (e.g., Dr. Smith before Doctor Consultation) indicates capacity shortage. Timestamp density analysis reveals peak arrival clustering.
- **Variability in service times**: Calculate duration statistics (mean, variance, distribution) per activity and resource. High variance (e.g., Doctor Consultation ranging 10–45 minutes) creates queue buildup via the "queueing paradox." Attribute-based slicing shows whether New patients or specific specialties drive variance.
- **Activity dependencies and handovers**: Social network mining on resource handovers identifies delays when a nurse or clerk must coordinate with the next actor. Bottleneck analysis (e.g., via performance spectrum or waiting time heatmaps) highlights where completion-to-start gaps are largest.
- **Scheduling and arrival patterns**: Aggregate case start timestamps to model arrival rates versus service capacity. Mismatch (e.g., many Normal patients arriving in a 30-minute window while resources are fixed) produces queues. Variant analysis compares paths: New patients may follow longer sequences, amplifying cumulative waits.
- **Patient-type and urgency effects**: Drill-down shows whether Urgent cases are truly prioritized or if they join the same queue, and whether Follow-up patients experience shorter but still problematic waits due to over-scheduling.

These techniques (resource performance analysis, variant mining, and time-based performance views) move from symptom (long waits) to cause using only the timestamp and attribute data.

**3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies**

**Strategy 1: Time-of-day capacity reallocation for high-utilization resources.**  
Targets the Doctor Consultation and Nurse Assessment queues (typically the highest-impact transitions). Root cause is peak-hour mismatch between fixed staffing and clustered arrivals. Data support comes from hourly arrival rate analysis and per-resource utilization curves derived from timestamps. Recommendation: Shift 20–30% of daily doctor/nurse capacity to the busiest 2–3 hours (identified via log) and introduce flexible part-time or cross-trained staff. Expected impact: 25–40% reduction in average wait for these queues (e.g., from 22 min to 13–15 min) and 12–15% drop in total visit duration, with minimal added cost if reallocation rather than net hiring.

**Strategy 2: Attribute-aware appointment slotting and pathway differentiation.**  
Targets Registration-to-Nurse and overall entry queues, especially for New patients. Root cause is uniform slotting that ignores historical duration distributions and patient-type differences revealed by variant analysis. Data support: Per-patient-type duration statistics and inter-arrival times. Recommendation: Implement two parallel scheduling streams—shorter, pre-populated slots for Follow-up patients (using prior visit data) and longer slots with buffer for New patients. Add a simple rule to stagger Urgent cases. Expected impact: 15–25% reduction in early-stage waits and lower variance overall, smoothing downstream queues without changing physical resources.

**Strategy 3: Decoupling and parallelization of independent diagnostic activities.**  
Targets post-Doctor queues (e.g., waiting for ECG or Blood Test). Root cause is strict sequential dependency even when tests are clinically orderable earlier. Data support: Variant analysis identifying common paths and correlation between Doctor Consultation duration and subsequent test waits. Recommendation: For selected low-complexity Follow-up patients (identified via log patterns), enable nurse-initiated test ordering during the Nurse Assessment stage, allowing tests to run in parallel with Doctor Consultation where medically safe. Expected impact: 30–50% reduction in post-doctor waiting for qualifying cases and 8–12% shorter total visit duration, while preserving care quality through protocol guardrails.

**4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints**

Each strategy carries risks. Strategy 1 may increase short-term labor costs or create new idle time elsewhere if demand forecasting is imperfect, potentially shifting the bottleneck to checkout or registration. Strategy 2 requires IT changes to the scheduling system and staff training; overly rigid differentiation could reduce flexibility for mixed-complexity days. Strategy 3 introduces clinical governance overhead and slight risk of ordering inappropriate tests if triage rules are imprecise.

Balancing objectives requires multi-criteria evaluation: simulate proposed changes on historical log data (discrete-event simulation seeded with observed distributions) to quantify wait reductions against cost and quality metrics. Prioritize interventions where patient-satisfaction gains (proxied by wait-time reductions) outweigh incremental costs, using a cost-per-minute-of-wait-saved metric. Maintain care quality by enforcing minimum activity durations in any redesign and monitoring clinical outcome indicators alongside operational ones. Staff workload is protected by capping utilization targets (e.g., <85%) and involving frontline personnel in rule design.

**5. Measuring Success**

Primary KPIs, tracked via ongoing event-log analysis:
- Average and 90th-percentile total visit duration (first START to last COMPLETE).
- Average and 90th-percentile waiting time for the three targeted queues.
- Throughput (completed visits per day/hour).
- Resource utilization rates (to detect under- or over-staffing).
- Percentage of Urgent patients with wait < threshold (e.g., 10 minutes).
- Patient satisfaction proxy (if survey data linked to Case ID) or complaint rate per 100 visits.

Post-implementation, the same log structure enables continuous monitoring: run automated process mining queries weekly to compare pre/post distributions (statistical tests for significance). Use control charts on the KPIs to detect drift. Set alert thresholds (e.g., 90th-percentile wait exceeding baseline by 20%). Conduct quarterly deep-dive variant and bottleneck analyses to confirm improvements are sustained and no new queues have emerged. A 3–6 month stabilization period with before/after cohort comparison provides robust evidence of impact while allowing iterative refinement.
