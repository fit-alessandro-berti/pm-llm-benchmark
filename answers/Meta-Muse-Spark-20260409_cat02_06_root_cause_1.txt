### 2. Potential root causes of the delays

Looking at the step-by-step durations, two patterns jump out:

**Pattern A: Escalation creates massive waiting gaps**  
Both escalated cases 102 and 105 have huge idle times right after escalation.
- **Case 102**: `Escalate to Level-2 Agent -> Investigate Issue` took 2.5 hrs, then `Investigate Issue -> Resolve Ticket` took 19.0 hrs
- **Case 105**: `Escalate to Level-2 Agent -> Investigate Issue` took 28.0 hrs, then `Investigate Issue -> Resolve Ticket` took 19.0 hrs  

That 28-hour gap in case 105 suggests the ticket sat in a Level-2 queue overnight and through most of the next day before anyone picked it up. Same with the 19-hour resolution delays — likely waiting for Level-2 agent availability or working across business hours.

**Pattern B: Long "Investigation" phases even without escalation**  
Case 104 never escalated but still took 24.17 hours. The culprit:
- `Assign to Level-1 Agent -> Investigate Issue`: 3.50 hrs 
- `Investigate Issue -> Resolve Ticket`: 19.00 hrs

This 19-hour investigation window matches the post-escalation delays in 102 and 105. It likely spans overnight: investigation started 2024-03-01 13:00 and resolved 2024-03-02 08:00.

**Pattern C: Triage/Assignment delays are minor**  
For slow cases, triage and assignment only took 0.42–0.67 hrs. The real time sink is *after* assignment — either waiting to start investigation, or investigation itself spanning non-working hours.

### 3. How these factors increase cycle time + recommendations

**Root cause 1: Queueing time at Level-2 and overnight delays**  
The biggest driver is tickets waiting for the next action, especially after escalation. A 28-hour gap means no SLA or handoff alert fired. Overnight 19-hour gaps suggest no after-hours coverage.

*Why it hurts*: Cycle time = Active work + Waiting time. Here, waiting is 90%+ of the delay.

**Root cause 2: Level-1 unable to resolve, causing escalation**  
Cases 102 and 105 both had Level-1 investigate first, then escalate. That means Level-1 spent 2.5 hrs and 0.83 hrs respectively before deciding they couldn't solve it. Escalation itself isn't bad, but late escalation wastes time.

*Why it hurts*: You pay the Level-1 investigation cost *plus* the full Level-2 queue delay.

**Insights & recommendations**

1. **Implement escalation SLAs and queue monitoring**  
   The 28-hour wait in case 105 is the smoking gun. Set a max time-in-queue alert for Level-2, e.g. 4 business hours. If breached, auto-page the Level-2 lead. Use dashboards to flag tickets idle >2 hrs post-escalation.

2. **Improve Level-1 triage accuracy**  
   If Level-1 can't resolve, they should escalate immediately after triage, not after 2.5 hours of investigation. Build a decision tree: "If issue involves X, Y, Z → escalate now." Cases 101/103 show Level-1 can resolve in <1 hr when the issue fits their scope.

3. **Address business-hours gaps**  
   The 19-hour `Investigate -> Resolve` steps all cross 5pm–8am. If customer expectation is 24/7, you need follow-the-sun staffing or on-call Level-2. If not, set customer expectations that tickets received after 3pm may resolve next business day, and exclude non-working hours from SLA clocks.

4. **Track "waiting" vs "working" time separately**  
   Right now we only see timestamps. Instrument the system to capture when an agent actually opens the ticket. Case 104's 3.5-hr `Assign -> Investigate` gap is likely pure queue time. Reducing that via better workload balancing would help.

5. **Post-mortem the long investigations**  
   19 hours to resolve suggests either high complexity, missing info from customer, or agent multitasking. Add a "Waiting on Customer" status so you can separate internal delay from external dependencies.

The data says your process is fast when Level-1 can handle it and slow when it can't — mainly due to Level-2 queue time and overnight pauses. Fix the handoff, and your 90th percentile cycle time should drop from 49 hours to under 8.
