#### 1. Verify RP Anomaly: Identify claims with abnormal RP timing or missing intermediate steps
This query checks for claims where the time between Receive and Approve falls outside the expected range (average ± 3 standard deviations) or is missing required Assign/Evaluate steps:
```sql
WITH claim_activity_timestamps AS (
    SELECT 
        claim_id,
        MAX(CASE WHEN activity = 'R' THEN timestamp END) AS receive_ts,
        MAX(CASE WHEN activity = 'A' THEN timestamp END) AS assign_ts,
        MAX(CASE WHEN activity = 'E' THEN timestamp END) AS evaluate_ts,
        MAX(CASE WHEN activity = 'P' THEN timestamp END) AS approve_ts,
        MAX(CASE WHEN activity = 'A' THEN resource END) AS assigned_adjuster
    FROM claim_events
    GROUP BY claim_id
),
r_to_p_metrics AS (
    SELECT 
        claim_id,
        EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (approve_ts - receive_ts)) AS r_p_seconds,
        assign_ts IS NOT NULL AS has_assign,
        evaluate_ts IS NOT NULL AS has_evaluate,
        BOOL_AND(assign_ts BETWEEN receive_ts AND approve_ts) AS assign_in_window,
        BOOL_AND(evaluate_ts BETWEEN receive_ts AND approve_ts) AS evaluate_in_window
    FROM claim_activity_timestamps
    WHERE receive_ts IS NOT NULL AND approve_ts IS NOT NULL
    GROUP BY claim_id, r_p_seconds, has_assign, has_evaluate
)
SELECT 
    c.claim_id,
    c.claim_type,
    c.claim_amount,
    c.submission_date,
    r.r_p_seconds / 3600 AS r_p_hours,
    r.has_assign,
    r.has_evaluate,
    r.assign_in_window,
    r.evaluate_in_window,
    a.name AS adjuster_name,
    a.region
FROM r_to_p_metrics r
JOIN claims c ON r.claim_id = c.claim_id
LEFT JOIN adjusters a ON r.assigned_adjuster = a.name
WHERE r.r_p_seconds NOT BETWEEN 90000 - 3*3600 AND 90000 + 3*3600 -- Outside 23-27 hour expected range
   OR r.has_assign = FALSE
   OR r.has_evaluate = FALSE
ORDER BY r.r_p_seconds;
```

## Identified Temporal Anomalies
1. **R (Receive)  P (Approve) Interval**
   Average time: 25 hours (90,000 seconds), standard deviation: 1 hour (3,600 seconds)
   Anomaly: The extremely low standard deviation relative to the average indicates almost no variation in submission-to-approval timelines. This is inconsistent with a multi-step process that includes assign and evaluate activities, which would naturally introduce timing variation. The rigid, consistent timeline suggests approval timing is predetermined rather than driven by actual claim processing needs.

2. **P (Approve)  N (Notify) Interval**
   Average time: 7 days (604,800 seconds), standard deviation: 2 days (172,800 seconds)
   Anomaly: The average delay is far longer than expected for a customer notification step, which is typically a quick, low-effort activity. The high standard deviation indicates extreme inconsistency: some notifications are sent promptly, while others are delayed by over a week. This suggests unaddressed bottlenecks or irregular processing for this step.

3. **A (Assign)  C (Close) Interval**
   Average time: 2 hours (7,200 seconds), standard deviation: 1 hour (3,600 seconds)
   Anomaly: The average closure time is far too short to complete the required intermediate steps (Evaluate, Approve, Notify) in the standard process. This indicates a large share of claims are being closed immediately after assignment, skipping required processing stages entirely.

   Anomaly: The average time is too short to accommodate the required Approve step between Evaluation and Notification in the standard process. ## Hypotheses for Anomaly Root Causes
### Hypotheses for RP Anomaly
1. A rigid service level agreement (SLA) mandates all claims be approved within 25 hours of submission, leading adjusters to cut corners on evaluation steps to meet the deadline, resulting in consistently tight approval timelines.
2. An automated workflow routes low-complexity claims (e.g., small auto claims) to auto-approval after a fixed 25-hour timer, bypassing manual evaluation for these cases and creating consistent timing.
3. Data entry errors lead adjusters to manually set the approval timestamp to ~25 hours after submission rather than recording the actual approval time, artificially reducing variance.

### Hypotheses for PN Anomaly
1. Notification processing is handled by a single small team that only sends batch notifications once per week, leading to a consistent 7-day delay for most claims, with variance from occasional urgent overrides or team availability issues.
2. Post-approval verification steps (e.g., confirming repair estimates with third-party vendors) are required before notification, and the time to complete these verifications varies widely based on vendor responsiveness, leading to long and inconsistent delays.
3. A system bug queues all approved claims for 7 days before triggering notification, with occasional manual overrides for high-priority claims that reduce the delay, creating high variance.

### Hypotheses for AC Anomaly
1. Adjusters are prematurely closing clearly invalid claims (e.g., duplicate submissions, out-of-scope coverage) immediately after assignment to reduce workload, skipping required evaluation and approval steps.
2. A workflow configuration error automatically triggers the Close activity immediately after a claim is assigned to an adjuster, leading to accidental premature closure.
3. Fraudulent activity: Adjusters are closing claims without processing them to misappropriate funds or avoid work, with the 2-hour window representing the time they take to complete the fraudulent closure after receiving the assignment.

### Hypotheses for EN Anomaly
1. An automated approval rule automatically approves claims immediately after evaluation if they meet pre-defined criteria (e.g., claim amount below $500, auto claims with straightforward damage), eliminating the need for a separate manual approval step and creating near-instant transition to notification.
2. Adjusters are recording the Evaluation and Approval timestamps as the same value (either to save time or due to a system error), leading to an artificially short measured time between Evaluation and Notification.
3. A faulty workflow configuration triggers the Notification activity immediately after the Evaluation activity is completed, skipping the required Approval step entirely for most claims.
