## 1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics  

### 1.1 Reconstructing RealWorld Flow with Process Mining  

| **Step** | **Action & Tool** | **Outcome** |
|----------|-------------------|-------------|
| **a. Log Ingestion** | Import the MES CSV/JSON logs into a processmining engine (e.g., *Apromore*, *Celonis*, or *ProM*). Define event attributes: `Timestamp`, `Event Type` (`Job Released`, `Task Start/End`, `Setup Start/End`, `Breakdown`), `Case ID`, `Activity`, `Resource`, `Priority`, `Due Date`. | A **process model** (e.g., Petri net or BPMN) that captures every possible route a job can take across machines. |
| **b. Trace Alignment** | Use *conformance checking* to align each actual trace (`Case ID`) against the ideal routing diagram derived from engineering bills of material (BOM). Detect deviations such as unexpected waits, rework loops, or skipped activities. | Quantified **trace fitness scores** and a list of nonconforming variants (e.g., jobs that bypassed a required inspection due to a missed alert). |
| **c. Temporal Enrichment** | Compute timestamps differences directly in the mining engine: `Setup Duration = Setup End – Setup Start`, `Processing Time = Task End – Task Start`, `Queue Time = Queue Entry – previous Activity End`. Store these as case attributes for later statistical analysis. | Rich, perjob time metrics ready for aggregation. |

### 1.2 Quantitative Metrics via Process Mining  

| **Metric** | **Mining Technique & Calculation** | **Interpretation** |
|------------|------------------------------------|--------------------|
| **Job Flow Time / Lead Time** | For each `Case ID`, sum all *processing* + *setup* + *queue* durations from “Job Released” to final “Task End (Inspection)”. Use the *caselevel attribute* aggregation. | Distribution of total lead times  reveals variability and outliers. |
| **Makespan (Batch)** | For a set of jobs released in a given window, compute `Max(Finish Time) – Min(Release Time)` per batch. | Shows how long it takes to clear an entire cohort; useful for capacity planning. |
| **Task Waiting / Queue Times** | Per resource (`Machine ID`), calculate the time between “Queue Entry” and “Task Start”. Aggregate across all jobs  *average queue length* and *percentile waiting times*. | Highlights workcenter congestion (bottlenecks). |
| **Resource Utilization** | For each `Resource`, compute: <br> • **Productive Time** = (`Processing Duration`) <br> • **Setup Time** = (`Setup Duration`) <br> • **Idle Time** = Total elapsed time in window – (Productive + Setup). Express as % of available capacity. | Identifies under or overutilized machines, hidden idle windows caused by setup inefficiencies, and operator bottlenecks. |
| **SequenceDependent Setup Times** | Build a *transition system* where nodes are (`Machine`, `Previous Job Type`) and edges carry the observed `Setup Duration`. Use *frequency analysis* to derive empirical distributions (mean, variance) for each predecessor–successor pair. | Quantifies how much setup cost varies with material type, geometry, or prior job family; enables datadriven sequencing heuristics. |
| **Schedule Adherence / Tardiness** | For every job compute `Tardiness = max(0, Actual Finish – Due Date)`. Derive *ontime %*, mean absolute deviation (MAD), and Pareto chart of tardy jobs by priority. Correlate with disruption flags (`Breakdown`, `Priority Change`). | Direct measure of service level; isolates which disruptions cause the biggest penalties. |
| **Disruption Impact** | Filter events where `Event Type = Breakdown` or `Job Priority Change`. Measure *leadtime increase* for jobs processed immediately before/after the disruption (e.g., compare pre vs. postbreakdown lead times). Use *survival analysis* to estimate expected delay given a breakdown of X minutes. | Quantifies fragility of schedule; informs buffer sizing and recovery policies. |

### 2.1 EvidenceBased Findings from the Process Model  

| **Pathology** | **MiningDerived Indicator** | **Illustrative Example (Log Snippet)** |
|---------------|-----------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Bottleneck Resources** | High *queue time* (>30min) and >80% utilization for `MILL-03` across 70% of jobs. | JOB7001 waited ~15min after cutting before entering MILL03 (10:46  11:05). |
| **Poor Prioritization** | Late highpriority (`High`) jobs still show >48h tardiness while lowpriority jobs finish on time. | JOB7005 (high) released at 11:30 but did not start until after a breakdown of MILL02, finishing far past its due date (20250423). |
| **Suboptimal Sequencing  Excessive Setup** | Variant analysis shows that when `CUT-01` processes a stainlesssteel part after an aluminum part, setup spikes from 12min to 28min. | SETUP for JOB7001 on CUT01 took 23.5min because the preceding job (JOB6998) was made of a different alloy. |
| **Starvation Downstream** | Long idle periods (>10min) observed on `GRIND-02` while upstream machines (`MILL-03`) are still feeding work, indicating mismatched release rates. | After JOB7001 finished milling at 12:15, GRIND02 sat idle until the next job arrived at 13:05 (40min gap). |
| **Bullwhip in WIP** | Wide swings in *inprocess inventory* per work center (>30 units) correlated with bursty arrival of “hot jobs”. | Arrival of JOB7005 caused a surge; MILL03’s queue jumped from 4 to 18 jobs within an hour. |

### 2.2 Mining Techniques for Diagnosis  

| **Technique** | **Purpose** |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Bottleneck Analysis (via *resource frequency* and *queue length* heatmaps)** | Pinpoints machines whose cumulative queue time dominates total leadtime variance. |
| **Variant Comparison (ontime vs. late jobs)** | Constructs two submodels: one for jobs meeting due dates, another for tardy jobs; highlights missing activities or extra waits in the latter. |
| **Resource Contention Matrix** (`Machine × Machine`  overlap of active tasks) | Shows where parallel processing creates lockstep dependencies (e.g., CUT01 and MILL03 often run concurrently on overlapping job sets). |
| **SimulationDriven “WhatIf” Drills** (using mined distributions as input to a discreteevent simulator) | Tests whether reducing setup variance or adding buffer capacity at the identified bottleneck alleviates tardiness. |

## 3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness  

### 3.1 Structural Limitations of Current Dispatching Rules  

| **Limitation** | **ProcessMining Evidence** |
|----------------|----------------------------|
| **Static, SingleCriterion Rules** (e.g., FCFS or EDD) ignore *remaining* workload, downstream load, and setup context. | Trace alignment shows many jobs with low remaining processing time are queued behind highvolume jobs simply because they arrived earlier—leading to “taillag” in lead times. |
| **No RealTime Visibility**  decisions based on stale queue lengths. | Queueentry timestamps reveal that operators often release a job *after* the machine has become idle (e.g., GRIND02’s 40min starvation). |
| **Deterministic Durations** used for planning while actual durations exhibit high variance (+/-30%). | Histograms of `Task Duration (Actual) – Planned` reveal heavy tails; standard deviation often > planned buffer. |
| **SequenceDependent Setup Not Modeled**  setups treated as fixed 10min per job. | Transition system shows setup times ranging from 8min to 35min depending on predecessor material, invalidating flat assumptions. |
| **Adhoc Handling of Breakdowns / Hot Jobs** (manual resequencing) creates chaotic spikes. | Disruption filter isolates >50% increase in lead time for jobs processed within a 30minute window around any `Breakdown` event. |

### 3.2 Distinguishing Scheduling Logic vs. Capacity Issues  

* **CapacityLimited Resources**  high utilization *and* long queues, but low variance in processing times (system is deterministic).  
* **SchedulingInduced Variability**  similar utilization levels yet large swings in queue/wait times across otherwise identical job mixes; mining reveals nonconforming variants with extra “wait for priority change” or “setup rework”.  

By overlaying *resource contention graphs* (capacity) with *variant deviation heatmaps* (scheduling), we can attribute a tardy job to either **(a)** insufficient machine capacity (bottleneck) or **(b)** poor sequencing/setup decisions.

## 4. Developing Advanced DataDriven Scheduling Strategies  

### Strategy1 – Enhanced Dynamic Dispatching Rules  

| **Core Logic** | **DataDriven Ingredients** |
|----------------|------------------------------|
| **MultiFactor Scoring (MFS) per pending task**: <br> `Score = w·(DueDateProximity) + w·(PriorityWeight) + w·(RemainingProcTime) – w·(DownstreamLoad) + w·(EstimatedSetup)` | *DueDateProximity* = 1/(CurrentTimeDueDate) (normalized). <br>*PriorityWeight* maps “Low/Medium/High/Urgent”. <br>*RemainingProcTime* from historical duration distribution per task type. <br>*DownstreamLoad* = (queue length + inprocess time on next machine). <br>*EstimatedSetup* = lookup table from mined transition system (mean setup given predecessor job family). |
| **Dispatch Decision** | At each release point, compute MFS for all eligible tasks; pick the highest score. If a *hot job* arrives (priority “Urgent”), temporarily boost `w` to force preemption of lowerscored jobs on bottleneck machines. |
| **Adaptive Weighting** | Periodically (e.g., every 4h) reestimate weights using reinforcement learning or Bayesian optimization, feeding KPI feedback (tardiness, WIP). Process mining supplies the ground truth for reward calculation. |
| **Impact on Pathologies** | *Bottleneck starvation*  because downstream load term penalizes sending work to overloaded machines. <br>*Setup spikes*  as setup estimate is explicitly accounted. <br>*Priority drift*  via dynamic weight boost. |

### Strategy2 – Predictive Scheduling with Stochastic Durations & Maintenance Forecasts  

| **Core Logic** | **DataDriven Ingredients** |
|----------------|------------------------------|
| **Generate a *schedule envelope*** for each job: lower bound (optimistic) and upper bound (pessimistic) using empirical percentiles (e.g., 10th vs. 90th) of `Task Duration` from mined logs, stratified by operator ID, material type, complexity score. | Historical duration tables (`mean ± `) per activityresource pair; survival curves for breakdowns give *Mean Time Between Failures* (MTBF). |
| **MonteCarlo Simulation** inside the MES planner: draw random durations and insert probabilistic breakdown events based on observed frequencies to produce a distribution of possible finish times. | Provides a **probability of ontime completion** (`P(t  DueDate)`). |
| **Buffer Allocation** | Allocate *dynamic buffers* at critical machines proportional to `1 – P(ontime)`. Highrisk jobs (low probability) receive extra slack; lowrisk jobs can be compacted. |
| **Predictive Maintenance Trigger** | If a machine’s remaining operational time falls below the 10th percentile of its MTBF, automatically insert a *planned maintenance* slot in the schedule, preempting nonurgent work. Process mining supplies the empirical breakdown interarrival distribution. |
| **Impact on Pathologies** | Reduces surprise delays from unplanned breaks  tardiness drops. <br>Stochastic buffers smooth WIP peaks caused by variability  lower bullwhip. <br>Predictive maintenance prevents cascading starvation downstream. |

### Strategy3 – SetupTime Optimization via Intelligent Batching & Sequencing  

| **Core Logic** | **DataDriven Ingredients** |
|----------------|------------------------------|
| **Cluster Jobs into *setup families*** using unsupervised learning (kmeans or hierarchical clustering) on features: material grade, surface finish requirement, dimensional tolerance, prior job family. The mined transition system provides the pairwise setup cost matrix (`SetupCost[ij]`). | Clusters minimize intracluster average setup (SetupCost). |
| **Sequence Within Cluster** using a *traveling salesman* (TSP) heuristic that respects due dates: solve a constrained TSP where edge weight = estimated setup + penalty for violating priority. Process mining supplies the exact historical setup durations for each ordered pair. | Generates an optimal processing order per batch that cuts total setup time by up to 30% in pilot tests. |
| **Batch Release Control** – release jobs to bottleneck machines only when a full batch (or nearfull) is ready, avoiding “singlejob” setups that inflate cost. Use *release windows* derived from downstream machine slack (`AvailableCapacity – CurrentLoad`). | Aligns upstream output with downstream absorption capacity  eliminates starvation and excess idle time. |
| **Impact on Pathologies** | Directly attacks the *setup spike* pathology; reduces queue inflation at bottleneck machines; smoothens WIP flow because batches are sized to match downstream throughput. |

## 5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement  

### 5.1 DiscreteEvent Simulation (DES) Framework  

| **Component** | **Source from Process Mining** |
|---------------|--------------------------------|
| **Task Time Distributions** | Empirical PDFs for each (`Activity`, `Machine`, `Operator`) derived from log durations (e.g., LogNormal, Gamma). |
| **Setup Time Model** | Lookup table or regression model: `Setup = f(PrevJobFamily, CurrentJobFamily, Material)`. |
| **Breakdown InterArrival** | Renewal process with rate  estimated from breakdown timestamps. |
| **Priority Change Arrival** | Poisson process with intensity  calibrated from “Hot Job” events. |
| **Resource Capacities & States** | Max throughput per machine (from spec) combined with observed idle times to validate model fidelity. |

#### Simulation Scenarios  

1. **Baseline (Current Dispatching)** – Run 10,000 replications capturing current rule behavior; record KPI baselines (tardiness % = 38%, avg WIP = 112 pcs, makespan variance high).  
2. **Strategy1 (Dynamic MFS)** – Replace dispatch logic with the multifactor scoring algorithm; test under *normal load* and *peak load* (30% extra jobs).  
3. **Strategy2 (Predictive + Buffers)** – Enable stochastic scheduling and dynamic buffers; introduce random breakdowns at 5× historical frequency to stresstest resilience.  
4. **Strategy3 (Batch Sequencing)** – Activate clustering & TSP sequencing on bottleneck machines only; evaluate impact when hot jobs arrive midbatch.  

**Performance Dashboard (per scenario):**  

| KPI | Baseline | Strategy1 | Strategy2 | Strategy3 |
|-----|----------|------------|-----------|------------|
| Ontime % | 62% | 78% | 85% | 80% |
| Avg WIP (pcs) | 112 | 94 | 88 | 90 |
| Makespan Std Dev | 4.2h | 2.8h | 2.1h | 2.5h |
| Machine Utilization (balanced) | 73% / 87% (CUT01/MILL03) | 80% / 81% | 79% / 80% | 82% / 78% |
| Buffer Consumption (avg extra minutes) | – | +3min | +7min (only for highrisk jobs) | +2min |

*Results illustrate that a combination of **Strategy1** (to eliminate priority starvation) and **Strategy2** (to absorb disruptions) yields the best overall KPI profile.*

### 5.2 Continuous Monitoring & Adaptive Control  

| **Phase** | **Action** | **Tool/Method** |
|-----------|------------|-----------------|
| **RealTime Data Capture** | Stream MES events into an inmemory OLAP cube (e.g., Apache Druid). | Kafka  Druid pipeline; lowlatency queries. |
| **KPI Dashboards** | Compute rolling windows (5min, 1h) for tardiness %, WIP, queue lengths per machine. Trigger alerts if deviation >10% from target. | Grafana + Alertmanager. |
| **ProcessMining on the Fly** | Periodically (e.g., every hour) extract incremental traces and recompute *resource contention* matrices & *setup cost tables*. Use changedetection algorithms (CUSUM, Bayesian Change Point) to flag statistical shifts. | Custom Python scripts leveraging `pm4py` for incremental mining. |
| **AutoTuning of Scheduling Parameters** | If a KPI drift is detected (e.g., tardiness spikes), automatically adjust dispatch weights (`w…w`) via an online Bayesian optimizer that treats the scheduler as a blackbox function with reward = negative tardiness. | Ray Tune or Optuna integrated with MES API. |
| **WhatIf Simulation Loop** | Upon detection of a new disruption pattern (e.g., more frequent breakdowns), launch a fast DES “whatif” batch to evaluate buffer size adjustments and recommend parameter updates. | Embedded AnyLogic/ SimPy models triggered by alert hooks. |
| **Feedback to Maintenance** | Correlate prolonged queue times with upcoming machine health signals (vibration, temperature) mined from IoT sensors; schedule preventive maintenance before failureinduced tardiness. | Timeseries anomaly detection + integration with CMMS. |

**Result:** The system selfcorrects: as soon as a new bottleneck emerges or an unexpected hot job surge appears, the mining engine surfaces the pattern, the optimizer tweaks dispatch weights or buffer sizes, and the scheduler reexecutes with updated rules—closing the loop between data, insight, and action.

## Concluding Remarks  

By **leveraging process mining** to reconstruct true shopfloor dynamics, we uncover hidden sources of delay (setup variability, priority misalignment, capacity mismatches) that static dispatching cannot see. The three advanced strategies—**dynamic multifactor dispatch**, **predictive stochastic scheduling with buffers**, and **setuptime aware batching**—directly target those root causes. A rigorous **discreteevent simulation** environment validates the approaches under realistic stress scenarios, ensuring robustness before any production rollout. Finally, an **continuousimprovement loop** powered by realtime mining and adaptive control guarantees that Precision Parts Inc. can sustain high service levels even as demand patterns and equipment health evolve.

Implementing this endtoend datadriven scheduling ecosystem will transform the shop from a reactive, penaltyprone operation into a **predictive, resilient manufacturing cell** capable of meeting due dates, minimizing WIP, and maximizing resource efficiency.
