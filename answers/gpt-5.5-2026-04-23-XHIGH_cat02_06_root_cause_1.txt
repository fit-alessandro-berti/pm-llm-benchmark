## Potential Root Causes

### 1. Escalations appear to increase cycle time

Cases **102** and **105** both involve escalation to Level-2 support and both are delayed.

- Case 102: escalation adds handoff delays, then resolution happens the next day.
- Case 105: escalation causes a very large delay — **28 hours** before the issue is investigated again.

This suggests a possible **Level-2 capacity or queue bottleneck**.

### 2. Long waits before investigation

Cases **102** and **104** wait several hours before meaningful investigation begins.

- Case 102: assigned at 09:00, but investigation does not happen until 14:00.
- Case 104: assigned at 09:30, but investigation does not start until 13:00.
- Case 105: initial investigation starts quickly, but after escalation there is a 28-hour wait before the next investigation.

This indicates possible problems with **agent availability, queue prioritization, or handoff management**.

### 3. Repeated 19-hour delay from investigation to resolution

Cases **102, 104, and 105** all have a **19-hour gap** between `Investigate Issue` and `Resolve Ticket`.

That repeated pattern suggests tickets investigated in the afternoon may not be resolved until the next morning. This could be caused by:

- end-of-day cutoffs,
- lack of after-hours coverage,
- unresolved handoffs between shifts,
- dependency on specialized staff,
- batching of resolution work the next morning.

### 4. Escalation is not the only issue

Case **104** is delayed even though it was not escalated. Its path is the standard process:

`Receive  Triage  Assign  Investigate  Resolve  Close`

However, it still takes **24h 10m** because of long waits before investigation and resolution. This suggests there may also be a **Level-1 workload or scheduling bottleneck**.

## Recommendations

1. **Monitor time between assignment and investigation**  
   Set SLA alerts if a ticket waits more than a defined threshold, for example 1 hour, before investigation begins.

2. **Improve escalation handling**  
   The 28-hour delay in Case 105 suggests Level-2 escalations need stronger queue management, ownership, and response-time targets.

3. **Introduce earlier escalation decisions**  
   If triage can identify complex tickets earlier, they can be routed directly to Level-2 instead of waiting in Level-1 first.

4. **Reduce overnight carryover**  
   The repeated 19-hour investigation-to-resolution gap suggests tickets are being pushed into the next day. Consider same-day resolution targets, better end-of-day handoffs, or limited after-hours coverage for high-priority tickets.

5. **Improve handoff documentation**  
   For escalated tickets, ensure Level-1 provides complete diagnostic notes so Level-2 does not need to repeat investigation unnecessarily.

6. **Analyze by ticket type, priority, and team**  
   Additional data such as ticket category, severity, assigned agent, and whether the ticket was waiting for a customer or vendor would help confirm whether the bottleneck is staffing, complexity, or process design.

Overall, the main bottlenecks appear to be **escalation delays, late investigation starts, and overnight resolution delays**.
