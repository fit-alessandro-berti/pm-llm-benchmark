### Explanation
I grouped events into cases based on the primary artifact or task being worked on, using window titles or inferred task names as Case IDs (e.g., document names for Word/Excel/PDF files, "Annual_Meeting_Email" for the specific email interaction). This creates logical units of user work, such as editing a specific document or handling an email, allowing for analysis of individual processes like document workflows or communication tasks. SWITCH events were included as transitional activities (e.g., "Switch to Email") and assigned to the target case to capture workflow handoffs without duplication. Low-level actions were mapped to higher-level, standardized activities (e.g., FOCUS/TYPING  "Open Document"/"Edit Document"; SAVE  "Save Document"; CLICK/TYPING for email  "Open Email"/"Compose Reply"; SCROLL/HIGHLIGHT  "Scroll Content"/"Highlight Text") for consistency and analyst-friendliness, with descriptions preserving key details like typed content. Events are timestamp-ordered within cases, enabling discovery of process models like sequential editing-saving-closing patterns, while interleaving reflects multitasking. Unrelated or redundant events (none in this log) were not included to focus on meaningful process steps.
