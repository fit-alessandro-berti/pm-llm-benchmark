This visualization depicts a **proclet-based process model** for a laboratory testing workflow, consisting of three main parts:

## (a) Two Proclet Classes Connected Through Two Channels

This is the main process diagram showing two parallel workflows:

**Left side - "Lab visit" proclet** (performed by doctor/lab assistant):
- Starts with *Order entry* (by doctor)
- *Take blood sample* (by lab assistant)
- *Receive result*
- *Judge results* — branching into either *Everything fine* or *Do tests again*
- *Receive updated result*
- *Send report* (by lab assistant)

**Right side - "Lab test" proclet** (performed by chemical analyst):
- Starts with *Perform test*
- *Make report*
- *Finish lab test*, or alternatively *Perform test again*  *Provide updated report*

The two proclets communicate through two channels (shown as dashed rectangles):
- **Order system** channel (connecting order entry with test performance)
- **HIS** (Hospital Information System) channel (connecting result transmission)

Cardinalities like `1..*` and `1..?` indicate how many messages are exchanged between proclets.

## (b) Class Diagram

A simple UML-style class diagram showing the relationship between the two proclet classes: one **Lab visit** *requires* one or more (**1..\***) **Lab tests**, while each lab test relates to exactly one (**1..1**) lab visit.

## (c) Example of a Performative

A table illustrating a sample message exchanged via a channel:
| Time | Channel | Sender | Receivers | Action | Content | Scope | Direction |
|------|---------|--------|-----------|--------|---------|-------|-----------|
| 11:00 | Order system | Lab visit - John | Lab test - HGB John | Create | "Can you perform a HGB test for John?" | Private | OUT |

## Overall Purpose

The visualization illustrates how **proclets** (lightweight, interacting workflow processes) can model complex healthcare processes where multiple related but independent workflows (a patient's lab visit and individual lab tests) need to coordinate through structured message exchanges rather than being forced into a single monolithic process.
