## 1) Anomalies in the temporal profile model

Given the intended sequence **R  A  E  P  N  C**, the following modeled pairs look inconsistent or suspicious:

1. **R  P (Receive to Approve): (AVG 90000s, STDEV 3600s)**
   - The STDEV is **very small ( 1 hour)** relative to the mean (~25 hours).
   - This suggests approval is happening on a **nearly fixed schedule** (e.g., batch job at a set time next day) or the timestamps for intermediate steps may be **mis-ordered/derived**, rather than reflecting true event timing.

2. **P  N (Approve to Notify): (AVG 604800s, STDEV 172800s)**
   - Mean  **7 days** with SD  **2 days** is unusually large and variable.
   - This indicates a **major waiting stage** or irregular process behavior: sometimes notify is delayed (backlog/compliance holds), sometimes it may occur earlier/later than expected.

3. **E  N (Evaluate to Notify): (AVG 300s, STDEV 60s)**
   - Only **~5 minutes** on average with SD **~1 minute** is extremely fast for ōEvaluate  Notifyö.
   - This often indicates that either:
     - the process is **skipping P** for some cases (notify triggered right after evaluation), or
     - the ōPö step is recorded inconsistently (wrong timestamps / wrong activity mapping), or
     - ōEvaluateö timestamps actually represent a different sub-step.

4. **A  C (Assign to Close): (AVG 7200s, STDEV 3600s)** *without consistently observed E/P*
   - If ōCloseö occurs on average **~2 hours** after assign, but your event stream usually includes **E and P**, that implies some claims may be:
     - **prematurely closed**, or
     - **missing/merged events** (E/P not recorded), or
     - handled via a ōfast trackö path that the model didnÆt stratify.

5. **E  C: (AVG 3600s, STDEV 3000s)**
   - Mean  **1 hour** but SD  **50 minutes** is broad.
   - Suggests **mixed populations**: some claims close quickly after evaluation, others take much longerŚpossibly due to claim complexity or exception handling.

## 2) Hypotheses for why these anomalies might exist

1. **Batch/scheduled automation driving fixed delays**
   - The very low SD for **RP** suggests a **deterministic scheduler** (e.g., approvals generated in a daily batch at a fixed cutoff time), or an ETL pipeline that stamps P times in a rigid way.

2. **Event recording inconsistencies / wrong activity classification**
   - If **N happens ~5 minutes after E**, it may mean:
     - approvals are not recorded properly for those cases,
     - ōNotifyö is being triggered off evaluation completion rather than approval completion, or
     - timestamps are being written with incorrect activity labels.

3. **Backlog or operational constraints between P and N**
   - The large mean and SD for **PN** can come from:
     - manual review/queueing before notification,
     - customer-contact effort scheduling,
     - compliance/legal holds,
     - regional staffing differences.

4. **Fast-track closure path / missing steps**
   - **AC ~2 hours** implies either:
     - a subset of claims follows a ōstraight-throughö path (limited evaluation/approval), or
     - events **E/P** are missing, not logged, or stored under different activity codes.

5. **Mixed claim types or complexity not modeled**
   - Broad variability for **EC** can reflect multiple workflows:
     - simple claims that close quickly,
     - complex claims that require additional investigation (possibly encoded as different resources or additional activity types not in the fixed 6-step model).

## 3) Verification approaches using SQL (PostgreSQL)

Below are practical queries to (a) find candidate anomalous claims, (b) quantify distribution by segments, and (c) check whether steps are being skipped/mis-ordered.

> Assumption: `claim_events` has multiple rows per claim and `activity` is coded exactly as: **R,A,E,P,N,C**.

### A. Compute actual times for key pairs and flag outliers

#### 1) Find claims with ōRPö near fixed delay (low variability) / or unusually consistent
```sql
WITH times AS (
  SELECT
    ce1.claim_id,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (MIN(ceP.timestamp) - MIN(ceR.timestamp))) AS r_to_p_seconds
  FROM claim_events ceR
  JOIN claim_events ceP
    ON ceP.claim_id = ceR.claim_id
   AND ceR.activity = 'R'
   AND ceP.activity = 'P'
   AND ceP.timestamp >= ceR.timestamp
  GROUP BY ce1.claim_id
)
SELECT
  PERCENTILE_CONT(0.5) WITHIN GROUP (ORDER BY r_to_p_seconds) AS median_seconds,
  AVG(r_to_p_seconds) AS avg_seconds,
  STDDEV_SAMP(r_to_p_seconds) AS stdev_seconds,
  COUNT(*) AS n_claims
FROM times;
```

#### 2) Flag individual claims where ōENö is extremely fast (suggest skip of P or mislabel)
```sql
WITH en AS (
  SELECT
    ceE.claim_id,
    MIN(ceN.timestamp) AS n_ts,
    MIN(ceE.timestamp) AS e_ts
  FROM claim_events ceE
  JOIN claim_events ceN
    ON ceN.claim_id = ceE.claim_id
   AND ceE.activity = 'E'
   AND ceN.activity = 'N'
   AND ceN.timestamp >= ceE.timestamp
  GROUP BY ceE.claim_id
)
SELECT
  claim_id,
  EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n_ts - e_ts)) AS e_to_n_seconds
FROM en
WHERE EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n_ts - e_ts)) < 600  -- <10 minutes (adjust threshold)
ORDER BY e_to_n_seconds ASC
LIMIT 200;
```

#### 3) Flag claims where ōPNö is excessively long
```sql
WITH pn AS (
  SELECT
    ceP.claim_id,
    MIN(ceN.timestamp) AS n_ts,
    MIN(ceP.timestamp) AS p_ts
  FROM claim_events ceP
  JOIN claim_events ceN
    ON ceN.claim_id = ceP.claim_id
   AND ceP.activity = 'P'
   AND ceN.activity = 'N'
   AND ceN.timestamp >= ceP.timestamp
  GROUP BY ceP.claim_id
)
SELECT
  claim_id,
  EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n_ts - p_ts)) AS p_to_n_seconds
FROM pn
WHERE EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n_ts - p_ts)) > 86400 * 5  -- >5 days
ORDER BY p_to_n_seconds DESC
LIMIT 200;
```

#### 4) Flag claims where ōACö is very fast (possible premature closure / missing events)
```sql
WITH ac AS (
  SELECT
    ceA.claim_id,
    MIN(ceC.timestamp) AS c_ts,
    MIN(ceA.timestamp) AS a_ts
  FROM claim_events ceA
  JOIN claim_events ceC
    ON ceC.claim_id = ceA.claim_id
   AND ceA.activity = 'A'
   AND ceC.activity = 'C'
   AND ceC.timestamp >= ceA.timestamp
  GROUP BY ceA.claim_id
)
SELECT
  claim_id,
  EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (c_ts - a_ts)) AS a_to_c_seconds
FROM ac
WHERE EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (c_ts - a_ts)) < 3*3600  -- <3 hours
ORDER BY a_to_c_seconds ASC
LIMIT 200;
```

### B. Check whether expected steps are missing/skipped (core for EN fast and AC fast)

#### 1) Claims where N occurs before P (strong indicator of wrong ordering or skipped approval)
```sql
SELECT
  ceN.claim_id,
  MIN(ceP.timestamp) AS p_ts,
  MIN(ceN.timestamp) AS n_ts
FROM claim_events ceN
LEFT JOIN claim_events ceP
  ON ceP.claim_id = ceN.claim_id
 AND ceP.activity = 'P'
 AND ceP.timestamp >= ceN.timestamp
WHERE ceN.activity = 'N'
GROUP BY ceN.claim_id
HAVING MIN(ceP.timestamp) IS NULL;  -- no P after N
```

#### 2) Claims that have E and N but have no P at all
```sql
SELECT c.claim_id
FROM claims c
WHERE EXISTS (
  SELECT 1 FROM claim_events ce
  WHERE ce.claim_id = c.claim_id AND ce.activity = 'E'
)
AND EXISTS (
  SELECT 1 FROM claim_events ce
  WHERE ce.claim_id = c.claim_id AND ce.activity = 'N'
)
AND NOT EXISTS (
  SELECT 1 FROM claim_events ce
  WHERE ce.claim_id = c.claim_id AND ce.activity = 'P'
)
LIMIT 200;
```

#### 3) Claims closed very quickly after assignment AND missing E or P
```sql
WITH quick_close AS (
  SELECT
    ceA.claim_id,
    MIN(ceC.timestamp) AS c_ts,
    MIN(ceA.timestamp) AS a_ts
  FROM claim_events ceA
  JOIN claim_events ceC
    ON ceC.claim_id = ceA.claim_id
   AND ceA.activity = 'A'
   AND ceC.activity = 'C'
   AND ceC.timestamp >= ceA.timestamp
  GROUP BY ceA.claim_id
)
SELECT qc.claim_id
FROM quick_close qc
WHERE EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (qc.c_ts - qc.a_ts)) < 3*3600
AND (
  NOT EXISTS (SELECT 1 FROM claim_events ce WHERE ce.claim_id = qc.claim_id AND ce.activity = 'E')
  OR NOT EXISTS (SELECT 1 FROM claim_events ce WHERE ce.claim_id = qc.claim_id AND ce.activity = 'P')
)
LIMIT 200;
```

### C. Segment the anomalies by adjuster/resource/claim_type/region

#### 1) Does EN fast cluster by resource?
```sql
WITH en AS (
  SELECT
    ceE.claim_id,
    ceE.resource AS e_resource,
    MIN(ceN.timestamp) AS n_ts,
    MIN(ceE.timestamp) AS e_ts
  FROM claim_events ceE
  JOIN claim_events ceN
    ON ceN.claim_id = ceE.claim_id
   AND ceE.activity = 'E'
   AND ceN.activity = 'N'
   AND ceN.timestamp >= ceE.timestamp
  GROUP BY ceE.claim_id, ceE.resource
)
SELECT
  e_resource,
  COUNT(*) AS n_claims,
  AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n_ts - e_ts))) AS avg_seconds,
  STDDEV_SAMP(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n_ts - e_ts))) AS stdev_seconds
FROM en
WHERE EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n_ts - e_ts)) < 600
GROUP BY e_resource
ORDER BY n_claims DESC;
```

#### 2) Does PN delay cluster by resource or adjuster region?
(If `resource` maps to adjuster_id, you can join; otherwise use `resource` directly.)
```sql
SELECT
  ceP.resource AS p_resource,
  COUNT(*) AS n_claims,
  AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ceN.timestamp - ceP.timestamp))) AS avg_seconds
FROM claim_events ceP
JOIN claim_events ceN
  ON ceN.claim_id = ceP.claim_id
 AND ceP.activity = 'P'
 AND ceN.activity = 'N'
 AND ceN.timestamp >= ceP.timestamp
WHERE ceP.activity = 'P'
GROUP BY ceP.resource
HAVING AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ceN.timestamp - ceP.timestamp))) > 86400 * 3
ORDER BY avg_seconds DESC;
```

### D. Quantify ōfixed scheduleö behavior for RP

If R and P timestamps are aligned to batch cutoffs, youÆll often see P times occur at specific clock minutes/hours.

```sql
WITH rp AS (
  SELECT
    ceR.claim_id,
    MIN(ceR.timestamp) AS r_ts,
    MIN(ceP.timestamp) AS p_ts
  FROM claim_events ceR
  JOIN claim_events ceP
    ON ceP.claim_id = ceR.claim_id
   AND ceR.activity = 'R'
   AND ceP.activity = 'P'
   AND ceP.timestamp >= ceR.timestamp
  GROUP BY ceR.claim_id
)
SELECT
  EXTRACT(HOUR FROM p_ts) AS p_hour,
  EXTRACT(MINUTE FROM p_ts) AS p_minute,
  COUNT(*) AS n_claims
FROM rp
GROUP BY 1,2
ORDER BY n_claims DESC
LIMIT 20;
```

A strong concentration in a few (hour, minute) buckets supports a batch/automation explanation.

### E. Validate the intended order statistically (global sanity checks)

#### 1) Percent of claims where N happens after P
```sql
SELECT
  AVG(CASE WHEN EXISTS (
    SELECT 1
    FROM claim_events ceP, claim_events ceN
    WHERE ceP.claim_id = c.claim_id
      AND ceN.claim_id = c.claim_id
      AND ceP.activity = 'P'
      AND ceN.activity = 'N'
      AND ceP.timestamp <= ceN.timestamp
  ) THEN 1 ELSE 0 END)::float AS pct_n_after_p
FROM claims c;
```

#### 2) Percent where C happens after A (trivial but checks timestamp ordering)
```sql
SELECT
  AVG(CASE WHEN EXISTS (
    SELECT 1
    FROM claim_events ceA, claim_events ceC
    WHERE ceA.claim_id = c.claim_id
      AND ceC.claim_id = c.claim_id
      AND ceA.activity = 'A'
      AND ceC.activity = 'C'
      AND ceA.timestamp <= ceC.timestamp
  ) THEN 1 ELSE 0 END)::float AS pct_c_after_a
FROM claims c;
```
