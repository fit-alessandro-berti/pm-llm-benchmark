**1. Analyzing Resource Behavior and Assignment Patterns**

The event log provides a rich dataset with Case ID, activities (Ticket Created, Assign L1/L2, Work Start/End, Escalate, Reassign), timestamps, Resource (Agent ID), Agent Tier, Agent Skills, Ticket Priority, Ticket Category, Required Skill, and notes. Preprocessing would involve cleaning timestamps, handling concurrent activities, and enriching cases with derived attributes such as total reassignments, escalation count, skill match (whether the assigned agent's documented skills include the Required Skill), resolution outcome (resolved at tier or escalated further), and SLA compliance (derived from resolution timestamp vs. priority-based target).

**Key metrics to analyze per agent and tier (L1, L2, L3):**
- Workload distribution: Number of cases assigned and completed per agent/tier, normalized by working hours or period. Use dotted charts or workload heatmaps to visualize over time and detect uneven distribution.
- Processing times: Average, median, and variance of time between "Work X Start" and "Work X End" per agent, tier, category, and priority. Highlight agents with unusually long times on specific skills.
- First-contact resolution (FCR) rate for L1: Percentage of cases completed by L1 without escalation.
- Escalation and reassignment frequency: Rate per agent/tier, broken down by category/required skill/priority.
- Skill utilization and match rate: Frequency of assignments where Required Skill matches agent's skills; success rate (resolution without further reassignment) per agent-skill combination.

Process mining techniques reveal *actual* patterns versus intended logic (round-robin within tiers plus manual escalation):
- **Process discovery** (e.g., fuzzy miner or inductive miner) on the full log and filtered views (by tier or priority) to visualize the real flow, including frequent loops in "Assign  Work  Reassign" and escalation paths.
- **Social network analysis / handover networks** on resources to map who escalates or reassigns to whom. This often uncovers preferred escalation pairs (e.g., certain L1 agents always escalate Network tickets to specific L2 agents) rather than skill- or availability-based routing.
- **Organizational mining and role discovery** to compare documented tiers/skills against actual behavior. This may reveal "hidden" specialists (L1 agents frequently handling complex skills) or L2/L3 agents performing basic L1-type work.
- **Resource activity matrix** showing which agents handle which categories/skills.

Skill utilization analysis involves aggregating cases by Required Skill and examining the distribution of handling agents and their tiers. This frequently shows highly skilled L2/L3 specialists spending time on low-complexity tickets (e.g., basic App-CRM issues that L1 could handle), indicating both under-utilization of expertise on critical tasks and potential skill-profile gaps or incorrect initial assignments. These insights directly contrast with the intended round-robin + manual model by exposing systematic deviations driven by agent preferences, availability, or dispatcher habits.

**2. Identifying Resource-Related Bottlenecks and Issues**

Building on the above analysis, bottlenecks are identified through performance analysis, filtering, and correlation:

- **Skill availability bottlenecks**: Waiting time analysis between "Assign" and "Work Start" activities, filtered by Required Skill. High waiting times for scarce skills (e.g., Security-IAM or Networking-Firewall) indicate insufficient specialized agents or poor routing.
- **Reassignment and escalation delays**: Calculate added cycle time for cases with multiple reassignments/escalations versus direct-resolution cases. Use timestamp differences to quantify average delay per reassignment (e.g., 2–4 hours added per extra handoff).
- **Incorrect initial assignments**: Low FCR rates for specific L1 agents or categories, combined with high "Escalate" frequency shortly after "Work L1 End." Analyze dispatcher vs. self-assigned tickets.
- **Overloaded vs. underutilized agents**: Identify via high concurrent open cases (inferred from overlapping activity periods), long queue times, or low activity volume. Dotted charts and resource utilization views make this visible.
- **Correlation with SLA breaches**: Enrich cases with an SLA breach flag (based on resolution time vs. priority target). Compute statistics such as "percentage of SLA breaches in cases with skill mismatch" or "average resolution time increase for cases with >1 reassignment." Typical findings might show 40–60% of P2/P3 breaches linked to reassignments or skill mismatches.

Conformance checking against an ideal model (minimal handoffs, skill-matched assignments) quantifies deviation frequency and impact. These issues directly explain increasing SLA breaches for P2/P3 tickets and specialist time wasted on lower-level work.

**3. Root Cause Analysis for Assignment Inefficiencies**

Potential root causes include:
- Current round-robin logic that ignores skills, current workload, and ticket complexity.
- Outdated or incomplete agent skill profiles (documented skills do not reflect actual capabilities or training).
- Inaccurate initial ticket categorization or Required Skill identification at creation/escalation.
- Lack of real-time visibility into agent availability and workload for dispatchers and L1 agents.
- Insufficient training or decision support for L1 agents, leading to premature escalations.

**Process mining techniques for RCA**:
- **Variant analysis**: Cluster or compare "happy path" cases (low handoffs, resolved within SLA, good skill match) against problematic variants (high reassignments, SLA breaches). This reveals differentiating attributes such as specific categories, creation channel (phone vs. portal), time of day, or particular dispatchers.
- **Decision mining** (decision trees or rule mining) at key decision points ("Assign L1", "Escalate", "Assign L2", "Reassign"). Features include ticket priority, category, required skill, current agent workload (derived), time of day, and historical performance of the potential assignee. The resulting rules often show that assignments are driven more by availability or habit than by skill or predicted resolution probability.
- Cross-referencing with agent skill logs versus actual performance (agents successfully resolving tickets outside their listed skills, or failing within them) highlights profile maintenance issues.

These analyses typically point to systemic rule deficiencies rather than isolated agent underperformance.

**4. Developing Data-Driven Resource Assignment Strategies**

**Strategy 1: Skill-based routing with proficiency scoring**  
Addresses skill mismatches and specialists performing low-level work.  
It leverages skill utilization analysis and historical success rates (resolution without reassignment, processing time) per agent-skill-category combination derived from the log to build a proficiency matrix. Assignment routes tickets to the highest-scoring available agent with matching skills (weighted by proficiency and workload).  
Data required: Required Skill, agent skills (periodically updated), historical performance per skill-agent pair, current availability.  
Expected benefits: 30–50% reduction in reassignments, faster resolution times, higher specialist utilization on complex tasks, and improved SLA compliance for P2/P3 tickets.

**Strategy 2: Workload-aware assignment algorithm**  
Addresses uneven workload and delays from overloaded agents.  
Replaces pure round-robin with a scoring function that considers current open cases, recent processing load, and queue length per tier/skill pool (calculated in real-time from the live event stream). Insights from workload distribution and waiting-time analysis inform thresholds.  
Data required: Real-time event stream for open cases per agent, historical average processing times per agent/ticket type, agent availability.  
Expected benefits: More balanced utilization, reduced waiting times between assignment and work start, fewer SLA breaches caused by queue delays.

**Strategy 3: Predictive routing and L1 empowerment model**  
Addresses excessive escalations and incorrect initial assignments.  
Uses historical data to train a predictive model (on ticket category, priority, description keywords, channel) that estimates probability of L1 resolution and likely required skill. Tickets with high L1-resolution probability are routed to top-performing L1 agents for that type; borderline cases get enhanced knowledge articles or are pre-escalated to the optimal L2. Derived from variant analysis and decision mining on successful vs. failed L1 cases.  
Data required: Full ticket attributes (including text if available), historical outcomes, agent performance per category.  
Expected benefits: Higher L1 FCR rate, reduced load on L2/L3 specialists, fewer unnecessary escalations, and overall faster cycle times.

**5. Simulation, Implementation, and Monitoring**

**Business process simulation** uses the discovered process model (from inductive/fuzzy mining), resource characteristics (processing time distributions per agent/tier/skill), arrival rates, and case attributes from the historical log. Tools supporting simulation (Celonis, Process Mining extensions, or discrete-event simulators like Arena fed with PM data) allow testing of proposed assignment rules. Multiple scenarios are run: current round-robin baseline versus the three new strategies (individually and combined). Key outputs include projected SLA compliance, average resolution time, reassignment rate, resource utilization rates, and cost implications. This quantifies expected impact (e.g., "15% SLA improvement with 20% reduction in L2 workload") and identifies risks (e.g., new bottlenecks under peak load) *before* live implementation. Sensitivity analysis on parameters like ticket volume growth is essential.

**Post-implementation monitoring** involves continuous process mining on the live event stream. A dashboard should track:
- Resource KPIs: reassignment rate, skill-match percentage, workload balance (Gini coefficient or max/min utilization ratio), FCR per tier/agent, agent utilization and idle time.
- Process views: conformance to the new target model, variant frequency (highlighting new problematic paths), bottleneck heatmaps filtered by priority/skill.
- Outcome KPIs: SLA adherence rate by priority and category, average resolution time, correlation between new assignment scores and outcomes.
- Trend analysis over time with alerts for deviations (e.g., sudden drop in skill match or increase in specific agent overload).

Periodic re-mining of roles and decision models ensures strategies remain effective as the organization and ticket patterns evolve. Reviews should occur monthly, with skill profiles and routing rules updated quarterly based on fresh insights.

This approach transforms the current reactive, experience-based assignment logic into a proactive, data-driven system that directly tackles the root causes of SLA breaches, reassignments, and inefficient skill utilization at TechSolve Solutions.
