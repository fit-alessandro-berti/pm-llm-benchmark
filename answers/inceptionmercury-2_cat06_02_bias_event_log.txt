## 2.Where bias manifests

| Dimension | How it appears in the log | Why it is a bias risk |
|-----------|---------------------------|-----------------------|
| **Communitygroup affiliation** (Highland Civic Darts Club) | A **hardcoded +10 points** is added to the PreliminaryScore for any applicant that belongs to this group (C001, C004). | The adjustment is **not based on any creditrisk evidence**; it is a blanket boost that privileges members of a specific social organization.  It creates a *groupbased advantage* that can tip borderline cases from “reject” to “approve”. |
| **LocalResident flag** (TRUE vs FALSE) | Although the flag itself never triggers a numeric adjustment, it is **correlated** with the communitygroup boost (both “TRUE” cases that also have a community affiliation receive the +10).  The only rejected case (C003) is **FALSE** and has no community affiliation, despite having a score (715) that is *higher* than the preadjusted score of C004 (690). | The combination of “LocalResident=TRUE” **and** “CommunityGroupNone” effectively creates a **dualgate**: you must be a resident *and* belong to the club to receive the boost.  Nonresidents (or residents without the club) are left to the raw score alone, even when that raw score is comparable to or higher than a boosted case. |
| **Absence of any offset for “nonaffiliated” applicants** | No negative adjustment is applied for lacking a community group; the system simply *does nothing*.  Consequently, the **baseline** is the raw credit score, while the **favored group** receives an extra 10 points. | This asymmetry creates a **systematic advantage** for the favored group and a **systematic disadvantage** for everyone else.  The effect is magnified when the decision threshold is close to the raw scores (as in C004). |

## 3.Impact on fairness & equity

| Scenario | What would happen with a *pure* scoreonly rule | What actually happens with the current rule | Fairness implication |
|----------|-----------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|----------------------|
| **Applicant A** – 690, resident, member of the club (C004) | Likely **reject** (score below typical approval threshold of ~700). | **Approved** after +10  700. | The applicant receives a *positive* outcome **solely** because of group membership, not because of lower risk. |
| **Applicant B** – 715, nonresident, no club (C003) | **Approve** (score > 700). | **Reject** (no boost). | The applicant is penalised for *not* belonging to the favored group, despite a higher underlying score than ApplicantA. |
| **Applicant C** – 720, resident, no club (C002) | **Approve** (score already above threshold). | **Approve** (no boost needed). | The resident flag alone does not help; only the club matters. |
| **Applicant D** – 740, nonresident, no club (C005) | **Approve** (well above threshold). | **Approve** (no boost needed). | Highscore applicants are unaffected, but the rule still creates a *relative* disadvantage for lowerscore, nonaffiliated applicants. |

**Key fairness concerns**

1. **Disparate impact on nonaffiliated individuals** – People who do not belong to the Highland Civic Darts Club (or any similar community) are *systematically* less likely to be approved when their raw scores sit near the decision boundary.

2. **Geographic / socialcapital bias** – The “CommunityGroup” field is a proxy for **social network, local civic involvement, and possibly geographic location**.  By rewarding that proxy, the model privileges applicants who happen to live in a particular neighborhood or who have the time/resources to join a club, while penalising those who lack those opportunities (e.g., recent movers, lowincome residents, newcomers).

3. **Hidden “doublepenalty”** – The “LocalResident” flag alone does not affect the score, but when combined with the communitygroup boost it creates a **twostep gate**: you must be a resident *and* belong to the club to get the benefit.  Nonresidents (or residents without the club) receive *no* compensating adjustment, even if they have comparable creditworthiness.

4. **Potential regulatory risk** – If the community group is correlated with protected characteristics (e.g., race, ethnicity, socioeconomic status), the +10 boost could be viewed as **disparate treatment** under fairlending statutes (e.g., ECOA, FHA, or local fairhousing laws).

## 4.Why the bias matters even when underlying creditworthiness is similar

*Creditworthiness* in this log is captured by the **PreliminaryScore** (710, 720, 715, 690, 740).  The **only** determinant of the final decision, besides the raw score, is the **+10 community adjustment**.  When two applicants have scores that differ by less than 10 points, the adjustment can **flip the outcome**:

| Applicant | Raw score | Adjusted score | Decision |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|----------|
| C004 (club) | 690 | 700 | Approved |
| C003 (no club) | 715 | 715 | Rejected |

Even though C003’s raw score is **25 points higher**, the system rejects it because the raw score is not “boosted”.  This demonstrates that **group membership outweighs actual risk assessment** for borderline cases, which is the hallmark of a biased decision pipeline.

## 5.How to mitigate the bias

| Step | Action | Rationale |
|------|--------|-----------|
| **1. Audit the feature set** | Verify whether *CommunityGroup* is a legally protected proxy (e.g., race, ethnicity, socioeconomic status) or a geographic proxy. | If it is, the feature should be removed or replaced with a **riskrelevant** variable. |
| **2. Remove the blanket +10** | Eliminate the hardcoded boost.  If community involvement is truly predictive of repayment, replace the boost with a **datadriven, calibrated factor** (e.g., a modest coefficient learned from historical outcomes). | Prevents arbitrary advantage and ensures any adjustment is evidencebased. |
| **3. Introduce fairnessaware modeling** | Apply **fairness constraints** (e.g., demographic parity, equalized odds) or **postprocessing** (threshold adjustments) to ensure that approval rates are comparable across groups defined by *LocalResident* and *CommunityGroup*. | Aligns the model with regulatory expectations and equity goals. |
| **4. Conduct disparity analysis** | Compute **approval rates**, **falsepositive/negative rates**, and **average score differentials** for: <br> • Residents vs. nonresidents <br> • Club members vs. nonmembers |br> • Intersectional groups (e.g., nonresident+nonmember). | Quantifies the impact and provides a baseline for remediation. |
| **5. Add transparent documentation** | Record why each feature is used, the source of any adjustments, and the fairness criteria applied.  Publish a **model card** and **data sheet**. | Improves accountability and facilitates external audits. |
| **6. Periodic reevaluation** | Schedule quarterly reviews of the decision log to detect any drift in disparity metrics. | Ensures that remedial actions remain effective over time. |

## 6.Bottom line

- **Bias manifests through a deterministic +10 point boost for members of the Highland Civic Darts Club**, a benefit that is **not tied to credit risk**.
- The boost **disproportionately favors local residents who belong to that club**, while **penalising equally or more creditworthy applicants who lack the affiliation** (e.g., C003).
- The net effect is a **systemic inequity** that can produce disparate outcomes based on **socialcapital, geography, or proxy protected attributes**, undermining the fairness of the lending process.
- **Removing the blanket adjustment**, replacing it with a **riskbased, fairnessaware approach**, and **monitoring disparity metrics** are essential steps to bring the process into alignment with equitable lending principles.
