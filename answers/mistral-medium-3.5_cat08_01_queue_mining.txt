*Data-Driven Approach to Reduce Patient Waiting Times and Improve Flow*

## **1. Queue Identification and Characterization**
### **1.1 Calculating Waiting Times from Event Logs**
**Definition of Waiting Time:**
In this context, *waiting time* refers to the **idle time between the completion of one activity and the start of the next activity** for a given patient (Case ID). It excludes the *service time* (duration of the activity itself).

**Calculation Method:**
For each patient visit (`Case ID`), we:
1. **Sort activities chronologically** by `Timestamp`.
2. For each consecutive pair of activities (`Activity A  Activity B`):
   - **Waiting Time** = `Timestamp (START of B)` – `Timestamp (COMPLETE of A)`
   - Example (from snippet):
     - For `V1001`: Nurse Assessment completes at **09:25:10**, Doctor Consultation starts at **09:45:55**  **Waiting Time = 20 minutes 45 seconds**.
3. **Aggregate waiting times** by:
   - **Queue Location**: Between which activities (e.g., "Registration  Nurse Assessment").
   - **Resource Involved**: Which staff/room/equipment is the next activity waiting for (e.g., "Dr. Smith" for Doctor Consultation).
   - **Patient Attributes**: Urgency, type (New/Follow-up), specialty.

**Tools/Techniques:**
- Use **Disco, Celonis, or Python (Pandas)** to:
  - Group by `Case ID` and sort timestamps.
  - Compute time deltas between `COMPLETE` of Activity X and `START` of Activity Y.
  - Filter out negative/zero waiting times (data errors).

---

### **1.2 Key Metrics for Queue Characterization**
For each identified queue (e.g., "Registration  Nurse Assessment"), calculate:

| **Metric**               | **Purpose**                                                                 | **Example Calculation**                          |
|--------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------|
| **Average Waiting Time** | Baseline performance measure.                                              | Mean of all waiting times for the queue.         |
| **Median Waiting Time**  | Robust to outliers (e.g., extreme delays).                                  | 50th percentile of waiting times.                |
| **90th Percentile**      | Identifies "worst-case" scenarios affecting patient satisfaction.          | Value below which 90% of waiting times fall.     |
| **Max Waiting Time**     | Highlights extreme outliers (e.g., a patient waiting 2 hours).            | Longest observed wait for the queue.             |
| **Queue Frequency**      | How often this queue occurs (e.g., 100% of patients experience it).        | Count of cases passing through the queue.       |
| **% Cases > Threshold**   | Measures severity (e.g., % of patients waiting >15 mins).                 | (Cases with wait >15 mins) / (Total cases) × 100.|
| **Throughput Time**      | Total visit duration (start of first activity  end of last).              | Sum of all service + waiting times per case.     |
| **Utilization Rate**     | % of time a resource (e.g., Dr. Smith) is busy vs. idle.                   | (Total service time) / (Total available time).   |

---

### **1.3 Identifying Critical Queues**
**Prioritization Criteria:**
1. **Impact on Patient Experience**:
   - Queues with **high 90th percentile waiting times** (e.g., >30 mins) or **high % of cases exceeding thresholds** (e.g., >20% of patients wait >20 mins).
   - Example: If "Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation" has a 90th percentile wait of 45 mins, it’s a top priority.

2. **Frequency and Throughput Impact**:
   - Queues that **affect the majority of patients** (e.g., Registration  Nurse Assessment occurs for 100% of cases).
   - Queues that **significantly contribute to total visit duration** (e.g., if this queue adds 30% to average visit time).

3. **Resource Constraints**:
   - Queues where **next activity’s resource is highly utilized** (e.g., Dr. Smith has 90% utilization, causing long waits for Doctor Consultation).
   - Use **resource profiles** (from process mining) to identify overloaded staff/equipment.

4. **Patient-Specific Pain Points**:
   - Queues disproportionately affecting **urgent patients** or **new patients** (who may require more time).
   - Example: If "Diagnostic Test  Specialist Review" has long waits for urgent cases, prioritize.

**Example Output:**
| **Queue**                     | Avg Wait (mins) | 90th %ile (mins) | % Cases >20 mins | Resource | Priority |
|-------------------------------|-----------------|------------------|------------------|----------|----------|
| Registration  Nurse Assessment | 12              | 25               | 15%              | Nurse 1  | High     |
| Nurse  Doctor (Cardio)       | 22              | 45               | 30%              | Dr. Smith| **Critical** |
| Doctor  ECG Test             | 8               | 15               | 5%               | Tech X   | Medium   |

** Focus on "Nurse  Doctor (Cardio)" first.**

## **2. Root Cause Analysis**
### **2.1 Potential Root Causes for Critical Queues**
Using the example of **"Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation (Cardio)"** with long waits:

| **Root Cause Category**       | **Possible Issues**                                                                 | **Process Mining Techniques to Validate**                          |
|--------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Resource Bottlenecks**       | Dr. Smith is overbooked; only 1 cardiology doctor available.                        | Resource utilization analysis, workload heatmaps.                |
| **Activity Dependencies**      | Doctor cannot start until lab results are back (but lab is slow).                  | Dependency graph, path analysis (e.g., % of cases waiting for labs). |
| **Variability in Service Time**| Some doctor consultations take 5 mins, others 40 mins (unpredictable).              | Histogram of activity durations, coefficient of variation (CV).  |
| **Scheduling Flaws**           | Appointments are double-booked or not staggered with nurse availability.          | Compare scheduled vs. actual start times, slot utilization.       |
| **Patient Arrival Patterns**   | Peak hours (e.g., 10 AM–12 PM) see 2× more patients than capacity.                  | Time-based analysis (hourly/daily queue lengths).                 |
| **Patient Mix**                | New patients (who need longer consultations) are mixed with follow-ups.            | Filter waiting times by `Patient Type`/`Urgency`.                 |
| **Handovers/Coordination**     | Nurse finishes assessment but doctor isn’t notified (manual process).              | Check for gaps between `COMPLETE (Nurse)` and `START (Doctor)`.    |
| **Room/Equipment Limits**      | Only 1 ECG room, causing backlog for cardiology patients.                          | Resource utilization per room/equipment.                         |

**Key Process Mining Techniques:**
- **Bottleneck Analysis**: Identify activities/resources with the longest queues (e.g., Dr. Smith’s schedule).
- **Variant Analysis**: Compare paths of patients with long vs. short waits (e.g., do urgent patients skip queues?).
- **Performance Spectrum**: Visualize waiting times per activity to spot outliers.
- **Resource Workload**: Check if certain doctors/nurses are consistently overloaded.
- **Conformance Checking**: Compare actual flow to ideal flow (e.g., are patients following the intended path?).

---

### **2.2 Example Root Cause Findings**
For **"Nurse  Doctor (Cardio)"**:
1. **Primary Bottleneck**: Dr. Smith has **95% utilization**, with **average consultation time of 25 mins** but **high variance (CV = 0.8)**.
   - Some consultations run long due to complex cases, delaying subsequent patients.
2. **Scheduling Issue**: Appointments are **block-scheduled** (e.g., 4 patients at 10:00 AM), but nurse assessments often finish **5–15 mins late**, causing a domino effect.
3. **Dependency Delay**: 20% of patients require a **pre-consultation blood test**, which adds **10–15 mins** if not completed in advance.
4. **Resource Imbalance**: Only **1 cardiology doctor** is available during peak hours (10 AM–2 PM), while **2 nurses** are free.

## **3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies**
### **Strategy 1: Dynamic Appointment Scheduling with Buffer Times**
**Target Queue**: Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation (Cardio).
**Root Cause Addressed**: Scheduling flaws + variability in service times.
**Data Support**:
- Historical data shows **30% of nurse assessments run >15 mins late**.
- Doctor consultations have **high variance (CV = 0.8)**.
**Proposal**:
- **Implement dynamic slot allocation**:
  - Use **machine learning** to predict consultation duration based on:
    - Patient type (New vs. Follow-up).
    - Reason for visit (from registration data).
    - Doctor’s historical performance.
  - Add **buffer times** (e.g., 10 mins) for high-variance doctors or complex cases.
- **Stagger appointments**:
  - Instead of 4 patients at 10:00 AM, schedule them at **10:00, 10:15, 10:30, 10:45** to account for delays.
**Expected Impact**:
- Reduce **average wait time by 20–30%** (from 22 mins  ~15 mins).
- Decrease **90th percentile wait from 45 mins  30 mins**.
**Implementation**:
- Integrate with **EHR scheduling system** to auto-adjust slots.
- Pilot with **Dr. Smith’s schedule** first (highest impact).

---

### **Strategy 2: Pre-Visit Lab Testing for Cardiology Patients**
**Target Queue**: Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation (Cardio).
**Root Cause Addressed**: Dependency on blood test results.
**Data Support**:
- 20% of cardiology patients require blood tests **before** doctor consultation.
- These patients have **30% longer waits** (avg. 28 mins vs. 20 mins).
**Proposal**:
- **Mandate pre-visit lab tests** for cardiology patients (where medically feasible).
  - Send lab orders **24–48 hours before appointment**.
  - Offer **on-site fast-track lab** for same-day urgent cases.
- **Flag patients in EHR** who need pre-lab work, so nurses can prioritize them.
**Expected Impact**:
- Eliminate **10–15 mins of waiting** for 20% of cardiology patients.
- Reduce **max wait time** for these cases by ~40%.
**Trade-offs**:
- Requires **patient compliance** (may need reminders).
- **Cost**: Additional lab capacity may be needed.

---

### **Strategy 3: Parallelize Nurse and Diagnostic Activities**
**Target Queue**: Nurse Assessment  ECG Test.
**Root Cause Addressed**: Sequential flow causes delays.
**Data Support**:
- ECG tests have **low utilization** (Tech X is idle 40% of the time).
- Nurse assessments and ECGs **do not depend on each other** for most patients.
**Proposal**:
- **Decouple Nurse Assessment and ECG**:
  - After registration, patients can **choose their next step**:
    1. **Nurse Assessment** (if needed for vitals/history).
    2. **Direct to ECG** (if nurse deems unnecessary or if ECG is the first step).
  - Use a **triage system** at registration to route patients.
- **Add a "Diagnostic First" path** for simple cases (e.g., follow-ups only needing an ECG).
**Expected Impact**:
- Reduce **average wait time for ECG by 50%** (from 8 mins  4 mins).
- Improve **throughput** by 15–20% during peak hours.
**Implementation**:
- **Staffing**: Assign 1 nurse to triage at registration.
- **Signage/Technology**: Use digital screens or tablets to guide patients.

## **4. Trade-offs and Constraints**
### **4.1 Potential Negative Side-Effects**
| **Strategy**               | **Trade-off/Risk**                                                                 | **Mitigation Plan**                                                                 |
|----------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Dynamic Scheduling          | May **reduce doctor productivity** if too many buffers are added.                 | Start with **5–10 min buffers**, monitor impact on throughput.                     |
|                            | Patients may **complain about longer appointment slots**.                          | Communicate that this reduces **overall visit time**.                               |
| Pre-Visit Lab Testing       | **Non-compliance**: 15–20% of patients may not complete labs in advance.            | Send **SMS/email reminders**; offer same-day fast-track for non-compliant patients. |
|                            | **Upfront costs** for additional lab capacity.                                     | Pilot with **existing lab capacity** first; scale if ROI is proven.                 |
| Parallelize Activities      | **Complexity**: Requires staff retraining and new workflows.                       | **Pilot in one specialty** (e.g., cardiology) before clinic-wide rollout.            |
|                            | **Risk of misrouting**: Patients may skip necessary steps.                        | **Strict triage protocols** + EHR flags to prevent errors.                          |

### **4.2 Balancing Conflicting Objectives**
| **Objective**               | **Potential Conflict**                              | **Balancing Approach**                                              |
|-----------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Reduce Wait Times           | vs. **Cost Control**                                | Prioritize **low-cost fixes** (e.g., scheduling) before high-cost (e.g., hiring). |
|                             | vs. **Staff Workload**                              | Use **data to right-size buffers** (avoid overloading nurses).       |
| Improve Throughput          | vs. **Quality of Care**                             | **Monitor clinical outcomes** post-implementation (e.g., no drop in diagnosis accuracy). |
| Patient Satisfaction        | vs. **Operational Complexity**                     | **Phase rollouts**: Test changes in one department first.           |

**Example:**
- If **hiring another doctor** reduces waits but increases costs, first try:
  1. **Scheduling optimization** (low cost).
  2. **Pre-visit labs** (moderate cost).
  3. **Only then consider hiring** if KPIs don’t improve.

### **5.2 Ongoing Process Monitoring**
**Tools & Methods:**
1. **Real-Time Dashboards**:
   - Use **Celonis/Disco** to monitor:
     - **Current queue lengths** (e.g., "5 patients waiting for Dr. Smith").
     - **Live waiting times** (color-coded alerts for >20 mins).
   - **Integration**: Pull data from EHR to update dashboards every 15 mins.

2. **Automated Alerts**:
   - Trigger alerts when:
     - A queue’s **90th percentile wait exceeds threshold**.
     - A resource’s **utilization >90%** for >1 hour.

3. **A/B Testing**:
   - Compare **before/after KPIs** for each optimization (e.g., pre-visit labs vs. no pre-visit labs).
   - Use **control groups** (e.g., one doctor keeps old scheduling, another uses dynamic).

4. **Continuous Feedback Loop**:
   - **Monthly reviews**: Analyze trends in waiting times, throughput, and costs.
   - **Staff feedback**: Regular meetings with nurses/doctors to identify new bottlenecks.
   - **Patient feedback**: Monitor NPS and complaints for unintended consequences.

5. **Predictive Analytics**:
   - Use **historical data** to predict:
     - **Peak hours** (allocate extra staff).
     - **Patient no-shows** (reduce overbooking).
     - **High-risk delays** (e.g., patients with pre-lab requirements).

**Example Workflow:**
1. **Week 1**: Deploy dynamic scheduling for cardiology.
2. **Week 2–4**: Monitor KPIs (wait times, throughput, NPS).
3. **Week 5**: If wait times drop but NPS is flat, investigate **communication issues** (e.g., patients unaware of new scheduling).
4. **Week 6**: Adjust strategy (e.g., add patient education).
