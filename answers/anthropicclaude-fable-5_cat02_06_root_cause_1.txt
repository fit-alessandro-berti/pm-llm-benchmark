**Slow cases: 102, 104, and 105** — roughly 10–35× longer than the fast cases (101, 103), which resolve within a couple of hours.

## 2. Root Cause Analysis

### Factor A: Escalations to Level-2 (Cases 102 & 105)
Both escalated cases are among the slowest. However, **the escalation itself is not the main cost — the waiting time around the escalation is:**

- **Case 102:** 2.5h gap between Level-1 assignment and escalation (09:00  11:30), then another 2.5h before Level-2 begins investigating (11:30  14:00), then **19 hours** from investigation start to resolution.
- **Case 105:** The most extreme case. After escalation at 10:00 on 03-01, Level-2 does not begin investigating until **14:00 the next day — a 28-hour handoff queue delay**. Then another 19 hours pass before resolution. This case also shows **rework** (Investigate  Escalate  Investigate again), meaning Level-1 effort was partially wasted.

### Factor B: Long waits even without escalation (Case 104)
Case 104 was never escalated yet still took ~24 hours:
- **3.5h delay** between assignment (09:30) and start of investigation (13:00) — suggesting Level-1 agent backlog or workload imbalance.
- **19h gap** between investigation (13:00) and resolution (next day 08:00) — likely an overnight stall (no after-hours coverage or the ticket sat untouched).

### Factor C: The recurring "overnight gap" pattern
All three slow cases share a ~19-hour investigation-to-resolution gap spanning the evening/night. This points to a **systemic coverage issue**: work that isn't completed by end of day waits until the next morning, regardless of complexity.

## 3. Insights and Recommendations

**How these factors inflate cycle time:**
- Escalations introduce **handoff queues**: the ticket sits idle waiting for Level-2 capacity (worst case: 28h in Case 105). Idle waiting—not active work—dominates total cycle time.
- Misrouting at triage (Cases 102, 105 went to Level-1 first) adds wasted Level-1 effort plus a full handoff delay.
- Lack of after-business-hours processing causes any unfinished ticket to absorb a fixed overnight penalty.

**Recommendations:**
1. **Improve triage accuracy:** Identify complex tickets at triage and route them **directly to Level-2**, eliminating the Level-1 detour and the escalation handoff (would save many hours in cases like 102 and 105).
2. **Set SLAs on escalation pickup:** A target such as "Level-2 must begin investigation within 2 hours of escalation" would have prevented the 28-hour queue delay in Case 105. Monitor this handoff as a KPI.
3. **Increase or rebalance Level-2 capacity:** The long escalation queue suggests Level-2 is a capacity bottleneck.
4. **Address Level-1 backlog:** Case 104's 3.5h assignment-to-investigation lag indicates uneven workload distribution; consider load-based assignment.
5. **Mitigate the overnight stall:** Introduce shift coverage / follow-the-sun support, or at minimum prioritize "carried over" tickets first thing each morning.
6. **Reduce rework loops:** Track InvestigateEscalateInvestigate patterns; better diagnostic tooling or knowledge bases for Level-1 can prevent duplicated investigation effort.

**Bottom line:** The delays are driven almost entirely by **waiting time** (escalation queues, agent backlog, overnight stalls) rather than actual processing effort. Reducing handoff latency and improving initial routing would yield the largest cycle-time improvements.
