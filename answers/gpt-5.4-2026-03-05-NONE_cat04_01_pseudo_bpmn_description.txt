Here’s a structured explanation of the pseudo-BPMN process.

---

# 1. Overall purpose of the process

This process describes how a business handles a **customer request** from intake through validation, possible approval, invoicing, and final confirmation.

At a high level, the process:

1. Receives a customer request
2. Determines whether it is **standard** or **custom**
3. Processes the request according to that type
4. Decides whether managerial approval is needed
5. If approved or no approval is required, generates an invoice
6. Sends confirmation to the customer
7. Ends the process

There is also a **rework loop** if approval is denied, allowing the request to be adjusted and resubmitted.

---

# 2. Step-by-step explanation

## Start Event
The **Start Event** marks the beginning of the process. It indicates that something has triggered the workflow, typically the arrival of a new customer request.

---

## Task A: "Receive Customer Request"
This is the intake step.

### Purpose:
- Capture the customer’s request
- Register the request in the system
- Gather basic information needed for processing

This task likely includes collecting:
- customer details
- product/service requested
- quantity/specifications
- urgency/timeline
- whether the request appears standard or custom

---

# 3. First decision: request type

## Gateway (XOR): Check Request Type
This is an **exclusive gateway (XOR)**, meaning **only one path can be taken**.

### Meaning:
The process examines the nature of the customer request and chooses between:
- **Standard request**
- **Custom request**

Only one of these branches is followed for a given case.

---

# 4. Standard request path

## [If Type = Standard] Task B1: "Perform Standard Validation"

### Purpose:
This step verifies that a normal, predefined request can be fulfilled under standard business rules.

Typical checks might include:
- completeness of the request
- validity of customer data
- whether the requested item/service matches existing catalog offerings
- compliance with standard pricing or policy rules

This is usually faster and more routine than the custom path.

---

## Gateway (AND): "Run Parallel Checks"
This is a **parallel gateway (AND)**.

### Meaning:
Both downstream tasks are started at the same time:
- **Task C1: Credit Check**
- **Task C2: Inventory Check**

Unlike XOR, where one path is chosen, an AND gateway means **all branches must be executed**.

---

## Task C1: "Credit Check"

### Purpose:
Evaluate the customer’s financial standing.

This may involve:
- checking payment history
- checking credit limits
- verifying outstanding balances
- ensuring the customer is eligible for the transaction

This protects the business from financial risk.

---

## Task C2: "Inventory Check"

### Purpose:
Verify whether the requested goods or resources are available.

This may include:
- checking stock levels
- checking production capacity
- checking supplier availability
- identifying shortages or lead-time constraints

This ensures the order can be fulfilled.

---

## All Parallel Checks Completed (Join)
This is the synchronization point for the parallel work.

### Meaning:
The process cannot continue until **both**:
- credit check, and
- inventory check

have been completed.

This is the **join** of the earlier AND split.

---

## Task D: "Calculate Delivery Date"

### Purpose:
Determine when the order can realistically be delivered.

This calculation depends on information from prior checks, such as:
- stock availability
- fulfillment capacity
- customer conditions
- logistics constraints

This step completes the standard processing path before the process moves into the common approval/invoicing section.

---

# 5. Custom request path

## [If Type = Custom] Task B2: "Perform Custom Feasibility Analysis"

### Purpose:
Evaluate whether the requested customization is technically, operationally, and commercially possible.

This step is different from standard validation because custom requests are not routine. They require deeper analysis.

Typical considerations:
- can the requested customization actually be produced?
- does the business have the needed skills/resources?
- is the customization profitable?
- does it fit technical constraints or compliance requirements?
- what delivery implications would it create?

This is usually more complex than standard validation.

---

## Gateway (XOR): "Is Customization Feasible?"
This is another **exclusive gateway**.

### Meaning:
Based on the feasibility analysis, the process chooses one of two outcomes:

- **Yes**  proceed with a quotation
- **No**  reject the request

Only one branch is taken.

---

## [If Yes] Task E1: "Prepare Custom Quotation"

### Purpose:
Create a tailored offer for the customer.

This may include:
- custom pricing
- estimated delivery time
- special materials/services
- engineering or design effort
- assumptions and conditions

This task effectively translates the feasible custom request into a formal business proposal.

---

## [If No] Task E2: "Send Rejection Notice"
If the request is not feasible, the business informs the customer that it cannot fulfill the customization.

### Purpose:
- communicate rejection clearly
- potentially explain the reason
- close the case formally

---

## End Event (after rejection)
If customization is infeasible and a rejection notice is sent, the process ends here for that request.

This is a **terminal path** specific to unsuccessful custom requests.

---

# 6. Convergence after standard/custom handling

## "After Standard or Custom Path Tasks Completed"
This indicates that the two successful branches converge:

- **Standard path:** after Task D
- **Custom path:** after Task E1

These are the points at which the request is ready for the next common decision: whether approval is needed.

### Important note:
The custom rejection path via **E2** does **not** converge here, because it already ends the process.

---

# 7. Approval decision

## Gateway (XOR): "Is Approval Needed?"
This is an **exclusive gateway**.

### Meaning:
The process determines whether the request needs managerial or special authorization.

Possible reasons approval may be needed:
- high order value
- unusual terms
- custom pricing
- elevated risk
- low margin
- policy exceptions
- strategic customers or contractual conditions

There are two possible outcomes:

- **Yes**  go to approval task
- **No**  skip approval and generate invoice directly

Only one path is taken.

---

# 8. Approval path

## [If Yes] Task F: "Obtain Manager Approval"

### Purpose:
A manager or authorized person reviews the request before commitment.

This review may assess:
- commercial viability
- margin
- risk
- pricing exceptions
- delivery commitments
- custom terms

This is a control step to reduce business risk and ensure oversight.

---

## Gateway (XOR): "Is Approval Granted?"
Another exclusive gateway.

### Meaning:
After managerial review, one of two outcomes occurs:

- **Yes**  proceed
- **No**  rework is required

Only one branch is chosen.

---

## [If Yes] Task G: "Generate Final Invoice"

### Purpose:
Create the official invoice or billing document.

This indicates the request has been accepted commercially and is moving toward fulfillment/confirmation.

This task may include:
- final pricing
- taxes
- payment terms
- customer billing details
- approved custom or standard conditions

---

## [If No] Task H: "Re-evaluate Conditions"

### Purpose:
If approval is denied, the request is not simply abandoned. Instead, the business revisits the offer or fulfillment terms.

This may involve:
- adjusting price
- changing delivery dates
- revising scope
- modifying customization details
- addressing risk concerns
- improving commercial terms

This is a **rework step**.

---

# 9. Loop-back behavior after rejection of approval

## Loop back to Task E1 (for Custom Path) or Task D (for Standard Path)

This is an important part of the design.

### Meaning:
After conditions are re-evaluated, the process returns to the relevant earlier step depending on the request type:

- **Custom request**  loop back to **Task E1: Prepare Custom Quotation**
- **Standard request**  loop back to **Task D: Calculate Delivery Date**

### Why these different loop targets?
Because the kind of rework needed differs by request type:

#### For standard requests:
Rework likely affects operational or commercial details such as:
- delivery timing
- conditions of fulfillment
- available options

So the process returns to **Task D**, where delivery commitments can be recalculated.

#### For custom requests:
Rework often changes the actual offer being made:
- revised pricing
- altered custom specifications
- updated feasibility assumptions
- modified proposal terms

So the process returns to **Task E1**, where a new custom quotation can be prepared.

### What happens after looping back?
After the revised task is completed:
- the process again reaches the **“Is Approval Needed?”** decision
- if approval is still needed, it goes through approval again
- this can repeat until approval is granted or the process is otherwise stopped outside the model

This is a classic **rework loop**.

---

# 10. No-approval-needed path

## [If No Approval Needed] Task G: "Generate Final Invoice"

If the request is low-risk, routine, or otherwise within policy, the process bypasses managerial review entirely.

### Purpose:
Allow straightforward cases to be processed faster without unnecessary delay.

This improves efficiency for:
- low-value standard orders
- pre-approved custom quotes
- orders within established authority limits

---

# 11. Final common step

## Task I: "Send Confirmation to Customer"

### Purpose:
Notify the customer that the order/request has been successfully processed.

This confirmation likely includes:
- acceptance of the request
- invoice information
- delivery date or expected timeline
- quotation acceptance details
- next steps

This is the final customer-facing communication in the successful process path.

---

## End Event
The process ends after confirmation is sent.

This indicates that all required internal handling for this workflow instance has been completed.

---

# 12. Meaning of the gateways

Here is a summary of the gateway logic.

## XOR Gateway
**Exclusive gateway = one path only**

Used in this process for:
1. **Check Request Type**
   - standard OR custom
2. **Is Customization Feasible?**
   - yes OR no
3. **Is Approval Needed?**
   - yes OR no
4. **Is Approval Granted?**
   - yes OR no

### Business meaning:
A decision is made, and only the relevant branch continues.

---

## AND Gateway
**Parallel gateway = all paths**

Used in:
- **Run Parallel Checks**

### Business meaning:
Multiple activities happen at the same time, and all must finish before continuing.

In this process:
- credit check
- inventory check

must both complete before calculating delivery date.

---

# 13. How the process differs for standard vs. custom requests

This is one of the most important distinctions.

## Standard request flow
A standard request follows a more operational, predefined path:

1. Perform standard validation
2. Run credit and inventory checks in parallel
3. Wait for both checks to finish
4. Calculate delivery date

### Characteristics:
- routine
- based on predefined offerings
- operational checks are emphasized
- likely faster and more automated

---

## Custom request flow
A custom request follows a more analytical and commercial path:

1. Perform custom feasibility analysis
2. Decide if customization is feasible
3. If feasible, prepare custom quotation
4. If not feasible, reject and end

### Characteristics:
- tailored to customer needs
- more complex assessment
- requires feasibility and quotation work
- includes a possible early rejection outcome

---

## Key difference
- **Standard path** focuses on validating and fulfilling something the company already knows how to deliver.
- **Custom path** focuses on deciding whether the company can and should deliver something non-standard, and under what terms.

---

# 14. Where paths converge

There are two main convergence patterns.

## A. Standard/custom convergence
The successful branches converge conceptually after:
- **Task D** on the standard side
- **Task E1** on the custom side

At that point, both types of requests enter the shared approval/invoicing section.

---

## B. Parallel-check convergence
The standard branch has an explicit convergence after:
- Task C1 Credit Check
- Task C2 Inventory Check

Both must complete before moving to Task D.

---

# 15. When approvals are triggered

Approval is triggered at the gateway:

## "Is Approval Needed?"
Approval is required only if certain business rules say so.

Typical trigger conditions might include:
- high-value order
- low profitability
- unusual delivery commitment
- custom work with elevated risk
- deviation from standard policy
- special discounts or exceptions

If none of these conditions apply, approval is skipped.

---

# 16. When rework is triggered

Rework is triggered when:

1. approval was needed, and
2. the manager did **not** grant approval

That sends the process to:

## Task H: "Re-evaluate Conditions"

After re-evaluation:
- standard cases go back to **Task D**
- custom cases go back to **Task E1**

So rework occurs specifically when the request is not acceptable in its current form but may become acceptable if revised.

---

# 17. Important business logic implied by the model

This process implies several business principles:

### Controlled differentiation
The company treats standard and custom requests differently because they carry different complexity and risk.

### Efficiency for routine work
Standard requests can be processed through predefined validation and parallel checks.

### Early filtering of unworkable custom requests
Infeasible custom requests are rejected before more effort is spent.

### Risk management
Approval is used as a governance mechanism for sensitive or exceptional cases.

### Rework instead of immediate cancellation
If approval is denied, the process allows revision and resubmission rather than ending immediately.

### Unified completion
Regardless of the path taken, successful requests eventually:
- generate an invoice
- send customer confirmation
- end consistently

---

# 18. Concise end-to-end summary

The process begins when a customer request is received. The request is classified as either **standard** or **custom**. Standard requests go through standard validation, then undergo **credit** and **inventory** checks in parallel, after which a delivery date is calculated. Custom requests go through a feasibility analysis; if the customization is not feasible, a rejection notice is sent and the process ends. If feasible, a custom quotation is prepared.

Once either the standard or successful custom preparation is complete, the process checks whether managerial approval is needed. If no approval is required, the final invoice is generated immediately. If approval is required, a manager reviews the case. If approval is granted, the invoice is generated. If approval is denied, the conditions are re-evaluated and the process loops back—either to recalculating delivery details for standard requests or to preparing a revised quotation for custom requests. After invoice generation, the customer receives a confirmation, and the process ends.
