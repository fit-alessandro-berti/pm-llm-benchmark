## 1) Queue Identification and Characterization

### 1.1 Build queue times from start/complete timestamps
From the event log, first reconstruct each activity instance per visit (`Case ID + Activity + START/COMPLETE`), then sort activities in execution order for each case.

For each case \(c\):

- **Service time** of activity \(A\):  
  \[
  ST(c,A)=t_{complete}(c,A)-t_{start}(c,A)
  \]
- **Inter-activity waiting time (queue time)** from \(A \rightarrow B\):  
  \[
  WT(c,A\rightarrow B)=\max(0,\ t_{start}(c,B)-t_{complete}(c,A))
  \]
  This is the patient’s idle time between finishing one step and starting the next.

- **Total visit lead time**: first start to last complete.
- **Total waiting burden per case**: sum of all inter-activity waits.
- **Value-added ratio**: \(\frac{\sum ST}{\text{Lead Time}}\).

> In this clinic context, “waiting time” is primarily the non-service time between stages (e.g., after nurse assessment completes but before doctor consult starts).

---

### 1.2 Queue metrics to compute
For every transition (e.g., RegistrationNurse, NurseDoctor, DoctorECG), compute:

- Mean, median wait
- P90/P95 wait (important for patient dissatisfaction tail)
- Max wait
- Std dev / IQR (variability)
- **Frequency**: number/% of visits that pass through that queue
- **Total delay hours contributed**: \(\sum WT\) across all cases
- **Excessive wait count** above SLA threshold (e.g., >20 min nurse wait)
- Segment-level metrics by specialty, urgency, patient type, day-of-week, hour-of-day

Also calculate queue dynamics:

- Estimated queue length over time (how many patients are “ready for next step” but not started)
- Arrival rate vs service rate by hour (\(\lambda\) vs \(\mu\))
- Resource utilization (staff/room occupancy)

---

### 1.3 How to identify the most critical queues
Don’t prioritize by mean alone. Use a **criticality score** combining:

1. **Severity** (P90/median wait)
2. **Volume** (how many visits affected)
3. **Total delay burden** (total wait hours)
4. **Clinical/patient risk** (urgent cases, vulnerable cohorts)
5. **Experience impact** (extreme waits, abandonment/complaints)

A practical rule:
- First, flag any queue where urgent patients breach target.
- Next, rank by total delay burden and P90.
- Then segment by patient type/specialty to find inequities (e.g., new patients disproportionately delayed before doctor consultation).

## 2) Root Cause Analysis

Once critical queues are identified, diagnose *why* they occur.

### 2.1 Likely root causes in this setting
- **Resource bottlenecks**: too few nurses/doctors/diagnostic rooms in peak periods.
- **Handover delays**: patient finished step A but not promptly “pulled” into step B.
- **High service-time variability**: new patients or certain specialties take much longer.
- **Scheduling policy mismatch**: appointment bunching (e.g., many arrivals at same hour), one-size slot lengths.
- **Arrival surges**: morning peaks, weekday patterns, urgent add-ons.
- **Priority effects**: urgent patients preempt normal patients, increasing normal queue tails.
- **Rework/loops**: additional diagnostics causing repeated waits.

---

### 2.2 Process mining techniques to pinpoint causes
Use more than basic wait calculations:

1. **Performance process map**  
   Overlay median/P90 waiting on edges to visualize worst transitions.

2. **Resource analysis / bottleneck mining**  
   - Utilization by role/resource/room and time-of-day  
   - If utilization >~85–90% during peaks, queue growth is expected.

3. **Temporal heatmaps**  
   Waiting by hour/day/month to expose demand-capacity mismatch windows.

4. **Variant analysis (fast vs slow visits)**  
   Compare pathways with low vs high lead times to detect delay-driving paths.

5. **Organizational/handover mining**  
   Find handoff pairs with long idle transfer times.

6. **Case-mix stratification / predictive modeling**  
   Regression or tree models using urgency, patient type, specialty, provider, time-of-day to explain long waits.

7. **Conformance checking**  
   Detect deviations from intended pathways that introduce unnecessary waiting/rework.

## 3) Data-Driven Optimization Strategies (Concrete)

### Strategy A: Peak-hour capacity rebalancing (cost-neutral first)
- **Targets**: RegistrationNurse, NurseDoctor queues
- **Root cause addressed**: Demand peaks exceed available staff capacity in short windows
- **Data support**: Queue heatmaps + utilization show overload (e.g., 9:00–11:00)
- **Actions**:
  - Stagger shift starts to align with arrival peaks
  - Cross-train clerical staff for temporary registration/checkout flex
  - Add “float nurse” coverage during peak window using redistributed hours
- **Expected impact (simulation-based)**:
  - 20–35% median wait reduction in targeted queues
  - 10–15% reduction in total visit duration for affected sessions

---

### Strategy B: Appointment template redesign (slot length + smoothing)
- **Targets**: NurseDoctor and Doctor-stage waiting
- **Root cause addressed**: Appointment bunching and inaccurate slot duration assumptions
- **Data support**: Service-time distributions show major differences by patient type/specialty (e.g., new vs follow-up)
- **Actions**:
  - Different slot lengths by visit type/specialty (data-driven)
  - Reduce same-time “batch starts”
  - Keep small protected urgent buffers to avoid cascade delays
  - Apply controlled overbooking only where no-show patterns justify it
- **Expected impact**:
  - 25–40% reduction in P90 doctor wait
  - Lower late-session spillover/overtime

---

### Strategy C: Parallelize predictable diagnostics (fast-track pathway)
- **Targets**: DoctorECG/Blood/X-ray and DiagnosticSpecialist/Checkout queues
- **Root cause addressed**: Strict sequential flow and delayed ordering/handoffs
- **Data support**: Variant mining shows common pathways where diagnostics are predictable but ordered late
- **Actions**:
  - Standing protocols for eligible cohorts (e.g., routine follow-up tests pre-doctor)
  - Pre-visit digital intake to trigger likely diagnostic prep
  - Reserve micro-slots in diagnostics linked to clinic schedule
- **Expected impact**:
  - 30–50% reduction in targeted diagnostic waiting
  - 15–25 minute reduction in total visit time for those cohorts

---

### Strategy D: Real-time queue orchestration dashboard
- **Targets**: Cross-stage handover waits clinic-wide
- **Root cause addressed**: Coordination latency despite occasional downstream availability
- **Data support**: Event logs show idle gaps not explained by pure capacity limits
- **Actions**:
  - Live queue board by stage/resource with SLA breach alerts
  - Auto-notifications when patient becomes ready for next step
  - Rule-based prioritization (urgency + longest-waiting)
- **Expected impact**:
  - 10–20% reduction in non-value-added handover delays
  - Significant drop in extreme waits (P95/P99)

---

## 4) Trade-offs and Constraints

### Potential side effects
- **Bottleneck shifting**: fixing doctor wait may overload diagnostics/check-out.
- **Staff burden**: peak reallocation can increase fatigue if not managed.
- **Cost pressure**: tech tools/training require upfront investment.
- **Quality risk**: shorter slots could reduce consultation thoroughness.
- **Clinical appropriateness risk**: pre-order diagnostics must avoid unnecessary testing.

### How to balance objectives
Use a **multi-objective governance model** with guardrails:

- Optimize wait + cost + quality together (not wait alone)
- Hard clinical constraints:
  - no increase in 7-day returns/adverse events
  - maintain minimum consult quality/time standards
- Prefer first-wave interventions that are cost-neutral (schedule redesign, coordination)
- Test changes via pilot + discrete-event simulation before full rollout
- Review with clinical, operations, and finance stakeholders jointly

---

## 5) Measuring Success and Sustained Monitoring

### 5.1 Post-implementation KPIs
Track at least these:

**Flow / Queue KPIs**
- Median and P90 total visit lead time
- Median/P90 wait per critical transition
- % visits meeting SLA per stage
- Queue length and time-in-queue profiles

**Operational KPIs**
- Throughput (visits/day/session)
- Resource utilization by role/room
- Overtime hours and schedule adherence

**Patient Experience KPIs**
- Wait-related satisfaction score / complaint rate
- Left-without-being-seen or abandonment rate

**Quality/Safety KPIs**
- Time-to-clinician for urgent cases
- Rework loops
- Short-term revisit rate (as balancing metric)

**Financial KPIs**
- Cost per visit
- Incremental staffing/technology cost vs savings from improved flow

---

### 5.2 Ongoing process monitoring approach
- Continue ingesting the same START/COMPLETE event logs.
- Build weekly dashboards and monthly process-mining refreshes.
- Use control charts for median/P90 waits to detect drift early.
- Monitor by segment (specialty, urgency, patient type), not only global average.
- Trigger alerts when thresholds are exceeded for consecutive periods.
- Re-run root-cause mining quarterly to prevent regression and catch new bottlenecks.

---

### Practical rollout suggestion
1. Baseline (6 months historical)  
2. Simulate interventions  
3. Pilot in 1–2 specialties  
4. Measure with KPIs + statistical comparison  
5. Scale successful changes and keep continuous mining loop active

That gives management a defensible, data-driven path to reduce waiting times without automatically increasing operating costs.
