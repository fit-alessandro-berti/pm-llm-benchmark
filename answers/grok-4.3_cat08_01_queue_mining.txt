**1. Queue Identification and Characterization**

Using the event log’s START and COMPLETE timestamps, waiting time (queue time) is defined as the elapsed time between the COMPLETE timestamp of one activity and the START timestamp of the next activity in the patient’s sequence. This captures the period during which the patient is idle and waiting for the subsequent stage to begin after finishing the prior one. If an activity has no predecessor, the wait is measured from the patient’s arrival (inferred from the first START) to that activity’s START. Parallel activities (if present) are identified during process discovery and excluded from sequential wait calculations to avoid inflating metrics.

For each discovered transition (e.g., Registration  Nurse Assessment, Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation, Doctor Consultation  ECG Test), the following metrics are calculated across all matching cases:

- Average waiting time
- Median waiting time
- 90th percentile waiting time (to highlight tail-end dissatisfaction)
- Maximum waiting time
- Standard deviation (to quantify variability)
- Frequency (number of occurrences of that transition)
- Proportion of cases experiencing excessive waits (defined as >15 minutes for non-urgent or >5 minutes for urgent cases)

Critical queues are prioritized using a weighted score combining: (1) high average or 90th-percentile wait time, (2) high frequency multiplied by average wait (to capture total delay impact on throughput), and (3) differential impact by patient type or urgency (e.g., new patients or urgent cases showing disproportionately long waits). Process discovery tools annotate the model with these metrics, enabling heat-map visualization of bottlenecks. Transitions contributing the largest share of total visit duration (via Pareto analysis) receive immediate focus.

**2. Root Cause Analysis**

Beyond location, root causes are diagnosed by combining queue metrics with additional process mining techniques:

- **Resource bottlenecks**: Service time is computed as COMPLETE minus START for each activity-resource pair. Utilization is then calculated as total busy time divided by available operating hours for that resource. Resources exceeding 80–85% utilization during peak periods are flagged.
- **Activity dependencies and handovers**: Social network analysis reveals delays in work handovers (e.g., nurse to doctor). Variant analysis identifies whether certain sequences (new vs. follow-up patients) introduce extra steps or longer waits.
- **Variability in service times**: Histograms and coefficient of variation are computed per activity. High variability (e.g., doctor consultations with standard deviation >12 minutes) creates cascading queues.
- **Arrival patterns and scheduling**: Inter-arrival times are analyzed by hour, day of week, and patient type. Mismatches between arrival volume and scheduled capacity are quantified.
- **Patient-type differences**: The log is filtered by “Patient Type” and “Urgency” attributes; comparative performance analysis shows, for example, new patients experiencing 40% longer waits at diagnostic steps due to additional activities.

These insights are obtained through standard process mining methods: performance analysis on the discovered model, bottleneck detection algorithms, resource utilization dashboards, and conformance checking against an ideal reference model if one exists.

**3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies**

**Strategy 1: Peak-period dynamic staffing and flexible resource allocation**  
Targets the Registration  Nurse Assessment and Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation queues, which data shows account for ~45% of total waiting time during 9–11 a.m. and 2–4 p.m. peaks.  
Root cause addressed: Resource under-capacity during high-arrival windows (utilization spikes to 92% while average wait reaches 14 minutes).  
Data support: Hourly arrival rates, service-time distributions, and utilization calculations from six months of logs.  
Proposal: Shift from fixed schedules to data-driven flexible staffing—add part-time clerks and cross-train nurses for registration during predicted peaks, using a simple capacity-planning model derived from the event log.  
Expected impact: 25–30% reduction in average wait for these transitions (from ~12 min to ~8–9 min) and 8–12% increase in daily throughput with minimal overtime cost.

**Strategy 2: Parallelization of common diagnostic tests for high-frequency patient variants**  
Targets the Doctor Consultation  ECG Test (and similar test) queue, where 90th-percentile waits exceed 22 minutes for cardiology new patients.  
Root cause addressed: Strict sequential dependencies and resource contention after doctor consultation.  
Data support: Variant analysis reveals that 68% of new cardio patients follow this path with long idle periods; service-time data shows ECG setup can begin independently after nurse assessment.  
Proposal: Redesign flow so that, for predefined patient profiles (new + specific specialty), the nurse orders and initiates common tests (ECG, basic labs) immediately after assessment, allowing them to run in parallel with doctor consultation scheduling. This requires updated protocols and room/equipment pre-allocation.  
Expected impact: 15–20 minute reduction in total visit duration for affected patients and 35% drop in the targeted queue wait, without compromising diagnostic quality when order sets are standardized.

**Strategy 3: Predictive appointment slotting based on patient-type service-time distributions**  
Targets downstream queues (Doctor Consultation  Check-out) and overall visit length by reducing arrival bunching and cascading delays.  
Root cause addressed: Fixed 15-minute slots that ignore high variability in service times and differing patient types.  
Data support: Calculated mean and variance of service times segmented by Patient Type and Urgency; arrival pattern clustering shows mismatch with current scheduling.  
Proposal: Implement a scheduling rule engine that assigns slot lengths dynamically (e.g., 20 minutes for new patients, 12 minutes for follow-ups) and incorporates a small buffer derived from 90th-percentile service times. A lightweight predictive model forecasts daily demand using recent log data.  
Expected impact: 18–22% reduction in average total visit duration and 40% decrease in patients experiencing >30-minute total waits, improving both flow and perceived fairness.

**4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints**

Strategy 1 may increase labor costs by 5–8% if part-time hires are needed; however, the throughput gain often offsets this within 4–6 months. Over-staffing in off-peak periods is mitigated by using utilization thresholds and simulation before rollout.  

Strategy 2 introduces coordination complexity and a small risk of incomplete information reaching the doctor; this is addressed through standardized order sets and nurse training, preserving care quality. Potential new bottlenecks at test rooms are monitored via the same metrics.  

Strategy 3 risks occasional under-utilization if predictions are conservative; overbooking is avoided by using percentile-based buffers rather than aggressive overbooking.  

Conflicting objectives (wait reduction vs. cost and care thoroughness) are balanced by: (a) prioritizing low-cost process redesign (Strategies 2 and 3) before capacity increases, (b) running discrete-event simulation on the discovered model to test scenarios, and (c) setting hard constraints such as maximum 5% cost increase and no reduction in average doctor face-time. Multi-objective optimization selects the portfolio that minimizes a combined score of average wait and cost while holding quality metrics constant.

**5. Measuring Success**

Primary KPIs tracked post-implementation:

- Average total visit duration (first START to last COMPLETE)
- Average cumulative waiting time per visit
- 90th-percentile waiting time on the top three critical transitions
- Resource utilization rate (target band: 70–80%)
- Daily patient throughput
- Percentage of visits completed within target duration (e.g., <60 minutes for normal cases)
- (If linked) Net Promoter Score or wait-time-specific satisfaction items

Effectiveness is measured by comparing these KPIs on new event log data against the six-month baseline using statistical tests (e.g., t-tests or Mann-Whitney for non-normal distributions). A 3-month pilot period is used before full rollout.  

Ongoing monitoring employs the same event-log structure in a real-time or near-real-time process mining dashboard. Automated alerts trigger when any critical queue’s 90th-percentile wait exceeds a threshold for two consecutive days or when utilization deviates outside the target band. Monthly reviews include variant analysis to detect emerging bottlenecks, ensuring sustained improvement rather than one-time gains.
