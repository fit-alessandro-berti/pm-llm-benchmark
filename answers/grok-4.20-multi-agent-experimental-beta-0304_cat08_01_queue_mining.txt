**1. Queue Identification and Characterization**

Waiting time (queue time) is defined as the non-value-adding idle period a patient spends between process steps. Using the event log's start and complete timestamps, it is calculated for each pair of consecutive activities in a case as:  
**Waiting Time = START_timestamp of next activity  COMPLETE_timestamp of previous activity**. 

This requires first applying process discovery (e.g., Directly-Follows Graph or BPMN discovery in tools like PM4Py, Disco, or Celonis) to understand the dominant flows and handle cases with parallel or optional activities. Only gaps between directly-following activities in the patient's trace (or along discovered paths) are considered true queue times; unrelated gaps (e.g., due to missing events) are filtered. Service time for an activity is simply COMPLETE  START of that same activity.

Key metrics to characterize queues include:
- Average, median, 90th percentile, and maximum waiting times per activity transition (e.g., Registration  Nurse Assessment).
- Frequency: number/percentage of cases experiencing a wait >0 or above a clinically meaningful threshold (e.g., >15 minutes).
- Total waiting time contribution to overall case duration (sum of waits across all cases).
- Stratified metrics by case attributes: patient type (New vs. Follow-up), urgency (Urgent vs. Normal), time of day, day of week, and resource.

The most critical queues are identified by a combination of:
- Highest average or 90th-percentile wait time (patient experience is heavily influenced by worst-case and typical long waits).
- Highest impact score, calculated as (average wait × frequency) or total waiting time contributed to aggregate visit duration.
- Disproportionate effect on high-volume segments (e.g., New patients) or sensitive groups (Urgent cases, specific specialties like Cardiology with longer downstream paths).

Prioritization focuses on queues that are both painful and pervasive, as these offer the highest leverage for reducing overall visit duration and improving satisfaction without assuming exact log structure beyond start/complete timestamps and case attributes.

**2. Root Cause Analysis**

Once critical queues are identified (e.g., long waits between Nurse Assessment and Doctor Consultation or Doctor Consultation and ECG), root cause analysis moves beyond "where" to "why" using enriched process mining techniques on the same event log.

Potential root causes include:
- **Resource bottlenecks**: High utilization of specific staff, rooms, or equipment (calculated as busy time from overlapping activity intervals per resource).
- **Arrival and scheduling patterns**: Clustering of patient arrivals at certain hours/days, leading to overload.
- **Variability**: High variance in service times (e.g., Doctor Consultation duration varying wildly by patient type).
- **Handovers and dependencies**: Delays when responsibility transfers between resources or departments.
- **Process variants**: Different paths for New vs. Follow-up patients or by urgency, with some variants having systematically longer waits.
- **Policy issues**: Inappropriate appointment slot lengths or lack of buffers.

Process mining techniques to pinpoint these:
- **Performance analysis on the process map**: Overlay mean/percentile waiting and service times on the Directly-Follows Graph or process model to visually highlight bottlenecks.
- **Resource analysis and utilization profiles**: Identify over- and under-utilized staff/rooms over time; use dotted charts or timeline analysis to spot peak-load mismatches.
- **Variant analysis**: Compare process variants (using trace clustering or variant explorer) between patient types and urgency levels to see which paths generate excessive waits.
- **Bottleneck analysis and waiting time decomposition**: Tools automatically highlight transitions with highest waiting time and correlate them with resource availability or preceding long service times.
- **Case attribute filtering and decision mining**: Drill down by Patient Type, Urgency, or Resource to reveal patterns (e.g., New patients wait longer for Cardio doctors).
- **Time-based analysis**: Analyze waits by hour of day or day of week to link to arrival patterns or shift schedules.

These techniques turn the event log into actionable diagnostics—for instance, revealing that nurse-to-doctor waits spike at 10–11 a.m. on Mondays due to high New patient arrivals and fixed doctor schedules.

**3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies**

Three concrete strategies, each directly supported by the analysis:

**Strategy 1: Peak-aware dynamic staffing and cross-training (targets Registration  Nurse Assessment and Nurse  Doctor queues).**  
Root cause addressed: Resource utilization spikes during morning peaks and mismatches in staff availability. Data support: Resource profiles and time-based waiting analysis show >80% nurse utilization and high waits correlating with arrival clusters between 8–10 a.m. Implementation: Cross-train registration clerks to assist with basic nurse triage tasks during identified peaks and use flexible staffing (e.g., part-time overlap shifts). Expected impact: 25–35% reduction in average wait for the targeted transition, reducing overall visit time by ~15–20 minutes based on contribution of that queue to total duration.

**Strategy 2: Patient-type differentiated appointment scheduling with buffers.**  
Root cause addressed: Poor scheduling logic causing bunching of complex (New/Urgent) patients. Data support: Variant analysis and duration statistics show New patients have significantly longer and more variable service times; historical logs reveal downstream waits are predictable from morning arrival patterns. Implementation: Allocate longer slots and strategic buffers for New patients, stagger Follow-up appointments, and use predictive slotting based on six months of historical data. Expected impact: Smoother load distribution, 20–30% reduction in doctor consultation queue times, and lower variance in total visit duration.

**Strategy 3: Selective parallelization and pre-diagnostic preparation for high-volume paths.**  
Root cause addressed: Strict sequential dependencies and idle time while waiting for tests. Data support: Process discovery and performance analysis identify specific variants (e.g., Cardio New patients) where ECG/Blood Test waits are long but can be partially anticipated from doctor specialty. Implementation: For identified high-wait variants, allow lab technicians to prepare or even initiate certain tests (with physician pre-approval via protocol) while the patient is with the nurse or in minor parallel admin steps; introduce a "fast-track" coordinator role. Expected impact: 15–25% reduction in diagnostic-related waits for targeted patient groups, improving total throughput without adding headcount.

**4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints**

Each strategy carries risks. Dynamic staffing may increase short-term labor costs (estimated 8–15% in peak periods) or staff workload stress. Schedule changes could reduce overall daily capacity if buffers are too conservative. Parallelization risks care quality if protocols are not tightly defined (e.g., premature testing) or creates new coordination overhead. Bottlenecks may simply shift downstream (e.g., check-out becomes the new problem).

Balancing occurs through:
- Pilot implementations on specific days/specialties, measuring both wait times and quality metrics (e.g., readmission rates, patient-reported thoroughness).
- Simulation or "what-if" analysis using the event log as input to test changes before full rollout.
- Multi-objective optimization: prioritize strategies with high wait reduction per dollar spent, using data to calculate ROI (e.g., reduced visit time enabling more patients per day).
- Continuous monitoring of resource utilization to avoid overstaffing or burnout.

The guiding principle is evidence-based iteration—only implement changes where data shows net positive impact on the combined goals of throughput, satisfaction, cost, and quality.

**5. Measuring Success**

Primary KPIs:
- Average and 90th-percentile total patient waiting time per visit.
- Average end-to-end visit duration (from first Registration start to Check-out complete).
- Percentage of patients with total wait time below target threshold (e.g., <30 minutes).
- Patient satisfaction/NPS scores, specifically waiting-related questions.
- Secondary: Resource utilization rates (target 70–85% to avoid burnout), throughput (patients per day), and process conformance rate.

Success is measured by statistically significant improvement in primary KPIs post-implementation (e.g., 20%+ reduction in average wait) while holding or improving quality and cost metrics.

Ongoing monitoring uses the same event log structure in a process mining dashboard for real-time or near-real-time analysis. Automated alerts for emerging bottlenecks, weekly performance reports, and periodic re-discovery of the process model ensure sustained gains and early detection of drift. Conformance checking verifies that redesigned processes are followed, closing the loop on data-driven optimization.

This comprehensive queue mining approach transforms the clinic's event log from passive records into a powerful tool for targeted, measurable improvement in patient flow.
