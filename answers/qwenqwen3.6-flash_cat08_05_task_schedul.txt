### **Advanced Process Mining and Scheduling Optimization Proposal for Precision Parts Inc.**

**To:** Management Team, Precision Parts Inc.  
**From:** Senior Operations Analyst  
**Subject:** Data-Driven Transformation of Scheduling Performance via Process Mining and Advanced Optimization

Precision Parts Inc. operates in a complex environment characterized by high variability, sequence-dependent constraints, and frequent disruptions. The current reliance on static, local dispatching rules is insufficient for this complexity. The following proposal outlines a comprehensive approach leveraging **Process Mining** to diagnose current performance and **Advanced Scheduling Techniques** to drive significant improvements in tardiness, WIP, lead time predictability, and resource utilization.

---

### **1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics**

To move from intuition to evidence, we will apply process mining techniques to the MES event logs to reconstruct the "as-is" reality of the shop floor. The logs provide a rich tapestry of control flow, data flow, and resource usage.

**A. Process Reconstruction and Trace Analysis:**
*   **Case Definition:** We define a "Case" as a `Job ID`. Traces are sequences of events ordered by timestamp for each job.
*   **Activity Mapping:** We map raw events to meaningful activities: `Queueing`, `Setup`, `Processing`, `Inspection`, `Idle/Breakdown`. This allows us to decompose lead time into value-added and non-value-added components.
*   **Variant Analysis:** Using algorithms like **Inductive Miner** or **Alpha Miner**, we will identify all distinct routing variants. In a job shop, we expect high variant diversity. We will quantify the frequency of each variant and correlate variants with performance outcomes (e.g., "Does Job-Flow-Variant-7 consistently miss deadlines?").

**B. Key Metrics and Techniques:**
*   **Job Flow Times & Lead Times:**
    *   *Technique:* **Trace Correlation Analysis**. We calculate the elapsed time from `Job Released` to the completion of the final task.
    *   *Metrics:* Mean/Median Lead Time, Lead Time Variance (Coefficient of Variation), and Percentile distributions (e.g., P90). This quantifies "Unpredictable Lead Times."
*   **Task Waiting Times (Queue Analysis):**
    *   *Technique:* **Inter-Event Time Decomposition**. We compute the time delta between `Task End` of the previous operation and `Queue Entry` of the next.
    *   *Metrics:* Average Waiting Time per Work Center, Waiting Time as % of Total Lead Time. High waiting times indicate WIP accumulation and poor flow.
*   **Resource Utilization:**
    *   *Technique:* **Resource Usage Analysis**. We calculate `Utilization = Active Time / Total Time`.
    *   *Metrics:* Productive Utilization vs. Setup Utilization vs. Idle Time. We will identify "Starved" resources (high idle time) vs. "Overloaded" resources (utilization > 90% with long queues).
*   **Sequence-Dependent Setup Times:**
    *   *Technique:* **Transition Mining & Data Enrichment**. The log contains `Previous job` notes. We will extract these to build a **Setup Transition Matrix**.
    *   *Analysis:* We calculate the distribution of setup durations for every pair `(Job A -> Job B)` or, more robustly, `(Job A Properties -> Job B Properties)`. We will compare `Planned Setup` vs. `Actual Setup` to identify systematic under-estimation.
*   **Schedule Adherence & Tardiness:**
    *   *Technique:* **Conformance Checking & Lateness Analysis**. We compare the actual completion time of the last task against the `Order Due Date`.
    *   *Metrics:* Tardiness (Positive lateness), Earliness, On-Time Delivery Rate. We will segment tardiness by `Order Priority` to check for prioritization failures.
*   **Impact of Disruptions:**
    *   *Technique:* **Event Filtering & Impact Correlation**. We filter for `Breakdown Start` and `Priority Change` events.
    *   *Analysis:* We perform a **difference-in-differences** analysis. We compare the lead times of jobs processed during/after a breakdown against a control group of similar jobs processed during stable periods. This quantifies the "cost" of disruptions.

### **2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies**

Based on the analysis, we expect to uncover specific pathologies inherent to the current scheduling approach. Process mining provides the evidence to pinpoint these issues.

*   **Bottleneck Misidentification and Congestion:**
    *   *Pathology:* The current rules may be treating all machines equally, causing WIP to pile up before true bottlenecks (e.g., Heat Treatment or specialized CNC).
    *   *Evidence:* **Bottleneck Analysis** will reveal work centers with the highest average waiting times and longest queues. Little's Law applied to the log (`WIP = Throughput ﾗ Lead Time`) will confirm if high WIP is driven by low throughput at specific resources.
*   **Prioritization Inversion:**
    *   *Pathology:* "High" priority hot jobs are missing deadlines due to being blocked by lower priority jobs or long setups.
    *   *Evidence:* **Correlation Matrix** between `Order Priority` and `Tardiness`. If High Priority jobs show higher average tardiness than Medium Priority, the dispatching rules are failing to protect urgent work.
*   **Setup Time Inefficiency:**
    *   *Pathology:* Sequence-dependent setups are causing significant delays, yet the scheduler ignores them.
    *   *Evidence:* **Setup Time Distribution Analysis** may show that setup times are not just long but highly variable depending on the predecessor. If the current rule (e.g., EDD) ignores the next setup cost, it may select a job that finishes early but triggers a massive setup for the following job, delaying the whole line.
*   **Starvation and Idleness:**
    *   *Pathology:* Flexible machines sit idle while specialized machines are overloaded, or vice versa.
    *   *Evidence:* **Resource Utilization Heatmaps**. We will identify machines with utilization < 40% while adjacent work centers have utilization > 95%, indicating a lack of load balancing.
*   **WIP Bullwhip Effect:**
    *   *Pathology:* Variability in processing times amplifies upstream, causing WIP to explode.
    *   *Evidence:* **WIP Trend Analysis**. We will plot WIP levels over time. If WIP spikes correlate with high variance in task durations (derived from `Planned vs. Actual` residuals), it confirms that variability is driving WIP accumulation.

### **3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness**

Process mining helps differentiate between *capacity* issues and *logic* issues.

*   **Static Rules vs. Dynamic Reality:**
    *   *Root Cause:* FCFS/EDD are myopic. They do not look ahead to sequence-dependent setups or downstream constraints.
    *   *PM Insight:* If we find that jobs with similar due dates have vastly different lead times depending on their position in the queue, it suggests the dispatching rule is sensitive to sequence effects that it cannot control.
*   **Inaccurate Duration Estimations:**
    *   *Root Cause:* Schedules are likely built on "Planned" durations, which may be optimistic.
    *   *PM Insight:* Analysis of `Task Duration (Planned)` vs. `Actual` will likely reveal a systematic bias (e.g., Actuals are 1.2x Planned). This "optimism bias" leads to schedules that are impossible to meet, causing cascading delays.
*   **Lack of Global Visibility:**
    *   *Root Cause:* Local decisions create global sub-optimization.
    *   *PM Insight:* **Resource Contention Analysis** will show periods where multiple work centers are simultaneously blocked, suggesting that a decision at one station (e.g., pushing a job early) caused a blockage downstream that wasn't visible to the local dispatcher.
*   **Poor Handling of Variability:**
    *   *Root Cause:* No buffering or slack management.
    *   *PM Insight:* **Variability Decomposition**. If we attribute lead time variance primarily to processing time variance rather than queue variance, the issue is process execution. If queue variance dominates, the issue is scheduling/prioritization.
*   **Disruption Response:**
    *   *Root Cause:* Reactive rather than proactive.
    *   *PM Insight:* Analysis of `Priority Change` events will show that "Hot Jobs" often arrive with very short windows, and the system lacks a mechanism to preemptively adjust the schedule to accommodate them without penalizing other jobs.

### **4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies**

We propose three strategies that leverage process mining insights to replace static rules.

#### **Strategy 1: Dynamic Multi-Criteria Dispatching (DMCD) with Setup Awareness**
*   **Core Logic:** Replace EDD/FCFS with a dynamic scoring function evaluated at every decision point.
    *   `Score(Job) = w1 * (Slack Time) + w2 * (Remaining Processing Time) + w3 * (Priority) + w4 * (Predicted Setup Time) + w5 * (Downstream Congestion)`
*   **PM Insights:**
    *   *Weights:* Use regression analysis on historical data to determine which factors most strongly correlate with tardiness. If PM shows that setup times at bottleneck machines drive 40% of tardiness, `w4` (Setup) is heavily weighted.
    *   *Setup Prediction:* Use the **Setup Transition Matrix** mined from logs to predict the setup time for a job based on the last completed job.
    *   *Downstream Congestion:* Use real-time WIP data to penalize jobs destined for congested work centers.
*   **Impact:** Reduces tardiness by prioritizing jobs that are truly urgent and avoiding sequences that trigger long setups. Smooths flow by preventing downstream overload.

#### **Strategy 2: Predictive Stochastic Scheduling with Operator Profiles**
*   **Core Logic:** Move from deterministic scheduling to **Stochastic Robust Scheduling**.
    *   Generate schedules using Monte Carlo simulations based on task time distributions.
    *   Insert dynamic buffers based on variability.
*   **PM Insights:**
    *   *Operator Profiles:* Cluster jobs by `Operator ID` and analyze performance. Some operators may be faster on Grinding but slower on Milling. The scheduler will assign jobs to operators based on their historical efficiency profiles.
    *   *Duration Distributions:* PM provides the full distribution (not just mean) of task times. The scheduler uses the P90 duration for planning critical paths to ensure high confidence in due dates.
    *   *Breakdown Prediction:* If downtime logs are available, use survival analysis to predict failure probabilities and schedule preventive maintenance during low-load windows.
*   **Impact:** Improves **Lead Time Predictability**. Customers receive accurate ETAs. Reduces bullwhip effect by buffering against variability rather than pushing jobs blindly.

#### **Strategy 3: Sequence-Dependent Setup Optimization (SDSO) via Clustering**
*   **Core Logic:** Focus specifically on the bottleneck machines where setup times are highest.
    *   Implement a **Group Technology** approach: Cluster jobs with similar setup requirements.
    *   Use a heuristic (e.g., Genetic Algorithm or Nearest Neighbor on the Setup Transition Matrix) to sequence jobs at bottlenecks to minimize total setup time.
*   **PM Insights:**
    *   *Setup Matrix:* The mined transition matrix reveals "easy" transitions (low setup time) and "hard" transitions.
    *   *Job Properties:* Correlate setup times with job attributes (e.g., material type, tolerance). Jobs with similar attributes group together.
    *   *Bottleneck Identification:* Focus optimization efforts only on resources identified as bottlenecks by the bottleneck analysis.
*   **Impact:** Directly increases effective capacity at bottlenecks by reducing setup waste. Reduces WIP by clearing queues faster. Improves makespan.

### **5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement**

#### **Discrete-Event Simulation (DES) for Validation:**
Before live deployment, we will build a **Digital Twin** of the shop floor using the MES data.
*   **Parameterization:**
    *   *Task Times:* Sampled from empirical distributions mined from logs.
    *   *Setups:* Modeled using the sequence-dependent transition matrix.
    *   *Disruptions:* Modeled using inter-arrival distributions of breakdowns and frequency of hot jobs.
    *   *Routing:* Based on variant analysis.
*   **Scenario Testing:**
    *   *Baseline:* Current FCFS/EDD rules.
    *   *Strategy Comparison:* DMCD, Predictive Scheduling, and SDSO.
    *   *Stress Tests:* Simulate 120% load and 3x breakdown frequency to test robustness.
*   **Evaluation Metrics:** Compare KPIs (Tardiness, WIP, Utilization, Makespan) across strategies. Select the strategy that offers the best trade-off, potentially a hybrid of DMCD and SDSO.

#### **Continuous Monitoring and Adaptation Framework:**
Scheduling is not a one-time fix. We implement a **Closed-Loop Process Mining System**:
*   **Real-Time Dashboard:** A monitoring dashboard displays real-time KPIs, WIP levels, and schedule adherence.
*   **Drift Detection:** Automated alerts trigger if:
    *   Setup times drift significantly from the mined baseline.
    *   Tardiness trends upward over a rolling window.
    *   New routing variants emerge that perform poorly.
*   **Feedback Loop:**
    *   If drift is detected, the process mining engine re-analyzes recent logs.
    *   The scheduling parameters (e.g., DMCD weights, buffer sizes) are automatically adjusted or flagged for analyst review.
    *   This ensures the scheduling logic evolves with changes in product mix, operator skill, or machine conditions.

By leveraging process mining, Precision Parts Inc. can transition from reactive, rule-based scheduling to a proactive, data-driven optimization engine. The proposed strategies address the root causes of tardiness and WIP by explicitly modeling sequence-dependent setups, operator variability, and disruption impacts. Simulation ensures risk-free validation, while continuous monitoring guarantees sustained performance. This approach will transform Precision Parts Inc.'s scheduling from a cost center into a competitive advantage, delivering reliable lead times and maximizing asset utilization.
