## 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

### 1.1 Defining “waiting time” in this clinic context

Because the event log contains both **START** and **COMPLETE** timestamps for each activity, we can distinguish between:

- **Service time / activity duration**: time spent actually receiving a service.
  - Example: `Registration COMPLETE – Registration START`
- **Waiting time / queue time**: time between the completion of one activity and the start of the next activity in the same patient visit.
  - Example: `Nurse Assessment START – Registration COMPLETE`
- **Total visit duration**: time from first recorded patient-facing event to final completion.
  - Example: `Check-out COMPLETE – Registration START`, or if arrival time is available, `Check-out COMPLETE – Patient Arrival`
- **Non-value-added time**: time not spent in clinical or administrative service, often largely composed of waiting.

For each patient visit, I would sort all events chronologically, pair each activity’s START and COMPLETE events, then identify the patient’s actual activity sequence:

> Registration  Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation  ECG Test  Check-out

For each transition between consecutive activities, queue time is calculated as:

> **Queue Time between Activity A and Activity B = Start Time of Activity B – Complete Time of Activity A**

Example:

- Registration COMPLETE: 09:08:45  
- Nurse Assessment START: 09:15:20  
- Queue time before Nurse Assessment = 6 minutes 35 seconds

This would be calculated for every observed transition, such as:

- Arrival or scheduled appointment time  Registration START
- Registration COMPLETE  Nurse Assessment START
- Nurse Assessment COMPLETE  Doctor Consultation START
- Doctor Consultation COMPLETE  Diagnostic Test START
- Diagnostic Test COMPLETE  Specialist Review START
- Final clinical activity COMPLETE  Check-out START

If appointment time and actual arrival time are available, I would also separate:

- **Patient early arrival time**
- **Clinic-caused waiting time**
- **Late-arrival impact**
- **Waiting before first service**

That distinction is important because patients may arrive early, and that should not necessarily be counted as clinic-caused delay unless the clinic explicitly asked them to arrive early.

---

### 1.2 Data preparation steps before queue mining

Before calculating queues, I would perform several event-log preparation checks:

1. **Pair START and COMPLETE events correctly**
   - Ensure every activity has both timestamps.
   - Handle missing completions, duplicate events, or reversed timestamps.

2. **Normalize activity names**
   - For example:
     - “Doctor Consultation Cardio”
     - “Doctor Consult – Cardiology”
     - “Cardio Doctor Visit”
   - These should be standardized to a consistent activity label.

3. **Distinguish activity subtypes**
   - Doctor consultation by specialty may need to remain separate:
     - Doctor Consultation – Cardiology
     - Doctor Consultation – Orthopedics
     - Doctor Consultation – Dermatology

4. **Handle parallel activities**
   - Some diagnostic tests or administrative checks may happen in parallel.
   - Queue time should be calculated only for genuine dependency relationships, not simply every chronological gap if activities overlap.

5. **Separate planned versus unplanned waiting**
   - For example, a patient may intentionally wait for lab results.
   - This is still waiting from the patient’s perspective, but the root cause is different from waiting for an available nurse or room.

6. **Segment by time period**
   - Weekday versus weekend
   - Morning versus afternoon
   - Specialty clinic day versus general clinic day
   - Flu season or other seasonal demand spikes

---

### 1.3 Key queue metrics to calculate

For each queue, meaning each transition between two activities, I would calculate the following metrics.

#### Basic waiting-time metrics

For each queue such as `Registration  Nurse Assessment`:

- **Average waiting time**
- **Median waiting time**
- **Minimum waiting time**
- **Maximum waiting time**
- **Standard deviation**
- **Coefficient of variation**
- **90th percentile waiting time**
- **95th percentile waiting time**
- **Interquartile range**

The average is useful, but in healthcare queues the **median and high percentiles** are often more informative because waiting times are usually skewed. A small number of patients may wait extremely long times, which can drive complaints and dissatisfaction.

#### Frequency and volume metrics

For each queue:

- **Number of cases passing through the queue**
- **Percentage of all visits that experience this queue**
- **Number of patients waiting more than a threshold**
  - Example: more than 15 minutes, 30 minutes, 60 minutes
- **Percentage of patients breaching service targets**
  - Example: “90% of patients should be seen by a nurse within 10 minutes after registration.”

#### Aggregate burden metrics

I would calculate:

- **Total waiting burden**
  - Sum of all waiting minutes across all patients for a given queue.
  - Example: A queue with an average wait of 12 minutes affecting 10,000 patients may be more important than one with an average wait of 45 minutes affecting 50 patients.

- **Contribution to total visit time**
  - Percentage of total visit duration caused by that queue.
  - Example: Doctor waiting accounts for 35% of the total visit time.

- **Waiting-to-service ratio**
  - Queue time divided by service time.
  - Example: patients wait 30 minutes for a 6-minute test.

#### Segment-specific metrics

Queues should be characterized by:

- Specialty
- Doctor
- Nurse team
- Resource
- Room
- Equipment
- Patient type: New versus Follow-up
- Urgency: Normal versus Urgent
- Appointment type
- Time of day
- Day of week
- Scheduled versus walk-in
- Diagnostic pathway
- Insurance or authorization requirement, if relevant

Example:

| Queue | Segment | Median Wait | P90 Wait | Cases | SLA Breach |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Nurse Assessment  Cardiology Doctor | New patients | 28 min | 72 min | 3,200 | 41% |
| Nurse Assessment  Cardiology Doctor | Follow-up patients | 14 min | 38 min | 4,700 | 19% |
| Doctor  ECG | Monday AM | 22 min | 55 min | 1,100 | 35% |
| Registration  Nurse | All patients | 9 min | 25 min | 18,000 | 12% |

---

### 1.4 Identifying the most critical queues

The most critical queues should not be selected only by the longest average wait. I would use a combined prioritization framework.

#### Criteria for criticality

A queue is critical if it has one or more of the following characteristics:

1. **Long average or median wait**
   - Indicates a persistent process delay.

2. **High 90th or 95th percentile wait**
   - Indicates severe delays for a subset of patients.

3. **High frequency**
   - Affects many patients.

4. **Large total waiting burden**
   - Large cumulative waste across the clinic.

5. **High SLA breach rate**
   - Many patients exceed acceptable waiting-time thresholds.

6. **Strong impact on total visit duration**
   - Queue is a major contributor to the overall patient stay.

7. **High impact on vulnerable or priority patients**
   - Urgent patients, elderly patients, pediatric patients, patients requiring fasting labs, oncology patients, etc.

8. **Downstream ripple effect**
   - Delay causes missed diagnostic windows, physician idle time, room congestion, overtime, or late-day schedule slippage.

9. **Patient satisfaction linkage**
   - If survey or complaint data can be linked to visits, queues correlated with low satisfaction should receive priority.

#### Example criticality score

A practical approach is to create a weighted score:

> **Criticality Score =**
> 0.25 × normalized total waiting minutes  
> + 0.20 × normalized P90 wait  
> + 0.20 × SLA breach rate  
> + 0.15 × case frequency  
> + 0.10 × impact on urgent/new patients  
> + 0.10 × downstream delay impact

This helps identify queues that are both painful and operationally important.

For example, a `Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation` queue may be more critical than a `Doctor  ECG` queue if:

- It affects 70% of patients.
- It contributes 40% of total waiting time.
- It has a high P90 delay.
- It drives patient complaints.
- It causes late-day congestion and physician overtime.

## 2. Root Cause Analysis

Once the major queues are identified, the next question is: **why are patients waiting?**

Queue mining tells us where delays occur. Process mining, resource analysis, and performance analysis help explain why.

---

### 2.1 Resource bottlenecks

A major root cause may be insufficient or poorly aligned resource capacity.

Examples:

- Too few registration clerks during morning arrival peaks.
- Nurse assessment capacity does not match appointment schedule.
- Certain doctors are overloaded compared with others.
- ECG room or technician availability is limited.
- X-ray equipment is shared with inpatient or emergency demand.
- Check-out clerks are also handling phone calls and scheduling.

#### Data-driven analysis

Using the event log, I would calculate:

- Resource utilization by hour and day
- Number of patients served per resource
- Average service time per resource
- Idle time between cases
- Queue length over time
- Workload distribution across staff
- Bottleneck resources associated with longest waits

For example:

| Resource | Activity | Utilization 9–11 AM | Avg Service Time | Queue Before Resource |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Nurse 1 | Nurse Assessment | 94% | 12 min | 31 min |
| Nurse 2 | Nurse Assessment | 68% | 10 min | 12 min |
| Dr. Smith | Cardiology Consult | 96% | 24 min | 46 min |
| Dr. Jones | Cardiology Consult | 71% | 19 min | 18 min |
| ECG Room 3 | ECG Test | 91% | 8 min | 28 min |

High utilization, especially above roughly 85%, often creates rapidly increasing waiting times when arrivals and service times are variable. A resource does not need to be at 100% utilization to generate queues.

---

### 2.2 Activity dependencies and handovers

Some queues may be caused by process coordination problems rather than pure capacity shortages.

Examples:

- Nurse completes assessment but doctor is not notified promptly.
- Doctor orders ECG, but the diagnostic team does not see the order immediately.
- Patient physically waits in the wrong area.
- Room turnover is slow.
- A paper form or authorization must be completed before testing.
- Staff wait for missing chart information or prior results.
- Check-out cannot start until doctor closes the encounter.

#### Process mining techniques

I would use:

- **Directly-follows graph with performance overlays**
  - Shows the actual patient flow and average delays between steps.

- **Handover-of-work analysis**
  - Identifies delays when work moves from one role to another, such as nurse to doctor or doctor to diagnostic technician.

- **Social network/resource interaction analysis**
  - Reveals problematic staff-to-staff handoffs.

- **Conformance checking**
  - Compares actual flows with intended workflows.
  - Example: patients should go from nurse assessment directly to doctor, but many detour to administrative clarification.

---

### 2.3 Variability in activity durations

Even if average capacity is sufficient, high variability can generate large queues.

Examples:

- New patients require much longer registration.
- Some doctors have highly variable consultation times.
- Complex patients require extended nurse assessments.
- Diagnostic tests occasionally take much longer due to equipment issues.
- Some specialties have many add-on urgent cases.

#### Data-driven analysis

For each activity, I would compare:

- Mean service time
- Median service time
- P90/P95 service time
- Service-time variability by resource
- Service-time variability by patient type
- Service-time variability by specialty
- Relationship between long service times and downstream waits

Example insight:

> New cardiology patients have a median doctor consultation time of 26 minutes but a P90 of 52 minutes. These long consultations create schedule slippage, causing the nurse-to-doctor queue to grow sharply after 10:30 AM.

This would suggest that appointment templates may not reflect real service-time distributions.

---

### 2.4 Appointment scheduling policies

Many outpatient clinic queues are created before the patient arrives due to appointment template design.

Potential issues:

- Too many patients scheduled at the top of the hour.
- New and follow-up patients scheduled in the same short slots.
- No buffer for complex patients.
- Overbooking to compensate for no-shows.
- All diagnostics scheduled after the physician visit rather than pre-visit.
- Multiple specialties sharing diagnostic resources without coordination.
- Urgent add-ons inserted without protecting capacity.

#### Data-driven analysis

I would compare:

- Scheduled appointment time versus actual arrival time
- Actual arrival time versus registration start
- Appointment density by 15-minute interval
- Queue size by time of day
- Delay propagation through the day
- No-show and late-arrival rates
- Overbooking impact
- Schedule adherence by specialty and provider

Example insight:

> The clinic schedules 40% of morning patients between 8:00 and 8:30, but only has registration capacity for 22 patients in that interval. This creates a registration backlog that persists until 10:15.

---

### 2.5 Patient arrival patterns

Actual arrival behavior may differ from the schedule.

Examples:

- Patients arrive in waves due to transportation patterns.
- Many patients arrive early, creating crowded waiting rooms.
- Some arrive late, causing rescheduling and doctor idle time followed by congestion.
- Walk-ins are not separated from scheduled visits.
- Urgent patients interrupt normal flow.

#### Data-driven analysis

I would examine:

- Arrival rate by 5-, 10-, or 15-minute interval
- Early and late arrival distribution
- Walk-in frequency
- Urgent case arrival patterns
- Arrival patterns by specialty and day
- Relationship between arrival peaks and queue formation

Arrival variability should be compared with service capacity by time interval.

---

### 2.6 Differences by patient type and urgency

Not all patients consume the same resources.

New patients may require:

- Longer registration
- More complete nurse assessment
- Longer physician consultation
- More diagnostics
- More check-out scheduling

Follow-up patients may require:

- Shorter registration
- Shorter consultation
- Fewer diagnostics

Urgent patients may preempt normal patients, causing delays for others.

#### Process mining techniques

I would use:

- **Variant analysis**
  - Compare most common process paths.
  - Example:
    - Follow-up: Registration  Nurse  Doctor  Check-out
    - New cardiology: Registration  Nurse  Doctor  ECG  Doctor Review  Check-out

- **Cohort analysis**
  - Compare New versus Follow-up, Urgent versus Normal, Specialty A versus Specialty B.

- **Performance slicing**
  - Calculate queue metrics separately for each patient group.

Example finding:

> New cardiology patients experience 2.1 times longer total visit duration than follow-up cardiology patients, mainly due to waits before diagnostic testing and post-diagnostic doctor review.

---

### 2.7 Bottleneck analysis using process mining

A process mining tool can generate a process map where:

- Nodes represent activities.
- Edges represent transitions.
- Thickness represents frequency.
- Color represents waiting time or performance delay.

This helps identify:

- Frequent high-delay transitions
- Rare but severe delay paths
- Rework loops
- Unnecessary detours
- Specialty-specific bottlenecks
- Resources associated with delays

Example bottleneck paths:

1. Registration  Nurse Assessment  
   - High delay Monday mornings.
2. Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation – Cardiology  
   - High delay for new patients.
3. Doctor Consultation  ECG Test  
   - High delay due to limited ECG room capacity.
4. ECG Test  Specialist Review  
   - High delay because doctors do not review results until later batch times.
5. Doctor Consultation  Check-out  
   - High delay because checkout depends on incomplete orders or scheduling instructions.

## 3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

Below are five concrete strategies. The clinic would not necessarily implement all at once. I would prioritize based on the identified critical queues and test proposed changes using simulation or pilot deployments.

---

### Strategy 1: Align front-desk staffing and digital pre-registration with arrival peaks

#### Target queues

- Arrival  Registration START
- Registration COMPLETE  Nurse Assessment START
- Final clinical activity  Check-out START

#### Root cause addressed

- Morning arrival peaks exceed registration capacity.
- New patients require longer administrative processing.
- Clerks are overloaded during predictable time windows.
- Check-out competes with registration for the same staff.

#### Data support

The event log can show:

- Registration queue length by time of day.
- Registration service time by patient type.
- Clerk utilization.
- New patient registration duration versus follow-up registration duration.
- Check-out delays during periods when clerks are occupied with registration.

Example evidence:

- 8:00–9:30 AM registration utilization is above 90%.
- New patient registration takes 9 minutes median versus 4 minutes for follow-up.
- 35% of patients wait more than 15 minutes before registration during Monday morning sessions.
- Check-out waits increase when the same clerks are covering both intake and discharge.

#### Proposed actions

1. Add one floating clerk or cross-trained staff member during peak arrival periods only.
2. Introduce online pre-registration for new patients.
3. Separate express registration for follow-up patients.
4. Use self-service kiosks for demographic confirmation and copay collection.
5. Split front-desk roles during peak times:
   - Intake clerks
   - Check-out clerks
   - Problem-resolution clerk

#### Potential impact

A targeted staffing adjustment may avoid the cost of hiring full-time staff while reducing peak queues.

Expected impact after simulation or pilot:

- 20–40% reduction in average registration wait during peak hours.
- 10–20% reduction in total visit duration for new patients.
- Reduced downstream nurse idle time caused by uneven patient release from registration.

---

### Strategy 2: Redesign appointment templates using actual service-time distributions

#### Target queues

- Nurse Assessment COMPLETE  Doctor Consultation START
- Doctor Consultation COMPLETE  Diagnostic Test START
- Doctor Consultation COMPLETE  Check-out START
- Late-day accumulated delays

#### Root cause addressed

- Appointment slots do not reflect actual care-time needs.
- New and complex patients are scheduled into slots that are too short.
- Too many high-complexity patients are clustered together.
- Provider schedules have insufficient buffers.
- Overbooking causes excessive waiting when no-show assumptions are wrong.

#### Data support

The event log can show:

- Actual doctor consultation durations by specialty.
- Service-time differences between new and follow-up patients.
- Delay propagation by provider schedule.
- Time of day when doctor queues begin to accumulate.
- Relationship between overbooking and patient waiting.
- No-show and late-arrival patterns.

Example evidence:

- New cardiology consultations have median duration of 28 minutes and P90 of 50 minutes.
- Follow-up consultations have median duration of 13 minutes.
- Current schedule assigns both to 15-minute slots.
- Doctor queue increases steadily after 10:00 AM and does not recover until lunch.

#### Proposed actions

1. Use differentiated appointment lengths:
   - New patient: 30–40 minutes
   - Follow-up: 15–20 minutes
   - Complex follow-up: 25–30 minutes
2. Avoid clustering multiple new patients back-to-back.
3. Add small recovery buffers after predictable high-variability slots.
4. Use historical no-show data to apply controlled overbooking only where appropriate.
5. Create specialty-specific templates rather than one generic template.
6. Reserve urgent add-on slots instead of inserting urgent cases into already full schedules.

#### Potential impact

Expected impact after simulation or pilot:

- 15–30% reduction in nurse-to-doctor waiting time.
- Lower late-day delays.
- Reduced provider overtime.
- Fewer patients waiting more than 45 minutes for doctor consultation.

The goal is not necessarily to reduce doctor utilization dramatically, but to prevent utilization from becoming so high that queueing delays explode.

---

### Strategy 3: Parallelize pre-visit diagnostics for predictable pathways

#### Target queues

- Doctor Consultation COMPLETE  ECG Test START
- Doctor Consultation COMPLETE  Blood Test START
- Diagnostic Test COMPLETE  Specialist Review START
- Total visit duration for diagnostic-heavy specialties

#### Root cause addressed

- Diagnostics are ordered only after the doctor visit, even when predictable.
- Patients wait twice:
  1. First for the doctor.
  2. Then for diagnostics.
  3. Then again for doctor review.
- Diagnostic resources experience post-consultation bursts.

#### Data support

Variant analysis can identify predictable diagnostic pathways.

Example evidence:

- 72% of new cardiology patients receive an ECG.
- 65% of endocrinology patients require blood tests.
- Most ECG orders occur after initial doctor consultation.
- ECG wait P90 is 48 minutes after 10:30 AM.
- Patients requiring ECG plus doctor review have total visit duration twice as long as those not requiring ECG.

#### Proposed actions

1. For high-probability diagnostic pathways, perform standing protocol-based tests before the doctor visit.
   - Example: new cardiology patients receive ECG after nurse assessment and before doctor consultation if clinically appropriate.
2. Use pre-visit order sets based on appointment reason and specialty.
3. Allow nurses or protocol-driven workflows to initiate routine diagnostics.
4. Reserve diagnostic capacity aligned with specialty clinic sessions.
5. Send patients directly from nurse assessment to diagnostics when indicated, then to doctor with results available.

#### Potential impact

Expected impact after simulation or pilot:

- 20–35% reduction in total visit duration for diagnostic-heavy patient groups.
- Reduced doctor-to-diagnostic waiting.
- Reduced need for post-diagnostic doctor re-review.
- Improved doctor decision-making because results are available during consultation.

This strategy must be clinically governed. Only diagnostics that are safe, appropriate, and guideline-supported should be moved earlier.

---

### Strategy 4: Introduce real-time queue visibility and handoff alerts

#### Target queues

- Registration COMPLETE  Nurse Assessment START
- Nurse Assessment COMPLETE  Doctor Consultation START
- Doctor Consultation COMPLETE  Diagnostic Test START
- Diagnostic Test COMPLETE  Specialist Review START
- Final clinical activity COMPLETE  Check-out START

#### Root cause addressed

- Patients are ready, but the next team does not know.
- Work is pushed to the next step without active ownership.
- Staff rely on manual tracking, phone calls, paper forms, or patients physically carrying instructions.
- Rooms or resources remain idle while patients wait.

#### Data support

Process mining can reveal unexplained idle gaps where:

- The next resource was not fully utilized.
- The patient waited despite capacity being available.
- Certain staff-to-staff handovers are consistently slower.
- Delays vary heavily by team, suggesting coordination rather than capacity problems.

Example evidence:

- Nurse-to-doctor wait averages 35 minutes, but doctor utilization during some periods is only 72%.
- Diagnostic-to-review wait is longer when results are completed after 11:30 AM.
- Check-out waits are longest when doctors delay closing orders.

#### Proposed actions

1. Implement a real-time patient flow dashboard showing:
   - Patients waiting at each stage.
   - Time waiting.
   - Priority/urgency.
   - Required next step.
   - Assigned room/resource.
2. Use automated alerts:
   - “Patient ready for doctor.”
   - “ECG complete; ready for review.”
   - “Discharge orders complete; ready for check-out.”
3. Establish ownership rules:
   - Who pulls the next patient?
   - Who escalates if a wait exceeds threshold?
4. Use visual queue prioritization:
   - Urgent patients.
   - Patients nearing SLA breach.
   - Patients needing short tasks.
5. Introduce daily huddles using yesterday’s queue metrics.

#### Potential impact

Expected impact after pilot:

- 10–25% reduction in handoff-related waiting.
- Fewer extreme waits caused by patients being overlooked.
- Improved staff awareness and coordination.
- Better ability to intervene before queues become severe.

This is often lower cost than adding staff because it targets coordination waste.

---

### Strategy 5: Balance diagnostic resource capacity by specialty schedule

#### Target queues

- Doctor Consultation COMPLETE  ECG START
- Doctor Consultation COMPLETE  X-Ray START
- Doctor Consultation COMPLETE  Blood Test START
- Diagnostic Test COMPLETE  Specialist Review START

#### Root cause addressed

- Multiple specialties create simultaneous diagnostic demand.
- Diagnostic rooms or technicians are not scheduled according to actual demand.
- Equipment utilization is high during some periods and low during others.
- Patients wait because diagnostic capacity is misaligned with clinic session timing.

#### Data support

The event log can reveal:

- Diagnostic demand by specialty and time of day.
- Utilization of rooms/equipment.
- Technician workload.
- Queues before each diagnostic resource.
- Frequency of diagnostic orders following specific consultation types.
- Waiting-time spikes linked to certain clinic sessions.

Example evidence:

- ECG demand peaks between 10:00 and 12:00 due to cardiology consultation flow.
- ECG technician utilization reaches 95% during that period but drops below 50% after 2:00 PM.
- X-ray demand spikes on orthopedic clinic mornings.
- Diagnostic queues account for 25% of total visit waiting time.

#### Proposed actions

1. Schedule diagnostic technicians to match specialty-driven demand.
2. Reserve ECG capacity during cardiology clinic blocks.
3. Reserve X-ray capacity during orthopedic clinic blocks.
4. Shift some routine diagnostics to lower-demand periods.
5. Use cross-trained diagnostic staff where feasible.
6. Create fast-track diagnostic slots for short tests such as ECG.

#### Potential impact

Expected impact after simulation:

- 15–30% reduction in diagnostic waiting time.
- Better equipment utilization across the full day.
- Reduced patient crowding after doctor consultation.
- Fewer delayed specialist reviews.

## 4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints

Optimization should not simply minimize waiting time at any cost. The clinic must balance patient experience, clinical quality, staff workload, and financial sustainability.

---

### 4.1 Bottleneck shifting

Reducing one queue may create another.

Example:

- Adding registration staff may move patients faster to nurse assessment, creating a larger nurse queue.
- Performing pre-visit ECGs may reduce doctor-to-ECG wait but increase nurse-to-diagnostic congestion.
- Faster doctor throughput may overload check-out.

Mitigation:

- Use end-to-end process simulation before implementation.
- Monitor all queues, not just the targeted one.
- Implement changes in pilots.
- Evaluate total visit duration, not only local queue time.

---

### 4.2 Cost versus waiting-time reduction

Adding resources reduces queues but increases cost.

Examples:

- More clerks
- More nurses
- More diagnostic technicians
- More rooms
- More equipment

Mitigation:

- Use targeted peak-period staffing rather than full-day staffing.
- Cross-train staff.
- Use part-time or float pools.
- Apply technology where cost-effective.
- Compare cost per waiting-minute reduced.

A useful metric is:

> **Cost per patient waiting-hour avoided**

This allows management to compare interventions.

---

### 4.3 Staff workload and burnout

Aggressive throughput goals can increase pressure on staff.

Risks:

- Reduced documentation quality.
- Less patient communication.
- Increased errors.
- Burnout.
- Resistance to process changes.

Mitigation:

- Include staff in redesign.
- Monitor overtime and break compliance.
- Avoid unrealistic utilization targets.
- Use queue dashboards to support staff, not punish them.
- Balance workload across resources.

---

### 4.4 Quality of care

Reducing wait time must not mean shortening care inappropriately.

Risks:

- Rushed nurse assessment.
- Shorter doctor consultation.
- Incomplete patient education.
- Missed clinical issues.
- Inappropriate pre-ordering of tests.

Mitigation:

- Track clinical quality KPIs alongside waiting-time KPIs.
- Use clinically approved protocols.
- Ensure diagnostic pre-ordering follows guidelines.
- Monitor adverse events, rework, and return visits.
- Keep minimum required consultation and assessment standards.

---

### 4.5 Fairness and prioritization

Prioritizing urgent patients may increase waits for routine patients. Fast-tracking short visits may disadvantage complex patients.

Mitigation:

- Define transparent priority rules.
- Separate urgent capacity from routine capacity where possible.
- Monitor waits by patient segment.
- Ensure vulnerable patients are not systematically delayed.
- Use equity metrics.

---

### 4.6 Overbooking risk

Overbooking can improve provider utilization when no-show rates are high, but it can also cause severe waits if too many patients arrive.

Mitigation:

- Use predictive no-show models.
- Overbook selectively, not uniformly.
- Avoid overbooking high-complexity sessions.
- Maintain buffers.
- Monitor the relationship between overbooking and P90 waiting time.

---

### 4.7 Balancing conflicting objectives

I would recommend a balanced optimization framework using constraints and weighted objectives.

Example objectives:

- Minimize total patient waiting time.
- Minimize P90 waiting time.
- Minimize total visit duration.
- Maintain clinical service time standards.
- Keep staff utilization within a sustainable range.
- Avoid increased overtime.
- Maintain or improve patient satisfaction.

Example constraints:

- Doctor consultation time should not be reduced below clinically appropriate levels.
- Nurse utilization should not exceed a sustainable threshold for long periods.
- Urgent patients must be seen within defined clinical timeframes.
- Additional staffing costs must stay within budget.
- Diagnostic equipment capacity is fixed unless investment is approved.

The best intervention is not necessarily the one that produces the lowest average wait. It is the one that improves end-to-end patient flow sustainably, safely, and affordably.

---

## 5. Measuring Success

After implementing optimization strategies, the clinic should continue using the event log as a process monitoring system.

---

### 5.1 Core KPIs

#### Patient waiting-time KPIs

For each major queue:

- Average waiting time
- Median waiting time
- P90/P95 waiting time
- Maximum waiting time
- Percentage of patients waiting more than target
- Total waiting minutes per day/week
- Waiting time by patient type, specialty, urgency, and provider

Example targets:

- 90% of patients start registration within 10 minutes of arrival.
- 90% of patients start nurse assessment within 15 minutes after registration.
- 90% of patients start doctor consultation within 20 minutes after nurse assessment.
- 90% of diagnostic tests start within 15 minutes after order.
- 90% of patients start check-out within 10 minutes after final clinical activity.

#### Total visit KPIs

- Average total visit duration
- Median total visit duration
- P90 total visit duration
- Total visit duration by pathway
- Percentage of visits completed within target time
- Value-added ratio:
  - Service time divided by total visit duration

#### Throughput KPIs

- Number of patients completed per clinic session
- Patients completed per provider per day
- Diagnostic tests completed per hour
- Room turnover time
- End-of-day backlog
- Number of patients still in process after scheduled clinic close

#### Resource KPIs

- Staff utilization by hour
- Room utilization
- Equipment utilization
- Overtime hours
- Idle time
- Workload balance across staff
- Patients waiting while resource is idle

#### Quality and safety KPIs

- Rework rate
- Patients requiring repeat tests due to process errors
- Missed or delayed diagnostic reviews
- Return visits related to incomplete care
- Incident reports
- Documentation completion timeliness

#### Patient experience KPIs

- Patient satisfaction score
- Net Promoter Score, if used
- Complaints about waiting
- Percentage of patients informed of delays
- Walkout or abandonment rate
- Patient-reported total time in clinic

#### Financial KPIs

- Cost per visit
- Cost per waiting-minute reduced
- Overtime cost
- Revenue impact from improved throughput
- Staff cost versus patient experience improvement

### 5.2 Ongoing process monitoring

The clinic should build a recurring process mining dashboard using the same event log structure.

The dashboard should show:

1. **Current queue performance**
   - By queue, specialty, provider, time of day, and patient type.

2. **Trend over time**
   - Weekly and monthly changes.
   - Before-versus-after comparisons.

3. **SLA breach alerts**
   - Real-time or near-real-time identification of patients waiting too long.

4. **Bottleneck heat maps**
   - Example:
     - Monday 8–10 AM registration bottleneck.
     - Wednesday cardiology doctor queue.
     - Friday afternoon check-out delay.

5. **Variant monitoring**
   - Detect if new inefficient patient pathways emerge.

6. **Resource utilization monitoring**
   - Identify overburdened or underused resources.

7. **Equity monitoring**
   - Ensure improvements apply across patient types and urgency levels.

---

### 5.3 Before-and-after evaluation design

To determine whether interventions worked, I would compare:

- Baseline period: prior 6 months or a representative subset.
- Pilot period: first 4–8 weeks after implementation.
- Stabilization period: subsequent 8–12 weeks.

I would use:

- Statistical process control charts.
- Confidence intervals for mean and median wait reductions.
- Percentile comparisons.
- SLA breach rate comparisons.
- Difference-in-differences if only some specialties adopt the change initially.
- Simulation validation against actual post-implementation outcomes.

Example evaluation:

| KPI | Baseline | Target | Post-Pilot |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Median Registration Wait | 12 min | 8 min | 7 min |
| P90 Registration Wait | 34 min | 20 min | 19 min |
| Median Nurse-to-Doctor Wait | 28 min | 20 min | 21 min |
| P90 Total Visit Duration | 180 min | 140 min | 145 min |
| Patient Satisfaction | 72/100 | 80/100 | 82/100 |
| Overtime Hours/Week | 45 | 45 | 39 |

---

### 5.4 Sustaining improvement

Process optimization should become continuous rather than a one-time project.

Recommended governance:

1. Weekly patient-flow review meeting.
2. Monthly process mining report.
3. Threshold-based escalation rules.
4. Quarterly appointment-template recalibration.
5. Continuous review of diagnostic demand patterns.
6. Staff feedback loop.
7. Patient feedback integration.
8. Annual capacity planning using historical event logs.

The clinic should also maintain a “digital twin” or simulation model calibrated with event-log data. Before changing schedules, staffing, or diagnostic capacity, management can test the expected effect on queues, utilization, cost, and patient experience.

---

## Summary

The clinic can use queue mining to convert raw start and completion timestamps into a precise view of where patients wait, how long they wait, and which queues create the greatest operational and patient-experience burden. The most important queues should be prioritized based on waiting-time severity, frequency, total waiting burden, SLA breaches, patient segment impact, and downstream effects.

Process mining then helps explain the root causes: resource bottlenecks, poorly aligned schedules, handoff failures, service-time variability, diagnostic dependencies, and differences across patient groups. Based on those findings, the clinic can deploy targeted improvements such as peak-period staffing, redesigned appointment templates, pre-visit diagnostics, real-time queue dashboards, and better diagnostic capacity alignment.

Success should be measured not only by lower average wait times, but also by lower P90 waits, shorter total visit duration, improved patient satisfaction, stable clinical quality, sustainable staff workload, and controlled costs. Continuous monitoring using the same event log structure will allow the clinic to detect new bottlenecks early and sustain improvements over time.
