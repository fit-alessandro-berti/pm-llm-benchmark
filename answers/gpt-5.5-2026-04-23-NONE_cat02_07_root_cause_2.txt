## 2. Attribute analysis and likely root causes

### A. Complexity is the strongest driver of long duration

There is a clear relationship between claim complexity and lead time.

| Complexity | Cases | Average Duration | Pattern |
|---|---|---:|---|
| Low | 2001, 2004 | ~1h 28m | Fast, no document requests |
| Medium | 2002 | 25h 55m | One document request, next-day completion |
| High | 2003, 2005 | ~62h 43m | Multiple document requests, multi-day completion |

High-complexity cases take much longer than low-complexity ones. The main reason appears to be repeated **Request Additional Documents** activities.

- Case **2003**, High complexity: 2 document requests, total duration 48h 20m.
- Case **2005**, High complexity: 3 document requests, total duration 77h 05m.
- Case **2002**, Medium complexity: 1 document request, total duration 25h 55m.
- Cases **2001** and **2004**, Low complexity: 0 document requests, completed in around 1.5 hours.

### Root cause hypothesis
High-complexity claims likely require more evidence, verification, or specialist review. If documentation requirements are not clear upfront, the process creates repeated back-and-forth cycles with the customer, causing major delays.

---

### B. Repeated “Request Additional Documents” activities create major waiting time

The largest delays occur around document request stages.

#### Case 2005
- Submit: Apr 1 09:25
- Evaluate: Apr 1 09:50
- 1st document request: Apr 1 11:30
- 2nd document request: Apr 2 17:00
- 3rd document request: Apr 3 15:00
- Approve: Apr 4 10:00
- Close: Apr 4 14:30

This case has **three separate document requests**, which suggests that either:
- required documents were requested in batches rather than all at once,
- the customer submitted incomplete or incorrect documents,
- the claim was complex and new information created new requirements,
- the adjuster lacked a checklist or decision support tool,
- or there were handoff/waiting delays between review cycles.

#### Case 2003
- Has two document requests.
- Approval occurs the next day at 16:00 after the final document request.
- Payment is delayed until the following morning.

### Root cause hypothesis
The document collection process is a bottleneck. Long cases are not necessarily delayed by the standard steps like submission, initial evaluation, or closing, but by the repeated need to request and wait for additional information.

---

### C. Region B appears to have the longest single case, but region alone is not the full explanation

Average durations by region:

| Region | Cases | Durations | Average |
|---|---|---|---:|
| A | 2001, 2003 | 1h 30m, 48h 20m | ~24h 55m |
| B | 2002, 2004, 2005 | 25h 55m, 1h 25m, 77h 05m | ~34h 48m |

Region B has the longest average duration and contains the worst-performing case, **2005**. However, Region B also has a low-complexity case, **2004**, completed in only 1h 25m.

Therefore, **Region B may contribute to delays, but it is not sufficient on its own to explain them**. The combination of **Region B + High Complexity + repeated document requests** is more convincing.

### Root cause hypothesis
Region B may have:
- higher claim volume,
- slower customer response times,
- stricter documentation requirements,
- less experienced staff for high-complexity claims,
- or regional compliance rules requiring additional checks.

But since low-complexity Region B claims are completed quickly, the issue seems concentrated in more complex cases.

### D. Adjuster_Lisa is associated with the longest case, but complexity and region confound the result

Adjusters involved:

| Adjuster | Cases | Complexity | Durations |
|---|---|---|---|
| Adjuster_Mike | 2001, 2003 | Low, High | 1h 30m, 48h 20m |
| Adjuster_Lisa | 2002, 2004, 2005 | Medium, Low, High | 25h 55m, 1h 25m, 77h 05m |

Adjuster_Lisa handled the longest case, **2005**, and also case **2002**, which took nearly 26 hours. However, she also handled case **2004**, which was completed very quickly.

This suggests that Adjuster_Lisa herself may not be the primary root cause. Rather, her cases include **Region B medium/high-complexity claims**, which naturally require more documentation.

However, there may still be a process issue:
- Adjuster_Lisa made multiple document requests in case 2005.
- If the requests could have been consolidated, duration may have been reduced.
- If Region B high-complexity claims are mostly routed to Lisa, she may be overloaded or handling more difficult work.

### Root cause hypothesis
Potential issues around Adjuster_Lisa may include:
- higher workload,
- assignment of more complex Region B claims,
- lack of standardized document checklist,
- or repeated review cycles instead of complete upfront documentation requests.

But the data is too small to conclude that Lisa is underperforming individually.

### E. Manager_Bill is associated with high-complexity approvals and longer cases

Manager involvement:

| Manager | Cases | Complexity | Durations |
|---|---|---|---|
| Manager_Ann | 2001, 2002, 2004 | Low, Medium, Low | 1h 30m, 25h 55m, 1h 25m |
| Manager_Bill | 2003, 2005 | High, High | 48h 20m, 77h 05m |

Manager_Bill appears only in high-complexity cases, both of which are long-running. This does not necessarily mean Manager_Bill causes the delay. More likely, high-complexity cases are routed to Manager_Bill for approval.

However, approval timing may still contribute:
- Case 2003: final document request Apr 1 17:00, approval Apr 2 16:00 — nearly 23 hours later.
- Case 2005: final document request Apr 3 15:00, approval Apr 4 10:00 — 19 hours later.

### Root cause hypothesis
High-complexity approvals may require managerial review, legal checks, fraud checks, or senior authorization. Manager_Bill may be a bottleneck because he handles complex approvals.

Payment is usually fast after approval, but there are some delays:

- Case 2003: Approve Apr 2 16:00  Pay Apr 3 09:00 = **17 hours**

- Case 2002: Approve Apr 2 10:00  Pay Apr 2 10:45 = **45 minutes**

The payment delay in case 2003 may be due to an end-of-day cutoff. Approval at 16:00 may have missed same-day payment processing.

### Root cause hypothesis
Finance is a secondary bottleneck in some cases, especially if approvals happen late in the day and miss payment batch windows.

## 3. Summary of root causes

### Primary root causes

#### 1. High claim complexity
High-complexity claims have much longer durations. They require more document collection, review, and approval effort.

#### 2. Multiple additional document requests
The number of document requests correlates strongly with longer lead time:

| # Document Requests | Cases | Typical Duration |
|---:|---|---:|
| 0 | 2001, 2004 | ~1.5 hours |
| 1 | 2002 | ~26 hours |
| 2 | 2003 | ~48 hours |
| 3 | 2005 | ~77 hours |

This is the clearest operational bottleneck.

#### 3. High-complexity approval bottleneck
High-complexity cases are approved by Manager_Bill and take much longer. This may reflect necessary senior review, but it may also indicate a capacity or scheduling bottleneck.

#### 4. Region B may amplify delays for complex cases
Region B contains the longest case, 2005. However, Region B low-complexity claims can still be completed quickly, so region is likely an amplifying factor rather than the sole root cause.

## 5. Recommendations to mitigate the root causes

### A. Improve upfront document collection
Introduce complexity-specific document checklists at claim submission.

For example:
- Low complexity: basic claim form and proof of incident.
- Medium complexity: claim form, proof, photos, supporting invoices.
- High complexity: full evidence package, police/medical reports if applicable, expert assessment, prior policy documentation.

This can reduce repeated document requests.

---

### B. Consolidate document requests
Instead of requesting documents one at a time, adjusters should perform a complete gap analysis before contacting the customer.

Suggested control:
- Before sending a document request, the system prompts the adjuster to confirm all required documents have been checked.
- Use standardized templates by claim type and complexity.
- Require managerial or peer review for second or third document requests.

---

### C. Introduce early triage for high-complexity claims
High-complexity claims should be identified immediately at submission and routed to specialized handling.

Possible actions:
- Assign high-complexity claims to senior adjusters.
- Create a dedicated complex-claims team.
- Apply automated completeness checks at intake.
- Notify customers upfront about likely documentation requirements.

---

### D. Monitor repeated document request rate by resource and region
Track KPIs such as:
- average number of document requests per case,
- percentage of cases with more than one document request,
- average time from document request to approval,
- lead time by complexity,
- lead time by region,
- lead time by adjuster.

This would help determine whether delays are due to resource practices, regional differences, or claim complexity.

---

### E. Investigate Region B high-complexity handling
Because the worst case is a Region B high-complexity claim, investigate whether Region B has:
- stricter documentation requirements,
- slower customer response times,
- workload imbalance,
- staffing shortages,
- different regulatory requirements,
- or lower first-time-right document submission rates.

---

### F. Reduce approval bottlenecks for complex claims
Since Manager_Bill handles the high-complexity approvals, check whether he is a bottleneck.

Possible mitigations:
- Add another manager authorized to approve high-complexity claims.
- Define approval SLAs.
- Use pre-approval review while documents are still being collected.
- Create automated risk scoring to support approval decisions.

---

### G. Improve payment timing after approval
If claims approved late in the day miss payment cutoffs, consider:
- additional payment batch runs,
- automated payment release immediately after approval,
- priority payment queue for delayed claims,
- or earlier approval cutoff alerts.

---

## Conclusion

The main performance issue is not the normal claim process itself, but the combination of **higher complexity**, **repeated additional document requests**, and possible **complex-case approval bottlenecks**.

The most problematic case is **2005**, followed by **2003** and **2002**. The strongest root cause is the number of document request cycles, which increases with complexity. Region B and certain resources, especially Adjuster_Lisa and Manager_Bill, appear associated with longer cases, but the data suggests this is largely because they are handling more complex or document-intensive claims rather than necessarily performing poorly.
