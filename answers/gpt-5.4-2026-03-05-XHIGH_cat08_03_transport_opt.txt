Below is the approach I would recommend for Speedy Parcels. The key idea is to treat this as a **multi-level last-mile process**: route execution, stop execution, package delivery attempts, and vehicle health all interact. The sample trace already shows why integration matters: **traffic delay -> failed delivery -> unscheduled vehicle stop -> overtime**. Process mining can show whether that is an isolated case or a recurring pattern, where it happens, and what it costs.

## 1. Process Discovery and Conformance Checking

### 1.1 Preprocess and integrate the data into a process-mining-ready event log

For Speedy Parcels, I would **not rely on one flat event log only**. A single “vehicle-day” case is useful, but it hides important package-level loops like failed delivery and re-delivery. I would build a **layered log design**:

#### A. Primary case/object structure
1. **Route execution case**: `Vehicle-Day`  
   - Best for end-to-end route analysis: start shift -> depart depot -> stops -> return depot -> end shift.
2. **Stop instance object**: `Vehicle-Day + Customer Visit`
   - Important because one stop may involve multiple packages.
   - Service time belongs to the **stop**, not always to a single package.
3. **Package attempt case**: `Package ID + Attempt Number`
   - Best for analyzing failed delivery loops, first-attempt success, and re-delivery burden.
4. **Vehicle maintenance case**: `Vehicle ID + Maintenance/Repair Episode`
   - Best for downtime and breakdown pattern analysis.
5. **Ideally: an object-centric event log**
   - One event can relate to multiple objects at once: vehicle, driver, package, route plan, customer stop, maintenance ticket.
   - This is particularly useful in logistics, where the same route involves many packages and the same vehicle appears across many route-days.

If the toolset does not support object-centric mining well, I would still create **multiple synchronized logs** rather than force everything into one simplistic case notion.

---

### 1.2 Event log engineering steps

#### Step 1: Standardize and clean source data
- Normalize timestamps to one timezone and clock standard.
- Use **device-recorded event time**, not upload time, especially for handheld scanners.
- Remove duplicates and impossible records:
  - GPS jumps
  - negative durations
  - duplicate scans
  - impossible speeds
- Standardize activity labels and failure reasons:
  - e.g. `Delivery Failed - Customer Not Home`, `Access Issue`, `Address Problem`
- Normalize free-text notes where possible into reason codes.

#### Step 2: Convert raw GPS telemetry into semantic events
Raw GPS pings are too granular for process mining. They must be transformed into meaningful activities.

Examples of **derived events/states**:
- `Depart Depot`
- `Transit`
- `Traffic Delay`
- `Enter Customer Zone`
- `Park / Search for Parking`
- `Arrive Customer`
- `Idle`
- `Unscheduled Stop`
- `Vehicle Warning`
- `Maintenance Start / End`
- `Arrive Depot`

Typical logic:
- **Traffic delay**: low speed for a sustained period outside a depot/customer geofence.
- **Unscheduled stop**: stationary outside planned service locations for more than a threshold.
- **Parking/approach time**: vehicle enters/halts near customer before scanner-based `Arrive Customer`.
- **Service time**: `Depart Customer - Arrive Customer`.
- **Travel time between stops**: `Arrive next stop - Depart previous stop`.

This abstraction step is critical. Otherwise the model becomes “GPS spaghetti” rather than a process.

#### Step 3: Geospatial enrichment
- Geofence:
  - depot
  - customer addresses
  - repair workshops
  - refuel/authorized stop points if available
- Map-match GPS traces to the road network.
- Derive:
  - actual distance traveled
  - road segment used
  - zone/territory
  - urban vs suburban route type
  - repeated congestion hotspots

#### Step 4: Join scanner, dispatch, and maintenance data
From the **scanner**:
- `Scan at Depot`
- `Arrive Customer`
- `Delivery Success`
- `Delivery Failed`
- `Depart Customer`

From the **dispatch system**:
- planned route/stop sequence
- assigned packages
- route ID / territory
- planned departure / ETA
- customer time windows
- vehicle capacity
- driver assignment

From **maintenance logs**:
- scheduled maintenance windows
- unscheduled repairs
- overlap with route execution
- days since last maintenance
- recent repair history

#### Step 5: Add derived process attributes
For each route/stop/package, I would derive attributes such as:
- planned stop order vs actual stop order
- planned ETA vs actual arrival/delivery time
- actual km vs planned km
- stop service duration
- parking/approach duration
- idle duration
- traffic delay duration
- route departure delay from depot
- route completion overrun / overtime
- packages per stop
- load factor / capacity utilization
- vehicle age / recent repair history
- driver, vehicle type, zone, day of week, departure time band

---

### 1.3 Likely data challenges

The main challenges will be:

1. **Different granularities**
   - GPS is continuous and high-volume.
   - scanner events are milestone-based.
   - maintenance logs are interval-based.
   - dispatch data is planned rather than actual.

2. **Clock synchronization issues**
   - scanner times and GPS times may not perfectly align.

3. **GPS noise**
   - urban canyons, poor coordinates, false stop detection.

4. **Case correlation difficulty**
   - one vehicle-day includes many packages.
   - one customer stop may include several package IDs.
   - one failed package may reappear on another day/route.

5. **Manual scanning behavior**
   - `Arrive Customer` may be scanned after parking or after walking to the door.
   - this can distort service-time measurement if GPS is not used alongside scanner data.

6. **Missing events**
   - missing depart scans
   - scanner offline uploads
   - incomplete failure reason coding

7. **Over-penalizing useful adaptation**
   - drivers may deviate from a planned sequence for good reasons; not every deviation is bad.

8. **Data quality / governance**
   - driver comparisons must be fair and normalized for route complexity.
   - privacy and workforce acceptance matter.

---

### 1.4 Discover the actual end-to-end process

I would use **multi-level process discovery**.

#### A. Route-level model
A typical discovered macro process may look like:

`Start Shift -> Route Assigned -> Scan/Load at Depot -> Depart Depot -> [Transit -> (Traffic Delay?) -> Arrive Stop -> (Delivery Success | Delivery Failed) -> Depart Stop] x N -> (Unscheduled Stop / Vehicle Warning / Maintenance?) -> Arrive Depot -> End Shift`

This gives the real execution pattern, including loops for repeated stops and exception branches.

#### B. Stop-level model
To understand bottlenecks like parking or customer interaction:
- `Enter Customer Area -> Park/Search -> Arrive Customer -> Deliver or Fail -> Depart`
This is where parking problems, access problems, or unusually long customer interaction times become visible.

#### C. Package-level model
For failed deliveries:
- `Out for Delivery -> Attempt 1 -> Failed -> Re-scheduled -> Attempt 2 -> Delivered`
This reveals hidden rework and the cost of first-attempt failure.

#### D. Algorithms / visualizations
I would combine:
- **Inductive Miner** for a sound, interpretable model
- **Heuristic/Fuzzy Miner** for high-variance, noisy logistics data
- **Directly-follows graphs** with frequency and performance annotations
- **Dotted charts / performance spectra** for time-based patterns
- **GIS overlays** to visualize process transitions on a map

Because last-mile routes vary greatly, I would cluster traces by:
- urban vs suburban
- route family / territory
- stop density
- time-window intensity
- vehicle type

This avoids one unreadable model for everything.

### 1.5 Conformance checking against planned routes

I would build a **reference model from dispatch data** and compare actual execution against it.

Important point: I would check conformance against **two references**:
1. **The official planned route**
2. **A best-practice benchmark model** learned from top-performing actual routes with similar complexity

Why both? Because:
- **High conformance + poor KPI** often means the **plan is unrealistic**
- **Low conformance + good KPI** often means drivers are **adapting intelligently**
- **Low conformance + poor KPI** suggests a real execution problem

#### Types of conformance checking

#### A. Structural / sequence conformance
Look for:
- stops served out of planned order
- skipped planned stops
- extra/unplanned stops
- repeated visits to same customer
- early return to depot
- packages assigned but not attempted
- packages served by different vehicle/driver than planned

#### B. Temporal conformance
Compare:
- planned vs actual departure time from depot
- planned vs actual travel time between stops
- planned ETA vs actual arrival/delivery
- customer time-window breaches
- excessive dwell/service time
- excessive route completion time / overtime

#### C. Spatial conformance
Compare:
- actual path vs planned path
- detour km
- leaving planned territory
- repeated use of suboptimal corridors

#### D. Business-rule/resource conformance
Check:
- wrong driver or wrong vehicle assignment
- capacity overrun
- working-hour and break violations
- route execution during/near maintenance conflicts

#### Important nuance
I would use **weighted conformance**, not rigid “exact order = good, deviation = bad”.
For example:
- swapping two nearby residential stops may be harmless or even beneficial
- missing a tight-window business stop is severe

Where route planners allow flexibility, the reference should be modeled as a **partial order**, not as one rigid sequence.

## 2. Performance Analysis and Bottleneck Identification

### 2.1 KPIs relevant to Speedy Parcels

Below is the KPI set I would prioritize.

| KPI | How to calculate from the event log | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **On-Time Delivery Rate** | `# Delivery Success events within requested time window / # deliveries with time window` | Core customer punctuality metric |
| **ETA Accuracy / Window Breach Rate** | Average absolute difference between planned ETA and actual arrival/delivery; plus `% outside time window` | Shows whether planning estimates are realistic |
| **First-Attempt Success Rate / Failed Delivery Rate** | `# packages delivered on first attempt / # packages attempted`; failed rate = `# Delivery Failed / # attempts` | Failed attempts create rework, extra fuel, and customer dissatisfaction |
| **Average Stop Service Time** | `avg(Depart Customer - Arrive Customer)`; also per-package if multiple packages per stop | Measures stop execution efficiency |
| **Parking / Approach Time** | `Arrive Customer(scan) - vehicle enters/stops in customer geofence` | Identifies parking and access problems |
| **Travel Time Between Stops** | `Arrive next stop - Depart previous stop` | Core routing and traffic KPI |
| **Travel Time vs Service Time Ratio** | `Total transit time / total service time` | Indicates whether inefficiency sits in driving or on-site service |
| **Idle Time Ratio** | `Total idle time with ignition on / route execution time` | Strong fuel and productivity indicator |
| **Traffic Delay Frequency and Duration** | Count and sum of low-speed delay episodes outside service locations | Measures congestion impact |
| **Route Departure Adherence / Route Overrun / Overtime Rate** | Departure delay = `actual depart depot - planned depart`; overrun = `actual end - planned end`; overtime rate = `% routes exceeding scheduled hours` | Shows schedule realism and labor cost pressure |
| **Vehicle Utilization** | `Active route time / vehicle available time` (excluding maintenance) | Shows whether vehicles are under/over-used |
| **Capacity Utilization** | `Loaded packages/volume / vehicle capacity` | Reveals route balancing quality |
| **Detour Factor** | `Actual km / planned km` or `actual km - planned km` | Directly linked to fuel waste |
| **Fuel per km / per successful package** | Prefer actual fuel if available; otherwise proxy from `distance + idle minutes + stop-start intensity + speed profile` | Needed for cost control; if no fuel feed exists, use proxy and later enrich |
| **Breakdown / Unscheduled Stop Rate** | `# vehicle warnings, unscheduled stops, or unscheduled repairs / 1,000 km` or per route | Measures fleet reliability |
| **Maintenance Downtime** | Sum of maintenance/repair duration overlapping available operating time | Quantifies fleet availability loss |

**Important caveat:** exact fuel and maintenance cost require additional data such as fuel-card, OBD/CAN, or repair invoice data. If those are absent, start with operational proxies and add finance/fleet cost data later.

---

### 2.2 Process mining techniques to identify bottlenecks

I would use a combination of **performance mining**, **variant analysis**, and **geospatial slicing**.

#### A. Performance-annotated process maps
Overlay each activity and transition with:
- frequency
- median duration
- P90/P95 duration
- share of late routes after passing that step

This identifies slow activities like:
- long depot release
- long inter-stop travel
- high parking/approach time
- long customer service time
- vehicle incident branches

#### B. Throughput time decomposition
Break each route into:
- pre-departure/depot time
- outbound travel
- inter-stop travel
- parking/approach
- customer interaction/service
- failed delivery handling
- breaks / idle
- unplanned stops / repair
- return-to-depot leg

This tells us whether delays are mostly:
- route design
- congestion
- stop execution
- vehicle reliability
- depot readiness

#### C. Variant analysis
Compare:
- on-time vs late routes
- low-cost vs high-cost routes
- top-quartile vs bottom-quartile drivers
- high-fitness vs low-fitness traces
- urban vs suburban route families

This shows which process patterns consistently produce better outcomes.

#### D. Process cube / multi-dimensional slicing
Analyze performance by:
- route / territory
- time of day
- day of week
- vehicle type / vehicle ID
- driver
- stop position within route
- customer/zone archetype
- load factor

This directly answers whether bottlenecks are related to:
- specific routes
- rush hours
- specific drivers
- specific van types
- known traffic corridors
- certain activities like parking or customer interaction

#### E. Geospatial hotspot mining
Overlay process delays on a map:
- repeated low-speed segments
- repeated unscheduled stops
- parking delay hotspots
- addresses/buildings with long dwell time
- neighborhoods with high failed-delivery rates

#### F. Delay propagation analysis
In logistics, the first delay often creates downstream failures. I would analyze:
- where lateness starts in the route
- whether it is recovered later
- whether it cascades into failed delivery or overtime

For example:
- if lateness typically jumps after stop 8 and never recovers, the route is structurally overloaded
- if routes start late from depot, downstream failures are not primarily a driving problem

---

### 2.3 Quantifying bottleneck impact

I would quantify each bottleneck not just by “how long it is”, but by **frequency x excess time x business impact**.

A practical impact formula:

**Impact of bottleneck**  
= `(number of occurrences x excess minutes over benchmark x labor cost/minute)`  
+ `(extra km x fuel cost/km)`  
+ `(extra failed attempts x re-delivery cost)`  
+ `(SLA breaches attributable to the bottleneck)`

Examples:
- A traffic hotspot may be frequent but moderate.
- A breakdown may be rare but catastrophic.
- Parking search may be small per stop but huge in aggregate.

Both frequency and severity matter.

## 3. Root Cause Analysis for Inefficiencies

Once bottlenecks are known, the next step is to determine **why** they occur. I would use three core techniques:

1. **Variant comparison**
   - Compare high-performing vs low-performing routes/drivers within similar route complexity.
2. **Temporal/spatial correlation**
   - Link travel delays to time bands, road segments, stop type, and downstream failure.
3. **Decision/root-cause mining**
   - At key branches like `Delivery Success vs Delivery Failed` or `No Incident vs Unscheduled Stop`, identify which attributes increase the probability of the bad outcome.

### Key root-cause hypotheses and how to validate them

#### A. Suboptimal route planning (static planning, weak sequencing, poor territory design)
**Hypothesis:** The planned route itself is inefficient.

**How to test:**
- Compare planned stop order vs actual stop order.
- Check whether drivers frequently reorder the route.
- See whether those alternative actual variants perform better or worse.
- Analyze delay accumulation by stop number.
- Check for cross-territory overlap where multiple vans repeatedly cover the same area.

**Evidence pattern:**
- Routes are late even when drivers follow the plan closely -> planning problem.
- Drivers consistently deviate in the same way and improve outcomes -> plan is outdated.
- Certain route IDs are bad regardless of driver -> structural route design issue.

---

#### B. Inaccurate travel time estimates
**Hypothesis:** Dispatch ETAs are systematically too optimistic.

**How to test:**
- Build an empirical travel-time matrix from actual logs by:
  - origin/destination zone
  - time of day
  - day of week
- Compare planned vs actual transition times.
- Use temporal conformance to spot persistent underestimation.

**Evidence pattern:**
- Morning downtown segments always take 30% longer than plan.
- Late routes are concentrated where ETA bias is largest.

This is often a major source of both lateness and customer dissatisfaction.

---

#### C. Traffic congestion patterns and hotspot corridors
**Hypothesis:** Recurrent congestion, not random noise, is driving delay.

**How to test:**
- Correlate low-speed GPS episodes with late deliveries and overtime.
- Map repeated congestion hotspots by hour/day.
- Enrich with external traffic/roadworks data if available.

**Evidence pattern:**
- Same road segments repeatedly produce excess minutes in specific time bands.
- Routes crossing those segments show lower on-time rates and higher fuel/idling.

---

#### D. High variability in service time at customer locations
**Hypothesis:** Customer stop times are unpredictable, and planners assume too much uniformity.

**How to test:**
- Analyze distributions, not just averages:
  - median, P90, P95 service time
  - parking time
  - customer interaction time
- Compare by location cluster/customer archetype:
  - dense urban apartment
  - detached residential
  - business customer
  - repeated chronic addresses
- Separate:
  - parking/approach time
  - delivery interaction time
  - post-delivery admin delay

**Evidence pattern:**
- Certain buildings or zones have consistently long parking/access times.
- Business deliveries have tight windows and long receiving procedures.
- A few chronic addresses consume disproportionate route time.

In last-mile delivery, a stop type with moderate average but very high variance can damage punctuality more than a slightly slower but predictable stop type.

---

#### E. Vehicle breakdowns / poor maintenance timing
**Hypothesis:** Reliability issues are causing route disruption and cost.

**How to test:**
- Join unscheduled stops and warning events to route outcomes.
- Analyze by vehicle:
  - age
  - mileage
  - recent repair history
  - stop-start intensity
  - idle ratio
- Check whether high-intensity urban routes are overrepresented in failures.

**Evidence pattern:**
- Same vehicles repeatedly show warning/repair branches.
- High stop-start routes have more breakdowns than highway-equivalent km would suggest.
- A mid-route warning often leads to missed stops and overtime.

---

#### F. Driver behavior or skill differences
**Hypothesis:** Some inefficiencies are execution-related rather than planning-related.

**How to test fairly:**
- Compare drivers only within similar route clusters and vehicle types.
- Control for route difficulty, load, time-window pressure, and geography.
- Compare:
  - idle time
  - detour factor
  - service time
  - parking/approach time
  - first-attempt success
  - overtime

**Evidence pattern:**
- Some drivers systematically idle more or have longer stop times on similar routes.
- Top performers share a similar sequence/stop-execution pattern that can be standardized.

This should be used for **coaching and best-practice transfer**, not simplistic blame.

---

#### G. Failed delivery attempts and re-delivery loops
**Hypothesis:** Failed deliveries are not random; they are tied to timing, customer type, or communication gaps.

**How to test:**
- Mine package-level variants: `Attempt -> Fail -> Reattempt`.
- Compare failure rates by:
  - time of day
  - route position
  - zone
  - customer type
  - arrival relative to promised window
- Use reason codes from scanner notes (`Customer Not Home`, `No Access`, etc.).

**Evidence pattern:**
- Residential failures cluster late in the day after ETA slips.
- Business failures cluster after closing hours.
- Certain addresses repeatedly fail due access problems.

This is important because failed delivery is hidden waste: it adds future route load, extra km, extra service time, and lower customer satisfaction.

---

#### H. Upstream depot/dispatch delay
Even though the question focuses on routing, I would also check:
- delay between `Start Shift`, `Route Assigned`, `Scan at Depot`, and `Depart Depot`

**Why:** if vans leave late, the whole route starts with inherited delay. In many operations, depot release is a major root cause that gets wrongly blamed on drivers or traffic.

## 4. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

Below are **five concrete strategies**. Any three would already be valuable; together they form a stronger improvement program.

---

### Strategy 1: Dynamic traffic-aware routing and ETA reforecasting

**Targets**
- Late deliveries caused by recurrent traffic hotspots
- High idling/fuel burn in congestion
- Overtime caused by static plans becoming infeasible during execution

**Underlying root cause**
- Static route plans and fixed ETA assumptions do not reflect actual road conditions by time of day.

**How process mining supports it**
- Temporal conformance shows where planned travel times are consistently optimistic.
- Geospatial hotspot analysis shows recurring low-speed corridors.
- Variant analysis may reveal that drivers who deviate from the planned path in specific ways achieve better punctuality.

**Action**
- Replace static travel-time assumptions with empirical, time-of-day-specific travel profiles learned from the log.
- Recalculate ETAs during the route when predicted lateness crosses a threshold.
- Reoptimize the *remaining* stops only, within constraints:
  - customer windows
  - driver hours
  - vehicle capacity / load accessibility
- Feed ETA updates to dispatch and customers.

**Expected KPI effect**
- On-Time Delivery Rate: **up**
- ETA Accuracy: **up**
- Traffic Delay Minutes: **down**
- Overtime Rate: **down**
- Fuel per successful package: **down**

---

### Strategy 2: Redesign delivery territories, route templates, and workload balancing

**Targets**
- Overloaded routes
- Uneven route duration
- Excess km and cross-territory overlap
- Routes that become late after a certain stop number

**Underlying root cause**
- Route planning likely uses coarse assumptions such as stop count or static geography, rather than actual workload:
  - real travel times
  - parking friction
  - service-time variability
  - time-window pressure
  - route density

**How process mining supports it**
- Route family analysis shows which planned route patterns are chronically late or expensive.
- Delay propagation by stop position shows where routes become infeasible.
- Geospatial overlays reveal overlapping vehicle footprints or poor territory boundaries.
- Capacity utilization analysis shows under- and over-loaded routes.

**Action**
- Build route templates using actual historical execution data.
- Define a **workload score** per route, e.g.:
  - expected travel time
  - expected service time
  - parking/access friction
  - time-window pressure
  - variability buffer
- Redesign territories to reduce overlap and better reflect true road-network travel.
- Consider different templates for:
  - dense urban
  - mixed
  - suburban
  - business-heavy vs residential-heavy routes
- Adjust departure waves for congestion-heavy zones.

**Expected KPI effect**
- Route Overrun / Overtime: **down**
- Detour Factor / km per route: **down**
- Vehicle and capacity utilization balance: **up**
- On-Time Delivery Rate: **up**
- Route duration variability: **down**

---

### Strategy 3: Improve time-window management and customer communication to reduce failed deliveries

**Targets**
- Failed delivery attempts
- Re-delivery loops
- Wasted fuel/time on customer-not-home events
- Customer complaints due unreliable arrival timing

**Underlying root cause**
- Customer availability is not aligned with actual route execution.
- Time windows may be too aggressive in unreliable zones.
- Customers may not receive sufficiently accurate ETAs or rescheduling options.

**How process mining supports it**
- Package-level mining shows which failure branches are most common.
- Failure rates can be linked to:
  - time of day
  - customer type
  - route position
  - lateness vs promised ETA
  - specific buildings/addresses
- Repeated failed-delivery patterns identify candidates for alternate fulfillment options.

**Action**
- Use historical process data to predict failed-delivery risk before dispatch.
- For high-risk stops:
  - send narrower ETA updates
  - ask for confirmation
  - offer rebooking
  - offer locker / pickup point / safe-place option
- Re-sequence risky stops earlier when feasible.
- Reconsider promised windows in low-reliability zones; do not promise precision the network cannot consistently deliver.

**Expected KPI effect**
- First-Attempt Success Rate: **up**
- Failed Delivery Rate: **down**
- Re-delivery workload: **down**
- Fuel per successful package: **down**
- On-Time Delivery Rate and customer satisfaction: **up**

---

### Strategy 4: Predictive maintenance and route-aware vehicle assignment

**Targets**
- Unscheduled vehicle stops
- Mid-route breakdowns
- Reactive maintenance cost
- Lost operating time due preventable failures

**Underlying root cause**
- Maintenance is often calendar- or mileage-based, while last-mile wear depends heavily on:
  - stop-start intensity
  - idling
  - urban driving conditions
  - warning-event history

**How process mining supports it**
- Object-centric analysis links vehicle incidents to route outcomes.
- Usage patterns from logs can reveal which duty cycles correlate with breakdowns.
- Maintenance history shows which vehicles repeatedly create exception traces.

**Action**
- Create a vehicle risk score using:
  - recent warning events
  - mileage
  - stop count
  - idle ratio
  - repair history
  - route intensity
- Schedule maintenance proactively during low-demand windows.
- Assign higher-risk vehicles to less critical/shorter routes until serviced.
- Reserve most reliable vans for routes with tight customer windows.

**Expected KPI effect**
- Breakdown / Unscheduled Stop Rate: **down**
- Maintenance Downtime (unscheduled): **down**
- On-Time Delivery Rate: **up**
- Vehicle availability reliability: **up**
- Total maintenance cost volatility: **down**

Note: proactive scheduled downtime may increase slightly, but that is usually a good trade if unscheduled disruption falls.

---

### Strategy 5: Targeted driver coaching and standardized stop execution

**Targets**
- Excessive idling
- Long stop times
- Poor route adherence where harmful
- Fuel waste and avoidable wear

**Underlying root cause**
- Execution practices differ between drivers:
  - parking strategy
  - scanner discipline
  - parcel pre-sorting
  - customer interaction handling
  - eco-driving behavior

**How process mining supports it**
- Variant analysis identifies best-performing route execution patterns.
- Peer comparisons within similar route clusters show fair driver differences.
- Stop-level analysis can distinguish:
  - long parking search
  - long customer interaction
  - long post-delivery admin

**Action**
- Build coaching around concrete behaviors:
  - reduce unnecessary idling
  - improve parking/approach patterns
  - pre-sort parcels for next stops
  - use scanner consistently
  - standardize failure-handling procedure
  - reinforce eco-driving where data supports it
- Use top-quartile variants as the “standard work” reference.
- Make the program supportive, not punitive.

**Expected KPI effect**
- Idle Time Ratio: **down**
- Average Stop Service Time: **down**
- Fuel per km/package: **down**
- Vehicle wear and maintenance burden: **down**
- On-Time Delivery Rate consistency: **up**

---

### How I would roll these out
I would not implement all at once. I would:
1. start with the discovery + KPI baseline
2. pick one depot or route family as a pilot
3. test 2-3 interventions
4. compare against matched control routes
5. scale what works

That avoids confusing seasonal changes with actual improvement.

## 5. Considering Operational Constraints and Continuous Monitoring

### 5.1 How the strategies should account for operational constraints

All optimization proposals must be implemented as **constraint-aware planning**, not as pure efficiency chasing.

#### A. Driver working hours and breaks
Any route redesign or dynamic rerouting must respect:
- shift limits
- break rules
- overtime policies
- safe driving expectations

In practice:
- if predicted route completion breaches legal hours, trigger dispatch intervention early
- do not “solve” lateness by shifting the burden into unsafe overtime

#### B. Vehicle capacity and load accessibility
Routing changes must respect:
- parcel count / cubic capacity / weight if available
- how the van is loaded
- whether re-sequencing is physically practical without digging through the van

A mathematically better stop order is not operationally better if the parcel is buried under the rest of the load.

#### C. Customer time windows
Dynamic changes must preserve:
- committed customer windows
- business opening hours
- any premium delivery promises

Also:
- use empirical variability from process mining to build realistic buffers
- in tight, unreliable zones, plan with percentile-based travel times rather than optimistic averages

#### D. Fleet availability / maintenance windows
Predictive maintenance should be coordinated with:
- spare vehicle availability
- peak demand periods
- critical route days

#### E. Fairness and route stability
Driver comparisons and reassignment should account for:
- route familiarity
- territory knowledge
- complexity differences

Too-frequent route changes can reduce execution quality even if the model says they are optimal.

---

### 5.2 Continuous monitoring plan using process mining dashboards

After implementation, I would set up a **process mining control tower** with role-based dashboards.

#### A. Daily operational dashboard
Used by dispatch and operations supervisors:
- routes predicted to miss time windows
- routes departed late from depot
- active unplanned stops / vehicle warnings
- live conformance deviations
- ETA accuracy trend
- congestion hotspots today vs historical pattern

#### B. Weekly tactical improvement dashboard
Used by route planners and continuous improvement teams:
- On-Time Delivery Rate by route family, zone, departure band
- top bottlenecks by excess minutes and cost
- actual vs planned sequence adherence
- P50/P90/P95 travel time and stop service time
- failed-delivery hotspots
- driver/vehicle normalized performance
- territory overlap / detour trends

#### C. Monthly fleet and strategy dashboard
Used by management:
- fuel per successful package
- overtime rate
- breakdown rate and maintenance downtime
- vehicle utilization and capacity utilization
- first-attempt success
- cost per successful delivery
- pre/post impact of each intervention

---

### 5.3 Process views I would track continuously

1. **Performance-annotated route process map**
   - Where time is spent end-to-end

2. **Conformance heatmap**
   - Which routes, drivers, or vehicles deviate most from plan
   - Which deviations are harmful vs adaptive

3. **Geospatial hotspot map**
   - traffic delay
   - parking search
   - failed deliveries
   - breakdown locations

4. **Stop-time decomposition view**
   - parking/approach
   - customer interaction
   - admin/post-delivery delay

5. **Package rework funnel**
   - first attempt -> failed -> reattempt -> delivered
   - helps quantify hidden waste

6. **Fleet-health process view**
   - warning events
   - unscheduled stops
   - unscheduled repair chains
   - vehicle risk score

7. **Variant trend / process drift view**
   - detects new patterns:
     - roadworks
     - seasonal traffic shifts
     - new route inefficiencies
     - aging vehicle effects

---

### 5.4 Metrics to watch as leading indicators

In addition to lagging KPIs, I would track **early warning indicators**:
- depot departure delay
- predicted lateness after stop 5 or stop 10
- rising idle ratio on a route
- increasing parking/approach time in a zone
- spike in `Delivery Failed - Customer Not Home`
- increased warning events on specific vehicles
- drop in scanner compliance / data completeness

These allow intervention before customer complaints or cost overruns appear in monthly reports.

---

### 5.5 Governance and sustainability

To ensure improvements are sustainable:
- compare against a fixed baseline and matched control group
- adjust for seasonality and parcel volume changes
- track distributions, not just averages
- use **P90/P95** metrics because customers feel the tail of performance
- monitor for unintended trade-offs:
  - on-time improves but fuel worsens
  - fuel improves but failed deliveries rise
  - capacity utilization rises but overtime spikes

Process mining should become a **continuous improvement loop**:

**Discover -> Check conformance -> Find bottlenecks -> Test intervention -> Monitor drift -> Recalibrate**

---

## Final takeaway

For Speedy Parcels, process mining can do much more than show that deliveries are late. It can separate:
- **bad planning** from **bad execution**
- **recurrent traffic issues** from **vehicle reliability problems**
- **customer-related failures** from **route design problems**

That leads to targeted actions: smarter routing, better territory design, fewer failed deliveries, predictive maintenance, and fairer driver coaching. If implemented with proper constraints and continuous monitoring, this should improve both **delivery punctuality** and **cost efficiency** in a measurable, sustainable way.
