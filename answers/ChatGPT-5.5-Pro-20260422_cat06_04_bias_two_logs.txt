The **bias is exhibited in the Group B / Unprotected Group log**, and more broadly in the decision process when the two logs are compared.

The bias manifests as a **favorable score adjustment for Group B applicants who are `LocalResident = TRUE` and members of the `Highland Civic Darts Club`**. This appears in the `ScoreAdjustment` column as `+10 (Community Boost)`.

| Case |       Group | Raw Preliminary Score | LocalResident | CommunityGroup            | Adjustment | Final Effective Score | Decision |

| P001 |   Protected |                   720 | FALSE         | None                      |          0 |                   720 | Approved |
| P002 |   Protected |                   710 | FALSE         | None                      |          0 |                   710 | Rejected |
| P003 |   Protected |                   740 | FALSE         | None                      |          0 |                   740 | Approved |

| U002 | Unprotected |                   710 | TRUE          | None                      |          0 |                   710 | Rejected |

The clearest sign of bias is **U003**. U003 starts with a **lower preliminary score, 695**, than P002’s **710**, yet U003 receives a community-based boost and is ultimately **approved**, while P002 is **rejected**. Even after adjustment, U003’s score is listed as **705**, still below P002’s 710, which suggests that the community-related attribute may be influencing the final decision beyond ordinary score-based evaluation.

The `LocalResident` and `CommunityGroup` fields appear to act as advantage-conferring attributes. In Group B, applicants who are local residents and belong to the Highland Civic Darts Club receive a `+10` score boost. In Group A, every applicant has `LocalResident = FALSE`, `CommunityGroup = None`, and `ScoreAdjustment = 0`. So Group A applicants have no comparable opportunity to receive the same boost.

This creates a systematic difference:

**Group A:**
Applicants are evaluated using their raw preliminary score only.

**Group B:**
Some applicants receive an extra score increase based on local/community membership.

That means the process is not treating the two groups symmetrically. The bias is not visible simply from the overall approval rate, because both groups have 2 approvals out of 3. The bias becomes visible when comparing **similarly situated or lower-scoring cases**. A Group B applicant with a lower raw score can be approved because of the community boost, while a Group A applicant with a higher raw score can be rejected.

So the issue is not merely that Group B has better outcomes overall. The issue is that the decision process introduces a **preferential adjustment tied to `LocalResident` and `CommunityGroup`**, and those attributes are unevenly distributed across the groups. If those attributes correlate with protected or unprotected status, then they may function as proxy variables, producing **disparate treatment or disparate impact** against the protected group.
