# Analysis of the Two POWL Models

## The Normative Hire-to-Retire Process

The standard sequence is:

**Post_Job_Ad  Screen_Candidates  Conduct_Interviews  Make_Hiring_Decision  Onboard_Employee  Add_to_Payroll  Close_Case**

Each activity should occur exactly once, in this order, since each step logically depends on the previous one (you cannot decide on a hire before interviewing, and a hired employee must be onboarded and paid).

---

## Anomalies in Model 1

The order relation is: Post  Screen  {Decide, Interview}, Decide  Onboard  Payroll  Close.

1. **Interviews are concurrent with (not before) the hiring decision.** Both `Decide` and `Interview` follow `Screen`, but there is no edge `Interview  Decide`. The hiring decision may therefore be made **before or in parallel with** the interviews — a logical violation, since the decision should be informed by interview results.
2. **Interview is "dangling."** `Interview` has no outgoing edges, so it can in principle be executed at any point after screening — even after onboarding, payroll, or case closure. Interviewing someone who is already hired and on payroll makes no business sense.

**Severity:** A genuine ordering flaw, but note that **all seven activities are still mandatory and executed exactly once**. The model guarantees that the employee is decided upon, onboarded, paid, and the case closed.

---

## Anomalies in Model 2

The order relation is: Post  {Screen, Interview}, Interview  Decide  loop(Onboard)  XOR(Payroll, skip)  Close.

1. **Screening is concurrent with interviewing and unanchored to the decision.** `Screen` has no outgoing edges, so candidates may be screened in parallel with — or even *after* — interviews, the hiring decision, onboarding, or case closure. Screening should filter candidates *before* interviews.
2. **Onboarding is in a loop.** `*(Onboard, skip)` means onboarding executes at least once but may **repeat arbitrarily many times**. Repeated onboarding of the same employee is a deviation from good practice (though at least it is guaranteed to happen once).
3. **Payroll is optional.** `X(Payroll, skip)` allows the process to **skip Add_to_Payroll entirely**. This is a severe, fundamental violation: an employee could be hired and onboarded but never added to payroll — a compliance and legal failure that defeats the purpose of the process.

---

## Conclusion: Which Model Is Closer to the Norm?

**Model 1 is closer to the normative Hire-to-Retire process.**

**Justification:**

- **Model 1's anomalies are ordering weaknesses, not existential ones.** Every mandatory activity (including payroll) is guaranteed to occur exactly once. The only flaw is that interviews are not enforced before the decision — undesirable, but the core hiring chain (Decision  Onboarding  Payroll  Closure) remains intact and complete.
- **Model 2 contains a fundamental integrity violation:** the XOR around payroll means the process can complete with a hired employee who is **never paid**. This breaks an essential obligation of the process, regardless of execution order. On top of that, Model 2 also has an ordering flaw symmetric to Model 1's (screening unanchored to the decision) *plus* the questionable onboarding loop.

In short, Model 1 deviates in *when* things may happen, while Model 2 additionally deviates in *whether* critical things happen at all. Guaranteeing the completeness of mandatory activities outweighs a single concurrency/ordering anomaly, so **Model 1 better preserves the correctness and integrity of the Hire-to-Retire process**.
