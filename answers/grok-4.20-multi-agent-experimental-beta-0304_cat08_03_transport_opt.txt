**1. Process Discovery and Conformance Checking**

To create a cohesive event log suitable for process mining, the primary case notion would be ōVehicle-Dayö (e.g., V12-20241205), which naturally captures an end-to-end daily delivery tour while allowing linkage to individual packages or stops for finer-grained analysis. Events from the four sources would be unified by standardizing timestamps, deriving consistent activity labels (e.g., ōDepart Depotö, ōTravel Segmentö, ōArrive Customerö, ōDelivery Successö, ōDelivery Failedö, ōUnscheduled Stop/Maintenanceö, ōArrive Depotö, ōEnd Shiftö), and enriching records with attributes such as driver ID, location (lat/lon), speed, planned vs. actual stop sequence, and package details where available. 

Integration steps include:
- Using timestamp proximity and shared identifiers (Vehicle ID, Driver ID) to correlate scanner events with GPS readings (e.g., speed=0 + location match  service time).
- Deriving higher-level activities from high-frequency GPS data (e.g., aggregating low-speed periods into ōTraffic Delayö or ōIdleö events).
- Importing planned route sequences and time windows directly from the dispatch system as case or event attributes.
- Inserting maintenance events into the trace at the appropriate timestamps.

Challenges include mismatched granularity (GPS streams vs. discrete scanner/dispatch events), noisy or inaccurate GPS data (signal loss, brief stops misclassified), incomplete correlation across systems, defining precise start and end markers for cases, handling parallel or multi-package stops, and dealing with concept drift over six months (seasonal traffic, new customers). Tools like Python (pm4py, pandas) or ETL platforms would be used to clean, filter, and export an XES or CSV event log.

Process discovery would apply the Inductive Miner (for sound, block-structured models) or Heuristics Miner (robust to noisy logistics data) to visualize the *actual* end-to-end process. The resulting model (BPMN or process map) would reveal the dominant flow: Start Shift  Route Assigned  Depart Depot  repeated (Travel  Arrive Customer  Service [Success/Failed]  Depart)  possible loops for failed deliveries or returns to depot  Unscheduled Stop/Maintenance  Arrive Depot  End Shift. Frequency and variant information would highlight common deviations such as extra idle time, unplanned stops, or overtime.

Conformance checking would use the planned routes and schedules from the dispatch system as a reference model (converted to a Petri net or BPMN). Techniques such as token-based replay or alignments would measure fitness, precision, and generalization. Key deviations to detect include: sequence/order violations (stops executed in different order than planned), insertions (unplanned maintenance, extra idle, or detours visible in GPS), skips (missed stops leading to overtime), and performance/timing deviations (travel or service times significantly exceeding planned estimates). These insights directly quantify how much the real operation deviates from the intended process.

**2. Performance Analysis and Bottleneck Identification**

Relevant KPIs derivable from the enriched event log include:
- On-Time Delivery (OTD) Rate: percentage of ōDelivery Successö events whose timestamp falls within the customer time window from the dispatch system.
- Average Service Time per Stop: duration between ōArrive Customerö and ōDepart Customerö.
- Travel Time vs. Service Time ratio: aggregated travel segments (derived from GPS movement between stops) versus service/idle time.
- Fuel/Idle proxy: total time spent at low speed (<10 km/h) or idle (speed=0, ignition on) per km or per package.
- Vehicle Utilization Rate: proportion of shift time spent moving or servicing versus total shift duration (from ōStart Shiftö to ōEnd Shiftö).
- Frequency and Duration of Traffic Delays: count and length of ōLow Speed Detectedö periods, grouped by time of day or location.
- Failed Delivery Rate and Rework Impact: percentage of failed attempts and extra time added to the tour.

These are calculated by computing durations between paired events, aggregating at case or event level, and slicing by attributes (driver, vehicle, location cluster, time of day, day of week) using process mining software.

Bottleneck identification would leverage performance-enhanced process maps (arcs and activities colored or annotated by average duration or waiting time), bottleneck analysis algorithms that highlight transitions with the highest cumulative delay, dotted charts to reveal temporal patterns (e.g., morning peak congestion), and attribute-based filtering/clustering. Bottlenecks could be localized to specific route segments, urban hotspots (repeated low-speed zones near certain coordinates indicating parking or traffic issues), times of day (rush-hour effects), individual drivers (higher service times suggesting training needs), or activities (long dwell after failed deliveries). Impact is quantified by the additional minutes or hours added to total tour duration and their correlation with OTD drops or fuel use increases.

**3. Root Cause Analysis for Inefficiencies**

Process mining moves beyond ōwhereö to ōwhyö through variant analysis, attribute correlation, and decision mining. Variant analysis compares high-performing vs. low-performing cases (grouped by OTD, total duration, or fuel use) to identify distinguishing paths Ś e.g., routes that frequently deviate from planned order or include extra loops after failed deliveries. Statistical tests on subsets of the log can confirm significance.

Specific root causes and validation approaches:
- Suboptimal/static route planning vs. dynamic needs: compare planned vs. actual travel times per segment; frequent large deviations at certain times/locations indicate inaccurate estimations.
- Traffic congestion patterns: cluster GPS low-speed events by location and time-of-day; correlate with delay durations to discover recurring hotspots or weekday patterns.
- High variability in service time: analyze dwell-time distributions per customer, package type, or location (parking difficulty in dense areas). Long tails may link to customer interaction or finding parking.
- Vehicle breakdowns/maintenance during shifts: examine frequency of ōUnscheduled Stopö or ōEngine Warningö events, correlate with cumulative mileage, idle time, or vehicle age from maintenance logs.
- Driver behavior/skill differences: filter traces by Driver ID and compare performance KPIs and variant frequency; social network or handover analysis can reveal collaboration patterns or outliers.
- Failed delivery impact: trace the downstream effect of ōDelivery Failedö events on total tour time, extra travel, and overtime; identify preceding factors (arrival outside optimal window).

Decision trees or rule mining on delay-causing attributes (time of day, location cluster, driver, weather if added) can provide interpretable root-cause models. These analyses validate hypotheses directly from the integrated event data.

**4. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies**

**Strategy 1: Dynamic routing with discovered traffic hotspots and real-time adjustments.**  
This targets travel delays and inaccurate travel time estimations. The root cause is reliance on static routes that ignore recurring congestion patterns revealed by GPS low-speed clusters. Process mining insights (performance maps showing delay hotspots by time-of-day and location, conformance deviations in travel segments) support feeding historical patterns into routing algorithms (e.g., time-dependent travel matrices) and enabling real-time re-sequencing when delays are detected. Expected impact: higher OTD rate, reduced travel time and fuel consumption, improved utilization by cutting idle time in traffic.

**Strategy 2: Predictive maintenance scheduling based on usage and precursor patterns.**  
This addresses unscheduled stops and breakdowns during delivery tours. Root cause is reactive maintenance ignoring usage-based wear detectable in the logs (high mileage/idle before warning events). PM-derived patterns (frequency of unscheduled stops correlated with cumulative distance, speed profiles, or prior maintenance) enable building predictive models that schedule downtime outside peak hours. Expected impact: lower frequency of in-shift disruptions, reduced maintenance costs, higher vehicle utilization and OTD by preventing mid-route failures.

**Strategy 3: Optimized customer time windows and proactive communication to reduce failed deliveries.**  
This targets high failed-delivery rates and subsequent rework travel. Root cause is mismatch between static windows, actual arrival times, and customer availability patterns. Process mining (variant analysis of traces with vs. without failures, dwell-time and failure correlation with arrival time/location) identifies optimal windows per zone or customer segment and flags high-risk stops for pre-delivery SMS or rescheduling. Expected impact: lower failed delivery rate, reduced total tour time and overtime, improved OTD and fuel efficiency by minimizing return visits.

**5. Considering Operational Constraints and Monitoring**

All strategies must respect hard constraints visible in the data: maximum driver working hours (derived from ōStart Shiftö to ōEnd Shiftö and overtime flags), vehicle capacity and package load from dispatch records, and customer time windows. Optimization models (routing algorithms, scheduling) would incorporate these as constraints or soft penalties, using the historical event log for what-if simulation and validation that new plans remain feasible and improve KPIs without creating new deviations (e.g., excessive overtime).

For continuous monitoring, implement a process mining dashboard (e.g., in Celonis, Disco, or pm4py-based custom view) that refreshes weekly or daily. Key elements to track: overall conformance fitness score against the target model, trend lines for core KPIs (OTD rate, average tour duration, failed delivery rate, fuel/idle proxies), updated process maps showing emerging variants or new bottlenecks, and drill-down views by driver, vehicle, route zone, or time period. Automated alerts for drops below thresholds, significant new deviations, or KPI regression would enable quick intervention. Periodic re-discovery of the process model ensures the organization can detect whether improvements are sustained or if new issues (e.g., after seasonal changes) arise, closing the continuous improvement loop.

This comprehensive process mining approach transforms the multi-source event data into actionable, quantifiable insights tailored to last-mile logistics realities.
