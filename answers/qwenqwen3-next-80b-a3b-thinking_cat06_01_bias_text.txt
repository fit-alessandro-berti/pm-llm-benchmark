**No.** Here’s why:  

#### **1. Ethical Failures**
- **Lack of transparency**: Applicants have no way to know their application was influenced by unspoken "community integration" criteria. Fair lending requires clear, understandable reasons for decisions (e.g., "Your debt-to-income ratio is 45%, exceeding our threshold"). Hiding the true basis for adjustments violates **procedural justice**.

- **Reinforcing privilege**: The policy assumes "community ties" are universally accessible—but they are not. For example:  
  - A single parent working two jobs may lack time to join a darts club.  
  - A new resident may not know about the club or be invited to join.  
  - Low-income individuals may not afford membership fees (e.g., $500/year).

#### **2. Legal Risks**

- The CFPB and DOJ have repeatedly prosecuted lenders for "neutral" policies with disparate impacts (e.g., using zip codes to price loans, which correlates with race). Argentum’s policy risks similar scrutiny.

- **Inefficient risk assessment**: Basing decisions on unproven correlations (e.g., "darts players are more reliable") ignores actual financial data. A borrower with a 700 credit score and stable income is objectively less risky than a darts club member with a 600 score and erratic employment.

### **Why This Matters: The Broader Implications**
- **Equity vs. equality**: This policy treats applicants unequally based on factors outside their control (e.g., where they live, social connections). True equity would require assessing **individual financial behavior**, not group-based assumptions.  
- **Systemic harm**: Over time, such practices perpetuate cycles of disadvantage. For example:

  - Meanwhile, privileged applicants gain advantages that compound generational wealth.
