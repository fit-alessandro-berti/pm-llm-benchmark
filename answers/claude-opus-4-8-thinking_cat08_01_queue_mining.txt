# Queue Mining for Outpatient Clinic Flow Optimization: A Data-Driven Approach

## 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

### Defining and Calculating Waiting Time

In an event log with both START and COMPLETE timestamps, we can cleanly separate two distinct time components for any activity:

- **Service Time (processing time):** `COMPLETE(Activity_n)  START(Activity_n)` — the time a resource actively works on the patient.
- **Waiting Time (queue time):** `START(Activity_n+1)  COMPLETE(Activity_n)` — the idle interval where the patient has finished one stage but has not yet begun the next.

The waiting time is the inter-activity gap. For patient V1001:
- Wait for Nurse = `09:15:20  09:08:45` = **6m 35s**
- Wait for Doctor = `09:45:55  09:25:10` = **20m 45s**
- Wait for ECG = `10:22:15  10:10:30` = **11m 45s**
- Wait for Check-out = `10:38:00  10:30:05` = **7m 55s**

A critical preliminary step is **handover-of-work validation**: I would distinguish *true queueing* (patient ready, resource unavailable) from *non-queue delays* such as planned breaks, patient-caused delays (e.g., a patient stepping out), or transport time between rooms. Where possible, I'd enrich the analysis with the resource's own activity log to confirm whether the next resource was busy with another case during that gap—this is the core insight of queue mining (e.g., the techniques of Senderovich et al.): a wait is a *resource-contention* wait if the downstream resource was occupied during the gap.

### Key Characterization Metrics

For each transition (queue point) between consecutive activities, I would compute:

| Metric | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Mean waiting time** | Overall magnitude, but sensitive to outliers |
| **Median (P50)** | Typical patient experience, robust to skew |
| **P90 / P95 waiting time** | Captures the "tail" — worst-case experiences that drive dissatisfaction |
| **Maximum waiting time** | Identifies extreme failures |
| **Standard deviation / IQR** | Variability — high variance signals unpredictable, frustrating waits |
| **Queue frequency** | Number/percentage of cases passing through this transition |
| **Cases exceeding threshold** | e.g., count of patients waiting >30 min — directly tied to satisfaction |
| **Total accumulated wait** (freq × avg) | The *aggregate burden* on the patient population |
| **Time-of-day / day-of-week profile** | Reveals temporal congestion patterns |

Waiting-time distributions in healthcare are **right-skewed**, so I would never rely on the mean alone. The median tells the typical story; the P90 tells the pain story.

### Identifying the Most Critical Queues

I would prioritize using a **weighted, multi-criteria scoring** rather than a single metric, because a queue that is long but rare matters less than one that is moderately long but affects every patient. My criteria:

1. **Aggregate impact** = `frequency × mean wait` — this is the total person-hours of waiting a queue generates and is the single best lever for system-wide improvement.
2. **Tail severity (P90)** — queues with extreme tails are the primary drivers of complaints, even if averages look acceptable.
3. **Equity / vulnerability impact** — queues disproportionately affecting urgent patients or specific patient types warrant priority on clinical-safety grounds.
4. **Variability** — high-variance queues undermine the ability to set patient expectations and schedule downstream.

**Practical approach:** Build a Pareto chart of aggregate waiting time per transition. Typically 2–3 transitions account for ~70–80% of total waiting. Based on the scenario's structure, I would hypothesize the **Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation** transition (specialist availability) and **Doctor  Diagnostic Test** transitions (shared equipment/rooms) as prime suspects — to be confirmed by data.

## 2. Root Cause Analysis

Identifying *where* queues form is only the diagnosis; we need the *why*. I would layer the following process mining techniques onto the queue findings.

### Potential Root Causes and Diagnostic Techniques

**a) Resource Bottlenecks (staff/room/equipment)**
- **Technique:** Resource utilization analysis. By summing each resource's busy time ( service times) against their available time per shift, I compute utilization. Resources running consistently >85% utilization are bottlenecks — queues will form upstream of them.
- **Indicator:** If the wait before "Doctor Consultation (Cardio)" correlates with Dr. Smith's utilization being near-saturated, that's a clear capacity constraint.
- **Resource Behavior Effects:** Analyze whether service times slow under high load ("fatigue/overload effect") or whether resources batch work.

**b) Activity Dependencies and Handovers**
- **Technique:** Social network / handover-of-work analysis. Maps how cases flow between resources. Reveals coordination breakdowns — e.g., a nurse must "release" a patient before a doctor is notified, with manual paging causing dead time.
- **Indicator:** Consistent fixed-duration gaps suggest communication/notification latency rather than capacity.

**c) Service-Time Variability**
- **Technique:** Distribution analysis of service times per activity, segmented by resource and patient type. High variability propagates downstream and inflates queues (a core queuing-theory insight: queue length grows with the *square* of variability per Kingman's formula).
- **Indicator:** "New" patient consultations may take 25 min vs. 12 min for follow-ups — if scheduled in fixed slots, the variance creates cascading delays.

**d) Appointment Scheduling Policy**
- **Technique:** Compare scheduled vs. actual start times; analyze arrival-time clustering. Block scheduling (e.g., all patients booked at the top of the hour) creates artificial demand spikes.
- **Indicator:** A sawtooth pattern in waiting time across the day, peaking after each scheduling block.

**e) Patient Arrival Patterns**
- **Technique:** Arrival-rate analysis by hour/day overlaid on capacity. Mismatch between demand curve and staffing curve is a classic root cause.
- **Indicator:** Early-morning and post-lunch surges exceeding registration/nurse capacity.

**f) Patient-Type / Urgency Differences**
- **Technique:** **Variant analysis** — segment the process by Patient Type and Urgency, then compare process maps and waiting times across cohorts. Discover whether "New" patients follow longer paths (more tests) or whether "Urgent" cases jump queues (creating delays for Normal cases).
- **Indicator:** Performance-based variant comparison shows New patients average 40% longer total visit time, concentrated at specific transitions.

### Integrative Diagnostic Workflow
I would discover the end-to-end process model (e.g., via inductive miner), enrich it with **performance overlays** (color-coded waiting times on each arc), then drill down on the critical arcs using resource and variant analyses. This separates *capacity* problems (need more/better-scheduled resources) from *flow* problems (handover/coordination) from *demand* problems (scheduling/arrivals) — each requiring a different remedy.

## 3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

### Strategy 1: Demand-Smoothing via Appointment Slot Redesign

- **Targets:** Registration and Nurse Assessment queues; the early-morning/post-lunch surge.
- **Root cause addressed:** Block scheduling causing artificial arrival spikes (demand-capacity mismatch).
- **Data support:** Arrival-pattern analysis showing demand peaks far exceeding capacity at specific intervals, with waiting time correlating to arrival clustering. Service-time distributions inform optimal slot length.
- **Mechanism:** Replace top-of-hour block booking with **staggered, variability-aware slots** — assign longer slots to "New" patients (whose service times the data shows are longer/more variable) and shorter slots to "Follow-ups." Smooth arrivals across the hour.
- **Expected impact:** Smoothing arrivals typically reduces front-end waiting by 20–35% by eliminating transient overload, without adding staff. Reduced variance also tightens the P90 tail.

### Strategy 2: Targeted Capacity Reallocation at the Bottleneck Resource

- **Targets:** The NurseDoctor and DoctorTest transitions (assuming utilization analysis confirms specialist/equipment saturation).
- **Root cause addressed:** Resource bottleneck (utilization >85%).
- **Data support:** Utilization analysis isolating *when* and *which* resources saturate. Critically, the analysis may show the bottleneck is **time-localized** (e.g., only 10–12am), meaning the solution is reallocation, not net new hires — controlling cost.
- **Mechanism:** Flex-staff — shift a nurse or open an additional consultation room/diagnostic slot precisely during the identified peak window. Cross-train staff for flexible deployment. For shared equipment (ECG/X-ray), introduce **dynamic slot reservation** so downstream tests are pre-booked when the doctor consultation begins.
- **Expected impact:** Relieving a saturated resource during peak windows can cut the upstream queue's P90 dramatically (queue length is hyper-sensitive near 100% utilization). Targeted reallocation avoids the cost of permanent headcount increases.

### Strategy 3: Parallelization and Flow Redesign (Early Diagnostics / Coordinated Handover)

- **Targets:** DoctorTest and TestCheck-out transitions; total visit duration.
- **Root cause addressed:** Sequential dependency and handover latency.
- **Data support:** Variant analysis showing predictable test patterns (e.g., 80% of Cardio New patients receive an ECG). Handover analysis quantifying notification dead-time between stages.
- **Mechanism:**
  - **Predictive early ordering:** Where the data shows a test is highly likely for a given patient type/specialty, trigger the order at *Nurse Assessment* (protocol-driven standing orders) so the test can occur in parallel or immediately, eliminating the doctor-decision-to-test wait.
  - **Coordinated handover:** Replace manual paging with an electronic status board/notification that flags a patient as "ready for next stage," eliminating fixed dead-time gaps identified in the handover analysis.
- **Expected impact:** Parallelizing diagnostics can compress total visit duration by 15–25% for affected pathways; eliminating notification latency removes consistent fixed gaps (often 5–15 min each) at every handover.

*(All three together attack demand, capacity, and flow respectively — a balanced portfolio rather than a single point fix.)*

## 4. Trade-offs and Constraints

No optimization is free; each carries risks I would explicitly model and monitor.

**Bottleneck migration (the universal risk):** Relieving the doctor queue may simply push the congestion downstream to diagnostics or check-out. Queuing systems behave as connected networks — I would use the discovered process model to **simulate** (via a data-driven discrete-event simulation calibrated on the log) the system-wide effect *before* implementation, ensuring we relocate rather than merely move the problem.

**Strategy 1 (Scheduling) trade-offs:** Staggered/longer slots for New patients may *reduce total daily throughput* (fewer patients per day) or under-utilize resources if no-shows occur. There's a tension between smooth flow and maximizing volume. Mitigation: model the throughput-vs-wait curve and choose the slot mix at the efficient frontier; build in overbooking calibrated to historical no-show rates.

**Strategy 2 (Reallocation) trade-offs:** Flexing staff to peaks may create *trough-period underutilization*, increase staff stress, or risk burnout if pushed too hard. Cross-training has upfront cost and a competency-quality risk. I'd cap utilization targets at a sustainable level (~80–85%) rather than chasing 100%, accepting some slack as a buffer against variability.

**Strategy 3 (Early ordering) trade-offs — the most clinically sensitive:** Protocol-driven standing orders risk **over-testing** (unnecessary tests, cost, patient burden, and potential clinical harm from incidental findings) if the test ultimately wasn't needed. This directly conflicts with care-quality and cost goals. Mitigation: restrict standing orders to *high-probability, low-risk, low-cost* tests confirmed by variant data, and require clinician override capability. Quality must not be traded for speed.

**Balancing conflicting objectives:** I would frame this as **multi-objective optimization**, not single-metric minimization. Concretely:
- Establish **guardrail metrics** that cannot degrade (e.g., diagnostic-error rate, consultation duration as a proxy for thoroughness, cost per visit).
- Use the simulation model to plot the **Pareto frontier** of wait-time vs. cost vs. throughput, and let management choose the trade-off point aligned with their stated constraint ("without significantly increasing costs").
- Pilot changes on a single specialty/clinic-day before scaling (A/B style), measuring both efficiency and quality guardrails.

## 5. Measuring Success

### Key Performance Indicators

**Primary (efficiency) KPIs:**
- Average and **P90 waiting time per critical transition** (the same metrics that defined the baseline — ensures apples-to-apples comparison).
- **Total visit duration** (door-to-door): COMPLETE(Check-out)  START(Registration), and its **waiting fraction** (total wait ÷ total duration).
- **Percentage of patients waiting beyond threshold** (e.g., >30 min at any stage).
- **Throughput:** patients completed per day.

**Guardrail (quality & cost) KPIs:**
- Average consultation/service time (ensure speed isn't compromising thoroughness).
- Diagnostic test volume per patient type (detect over-testing from Strategy 3).
- Resource utilization (ensure within sustainable 80–85% band — neither overloaded nor idle).
- Cost per visit / overtime hours.

**Experience KPIs:**
- Patient satisfaction scores (linked to wait-time data by visit).
- Waiting-time **predictability** (variance/IQR) — important since predictable waits are tolerated better than erratic ones.

### Ongoing Process Monitoring

The beauty of this approach is that the **same event-log structure powers continuous monitoring** — no new data infrastructure needed.

1. **Automated dashboards:** Build a live process-mining dashboard recalculating queue metrics weekly, with the process map performance-overlay updated automatically. Stakeholders see the critical-transition KPIs at a glance.

2. **Statistical control & before/after validation:** Apply control charts (e.g., on weekly P90 waits) to distinguish genuine improvement from normal variation. Run statistical tests comparing pre- vs. post-implementation distributions, controlling for confounders (seasonality, patient-mix shifts via variant segmentation).

3. **Concept-drift detection:** Continuously monitor for the process drifting back (regression) or for **bottleneck migration** — automated alerts if a previously healthy transition's wait time crosses a threshold, catching unintended side-effects early.

4. **Closed-loop iteration:** Treat optimization as a continuous cycle, not a one-off project. Each quarter, re-run the full diagnostic (Sections 1–2) to identify the *new* top-priority queue (since fixing one reveals the next), and re-tune scheduling parameters against the latest demand patterns. As the system changes, the data-driven baseline updates with it.

5. **Conformance checking:** Verify that redesigned flows (e.g., early test ordering, electronic handovers) are *actually being followed* by checking the live log against the intended model — adoption gaps are a common reason improvements fail to materialize.

---

### Summary

This approach moves from **rigorous measurement** (separating service from queue time, characterizing distributions not just means)  **causal diagnosis** (utilization, handover, variant, and arrival analyses to find the *why*)  **a balanced portfolio of interventions** targeting demand, capacity, and flow  **explicit trade-off management** with quality guardrails and simulation-based validation  **continuous, log-driven monitoring**. The throughline is that every recommendation is anchored in a specific, measurable pattern in the event data and is validated both before deployment (simulation) and after (control-charted KPIs with quality guardrails).
