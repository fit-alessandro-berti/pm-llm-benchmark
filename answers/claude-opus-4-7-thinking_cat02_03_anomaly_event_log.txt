# Analysis of P2P Event Log: Identified Anomalies

## Standard P2P Reference Flow
The expected sequence is:
1. Create Purchase Requisition  2. Approve Purchase Requisition  3. Issue Purchase Order  4. Receive Goods  5. Vendor Invoice Received  6. Match Invoice to PO (3-way match)  7. Release Payment  8. Close Case

---

## Case 1001  Normal
This case follows the standard sequence with all activities in the correct order. It serves as a useful baseline.

---

## Case 1002  Multiple Anomalies

**Observed sequence:** Create PR  Issue PO  Vendor Invoice Received  **Release Payment**  **Approve PR**  Receive Goods  Match Invoice to PO  Close Case

### Problems:
1. **PO issued before PR approval.** The Purchase Order was issued only 10 minutes after PR creation — and approval came *days later*. This bypasses the formal authorization control, exposing the company to unapproved spending.
2. **Payment released before goods receipt and before invoice matching.** Paying before goods are received and before the 3-way match (PO  Goods Receipt  Invoice) defeats the entire purpose of the matching control. Risks include:
   - Paying for goods that never arrive
   - Paying incorrect amounts
   - Duplicate payments / fraud
3. **Approval performed retroactively.** Approving the requisition *after* the PO, invoice, and payment is a "rubber stamp" — a strong indicator of weak segregation of duties or circumvention of internal controls.

---

## Case 1003  Significant Anomaly

**Observed sequence:** Create PR  Approve PR  Issue PO  **Release Payment**  Vendor Invoice Received  Receive Goods  Match Invoice to PO  Close Case

### Problems:
1. **Payment released before invoice was even received.** The company paid the vendor without an invoice on file — a serious financial control violation.
2. **Payment released before goods were received.** No verification that the ordered items ever arrived.
3. **Invoice matching occurred *after* payment.** The 3-way match is meant to *prevent* incorrect payment, not validate one already made. This eliminates the control's purpose.
4. **Risk implications:** Potential fraud, overpayment, no recourse if goods are defective or never delivered, and audit/compliance violations (e.g., SOX).

---

## Case 1004  Anomaly

**Observed sequence:** Create PR  Approve PR  **Receive Goods  Issue PO**  Vendor Invoice Received  Match Invoice to PO  Release Payment  Close Case

### Problems:
1. **Goods received before PO issued.** This is a classic **"after-the-fact PO"** or *maverick buying* pattern. Possible causes:
   - Informal/verbal ordering by an employee, with the PO created later just to make accounting work
   - Emergency procurement without proper governance
   - Bypassing procurement policies entirely
2. **Risks:**
   - No price/terms agreement before commitment was made
   - Loss of negotiating leverage with vendor
   - Inability to dispute quantity/quality at delivery (no PO to compare against)
   - Compliance and budget control failures
3. Although the rest of the case proceeds correctly (match  pay), the integrity of the PO is questionable — it was likely backdated or reverse-engineered to match what already arrived.

## Overall Concerns
The anomalies in cases 1002–1004 all share a common theme: **bypassing or reversing key control points** (approval, 3-way matching, goods receipt verification). In a well-governed P2P process, these controls exist to prevent fraud, overpayment, and unauthorized spending. Patterns like these warrant investigation by internal audit, particularly for any signs of recurring vendor relationships, common requesters, or systemic workflow misconfiguration.
