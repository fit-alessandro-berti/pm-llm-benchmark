After analyzing the event sequences for both cases A1 and B2, I can identify a clear, repeating pattern of activities. The low-level events cluster into four distinct phases based on their purpose, the resources involved, and their temporal sequence. Here are the proposed high-level process steps:

---

### 1. **Material Preparation & Staging**
**Low-Level Events Grouped:**
- Retrieve raw metal sheet
- Scan barcode
- Place metal sheet on table
- Align metal sheet
- Preheat metal sheet

**Rationale:** These events represent the initial handling and conditioning of the raw material before any value-adding transformation begins. They are performed sequentially by a combination of operators and preparatory machines (Robot Arm, Heating Unit) and are focused solely on getting the material ready for the next stage. The activities transition from physical retrieval to identification, positioning, and thermal treatment, all setting the stage for assembly.

---

### 2. **Core Assembly (Welding)**
**Low-Level Events Grouped:**
- Pick up welding tool
- Weld corner A
- Weld corner B

**Rationale:** This group represents the primary value-adding transformation step. The events are tightly coupled: picking up the specific tool immediately precedes its use in the welding operations. They are performed by the same resource type (Operator B), focus on a single joining technique (spot welding), and result in a permanent structural change to the workpiece. This is the manufacturing "core."

---

### 3. **Quality Assurance & Coating Application**
**Low-Level Events Grouped:**
- Measure weld integrity
- Apply protective coating
- Dry coating

**Rationale:** This phase combines an immediate quality check of the core assembly with the application of a protective finish. The weld integrity measurement (a sensor check) logically gates the application of the coating; if the weld failed, the process would likely branch to rework. The coating application and its subsequent drying are consecutive finishing operations performed by specialized units (Coating Unit, Heating Unit), moving the item toward completion.

---

### 4. **Final Inspection**
**Low-Level Events Grouped:**
- Visual check

**Rationale:** This is a discrete, final quality gate performed by a human operator (Operator C) after all mechanical processes (assembly, coating, drying) are complete. It represents the last human oversight before the item is likely approved for dispatch or further processing. Its singularity and placement at the end of the sequence mark it as a distinct, conclusive step.

---

### Structured Representation of the High-Level Process:

**High-Level Process Flow:**
```
Material Preparation & Staging  Core Assembly (Welding)  Quality Assurance & Coating Application  Final Inspection
```

**Process Model Summary Table:**
| High-Level Step | Purpose | Key Resources Involved | Typical Trigger | Typical Outcome |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Material Preparation & Staging** | Retrieve, identify, position, and pre-treat raw material. | Operator A, Robot Arm #2, Heating Unit #1 | Case start | Aligned, preheated metal sheet ready for joining. |
| **Core Assembly (Welding)** | Perform permanent joining to create the product structure. | Operator B | Completion of preheating. | Welded assembly with corners joined. |

**Grouping Logic Summary:**
The grouping follows a **phase-based** logic aligned with classic manufacturing flow: **Prepare  Transform  Finish & Validate  Approve**. Each group is defined by a shared primary objective, a logical "hand-off" point to the next phase, and a cluster of activities that are more meaningfully understood together than in isolation. This abstraction removes the noise of individual machine actions, making the overall process flow instantly comprehensible.
