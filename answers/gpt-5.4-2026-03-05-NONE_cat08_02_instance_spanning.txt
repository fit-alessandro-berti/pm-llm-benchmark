## 1. Identifying Instance-Spanning Constraints and Their Impact

The key analytical challenge here is that delays are not only caused by what happens *inside* one order, but also by what other orders are doing at the same time. So I would combine classic case-centric process mining with **object/resource-aware** and **time-based concurrency analysis**.

### 1.1 Build an analysis-ready event structure

From the log, I would first reconstruct for each order:

- activity start and complete timestamps
- activity duration
- waiting time between activities
- assigned resource/station
- order attributes:
  - express vs standard
  - destination region
  - cold-packing required
  - hazardous material flag

Then I would enrich each event with **system-state features at the timestamp**, such as:

- number of cold-packing stations occupied
- queue length for cold-packing
- number of hazardous orders currently in Packing or Quality Check
- number of completed orders waiting for the same region batch
- number of express orders currently active
- whether the current activity was interrupted/preempted

This turns the log from a simple sequence into a basis for identifying **instance-spanning constraints**.

---

### 1.2 Shared Cold-Packing Stations

#### How to identify
Orders with `Requires Cold Packing = TRUE` must use one of the limited cold stations. Using start/complete events by station, I would calculate:

- actual cold-station occupancy over time
- queue before Packing for cold orders
- whether cold-packing start is delayed after Item Picking completion
- utilization of each cold station and all cold stations combined

#### Process mining techniques
- **Performance mining** on the transition into Packing for cold orders
- **Resource analysis** focused on specialized stations
- **Queue mining / waiting-state reconstruction**
- **Temporal conformance checking** against the rule “cold orders must use cold stations only”

#### Impact metrics
- average and percentile waiting time from `Item Picking COMPLETE` to `Packing START` for cold orders
- queue length at cold stations over time
- cold-station utilization %
- number and duration of times all 5 stations are fully occupied
- throughput loss attributable to cold-station contention
- SLA breach rate for cold express vs cold standard orders

#### Attribution of impact
To quantify how much delay is due to this constraint:
- compare waiting times of cold vs non-cold orders entering Packing, controlling for time of day, order type, and workload
- measure how waiting time rises as cold-station occupancy approaches capacity
- estimate marginal delay per additional cold order in queue

---

### 1.3 Shipping Batches by Region

#### How to identify
Orders may complete Quality Check but wait until enough orders for the same region are available before Shipping Label Generation. This is a clear between-instance dependency.

#### Process mining techniques
- detect **synchronization points** where multiple cases converge into a batch object
- create inferred or explicit **batch IDs** per region/time window
- perform **batch mining**
- analyze waiting between `Quality Check COMPLETE` and `Shipping Label Generation COMPLETE/START`

#### Impact metrics
- waiting time from QC completion to Shipping Label Generation
- batch formation time by region
- batch size distribution
- proportion of orders delayed by batching
- average benefit of batching, e.g. transport efficiency proxy, if available
- end-to-end lead-time increase caused by batching

#### Attribution
Differentiate “normal waiting” from batch waiting by checking:
- whether Shipping Label Generation requires a batch trigger
- whether order was technically ready but held until region batch closed
- how many same-region orders were incomplete when this order finished QC

For example:
- if an order completed QC at 10:35 but label generation happened at 10:45 due to batch B1 closure, then most of that 10-minute gap is batching delay, not internal processing.

---

### 1.4 Priority Handling of Express Orders

#### How to identify
Express orders may preempt standard orders at constrained resources. This creates delays across instances.

#### Process mining techniques
- **priority-aware resource analysis**
- detect likely **preemption patterns**:
  - standard order activity starts
  - express order arrives for same constrained resource
  - standard activity pauses or is reassigned
  - express activity starts quickly
- compare queue behavior by priority class

If explicit pause/resume is not logged, infer preemption from:
- prolonged activity duration on standard orders
- resource switching
- gaps in work on a standard order while another express order uses same resource/station

#### Impact metrics
For express orders:
- queue time reduction due to priority
- lead time from receipt to shipment
- probability of immediate service at constrained steps

For standard orders:
- additional waiting caused by express arrivals
- interruption frequency
- interruption-induced rework or longer effective processing time
- SLA degradation for non-express orders

System-level:
- total delay transferred from standard to express
- fairness index between classes
- throughput impact under different express volumes

#### Attribution
To isolate between-instance priority effects:
- model expected waiting for standard orders when no express order arrives during their queue/service interval
- compare against actual waiting when express arrivals occur
- quantify incremental delay caused by express preemption events

---

### 1.5 Hazardous Material Limit

The rule is global: no more than 10 hazardous orders may be in Packing or Quality Check simultaneously.

#### How to identify
Using hazard flags and event intervals, compute for every timestamp:
- number of hazardous orders concurrently in Packing or QC

Then identify:
- violations, if any
- blocking events where hazardous orders were ready but could not enter Packing/QC due to the cap

#### Process mining techniques
- **concurrency analysis**
- **rule-based conformance checking**
- **state-space reconstruction**
- **capacity constraint mining**

#### Impact metrics
- waiting time for hazardous orders before Packing and before QC due to cap
- percentage of time hazardous WIP is at limit = 10
- lost throughput due to cap
- queue lengths of hazardous orders
- spillover effects on non-hazardous orders if shared resources are reserved or blocked

#### Attribution
Differentiate hazardous-cap delays from normal delays by checking:
- was the order ready for Packing/QC?
- was a suitable resource available?
- was entry blocked only because hazardous count already equaled 10?

This is critical: a hazardous order may wait because no station is free, or because the cap is active, or both. The event log should be enriched with the prevailing system state to classify the dominant cause.

---

### 1.6 Distinguishing within-instance vs between-instance waiting

This is central.

For each activity transition, I would decompose elapsed time into:

1. **Processing time**  
   Activity start to complete.

2. **Intrinsic within-instance delay**  
   Delay due to case-specific factors:
   - missing item
   - order complexity
   - rework
   - long handling time
   - quality issue

3. **Between-instance delay**  
   Delay due to shared system state:
   - resource occupied by another order
   - waiting for region batch completion
   - preemption by express order
   - hazardous cap reached

#### Practical method
For each transition, create a cause classification:

- `resource_contention_delay`
- `batch_wait_delay`
- `priority_preemption_delay`
- `hazmat_cap_delay`
- `case_complexity_delay`
- `unknown/mixed`

Use:
- timestamp gaps
- resource occupancy
- queue state
- concurrent case counts
- order attributes
- if needed, statistical models such as survival analysis or regression to explain waiting time by both case-level and system-level variables

A strong approach is:
- estimate waiting time as a function of case features only
- then add cross-case features
- the additional explanatory power quantifies the role of instance-spanning constraints

## 2. Analyzing Constraint Interactions

The constraints will not operate independently. In this warehouse, they likely amplify one another.

### 2.1 Express + Cold-Packing
An express order requiring cold-packing is likely the most disruptive combination.

Effects:
- express order jumps queue at one of only 5 cold stations
- standard cold orders wait longer
- if preemption is allowed, partially processed standard work may be interrupted
- cold-station utilization may remain high, but flow efficiency worsens due to interruptions

So the priority rule and resource scarcity interact to create concentrated delay.

---

### 2.2 Batching + Priority
An express order may be processed quickly through Picking, Packing, and QC, but then still wait for a regional batch unless batching rules exempt it.

Possible consequence:
- operational acceleration earlier in process is wasted at the end
- express orders may distort upstream priorities without improving actual delivery lead time

This interaction often reveals a policy contradiction:
- “expedite express” upstream
- “hold by region batch” downstream

---

### 2.3 Batching + Hazardous Limit
If many hazardous orders go to the same region:
- they may accumulate waiting for batch formation
- or they may reach the hazardous WIP cap in Packing/QC and be throttled
- completed hazardous orders may hold up regional batches
- alternatively, batching may intentionally delay some hazardous orders, reducing hazardous WIP peaks

So batching can either relieve or worsen hazardous-limit pressure depending on timing and release logic.

---

### 2.4 Hazardous Limit + Cold-Packing
If some hazardous orders also require cold-packing:
- they compete for specialized cold resources
- and their entry to downstream steps is further constrained by the global hazardous cap
- this can create starvation or stop-start behavior

This is a classic multiple-constraint bottleneck.

---

### 2.5 Express + Hazardous Limit
Even express hazardous orders may be legally unable to bypass the hazardous WIP cap.

This means:
- the priority policy has a hard boundary
- if not well designed, staff may repeatedly attempt to expedite orders that cannot legally proceed, causing planning noise and queue instability

---

### 2.6 Why understanding interactions matters
If we optimize one constraint in isolation, we may worsen another.

Examples:
- adding stronger express preemption may improve express cycle time but explode standard delays at cold stations
- increasing batch size to optimize shipping may worsen lead times and SLA compliance
- aggressively filling hazardous processing slots may block urgent cold express orders later

Therefore, optimization must be **constraint-aware and system-wide**, not local. This is exactly where process mining plus simulation is valuable.

---

## 3. Developing Constraint-Aware Optimization Strategies

Below are four concrete strategies.

---

### Strategy 1: Dynamic Allocation and Reservation Policy for Cold-Packing Stations

#### Primary constraints addressed
- shared cold-packing stations
- express priority handling
- secondarily hazardous interaction if some cold orders are hazardous

#### Specific changes proposed
Replace a simple first-come/first-served or ad hoc priority rule with a **dynamic station allocation policy**:

- reserve 1 of 5 cold stations as a flexible express buffer during peak express windows
- use the remaining stations with queue rules based on:
  - order priority
  - due time/SLA risk
  - waiting time already accumulated
  - hazardous status if downstream capacity is near cap
- allow preemption only before packing starts, not mid-pack, unless operationally unavoidable
- if express demand is low, release reserved capacity back to standard cold orders

#### How it uses data
Use historical logs to forecast by hour:
- cold-order arrival rate
- express cold-order arrival rate
- average packing duration
- queue growth patterns

Then optimize reservation thresholds:
- when to reserve capacity
- how many stations to reserve
- when to release unused reserved slots

#### Expected outcomes
- reduced waiting time for express cold orders
- lower disruption to standard orders than full preemption
- fewer mid-task interruptions
- smoother utilization of cold stations
- improved SLA compliance without excessive fairness loss

#### Why it works
It acknowledges that the problem is not just lack of capacity, but **competition across instances under different priorities**.

---

### Strategy 2: Dynamic Region Batching with Time-and-Size Triggers

#### Primary constraints addressed
- shipping batch delays
- interaction with express priority
- interaction with hazardous orders by region

#### Specific changes proposed
Replace fixed or passive batching with **adaptive batching logic**:

- create batches by region using dual triggers:
  - size trigger: release when enough orders accumulate
  - time trigger: release when oldest waiting order exceeds threshold
- exempt express orders from standard batch waiting, or use smaller express-only micro-batches
- for low-volume regions, shorten maximum batch waiting time
- if hazardous orders in a region are building up and nearing upstream congestion, trigger earlier batch release

#### How it uses data
Use process mining to estimate:
- batch size distributions by region
- transport savings vs waiting cost
- regional arrival patterns by hour/day
- impact of different batch windows on total lead time

This can support region-specific policies:
- North: batch size threshold 8, max wait 15 min
- West express: threshold 2, max wait 5 min
- South low volume: threshold 4, max wait 10 min

#### Expected outcomes
- reduced idle waiting after QC
- better alignment between express service promise and actual downstream handling
- less unnecessary holding of completed orders
- more stable flow into shipping
- lower end-to-end lead time with limited sacrifice in route efficiency

#### Why it works
It directly targets a pure instance-spanning dependency: waiting for *other cases* to be ready.

---

### Strategy 3: Constraint-Aware Scheduling for Hazardous Orders

#### Primary constraints addressed
- hazardous material simultaneous processing cap
- interaction with batching and specialized resources

#### Specific changes proposed
Introduce a **WIP-release and scheduling mechanism** for hazardous orders:

- before releasing a hazardous order into Packing, check predicted occupancy of hazardous slots across Packing + QC
- maintain a soft threshold below the legal cap, e.g. target 8–9 rather than 10, to reduce blocking shocks
- sequence hazardous orders to avoid bunching:
  - smooth their release from Picking into Packing
  - prioritize those with shortest expected downstream path when cap is tight
- if many hazardous orders are destined for same region, coordinate release with likely batch timing

#### How it uses data
From the log, estimate:
- hazardous order arrival patterns
- service-time distributions in Packing and QC
- occupancy peaks
- probability of blocking when occupancy reaches 9 or 10
- regional clustering of hazardous orders

Potentially use predictive models to estimate:
- expected duration in hazardous-constrained area
- near-future hazardous occupancy

#### Expected outcomes
- fewer times at the hard cap
- less stop-start waiting for hazardous orders
- maintained compliance
- improved throughput stability
- reduced spillover onto non-hazardous orders through smoother shared-resource usage

#### Why it works
Instead of reacting when the cap is hit, it proactively manages the cross-case WIP level.

---

### Strategy 4: Policy Redesign for Express Orders to Prevent Priority Waste

#### Primary constraints addressed
- express priority handling
- interaction with batching
- interaction with constrained resources

#### Specific changes proposed
Redesign the express policy so that acceleration is applied only where it changes end-to-end performance:

- define “protected fast lane” only for steps that are true bottlenecks
- do not preempt standard orders at non-bottleneck steps if downstream constraints will nullify the gain
- guarantee no batching hold for express orders, or cap hold at a very short threshold
- if hazardous cap or legal restrictions prevent advancement, mark express order as “temporarily non-expeditable” to avoid repeated unnecessary escalations

#### How it uses data
Use bottleneck and delay-cause analysis to identify:
- which activities create real express delays
- where preemption helps
- where preemption only shifts delay downstream

#### Expected outcomes
- better express lead-time performance
- less collateral damage to standard orders
- less operational chaos from excessive interruption
- higher overall throughput and fairness

#### Why it works
It aligns the priority rule with actual system constraints rather than applying it blindly.

## 4. Simulation and Validation

Before implementation, I would build a **discrete-event simulation** informed by the process-mining findings.

### 4.1 Why simulation is needed
Process mining reveals what happened and where delays occur. Simulation tests:
- what happens if policies change
- how constraints interact under different demand mixes
- whether local improvements create downstream congestion

This is essential because instance-spanning constraints are dynamic and nonlinear.

---

### 4.2 What the simulation model should include

#### Entities
- orders with attributes:
  - standard/express
  - region
  - cold-packing required
  - hazardous material flag
  - due time / SLA class

#### Activities
- Order Received
- Item Picking
- Packing
- Quality Check
- Shipping Label Generation

#### Resources
- pickers
- standard packing stations
- 5 cold-packing stations
- QC staff
- shipping label generation capacity

#### Logic
- queues before each activity
- preemption rules for express orders
- batching rules by region
- hazardous concurrent WIP cap across Packing + QC
- routing rules for cold vs standard packing

---

### 4.3 Calibration from event log
Use process mining outputs to parameterize:
- arrival distributions by hour/day/season
- service-time distributions per activity and order type
- resource calendars
- observed queue disciplines
- interruption probabilities and durations
- batch size and timing distributions
- transition probabilities and exceptions

This ensures the simulation reflects the actual warehouse.

---

### 4.4 Scenarios to test
I would simulate at least:

1. **Baseline**
   - current process behavior

2. **Dynamic cold-station reservation**
   - various reservation levels and release rules

3. **Adaptive batching**
   - different region-specific time/size thresholds
   - express exemption scenarios

4. **Hazardous WIP smoothing**
   - release thresholds of 8, 9, 10
   - different sequencing rules

5. **Combined strategies**
   - because interactions matter most here

6. **Peak-season stress tests**
   - high order volume
   - high express share
   - high hazardous mix

---

### 4.5 KPIs to evaluate in simulation
- end-to-end order lead time
- SLA compliance rate by order type
- waiting time before Packing, QC, and Shipping Label Generation
- cold-station queue length and utilization
- average batch waiting time by region
- standard-order delay caused by express preemption
- hazardous-cap blocking time
- throughput per hour/day
- fairness metrics between express and standard
- compliance with hazardous limit

---

### 4.6 Accuracy focus areas
To capture instance-spanning constraints well, the simulation must faithfully represent:

#### Resource contention
- especially cold-station occupancy and queueing

#### Batching delays
- orders waiting for region-mates
- effect of varying region arrival patterns

#### Priority interruptions
- whether preemption happens
- whether interruption causes setup loss or restart penalty

#### Regulatory limits
- hard cap of 10 hazardous orders in Packing + QC
- blocking behavior when cap is reached

Without these four elements, the simulation will miss the true bottlenecks.

## Closing Recommendation

The fulfillment center should treat this as a **system of interacting instance-spanning constraints**, not as separate local bottlenecks. The right approach is:

1. use process mining to reconstruct delays and attribute them to cross-instance causes,
2. analyze interactions among cold capacity, batching, priority, and hazardous limits,
3. redesign rules using dynamic, data-driven policies,
4. validate with simulation before rollout,
5. monitor continuously with constraint-specific dashboards.
