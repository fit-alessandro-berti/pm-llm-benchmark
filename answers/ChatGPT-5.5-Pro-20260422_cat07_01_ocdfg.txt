This visualization appears to be an **object-centric process map**, likely an **object-centric directly-follows graph**. It shows how different business objects move through activities, and where their lifecycles overlap.

## How to read it

The **hatched boxes at the top** are object types and their volumes:

* **PurchaseOrderItem**: 5.0M objects
* **SalesOrderItem**: 3.5M objects
* **PurchaseRequisitionItem**: 2.0M objects
* **VendorInvoiceItem**: 1.2M objects
* **ReturnOrderItem**: 0.8M objects

The **rectangles** are activities, such as `Create Order`, `Approve Requisition`, `Ship Goods`, and `Create Invoice`.

The **“Overall: X M”** value inside each activity is the total observed volume for that activity. For example:

* `Create Order` has **8.5M** occurrences, making it the largest activity.
* `Approve Requisition` has **5.7M** occurrences.
* `Send Order` has **5.2M** occurrences.

The **colored dots inside activities** indicate which object types participate in that activity. For example:

* `Create Order` involves both **SalesOrderItem** and **PurchaseOrderItem**.
* `Approve Requisition` involves several object types, making it a major cross-object activity.
* `Create Invoice` involves **PurchaseOrderItem** and **VendorInvoiceItem**.
* `Process Return` involves **VendorInvoiceItem** and **ReturnOrderItem**.

The **colored arrows** show directly-following relationships for each object type. In other words, for objects of that color, one activity was observed directly after another. The labels such as `300ms`, `650ms`, or `800ms` show the elapsed-time metric between those activities, usually an average or configured aggregation.

## Main process areas

On the **right side**, there is a relatively clean sales/order fulfillment flow:

`Create Order → Pack Goods → Ship Goods → Confirm delivery → Close Order → End`

This path is mostly associated with **SalesOrderItem**, shown in orange. It has activity volumes around 1.7M–2.0M after order creation.

The `Create Order` activity is especially important because it is shared by **SalesOrderItem** and **PurchaseOrderItem**, and it has the largest overall volume at **8.5M**.

In the **center-left**, the process revolves around procurement and requisitions:

`Create Requisition → Approve Requisition`

`Approve Requisition` is a central hub. It connects to activities such as:

* `Send Order`
* `Check Stock`
* `Process Return`
* `Approve payment`
* `Create Order`

This suggests that approvals are not isolated to purchase requisitions only; they are connected with purchase orders, returns, and invoices.

At the **bottom center**, invoice and payment-related activities appear:

`Approve payment → Create Invoice → End`

`Create Invoice` has **2.0M** occurrences and connects multiple object types, especially purchase order and vendor invoice items.

On the **left and middle-right**, returns and vendor invoices interact with the rest of the process:

`Process Return → Close Order`

and

`Process Return → Create Invoice`

This indicates that returns can lead either toward closing an order or toward invoice-related processing.

## Key takeaways

The graph shows **multiple object lifecycles overlaid in one view**, rather than a single linear process. The two biggest integration points are:

1. **Create Order**, which connects sales and purchase order objects.
2. **Approve Requisition**, which connects requisitions, purchase orders, vendor invoices, and returns.

The right-hand order fulfillment path is comparatively linear, while the procurement, payment, invoice, and return area is more interconnected and contains more branching and looping. The longer displayed delays, such as **650ms**, **800ms**, and **600ms**, may be places to inspect further if the goal is performance analysis. The `0ms` edge suggests activities that were recorded at the same timestamp or occurred immediately one after another.
