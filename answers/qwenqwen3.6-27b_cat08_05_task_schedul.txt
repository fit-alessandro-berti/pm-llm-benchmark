### **Data-Driven Process Optimization and Advanced Scheduling for Precision Parts Inc.**

**To:** Executive Management, Precision Parts Inc.  
**From:** Senior Operations Analyst  
**Subject:** Strategic Overhaul of Scheduling via Process Mining and Advanced Analytics

### **1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics**

To transform Precision Parts Inc.'s scheduling, we must first move from intuition to evidence. We will leverage the MES event logs to reconstruct the "As-Is" process model and quantify performance using advanced process mining techniques.

**Reconstruction and Flow Analysis:**
*   **Event Log Transformation:** We map the raw logs to a standard process mining format. `Case ID` represents the Job, `Timestamp` provides temporal order, and `Activity` is derived from `Event Type` and `Task`. Crucially, we enrich the log by joining `Job` attributes (Due Date, Priority) to every task event.
*   **Conformance Checking:** We compare the actual execution sequence against the planned routing (BOM/Routing data). In a job shop, deviations are common. Conformance metrics will quantify the frequency of routing changes, skipped operations, or rework loops, which distort lead time predictions.
*   **Variant Analysis:** We cluster jobs by their execution variants (unique sequences of tasks). This reveals if certain routings consistently cause delays or if "hot jobs" follow distinct, disruptive paths.

**Quantitative Metrics & Techniques:**
*   **Job Flow Times & Lead Times:**
    *   *Metric:* End-to-end duration (`Job Released` to `Job Completed`). We analyze the full distribution (percentiles, not just means) to capture tail risks causing tardiness.
    *   *Technique:* **Performance View** in process mining to visualize lead time heatmaps across the process map. We correlate lead times with job complexity (number of tasks) and priority.
*   **Task Waiting Times (Queue Times):**
    *   *Metric:* Time between `Queue Entry` and `Setup Start` (or `Task Start` if no setup).
    *   *Technique:* **Queue Length Analysis** over time per resource. We calculate the coefficient of variation (CV) of queue times to identify instability. High CV indicates erratic release patterns or bottlenecks.
*   **Resource Utilization:**
    *   *Metric:* Breakdown of resource time into `Productive` (Task), `Setup`, `Idle`, and `Breakdown`.
    *   *Technique:* **Resource Utilization Mining**. We aggregate time intervals per `Resource ID`. We specifically calculate **Effective Utilization** (Productive / (Productive + Setup)) to assess the impact of setups on capacity.
*   **Sequence-Dependent Setup Times:**
    *   *Analysis:* This is critical. We extract all instances where `Setup Required = TRUE`. Using the `Notes` field (e.g., "Previous job: JOB-6998"), we construct a **Setup Time Matrix**.
    *   *Output:* For each machine, we build a directed graph where nodes are job types/features and edge weights are average setup times. We analyze if setup times are truly sequence-dependent or if they correlate with job attributes (e.g., material thickness, tooling type). This matrix is the foundation for optimization.
*   **Schedule Adherence & Tardiness:**
    *   *Metric:* Tardiness = `Completion Time` - `Due Date`. We measure % On-Time Delivery, Mean Tardiness, and Maximum Tardiness.
    *   *Technique:* **Due Date Analysis**. We compare `Order Due Date` against actual completion. We perform **Correlation Analysis** to see if tardiness spikes correlate with specific operators, machines, or times of day.
*   **Impact of Disruptions:**
    *   *Analysis:* We tag periods of `Breakdown Start/End` and `Priority Change`.
    *   *Technique:* **Event Impact Analysis**. We measure the "Recovery Time" after a breakdown (time until WIP clears and normal flow resumes). We quantify the "Priority Inversion Cost" by measuring delays to existing jobs when a `Hot Job` is inserted.

### **2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies**

Using the analysis above, we diagnose the systemic failures of the current dispatching rules.

*   **Bottleneck Starvation and Overload:**
    *   *Evidence:* Process mining will likely reveal resources (e.g., `MILL-03` or `HEAT-01`) with utilization >90% and queue times increasing exponentially over time. Meanwhile, flexible resources may show high idle time.
    *   *Pathology:* Current rules do not manage flow into bottlenecks. Jobs arrive at bottlenecks faster than they can be processed, creating massive WIP accumulation (Bullwhip effect) upstream, while downstream resources starve because bottleneck output is erratic.
*   **Ineffective Prioritization:**
    *   *Evidence:* **Variant Analysis** comparing "Late" vs. "On-Time" jobs may show that High Priority jobs are not completing faster than Medium ones. `Priority Change` events might show that urgent jobs are delayed by long queue times at bottlenecks because they cannot jump the queue effectively if the setup for the current job is long.
    *   *Pathology:* Static rules like EDD fail when setup times are significant. A job with an earlier due date might be skipped in favor of a later one if the setup for the earlier one is prohibitive, or conversely, high-priority jobs might be starved if the rule doesn't account for remaining processing time.
*   **Setup Time Inflation:**
    *   *Evidence:* The **Setup Time Matrix** analysis will likely show high variance in setup times. If sequencing is random (FCFS), the average setup time will be significantly higher than the theoretical minimum achievable through optimal sequencing.
    *   *Pathology:* Ignoring sequence dependence wastes capacity. On a bottleneck, 30 minutes of avoidable setup time is equivalent to losing 30 minutes of production, directly reducing throughput and increasing tardiness.
*   **Release Control Failure (High WIP):**
    *   *Evidence:* Analysis of `Job Released` timestamps vs. `Queue Entry` at first operation will show jobs entering the system well before they are needed. WIP analysis will show inventory piling up at non-bottleneck resources.
    *   *Pathology:* Lack of "Appointment Scheduling" or release control. Releasing jobs too early creates congestion, increases handling time, and obscures true lead times, making the shop unpredictable.
*   **Disruption Amplification:**
    *   *Evidence:* **Event Impact Analysis** will show that after a breakdown, the time to clear queues is prolonged, and tardiness spikes.
    *   *Pathology:* No dynamic re-scheduling. When a machine breaks, jobs are left waiting without re-routing or re-prioritization, causing cascading delays.

### **3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness**

We must distinguish between capacity constraints, variability, and scheduling logic failures.

*   **Static Rules vs. Dynamic Environment:**
    *   *Root Cause:* FCFS and EDD are myopic. They optimize locally for a single machine without considering the global state. Process mining confirms that local optimization leads to global sub-optimality (e.g., maximizing utilization of a non-bottleneck creates WIP that blocks the bottleneck).
    *   *Mining Evidence:* High correlation between non-bottleneck utilization and bottleneck queue length suggests upstream over-production.
*   **Inaccurate Estimations:**
    *   *Root Cause:* Schedules likely rely on standard times that do not reflect reality.
    *   *Mining Evidence:* Comparison of `Task Duration (Planned)` vs. `Actual` will reveal systematic bias (e.g., actuals are consistently 15% higher). If the scheduler uses optimistic times, due dates will be missed. Process mining quantifies this bias and the variance (noise) around actuals.
*   **Sequence-Dependent Setup Blindness:**
    *   *Root Cause:* The scheduler treats setup as a constant or ignores it.
    *   *Mining Evidence:* The Setup Matrix proves strong dependence. If the scheduler cannot see this dependency, it cannot make intelligent sequencing decisions, leading to the "Setup Inflation" pathology.
*   **Lack of Real-Time Visibility:**
    *   *Root Cause:* Dispatchers react to events rather than proactively managing flow.
    *   *Mining Evidence:* Long reaction times to `Breakdown` events. Jobs remain queued on broken machines in the system until manually reassigned, wasting time.
*   **Differentiation Strategy:**
    *   *Scheduling vs. Capacity:* If average utilization of bottlenecks is <85% but tardiness is high, the root cause is scheduling/variability, not capacity. If utilization is >95%, capacity expansion is needed.
    *   *Scheduling vs. Variability:* If the Coefficient of Variation (CV) of task times is high, even perfect scheduling struggles. Process mining quantifies CV. If CV is high, we need buffer management strategies alongside scheduling improvements.

### **4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies**

Based on the mining insights, we propose three synergistic strategies.

#### **Strategy 1: Dynamic Multi-Criteria Dispatching with Downstream Awareness**
*   **Core Logic:** Replace static rules with a weighted scoring function for each job at each queue. The score determines priority.
    *   *Score = w1*(Slack per Remaining Operation) + w2*(Setup Cost to Next) + w3*(Downstream Load Penalty) + w4*(Priority Weight).*
*   **Data-Driven Enhancements:**
    *   **Setup Cost:** Uses the **Setup Matrix** mined from logs. If Job A follows Job B, the setup cost is retrieved directly. The scheduler prefers sequences that minimize this cost.
    *   **Downstream Load:** Uses real-time queue lengths (or predicted load) of downstream resources. If the next machine for a job is heavily loaded, the job's priority is reduced slightly to prevent blocking, or increased if the bottleneck is ahead.
    *   **Weights:** Weights (`w1`...`w4`) are optimized via simulation based on company goals (e.g., minimize tardiness vs. minimize WIP).
*   **Impact:** Reduces tardiness by balancing due dates with feasibility; reduces setup times; prevents starvation of downstream resources.

#### **Strategy 2: Predictive Appointment Scheduling & Release Control**
*   **Core Logic:** Implement a "Pull" system where jobs are released to the shop floor only when needed, based on predicted lead times. This controls WIP and stabilizes flow.
*   **Data-Driven Enhancements:**
    *   **Lead Time Prediction:** Train a regression model (e.g., Gradient Boosting) on the mined event logs to predict job lead time based on features: number of tasks, routing variant, priority, and current shop load. Features are engineered using process mining metrics (e.g., "Avg queue time at bottleneck").
    *   **Dynamic Release:** Calculate `Release Time = Due Date - Predicted Lead Time - Safety Buffer`. The Safety Buffer is dynamic, increasing when predicted variability or load is high.
    *   **Predictive Maintenance Integration:** If logs show breakdown patterns, the model adjusts release times to avoid releasing jobs that require machines likely to be down.
*   **Impact:** Significantly reduces WIP; improves lead time predictability; prevents congestion at bottlenecks; allows for accurate promises to customers.

#### **Strategy 3: Setup-Time Optimized Sequencing at Bottlenecks**
*   **Core Logic:** Treat bottleneck machines as "Traveling Salesman" problems. Instead of dispatching one job at a time, look ahead at the queue and batch jobs to minimize total setup time.
*   **Data-Driven Enhancements:**
    *   **Clustering:** Use the **Setup Matrix** to cluster jobs into "families" with low intra-family setup times.
    *   **Optimization Heuristic:** At each bottleneck, when a machine becomes free, the scheduler evaluates the next `k` jobs (rolling horizon). It selects the sequence that minimizes setup time while respecting critical due dates.
    *   **Constraint Handling:** If a job is critical (Slack < 0), it breaks the sequence to ensure on-time delivery. The trade-off between setup savings and tardiness risk is quantified using mined tardiness costs.
*   **Impact:** Increases effective capacity of bottlenecks by recovering lost setup time; reduces average flow time; improves throughput without capital investment.
