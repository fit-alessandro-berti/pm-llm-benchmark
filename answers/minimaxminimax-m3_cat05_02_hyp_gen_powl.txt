# Analysis of POWL Process Model Anomalies in Insurance Claim Handling

## 1. Anomalies Identified in the POWL Model

| # | Anomaly | Description | Impact |
|---|---------|-------------|--------|
| **1** | **Loop between E and P** (`* (E, P)`) | The model allows Evaluate and Approve to be repeated indefinitely. Each iteration executes E, then optionally P, then E again. | Claims can be evaluated and approved multiple times, which may indicate re-work, quality issues, or iterative refinement. |
| **2** | **XOR skipping N** (`XOR(N, skip)`) | Customer notification can be bypassed entirely via the silent transition. | Customers may never be informed about claim status, causing dissatisfaction and potential compliance issues. |
| **3** | **Premature closing** (`A  C` direct edge) | The model adds a direct edge from Assign Adjuster to Close Claim, without requiring loop (E/P) or xor (N) to complete. | Claims can be closed immediately after assignment, skipping evaluation, approval, and notification. |
| **4** | **Missing `xor  C` edge** | No edge enforces that Close Claim happens after the XOR node. | Close can be concurrent with or precede notification. |
| **5** | **Weak precedence constraints** | The partial order only requires R  A  loop  xor; nothing forces these to complete before C. | The process becomes permissive, allowing out-of-sequence execution. |

### Visual Representation of the Anomaly

```
   R  A  loop (EP)  xor (N|skip)
                 
         C  (premature close possible)
```

The edge `A  C` means **C can execute as soon as A completes**, even while `loop` and `xor` are still in progress (or never executed at all).

## 2. Hypotheses on Why These Anomalies Exist

### Hypothesis A — Partial Implementation of New Business Rules
The organization may have introduced a **"fast-track" closing policy** for low-value or duplicate claims, but the process modeler only added the short-cut edge (`A  C`) without restricting it with conditions (e.g., `claim_amount < threshold`).

### Hypothesis B — Miscommunication Between Departments
- The **claims team** assumes evaluation and approval are mandatory.
- The **IT team** building the workflow interpreted the system requirements too loosely.
- **Compliance** was not consulted when the skip path was added.

### Hypothesis C — Technical / Configuration Errors
The workflow engine may have a bug that allows transitions not explicitly enabled, or the modeling tool's partial-order semantics are misinterpreted, leaving implicit assumptions unmodeled.

### Hypothesis D — Inadequate Controls in the Process Modeler
The PM tool (pm4py/Powl) does not enforce the intended sequence — a modeler must add *all* required edges explicitly. The author may have assumed transitive closure or default sequencing, but POWL **does not** apply transitive closure automatically for all nodes (e.g., AC is added but xorC is not).

### Hypothesis E — Genuine Business Need for Flexibility
- The loop may reflect **re-evaluation** when additional information arrives.
- The skip-notification XOR may exist for **internal claims** or **withdrawn claims** where no notification is required.
- These are legitimate but **under-documented** exceptions.

### Hypothesis F — Data-Logging Deficiency
The anomalies may not be real — they may simply reflect **missing events** in `claim_events` due to incomplete logging, suggesting a data-quality issue rather than a process one.

### Query 7 — Premature Close: Time Between A and C
*Quantifies how quickly a claim was closed after assignment.*

```sql
SELECT

    ce_a.timestamp  AS assigned_at,
```

### Query 8 — Correlation With Adjusters (Specialization Mismatch)

```sql
SELECT

    a.region,
    COUNT(DISTINCT ce.claim_id) FILTER (WHERE ce.activity = 'C') AS closed_claims,

GROUP BY a.adjuster_id, a.name, a.specialization, a.region
ORDER BY close_to_eval_ratio DESC NULLS LAST;
```
