### 2. Potential Root Causes of Performance Issues

By breaking down the timelines, three key patterns emerge as root causes for delays:

#### a. **Escalations to Level-2 Agent (Primary Cause)**
   - **Affected cases**: 102 and 105 (both >25 hours).
   - Escalation occurs after initial Level-1 assignment/investigation, introducing handoff delays.
     - Case 102: Level-1 assigned at 09:00  Escalated at 11:30 (**2.5-hour wait**), then Level-2 investigation starts at 14:00 (**2.5 more hours**), resolve next day.
     - Case 105: Level-1 investigation ends quickly (09:10–10:00 escalation), but Level-2 investigation delayed until **next day 14:00** (28-hour gap), resolve on day 3.
   - **Non-escalated cases** (101, 103): No delays; resolved same morning.

#### b. **Long Waiting Times Between Activities**
   - **Case 102**: Post-escalation wait (11:30–14:00 = **2.5 hours**) before Level-2 investigation.
   - **Case 104** (no escalation, but still delayed): Assigned at 09:30  Investigate starts at 13:00 (**3.5-hour wait** after assignment), resolve next day (**~19-hour investigation**).
   - **Case 105**: Massive post-escalation gap (10:00 on 03-01  14:00 on 03-02 = **28 hours**).
   - Early stages (Receive  Triage  Assign) are consistently fast (~10–40 mins) across all cases, indicating no bottleneck there.

#### c. **Overnight/Weekend Carryover**
   - All delayed cases spill into the next day(s): 102/104 (day 2), 105 (day 3).
   - Suggests unresolved work queues overnight, forcing resumption next business day.

### 3. How Factors Increase Cycle Times and Recommendations

#### Impact on Cycle Times
- **Escalations amplify delays**: They add handoff friction (e.g., documentation transfer) and availability issues for scarcer Level-2 agents, turning ~2-hour resolutions (101/103) into multi-day processes. In 102/105, escalations account for ~80% of total time via waiting.
- **Waiting times compound**: Idle periods (e.g., 2.5–28 hours) represent **non-value-adding time**, bloating cycle times. Case 104 shows even Level-1 can bottleneck if investigation stalls.
- **Overnight spillovers**: Business-hour assumptions mean delays cascade across days, exponentially increasing total time (e.g., 105's 49 hours vs. 2 hours).
- **Overall**: Escalations correlate perfectly with outliers (100% of long cases), suggesting ~20–40x slowdown vs. baseline.

#### Insights and Recommendations
- **Insight**: ~40% of cases (102, 105) involve escalations, which are the strongest predictor of delay. Non-escalated delays (104) point to Level-1 capacity issues.
- **Recommendations**:
  1. **Reduce Escalation Rate**: Train Level-1 agents on common issues (e.g., via quick-reference guides or AI triage tools) to handle 80% in-house. Target: Cut escalations by 50%.
  2. **SLA-Driven Assignment**: Implement auto-assignment with time triggers (e.g., alert if >30 mins post-assignment without progress). Use queues to balance Level-1/2 load.
  3. **24/7 Triage for Escalations**: On-call Level-2 rotation or outsourced investigation to prevent overnight gaps. Aim for <4-hour escalation response.
  4. **Monitoring Dashboard**: Track bottlenecks real-time (e.g., avg. wait post-assignment/escalation). Flag cases >2 hours from expected (benchmark: 101/103 path).
  5. **Root Cause Analysis**: Log ticket categories (missing in log) to identify escalation triggers (e.g., complex bugs) and upskill accordingly.

Implementing these could reduce average cycle time to <5 hours, with outliers under 10 hours.
