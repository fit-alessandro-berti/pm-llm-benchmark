**Anomalies Identified**

- Receive (R) to Approve (P) shows an average interval of approximately 25 hours with an extremely low standard deviation of 1 hour. This level of consistency is atypical for a multi-step claim process that should exhibit natural variation due to claim complexity, workload, or resource availability.
- Approve (P) to Notify (N) has a long average delay of 7 days accompanied by a high standard deviation of 2 days. The combination of extended duration and high variability points to inconsistent notification handling.
- Assign (A) to Close (C) averages only 2 hours with a 1-hour standard deviation. This interval is too short to accommodate the intervening Evaluate (E) and Approve (P) steps in most cases.
- Evaluate (E) to Notify (N) averages just 5 minutes with a 1-minute standard deviation. Such rapid transition suggests possible bypassing of required reviews or automated actions that occur without proper sequencing.

**Hypotheses on Causes**

- The rigid R-to-P timing may result from an automated approval rule or scheduled batch process that triggers at a fixed interval for certain claim types, overriding normal human review variability.
- The extended and variable P-to-N delay could stem from manual notification workflows subject to backlogs, resource shortages, or prioritization rules that differ by claim amount or region.
- Quick A-to-C closures might indicate cases where claims are being closed prematurely—possibly due to system defaults, data entry shortcuts, or attempts to bypass full evaluation for low-value or duplicate claims.
- The near-instant E-to-N transition could reflect fully automated notification logic triggered immediately after evaluation, potentially skipping required human oversight or documentation steps.

**Verification Approaches Using SQL Queries**

These queries focus on the `claim_events` table (joined with `claims` where needed for context) to surface specific instances and patterns.

```sql
-- 1. Identify claims with unusually consistent R-to-P timing (near 25 hours)
SELECT 
    ce.claim_id,
    MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'R' THEN timestamp END) AS receive_time,
    MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'P' THEN timestamp END) AS approve_time,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (
        MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'P' THEN timestamp END) - 
        MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'R' THEN timestamp END)
    )) / 3600.0 AS hours_between_r_p,
    c.claim_type,
    c.claim_amount
FROM claim_events ce
JOIN claims c ON ce.claim_id = c.claim_id
GROUP BY ce.claim_id, c.claim_type, c.claim_amount
HAVING 
    MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'P' THEN timestamp END) IS NOT NULL
    AND ABS(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (
        MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'P' THEN timestamp END) - 
        MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'R' THEN timestamp END)
    )) - 90000) < 7200  -- within ~2 hours of expected average
ORDER BY hours_between_r_p;
```

```sql
-- 2. Find claims with long or highly variable P-to-N delays
SELECT 
    ce.claim_id,
    MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'P' THEN timestamp END) AS approve_time,
    MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'N' THEN timestamp END) AS notify_time,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (
        MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'N' THEN timestamp END) - 
        MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'P' THEN timestamp END)
    )) / 86400.0 AS days_between_p_n,
    c.claim_type,
    ce.resource
FROM claim_events ce
JOIN claims c ON ce.claim_id = c.claim_id
GROUP BY ce.claim_id, c.claim_type, ce.resource
HAVING 
    MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'N' THEN timestamp END) IS NOT NULL
    AND EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (
        MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'N' THEN timestamp END) - 
        MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'P' THEN timestamp END)
    )) > 432000  -- more than 5 days
ORDER BY days_between_p_n DESC;
```

```sql
-- 3. Detect claims closed quickly after assignment (possible skipped steps)
SELECT 
    ce.claim_id,
    MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'A' THEN timestamp END) AS assign_time,
    MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'C' THEN timestamp END) AS close_time,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (
        MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'C' THEN timestamp END) - 
        MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'A' THEN timestamp END)
    )) / 3600.0 AS hours_a_to_c,
    c.claim_type,
    ce.resource
FROM claim_events ce
JOIN claims c ON ce.claim_id = c.claim_id
GROUP BY ce.claim_id, c.claim_type, ce.resource
HAVING 
    MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'C' THEN timestamp END) IS NOT NULL
    AND EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (
        MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'C' THEN timestamp END) - 
        MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'A' THEN timestamp END)
    )) < 14400  -- less than 4 hours
    AND NOT EXISTS (
        SELECT 1 FROM claim_events ce2 
        WHERE ce2.claim_id = ce.claim_id 
        AND ce2.activity IN ('E', 'P')
    )
ORDER BY hours_a_to_c;
```

```sql
-- 4. Analyze E-to-N timing by resource or claim type (group-level patterns)
SELECT 
    ce.resource,
    c.claim_type,
    AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (
        n.timestamp - e.timestamp
    ))) / 60.0 AS avg_minutes_e_to_n,
    STDDEV(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (
        n.timestamp - e.timestamp
    ))) / 60.0 AS stddev_minutes_e_to_n,
    COUNT(*) AS claim_count
FROM claim_events e
JOIN claim_events n ON e.claim_id = n.claim_id 
    AND n.activity = 'N' 
    AND n.timestamp > e.timestamp
JOIN claims c ON e.claim_id = c.claim_id
WHERE e.activity = 'E'
GROUP BY ce.resource, c.claim_type
HAVING AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n.timestamp - e.timestamp))) < 600  -- under 10 minutes
ORDER BY avg_minutes_e_to_n;
```

These queries can be extended with filters on `claim_amount`, `region` (via join to adjusters if resource maps to adjuster names), or date ranges to further isolate whether anomalies concentrate in specific customer segments or adjuster specializations.
