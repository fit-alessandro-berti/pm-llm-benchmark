# **Data-Driven Queue Mining Analysis for Outpatient Clinic Optimization**

## **1. Queue Identification and Characterization**

### **Calculating Waiting Times**
Using the event log with START/COMPLETE timestamps, waiting time (queue time) between two consecutive activities for a patient is calculated as:
`Waiting Time = Start Time of Activity B û Complete Time of Activity A`
*Example:* If Registration completes at 09:08:45 and Nurse Assessment starts at 09:15:20, the wait is 6 minutes 35 seconds.

**Key Queue Metrics to Calculate:**
- **Average Waiting Time:** Mean queue duration per transition.
- **Median Waiting Time:** Better indicator of typical experience, less skewed by outliers.
- **Maximum Waiting Time:** Identifies worst-case scenarios.
- **90th Percentile Waiting Time:** Critical for patient satisfaction (90% of patients wait less than this).
- **Queue Frequency:** Percentage of patient visits experiencing a wait at a specific transition.
- **Number of Cases with Excessive Waits:** Count of visits exceeding a clinically/patient-defined threshold (e.g., >30 minutes).

### **Identifying Critical Queues**
**Prioritization Criteria:**
1. **Highest 90th Percentile Wait:** Indicates frequent poor experiences for a subset of patients.
2. **High Queue Frequency Î Long Average Wait:** Combines prevalence and severity (e.g., 70% of patients wait 20+ mins for ECG).
3. **Impact on Vulnerable Cohorts:** Queues disproportionately affecting **Urgent** patients or **New** patients (who often require more time/resources).
4. **Bottleneck Propagation:** Queues that delay downstream high-value activities (e.g., long waits for Nurse Assessment delay Doctor Consultations).

*Example Prioritization:* If "Doctor Consultation  Check-out" has a 90th percentile wait of 45 minutes but only 10% of patients experience it, while "Registration  Nurse" has a 90th percentile of 25 minutes affecting 80% of patients, the latter is more critical for overall satisfaction.

## **2. Root Cause Analysis**

### **Potential Root Causes**
1. **Resource Bottlenecks:**
   - Insufficient number of nurses/rooms for peak demand periods.
   - Doctor specialization mismatch (e.g., all cardiologists busy while other specialists idle).
   - Diagnostic equipment (ECG/X-Ray) downtime or over-scheduling.

2. **Activity Dependencies & Handovers:**
   - Sequential vs. parallel task design (e.g., forcing all patients through Nurse Assessment before Doctor, even when unnecessary).
   - Manual handovers between departments causing idle time.

3. **Variability in Service Times:**
   - High variance in Doctor Consultation durations for similar patient types (e.g., complex vs. routine cases).
   - Unpredictable diagnostic test durations.

4. **Appointment Scheduling Policies:**
   - Overbooking or poor time slot allocation (e.g., clustering all New patients in morning).
   - Lack of differentiation between New/Follow-up appointment lengths.

5. **Patient Arrival & Urgency Patterns:**
   - Walk-in Urgent patients disrupting scheduled flows.
   - Patient no-shows or late arrivals creating idle gaps then sudden rushes.

### **Process Mining Techniques for Root Cause Analysis**
- **Resource Capacity & Utilization Analysis:** Identify staff/rooms with >85% utilizationùclassic bottleneck indicators.
- **Throughput Time Heatmaps:** Visualize average time spent per activity and wait between activities across the day. Peaks highlight systemic delays.
- **Variant Analysis:** Compare process paths of patients with short vs. long overall visits. Identify which activities/divergences correlate with delays.
- **Causal Association Mining:** Discover rules like *"IF patient type = New AND urgency = Normal THEN high probability of long wait at Nurse Assessment"*.
- **Social Network Analysis (SNA) of Handovers:** Map frequency/quality of handovers between roles (e.g., Clerk  Nurse  Doctor). Identify "central" roles causing congestion.

*Example Insight:* Variant analysis might reveal that patients routed directly from Registration to a specific diagnostic test (bypassing Nurse Assessment) have 40% shorter visits, suggesting the Nurse Assessment is a non-value-added step for certain cases.

## **3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies**

### **Strategy 1: Dynamic Resource Allocation Based on Real-Time Queue Triggers**
- **Targets:** Queues at **Nurse Assessment** and **Doctor Consultation** during peak hours (09:00û11:00).
- **Root Cause Addressed:** Static staffing schedules not aligned with demand variability.
- **Data Support:** Historical event log shows Nurse utilization >90% from 09:30û10:30, with average wait for Nurse Assessment spiking to 22 minutes. Resource analysis reveals one nurse often idle post-11:00.
- **Implementation:** 
  - Use live queue length data (from appointment system) to trigger floating resources (e.g., medical assistants cross-trained for basic assessments).
  - Schedule senior nurses for peak hours only, with part-timers covering late morning.
- **Expected Impact:** Reduce average Nurse wait by 35û50%, decrease overall visit duration by 15û20 minutes for 60% of patients.

### **Strategy 2: Revised Appointment Scheduling Logic with Patient Tiering**
- **Targets:** **Registration  Nurse** and **Doctor  Check-out** queues for New patients.
- **Root Cause Addressed:** Uniform 20-minute slots for New and Follow-up patients, despite New patients requiring 40% more time on average.
- **Data Support:** Variant analysis shows New patients have 35% longer Doctor Consultation durations. Scheduling analysis reveals 80% of 09:00û10:00 slots are for New patients, causing cascading delays.
- **Implementation:**
  - Implement **differentiated time slots**: 30 minutes for New patients, 15 minutes for Follow-ups.
  - Stagger New patient arrivals (e.g., 09:00, 09:30, 10:00) rather than clustering at 09:00.
  - Reserve 20% of slots as "flex" for walk-in Urgent cases.
- **Expected Impact:** Reduce New patient overall visit time by 25%, lower late-day waits for Follow-up patients by 40%.

### **Strategy 3: Parallel Processing of Diagnostic Tests for Low-Urgency Cases**
- **Targets:** Long **Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Test** waits (e.g., ECG, Blood Work).
- **Root Cause Addressed:** Sequential process design where all patients must wait for Doctor order before test, even when test is routine.
- **Data Support:** Event log shows 60% of ECG tests are for low-urgency, predictable cases (e.g., annual check-ups). Queue mining reveals average wait of 18 minutes for ECG post-consultation.
- **Implementation:**
  - Introduce **"pre-order diagnostic queues"**: For eligible low-urgency patients, order diagnostic test *during* consultation but allow patient to proceed to test *immediately after consultation* without returning to waiting area.
  - Use a dedicated "diagnostic fast-lane" staffed by a tech during peak hours.
- **Expected Impact:** Eliminate Doctor  Test wait for 60% of patients, reducing overall visit time by 12û18 minutes for these cases.

## **4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints**

### **Potential Trade-offs:**
- **Strategy 1 (Dynamic Staffing):** 
  - *Cost:* Increased labor costs for part-time/float staff.
  - *Shift in Bottleneck:* May reduce Nurse waits but increase waits at Check-out if Clerks are not similarly adjusted.
- **Strategy 2 (Tiering Slots):**
  - *Access Equity:* New patients may face longer wait for preferred appointment times.
  - *Provider Utilization:* Doctors may have underutilized time late morning if Follow-up slots are too short.
- **Strategy 3 (Parallel Processing):**
  - *Quality Risk:* Rushing tests may increase errors; requires robust eligibility criteria.
  - *Equipment Utilization:* Fast-lane may cause idle time for diagnostic equipment during off-peak.

### **Balancing Conflicting Objectives:**
- **Cost vs. Wait Time:** Use **simulation modeling** (based on event log data) to test staffing scenarios before implementation. Aim for the "knee of the curve" where incremental cost equals incremental wait reduction.
- **Speed vs. Care Quality:** Parallel processing only for **low-complexity, protocol-driven tests** (e.g., routine ECG, not for cardiac symptom evaluation). Monitor error rates post-implementation.
- **Staff Workload:** Any change must be co-designed with frontline staff to avoid burnout. Use process mining to monitor post-change resource utilizationùtarget 70û80% utilization (not 90%+), leaving buffer for variability.

## **5. Measuring Success**

### **Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)**
- **Primary:**
  - **Overall Throughput Time:** Average total visit duration from Registration to Check-out.
  - **90th Percentile Visit Time:** Ensures patient experience focus.
  - **Queue Frequency:** % of visits with any wait >15 minutes.
- **Secondary:**
  - **Resource Utilization:** % of time staff/rooms are actively engaged (target: 70û80%).
  - **Patient Satisfaction Scores:** Post-visit surveys (specific questions on wait times).
  - **No-Show/Cancellation Rates:** Indicator of scheduling acceptance.
  - **Care Quality Metrics:** Readmission rates, test error rates (to ensure speed didnÆt compromise quality).

### **Ongoing Process Monitoring**
- **Continuous Event Log Collection:** Maintain the same START/COMPLETE structure in the clinic's appointment/tracking system.
- **Automated Dashboards:**
  - Real-time queue length heatmaps by hour/activity.
  - Daily alerts for waits exceeding thresholds (e.g., Nurse wait >20 mins for >30% of patients).
  - Comparative analysis of pre/post-implementation KPIs with statistical significance testing.
- **Monthly Root Cause Reviews:** Re-run variant and bottleneck analysis to detect new emerging issues (e.g., "Check-out  Pharmacy" becoming new bottleneck after Strategy 3).

**Sustained Improvement:** Establish a **Process Mining Center of Excellence** within the clinic to institutionalize data-driven flow optimization, training staff to interpret queue metrics and propose micro-adjustments (e.g., daily staffing tweaks based on weather/calendar events known to affect patient volume).
