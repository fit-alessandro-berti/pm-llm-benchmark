## 1.  Highlevel process model (derived from the sample log)

| Highlevel step | Typical lowlevel activities (examples) | Typical responsible resource type | Why the activities belong together |
|-----------------|------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Material Reception & Identification** | Retrieve raw metal sheet, Scan barcode | Operator (human) | Both actions happen at the very beginning of a case, are performed by the same operator, and serve to bring the raw part into the workarea and uniquely identify it. |
| **Workpiece Setup** | Place metal sheet on table, Align metal sheet, Preheat metal sheet | Operator  Robot Arm  Heating Unit | These actions prepare the physical part for downstream. The sequence is tightly coupled in time (seconds apart) and each step depends on the previous one (placement  alignment  heating). |
| **Welding Assembly** | Pick up welding tool, Weld cornerA, Weld cornerB | Operator (welding technician) | The toolpickup is the immediate precursor to the welding actions; all three are performed by the same operator and constitute the core assembly operation for the part. |
| **Weld Quality Assurance** | Measure weld integrity | Quality Sensor | A dedicated measurement step that follows welding and precedes any downstream treatment. It is performed by a sensor, not a human, and its purpose is to verify the assembly quality. |
| **Protective Coating** | Apply protective coating, Dry coating | Coating Unit  Heating Unit | Coating and its subsequent drying are a logical pair that together deliver the protective finish. They are executed by two different machines but are part of the same “finish” phase. |
| **Final Visual Inspection** | Visual check | Operator (inspector) | The last humanperformed check that signs off the part before it leaves the line. It is temporally the final event in the case and is performed by a different operator from the earlier steps. |

## 2.  Rationale & grouping rules

| Rule | Description | Example from log |
|------|-------------|------------------|
| **Temporal proximity** | Events that occur within a few seconds to a minute of each other and have a clear causeeffect relationship are grouped together. | “Retrieve raw metal sheet”  “Scan barcode” (5s gap). |
| **Resource continuity** | When the same resource (or resource type) performs a series of actions, they are usually part of the same subprocess. | OperatorA does the first three actions  they form **Material Reception & Identification**. |
| **Logical dependency** | A step that cannot be performed before a predecessor belongs to the same highlevel phase. | Alignment cannot be done before the sheet is placed on the table  both belong to **Workpiece Setup**. |
| **Domain semantics** | Activities that share a common manufacturing purpose (e.g., “assembly”, “inspection”, “finishing”) are aggregated even if the resources differ. | “Apply protective coating” (Coating Unit) and “Dry coating” (Heating Unit) are both about **Protective Coating**. |
| **Boundary markers** | The start of a new highlevel step is signaled by a change in activity type or a new resource class. | The transition from welding (OperatorB) to measuring weld integrity (Quality Sensor) marks the start of **Weld Quality Assurance**. |

Applying these rules to the sample data yields the same six highlevel steps for **both** cases (A1 and B2).

## 4.  How to apply the same logic to the full log

1. **Sort events by `CaseID` and `Timestamp`.**  
2. **Detect “phase change” points** using the rules above:  
   * A change in resource class (human  robot  sensor).  
   * A gap larger than a configurable threshold (e.g.,>30s) that is not explained by a known handoff.  
   * A shift in activity semantics (e.g., from “weld* to “measure”).  
3. **Collect all events between two consecutive change points** into a highlevel step.  
4. **Assign the step a name** based on the dominant activity type and resource class (see the six names above).  
5. **Store the aggregated step** with its start/end timestamps and the list of lowlevel activities for downstream analysis (process mining, performance KPI, etc.).

By following this procedure the entire event log can be collapsed into a concise, humanreadable process model that preserves the essential workflow while hiding the noisy, granular details.
