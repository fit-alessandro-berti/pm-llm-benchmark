### **Process Mining & Queue Mining Analysis: Outpatient Clinic Optimization**

**Role:** Process Analyst, Healthcare Process Optimization  
**Objective:** Reduce patient waiting times and visit duration while maintaining care quality and cost efficiency using event log data.

---

### **1. Queue Identification and Characterization**

To move from raw data to actionable insights, we must first reconstruct the patient journey and isolate the non-value-added time (waiting) from value-added time (service).

#### **Calculating Waiting Times**
Using the event log, we reconstruct the trace for each `Case ID` by sorting events chronologically.
*   **Service Time:** For any activity, `Service Time = Timestamp(COMPLETE) - Timestamp(START)`.
*   **Waiting Time (Queue Time):** Defined as the idle time where the patient is ready for the next step but the resource/activity has not started.
    *   `Wait Time(Activity A  Activity B) = Timestamp(START of B) - Timestamp(COMPLETE of A)`.
    *   *Example:* For V1001, Wait(Registration  Nurse) = `09:15:20` (Nurse Start) - `09:08:45` (Reg Complete) = **6 minutes 35 seconds**.
*   **Pre-Process Wait:** `Timestamp(START of First Activity) - Scheduled Appointment Time` (if appointment data is linked) or arrival timestamp if available.

#### **Key Queue Metrics**
We will calculate the following metrics for every transition (queue) in the process:
*   **Central Tendency:** Mean and Median Wait Time. Median is crucial in healthcare as wait distributions are often right-skewed due to outliers.
*   **Tail Risk:** 90th and 95th Percentile Wait Times. This identifies the "worst-case" experience affecting a significant minority of patients.
*   **Volume & Frequency:** Total number of cases passing through the queue and the frequency of "Excessive Waits" (e.g., waits > 20 minutes).
*   **Queue Length Reconstruction:** Using queue mining techniques, we can estimate the instantaneous queue length at any timestamp by counting cases where `Activity A` is complete but `Activity B` has not started.
*   **Service Time Variability:** Coefficient of Variation (CV) of service times for the downstream activity. High variability often drives queue instability.

#### **Identifying Critical Queues**
Not all queues are equal. We prioritize using a **Weighted Impact Score**:
$$ \text{Impact Score} = (\text{Volume of Cases}) \times (\text{Average Wait Time}) \times (\text{Urgency Weight}) $$
*   **Criteria:**
    1.  **High Impact Score:** Queues affecting many patients with long waits (e.g., Wait for Doctor Consultation).
    2.  **Urgency Sensitivity:** Queues where `Urgency = Urgent` patients experience delays. Even low volume, high urgency waits are critical for safety and compliance.
    3.  **Bottleneck Propagation:** Queues that cause downstream starvation or upstream blocking.
*   **Justification:** This approach ensures we focus on changes that yield the highest aggregate reduction in patient wait time and address clinical risk, rather than optimizing rare or trivial delays.

### **2. Root Cause Analysis**

Identifying the queue is diagnostic; understanding the cause is prescriptive. We will employ advanced process mining and queue mining techniques to isolate root causes.

#### **Potential Root Causes & Analytical Techniques**

*   **Resource Bottlenecks & Utilization Imbalance:**
    *   *Hypothesis:* Specific staff or rooms are over-utilized, creating queues, while others are idle.
    *   *Analysis:* **Resource Utilization Heatmaps** and **Resource Profile Analysis**. We compare utilization rates across resources performing the same activity (e.g., Clerk A vs. Clerk B, or Dr. Smith vs. Dr. Jones). If Clerk A is at 95% utilization and Clerk B at 60%, the queue is caused by load imbalance, not lack of total capacity.
    *   *Queue Mining:* Plot **Arrival Rate vs. Service Rate**. If arrival rate consistently exceeds service rate during specific windows, queues are inevitable.

*   **Variability in Service Times:**
    *   *Hypothesis:* Unpredictable consultation durations cause schedule slippage and queue buildup.
    *   *Analysis:* **Service Time Distribution Fitting**. Analyze the variance of `Doctor Consultation` durations segmented by `Patient Type` and `Specialty`. If "New" patients have a standard deviation of 15 minutes while "Follow-up" has 5 minutes, scheduling them in identical slots causes queues.

*   **Appointment Scheduling Mismatches:**
    *   *Hypothesis:* Patient arrivals are bunched (e.g., all scheduled at :00 and :30), overwhelming capacity, or slot durations do not match actual service times.
    *   *Analysis:* **Dotted Chart Analysis** of arrival patterns vs. resource availability. Correlate `Wait Time` with `Time of Day`. A spike in waits at 09:30 suggests a "morning rush" batching effect.

*   **Process Variants & Rework:**
    *   *Hypothesis:* Certain patient paths involve loops or unnecessary handovers that increase wait times.
    *   *Analysis:* **Variant Analysis** and **Process Map Overlay**. Compare the "Happy Path" against variants with high wait times. For example, do patients requiring `ECG Test` wait significantly longer because the process forces a sequential `Doctor  ECG  Doctor` loop, whereas a parallel workflow could reduce wait?
    *   *Segmentation:* Filter logs by `Patient Type`. "New" patients may experience longer waits due to missing pre-visit data, causing delays at Registration or Nurse Assessment.

*   **Handover Delays:**
    *   *Hypothesis:* Delays occur not because of resource lack, but because the next resource is unaware the patient is ready.
    *   *Analysis:* Analyze waits between activities involving different resource groups (e.g., Nurse to Doctor). If service times are low but waits are high at handovers, the issue is coordination/notification, not capacity.

### **3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies**

Based on the analysis, here are three concrete strategies. These assume the analysis reveals common clinic patterns: variability-driven doctor queues, peak-load registration queues, and sequential diagnostic delays.

#### **Strategy 1: Predictive, Segmented Appointment Scheduling**
*   **Target Queue:** Wait for Doctor Consultation; Wait for Nurse Assessment.
*   **Root Cause:** High variability in service times based on `Patient Type` and `Specialty`; mismatch between slot duration and actual need; bunching of arrivals.
*   **Data Support:** Analysis shows `New` patients take 40% longer than `Follow-up` with higher variance. Current scheduling uses fixed 15-minute slots for all. Dotted charts show arrival spikes at the top of the hour correlating with wait spikes.
*   **Action:**
    *   Implement **Variable Slot Scheduling**: Assign slot durations based on historical median service time + buffer for the specific `Patient Type` and `Specialty`.
    *   **Staggered Arrivals**: Shift appointment templates to stagger arrivals (e.g., :00, :10, :20) rather than batching, smoothing the arrival rate to match service capacity.
    *   **Predictive Overbooking**: Use data on no-show probabilities (if available) to intelligently overbook only low-risk slots, maximizing utilization without increasing waits.
*   **Expected Impact:** Reduction in average Doctor Wait Time by **20-25%**; reduction in 90th percentile wait by **30%** due to reduced schedule slippage.

#### **Strategy 2: Dynamic Resource Leveling and Cross-Training**
*   **Target Queue:** Wait for Registration; Wait for Check-out.
*   **Root Cause:** Load imbalance between resources; temporal mismatch where administrative staff are idle during mid-day but overwhelmed during morning peaks.
*   **Data Support:** Resource heatmaps show Clerk A utilization >90% while Clerk B <65% during peak hours. Queue length reconstruction shows Registration queues peak 08:30–09:30 and vanish by 11:00, while Check-out queues peak 11:00–12:00.
*   **Action:**
    *   **Load Balancing Rules**: Implement a "Next Available" routing logic in the patient management system rather than static assignment to clerks.
    *   **Cross-Training & Flex Staffing**: Cross-train Check-out staff to assist with Registration during the morning peak. Shift staff schedules by 30 minutes to align with the data-driven demand curve.
    *   **Self-Service Kiosks**: For `Follow-up` patients with low complexity, data may show registration is purely confirmation. Deploy kiosks to offload volume from clerks.
*   **Expected Impact:** Reduction in Registration Wait Time by **40%** during peak hours; improved staff utilization balance; cost-neutral as it uses existing staff more efficiently.

#### **Strategy 3: Protocol-Driven Parallel Diagnostics (Pre-Consultation Testing)**
*   **Target Queue:** Wait for Diagnostic Tests; Wait for Specialist Review; Total Visit Duration.
*   **Root Cause:** Sequential dependency where patients wait for the doctor to order a test, wait for the test, and wait for the doctor to review results. Variant analysis shows this loop adds significant cycle time.
*   **Data Support:** Variant analysis reveals that 30% of `Cardio` visits include an `ECG Test`. In these cases, the `Doctor  ECG  Doctor` sequence adds an average of 35 minutes to visit duration, with 15 minutes being wait time for the ECG tech and re-queueing for the doctor.
*   **Action:**
    *   **Standing Orders**: Implement clinical protocols where `Nurse Assessment` triggers automatic ECG orders for specific patient profiles (e.g., Chest Pain, Hypertension follow-up) based on data patterns.
    *   **Parallel Workflow**: The ECG is performed *before* or *during* the doctor consultation preparation, rather than after. The doctor reviews results immediately during the consult.
    *   **Result:** Eliminates the second queue for the doctor and reduces the dependency wait for the test.
*   **Expected Impact:** Reduction in Total Visit Duration for diagnostic cases by **25%**; reduction in Doctor Re-consult Wait by **100%** (eliminated); improved patient experience due to "one-stop" flow.

### **4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints**

Optimization is rarely free of side effects. We must manage these trade-offs carefully.

*   **Shifting Bottlenecks:**
    *   *Risk:* Reducing Registration waits may flood the Nurse queue earlier.
    *   *Mitigation:* Use **System-Wide Simulation**. Before implementation, simulate the proposed changes using the event log data to predict downstream effects. Ensure the "bottleneck" moves to a controlled point or is dissolved entirely.
*   **Cost vs. Wait Time:**
    *   *Risk:* Adding resources reduces waits but increases costs.
    *   *Mitigation:* Strategies 1 and 3 are process/protocol changes with minimal cost. Strategy 2 optimizes existing resources. We prioritize **efficiency gains over capacity additions**. If costs must increase, the ROI must be justified by increased throughput (revenue) or reduced penalty risks.
*   **Quality of Care:**
    *   *Risk:* Reducing service times or parallelizing tests might lead to rushed consultations or unnecessary tests.
    *   *Mitigation:* **Monitor Service Time KPIs**. Ensure average consultation duration does not drop below clinical standards. For Strategy 3, strict clinical criteria for standing orders must be enforced to prevent over-testing. Quality audits should be part of the feedback loop.
*   **Staff Workload & Satisfaction:**
    *   *Risk:* Dynamic scheduling or cross-training may increase cognitive load or resistance from staff.
    *   *Mitigation:* Involve staff in the design. Show data proving that current chaos causes more stress than structured flexibility. Ensure cross-training comes with appropriate incentives or workload recognition.
*   **Urgency Prioritization:**
    *   *Risk:* Optimizing for average wait might disadvantage `Urgent` patients if not carefully managed.
    *   *Mitigation:* Maintain **Priority Queuing Rules**. The scheduling and routing logic must always respect `Urgency` flags. KPIs must be segmented by urgency to ensure urgent waits remain near zero.

---

### **5. Measuring Success**

To ensure sustained improvement, we will establish a continuous monitoring framework using the same event log structure.

#### **Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)**
1.  **Patient Flow Efficiency:**
    *   *Average & 90th Percentile Wait Time* per activity and total.
    *   *Total Cycle Time* (Door-to-Door).
    *   *Flow Efficiency Ratio* = (Total Service Time / Total Cycle Time). Target: Increase ratio.
2.  **Service Quality & Safety:**
    *   *% of Urgent Patients Seen within Target Time* (e.g., <10 mins).
    *   *Consultation Duration Stability* (Ensure no reduction in care time).
    *   *Re-work Rate* (Frequency of patients returning for missing steps).
3.  **Resource Performance:**
    *   *Resource Utilization Balance* (Variance across staff).
    *   *Overtime Hours* (Proxy for scheduling failure).
4.  **Patient Experience:**
    *   *Patient Satisfaction Score* (Linked to Visit ID if possible).
    *   *Left Without Being Seen (LWBS) Rate*.

#### **Ongoing Process Monitoring**
*   **Operational Dashboards:** Deploy real-time dashboards powered by process mining tools that ingest daily event logs. Display current queue lengths and predicted waits to floor managers for immediate intervention.
*   **Control Charts:** Implement Statistical Process Control (SPC) charts for key wait times. Set upper control limits based on post-optimization baselines. Alerts trigger when performance drifts, indicating a need for investigation.
*   **Conformance Checking:** Continuously run conformance checks against the optimized process model. Detect if staff are reverting to old habits or if new deviations are emerging.
*   **Periodic Review:** Monthly "Process Health" reviews using variant analysis to identify new bottlenecks or changes in patient mix that require scheduling adjustments.

By rigorously applying queue mining to characterize delays, using process mining to diagnose root causes, and implementing data-backed strategies while monitoring trade-offs, the clinic can achieve a significant, sustainable improvement in patient throughput and satisfaction without compromising care quality or financial viability.
