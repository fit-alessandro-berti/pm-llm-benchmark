### 2. Constraint Analysis: Ensuring Compliance and Order

The DECLARE model uses specific constraints to enforce a "Compliance-First" architecture. Here is how each logical rule translates to process control:

*   **`init` & `existence`:** These ensure the process is **traceable**. A loan process cannot be considered valid unless an application was formally received and documented.
*   **`response` & `succession`:** These enforce **causality**. You cannot gather documents before checking credit (`succession`), and receiving an application guarantees a credit check will occur (`response`). This prevents "blind" processing where documents are collected without risk assessment.
*   **`precedence`:** This enforces **temporal order**. `Quality_Assurance_Review` must precede `Authorize_Contract_Terms`. This prevents a manager from signing off on a loan offer before it has been vetted for regulatory compliance.
*   **`coexistence`:** This enforces **consistency**. `Gather_Additional_Documents` and `Authorize_Contract_Terms` must either both happen or neither. This prevents a scenario where a contract is authorized without the necessary supporting documentation on file.
*   **`absence`:** This acts as a **hard stop**. `Proceed_Without_Compliance` is strictly prohibited. In a real system, this would likely trigger an automatic alert or halt the transaction if a "skip compliance" flag is detected.
*   **`chain` constraints:** These enforce **immediate adjacency**. `chainsuccession(QA, Assemble)` means the QA review must happen immediately before assembly. This minimizes the risk of the offer being altered after the review but before finalization.
