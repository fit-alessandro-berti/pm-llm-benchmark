# Bias Analysis: Comparing Group A and Group B Event Logs

## Which Log Exhibits Bias?

**Group B's event log exhibits bias.** The mechanism is the **+5 "Association Boost"** applied to the CulturalFit score for candidates affiliated with the *LocalBusinessLeadersClub*. Group A's log, by contrast, shows a uniform process where all candidates are evaluated solely on their measured scores (ScoreAdjustment = 0 for every case).

## How the Bias Manifests

### 1. Score manipulation at a decision-critical stage
The boost is injected at the **CulturalFitCheck** activity and carried forward into the FinalInterview and HiringDecision — precisely the stages where outcomes are determined:

- **U001:** CulturalFit raised from 60  65  **Hired**
- **U003:** CulturalFit raised from 58  63  **Hired**
- **U002** (no association): CulturalFit stayed at 60  **Not Hired**

### 2. Cross-group inconsistency reveals the unfairness
The most telling comparisons are *across* the logs:

| Case | SkillScore | Personality | CulturalFit (raw) | Adjustment | Decision |
|------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|------------|----------|
| P002 (Group A) | 78 | 75 | 60 | 0 | **Not Hired** |
| U002 (Group B) | 78 | 70 | 60 | 0 | **Not Hired** |
| U003 (Group B) | **75** | **65** | **58** | +5 | **Hired** |

U003 has the **weakest raw profile of any candidate in either log** — lower skill, personality, and cultural fit scores than P002 and U002 — yet is hired purely because the adjustment lifted the cultural fit score above an apparent threshold (~63–65). Meanwhile, P002, who is objectively stronger on every dimension, is rejected.

### 3. Proxy discrimination
The bias is indirect but structural:

- The *LocalBusinessLeadersClub* membership correlates perfectly with **LocalResident = TRUE** in this data.
- All Group A (protected) candidates are non-local residents (LocalResident = FALSE) and have **no realistic access** to this association.
- Thus, "professional association" functions as a **proxy attribute** for residency/group membership. Even though the rule never mentions the protected attribute, it systematically advantages the unprotected group — a classic case of disparate impact.

### 4. Erosion of consistent decision thresholds
The data suggests a hiring cutoff around CulturalFit  63–65. Group A candidates must *earn* this score outright (P001: 65, P003: 65 — hired); Group B candidates can fall short and still be lifted across the threshold artificially. The same nominal process is applied with different effective standards.

## Implications for Fairness and Equity

1. **Disparate impact:** Candidates from the protected group face a stricter de facto bar, since they cannot obtain the boost. Hiring rates become decoupled from merit.

2. **Merit inversion:** Less qualified candidates (U003) displace more qualified ones (P002), undermining the validity of the entire assessment pipeline and harming organizational quality.

3. **Network privilege entrenchment:** Rewarding membership in an exclusive local club institutionalizes existing social capital — a "who you know" advantage — and perpetuates homogeneity, contradicting the purpose of structured, score-based hiring.

4. **Legal and compliance risk:** Even facially neutral criteria that disproportionately exclude a protected class can constitute unlawful indirect discrimination under anti-discrimination frameworks (e.g., Title VII disparate impact doctrine, EU equal treatment directives).

5. **Auditability concern (process mining perspective):** Encouragingly, the bias *is* detectable here because the ScoreAdjustment is explicitly logged. In practice, such boosts are often hidden inside aggregate scores. This underscores the value of fine-grained event logs for fairness auditing — and the danger when adjustments are applied silently.

## Recommendation

Remove the Association Boost entirely, or replace it with criteria that are (a) demonstrably job-relevant, (b) equally accessible to all applicants, and (c) validated for adverse impact. Hiring decisions should be re-evaluated against raw scores with a single, consistently applied threshold, and the event log should continue capturing any adjustments explicitly to enable ongoing algorithmic and procedural fairness audits.
