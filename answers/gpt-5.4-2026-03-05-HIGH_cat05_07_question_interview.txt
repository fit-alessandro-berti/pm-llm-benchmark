Here is a structured set of targeted, open-ended questions I would ask to better understand the property onboarding process:

## 1. Process scope and trigger
1. What exactly triggers the start of the onboarding process, and are there different entry points depending on how the landlord reaches out?
2. How do you define when a property is officially considered “in onboarding” versus still in an early inquiry stage?
3. Are there any types of properties, landlords, or arrangements that fall outside this process or follow a different version of it?
4. What would you say is the formal end point of onboarding: listing activation, tenant placement, move-in, or some later milestone?

## 2. Initial intake and documentation review
5. What information do you typically gather from the landlord at the very beginning, before documentation review starts?
6. How do you determine which documents are required for a given property, and when do requirements vary by city, property type, or ownership structure?
7. What are the most common reasons documentation is considered incomplete or insufficient?
8. How do you decide whether an outdated document is acceptable temporarily versus requiring a refreshed version before moving forward?
9. Who has authority to approve documentation as “verified,” and is there ever a second-level review?
10. How are urgent or high-value properties handled if some documents are still pending?
11. What happens when a landlord is slow to provide updates or disputes a documentation requirement?

## 3. Data capture and property setup
12. What property details are most important to capture accurately at the database-entry stage, and why?
13. How do you validate that the property information entered into the central database is complete and accurate?
14. Are there fields or attributes that are often difficult to collect or frequently change during onboarding?
15. At what point is a property record created in the system: before document verification, after verification, or somewhere in between?
16. How do you handle corrections if property details change after they have already been entered?

## 4. Roles and responsibilities
17. Can you walk me through the main roles involved in onboarding and what each one owns from start to finish?
18. Where do responsibilities overlap between the coordinator, property manager, leasing agent, compliance officer, financial analyst, and IT team?
19. Who is ultimately accountable for moving the process forward when multiple teams are involved?
20. How do you decide when the property manager handles something directly versus handing it to a specialist or support team?
21. Are there situations where a landlord interacts with several internal teams directly, and if so, how is communication coordinated?

## 5. Property manager assignment
22. How is the property manager assignment actually made in practice when workload, geography, experience, and specialization point to different candidates?
23. Which assignment criteria tend to carry the most weight, and how do you handle tradeoffs between them?
24. Are there formal rules for assignment, or is it largely based on manager judgment and availability?
25. What happens if the assigned property manager later becomes unavailable or the landlord requests a different manager?
26. How do you handle onboarding for properties that span multiple regions or don’t fit neatly into one manager’s specialization?

## 6. Introductory meeting with the landlord
27. What are the main objectives of the introductory call or meeting from your perspective?
28. What topics must always be covered in that conversation, regardless of property type?
29. How are special landlord requirements documented and translated into actionable next steps?
30. If the landlord requests terms that differ from your standard practices, how is that evaluated and approved?
31. How do you confirm that expectations between the landlord and property manager are aligned before proceeding?

## 7. Inspection, photography, and readiness
32. How do you decide when the property is ready for photography and inspection?
33. What is the usual sequencing between photography, inspection, and any required repairs?
34. Are the photographer and inspector scheduled in parallel or only after certain prerequisites are met?
35. What kinds of inspection findings are most common, and which ones typically delay onboarding?
36. How do you determine whether issues can be resolved quickly versus requiring a broader remediation plan?
37. Who decides what repairs are necessary before listing versus what can be addressed later?
38. If contractors are needed, how are they selected and who manages their work?
39. How do you handle disagreements with the landlord about inspection findings or repair recommendations?
40. What happens if a property repeatedly fails inspection or cannot be brought into compliance quickly?

## 8. Marketing and pricing
41. Once a property passes inspection, what information is handed off to the marketing team, and in what form?
42. How does the marketing team decide what listing content, imagery, and messaging are appropriate for different property types?
43. How are pricing recommendations developed, and who has the final say on the published rental price?
44. What happens if the landlord wants a price that differs from your pricing model or market recommendation?
45. How often is pricing revisited during onboarding if market conditions or property readiness change?
46. Are there cases where a property is approved internally but marketing is delayed, and what usually causes that?

## 9. Activation in systems
47. What conditions must be met before a property can be activated in the rental portal and internal systems?
48. Are there any approvals or checkpoints required before activation, and who signs off on them?
49. How do you know that a property is fully visible and functioning correctly across all relevant systems after activation?
50. What kinds of activation or integration issues occur most often, and how are they usually detected?

## 10. Tenant screening and selection
51. How does the tenant screening process begin once the property is active and applications start coming in?
52. What are the major criteria used to evaluate applicants, and how flexible are those criteria across different properties or landlords?
53. How do you handle situations where the landlord wants to influence tenant selection in ways that may conflict with company policy or regulations?
54. When there are multiple qualified applicants, how do you decide between them?
55. What role does the property manager play versus the leasing agent in screening, negotiation, and final selection?
56. How do you approach exceptions, such as applicants with limited credit history, international tenants, or self-employed applicants?
57. What usually leads to lease term negotiation, and who is authorized to make concessions like discounts or longer commitments?
58. How do you document the rationale for selecting one tenant over another?

## 11. Lease finalization and move-in preparation
59. What steps are involved in moving from tenant selection to lease execution?
60. How are custom lease clauses reviewed, approved, and incorporated when landlords request them?
61. What checks are done before the lease is considered final and ready for signature?
62. How do you handle delays in deposit collection, lease signing, or move-in scheduling?
63. What needs to happen operationally between lease signing and the actual move-in date?
64. Are there any final property readiness checks performed specifically for move-in?

## 12. Compliance, audit, and internal review
65. At what points in the process do compliance and audit reviews occur, and what triggers them?
66. What are the most common compliance risks during onboarding?
67. How does the compliance officer review fair housing adherence in practice?
68. What does the financial analyst evaluate when reviewing rental price and profitability, and what happens if there is a mismatch?
69. What does IT verify during the onboarding process beyond just the listing being visible?
70. If one of these internal reviewers identifies a problem late in the process, how is that escalated and resolved?

## 13. Exceptions and special cases
71. What are the most frequent exceptions that cause the standard onboarding flow to change?
72. How do you handle properties that require special insurance, unusual licensing, or city-specific approvals?
73. What happens when a landlord insists on non-standard business terms or unusual lease provisions?
74. How do you manage onboarding for properties with significant repairs, partial readiness, or phased unit availability?
75. Are there cases where onboarding is paused, canceled, or restarted, and what typically causes that?
76. How do you document and track exceptions so they don’t get lost across teams?

## 14. Communication and coordination
77. How do teams typically communicate during onboarding, and how do you decide which tool is used for what purpose?
78. How do you ensure that important decisions made over email or in meetings are reflected in the official process record?
79. Where do handoffs between teams tend to break down or require extra follow-up?
80. How do landlords receive status updates, and how frequently are those updates provided?
81. When there is conflicting information across email, CRM records, and project tools, how do you determine the source of truth?
82. What kinds of communication issues create the biggest delays or misunderstandings?

## 15. Timing, sequencing, and service levels
83. What is the typical timeline for onboarding a straightforward property from first contact to activation?
84. Which steps tend to be the longest or most variable?
85. What dependencies are most important to understand in the sequence of activities?
86. Which tasks can happen in parallel, and which must wait for prior approval or completion?
87. Are there service-level expectations or target turnaround times for specific teams involved in onboarding?
88. What factors most often cause the process to accelerate or slow down?

## 16. Decision-making and governance
89. What are the biggest judgment calls people make during onboarding, and who makes them?
90. Where in the process do you rely on formal policy versus individual experience or discretion?
91. How are disputes resolved when different stakeholders have different priorities, such as speed, compliance, profitability, or landlord preference?
92. Are there thresholds that trigger escalation to regional leadership or legal review?
93. What approvals are required for exceptions to standard pricing, lease terms, or readiness requirements?

## 17. Volume, complexity, and variation
94. How much does this process vary by city, region, or regulatory environment?
95. How does the onboarding process differ for single-family homes, multifamily buildings, luxury units, furnished rentals, or student housing?
96. How does the process change when you are onboarding one unit versus a whole portfolio from the same landlord?
97. What kinds of landlords tend to require the most customized handling?

## 18. Quality, performance, and improvement
98. How do you measure whether the onboarding process is working well?
99. What metrics or signals tell you that a property was onboarded successfully versus with avoidable issues?
100. Where do you see the most rework in the current process?
101. What are the most common bottlenecks or pain points for staff?
102. What are the most common frustrations for landlords during onboarding?
103. Have there been recent changes to this process, and what drove those changes?
104. If you could improve one part of the onboarding journey immediately, what would it be and why?

## 19. End-to-end walk-through and examples
105. Could you walk me through a recent example of a property that moved through the process smoothly from start to finish?
106. Could you also describe a difficult onboarding case and explain what made it complex?
107. In those examples, where were the key decision points and who made the decisions?
108. What lessons from those cases have influenced how the process works today?
