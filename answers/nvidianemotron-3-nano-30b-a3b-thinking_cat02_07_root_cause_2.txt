## 3.  Why might these attributes cause the extra time?

| Rootcause hypothesis | Supporting evidence | How it translates into delay |
|-----------------------|---------------------|------------------------------|
| **1. Highcomplexity triggers a “documentrequest cascade.”** | Both long cases have **2** “Request Additional Documents” steps, each followed by a waiting period before the next step can be taken. | Each roundtrip (insurer  claimant) adds *at least* several hours (often days) and the system must wait for the claimant’s reply. |
| **2. Certain adjusters are overloaded or lack enough expertise.** | The same two adjusters (Mike & Lisa) handle all highcomplexity claims; they perform the bulk of the documentrequest activities. | Their backlog forces them to schedule new requests later in the day, creating idle gaps and pushing the “Approve” step to the next business day. |
| **3. No explicit SLA for “time to close after a request.”** | The log shows no timestamp of *when* a request was sent vs. when a response was received; the system simply logs the next activity when it occurs. | If a response arrives late, the process automatically stretches; there is no earlywarning or escalation trigger. |
| **4. Regionlevel routing does not differentiate workload.** | Both slow cases are in different regions (A and B) but share the same highcomplexity label. | If RegionB historically receives more highcomplexity claims, its adjusters may already be saturated, leading to longer wait times. |
| **5. Manual handoffs between resources cause “handoff latency.”** | “Evaluate Claim” is done by an Adjuster, then a *different* resource (Adjuster again) performs the request for documents, then a Manager must reapprove. | Each handoff adds a timestamp gap; the more handoffs, the larger the cumulative latency. |

## 4.  Mitigation recommendations  

| Recommendation | Rationale (maps to rootcause) | Expected impact |
|----------------|-------------------------------|-----------------|
| **A. Implement a “ComplexClaim Queue” with dedicated adjusters** | Concentrates highcomplexity work away from general adjusters (Mike & Lisa) and prevents them from being overloaded. | Reduces bottlenecks; each claim stays within the same specialist’s pipeline, cutting handoff latency. |
| **B. Set an SLA for “DocumentRequest  Response”** (e.g., 4h) and automate a reminder/escalation if breached | Directly tackles the long waiting periods that inflate lead time. | Guarantees quicker turnaround on each request, shrinking the overall cycle. |
| **C. Introduce a “singlesubmission” rule for additional documents** – claimants upload all needed files in one go, and the system autoclassifies missing items | Eliminates multiple backandforth requests. | Cuts the number of “Request Additional Documents” events from 24 down to 01, slashing lead time dramatically. |
| **D. Enable automatic routing of highcomplexity claims to a *prescreened* “documentrequest specialist” team** | Leverages existing expertise (e.g., senior adjusters) rather than relying on a single person’s schedule. | Improves speed and quality of document collection, reducing rework. |
| **E. Monitor and visualize “timeinstep” metrics per resource/region** (e.g., average hours spent in “Evaluate Claim” or “Request Additional Documents”) | Provides early warning when a particular resource or region is trending upward. | Allows proactive workload rebalancing before SLA breaches become systemic. |
| **F. Offer targeted training on “highcomplexity claim handling”** (e.g., bestpractice checklist for document requests) | Improves firsttime completeness of document requests, lowering the need for followups. | Fewer rerequests  shorter cycles. |
| **G. Consider regionspecific capacity planning** – if RegionB consistently receives more highcomplexity claims, add temporary staff or shift workload from RegionA. | Addresses any latent regional imbalance that may surface in the data. | Balances workload, prevents any single region from becoming a bottleneck. |

## 5.  Quick “whatif” sanity check  

If we **remove the extra documentrequest steps** from the two slow cases (i.e., assume they only needed one request), the leadtime would drop from ~54h and ~79h to roughly **30h** and **45h** respectively – still longer than the lowcomplexity cases, but far less extreme. This illustrates how powerful the *documentrequest loop* is as a driver of delay.

### TL;DR

1. **Cases 2003 and 2005** are the outliers (1day).  
2. Both are **highcomplexity**, involve **multiple “Request Additional Documents”** events, and are processed by the same limited set of adjusters (Mike & Lisa).  
3. The underlying root cause is **the repetitive requestforinformation loop**, which adds waiting time and forces later approvals.  
4. **Fixes**: dedicate specialists, enforce an SLA on document turnaround, automate the collection of all needed paperwork, and monitor perresource cycle times. Implementing these steps should bring the longrunning cases down to a normal, subday range.
