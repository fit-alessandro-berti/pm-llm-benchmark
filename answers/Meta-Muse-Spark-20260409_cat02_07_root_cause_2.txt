To find the root causes, let's first calculate lead times per case and then correlate them with Resource, Region, and Complexity.

**Performance issue threshold**: Cases 2002, 2003, and 2005 take 17x to 51x longer than the fastest cases. All three have `Request Additional Documents` steps, while 2001 and 2004 do not.

### 2. Attribute analysis: What's driving long lead times?

**Complexity**  
- **Low**: 1.4-1.5 hours, no document requests, straight-through processing.  
- **Medium**: 25.9 hours, 1 document request, ~20h wait between request and approval.  
- **High**: 48.3-77.1 hours, 2-3 document requests, multi-day waits.  
**Correlation**: Very strong. Higher complexity → more document requests → much longer lead time. The wait time after each `Request Additional Documents` is the main bottleneck: 2002 waited 20h, 2003 waited 23h after the 2nd request, 2005 waited 24h and 22h after requests.

**Resource**  
- **Adjuster_Lisa**: Appears in 2002, 2004, 2005. Cases 2002 and 2005 are slow, but 2004 is fast. Lisa only requests docs on Medium/High claims, not Low.  
- **Adjuster_Mike**: In 2001 fast, 2003 slow. Same pattern: requests docs only on High.  
- **Manager_Bill**: Only approves High complexity cases 2003, 2005, both slow. His approval step comes after multiple doc requests. Manager_Ann approves Low/Medium faster.  
- **CSR_Paul**: Involved in two slowest cases 2002, 2005.  
**Correlation**: No resource is universally slow. Instead, specific resource-complexity combinations matter. `Manager_Bill` only handles High claims, which take longer by nature. `Adjuster_Lisa` has the highest number of doc requests, but only on complex cases.

**Region**  
- **Region A**: Cases 2001: 1.5h Low, 2003: 48.3h High. Avg 24.9h  
- **Region B**: Cases 2002: 25.9h Medium, 2004: 1.4h Low, 2005: 77.1h High. Avg 34.8h  
**Correlation**: Weak. Both regions handle fast Low claims well. Region B has the slowest case, but it also has a fast one. The difference seems driven by complexity mix, not region itself. However, Region B has 2/3 of the doc-request cases.

### 3. Root cause hypotheses and why these attributes matter

**Primary root cause: Complexity drives document requests**  
High and Medium complexity claims inherently need more verification. Each `Request Additional Documents` adds ~20-24 hours. This is likely waiting for the customer to respond, not internal delay. Three requests in case 2005 explain its 77h duration. So complexity isn't the direct cause; it's the *rework loop* triggered by complexity.

**Secondary root cause: Potential resource specialization/bottleneck**  
1. **Manager_Bill**: Only appears on High complexity cases, which all have long cycles. He may be the only manager authorized for High claims, creating a bottleneck. His approval in 2005 came 19h after the last doc request, suggesting either batching or workload issues.  
2. **Adjuster_Lisa**: Responsible for all 4 document requests in Region B. She may be more conservative and request docs more often, or she handles the most complex claims. Her two requests on 2005 were 1.5 days apart, indicating possible re-work due to incomplete first submission.  
3. **CSR_Paul**: On both multi-day cases. Could be workload or customer follow-up practices.

**Tertiary: Process design, not geography**  
Region doesn't directly cause delay. But Region B has more complex claims in this sample. If claim complexity isn't evenly distributed geographically, Region B will look slower.

### 4. Suggestions to mitigate root causes

**For Complexity / Document Requests**  
- **Upfront checklist**: Give customers a clear list of required docs by complexity level at `Submit Claim`. This prevents the request loop. Cases 2001 and 2004 show that when all info is present, closure is <2h.  
- **Partial submission rules**: For High claims, make initial submission impossible without key docs. Reduces 2-3 request cycles seen in 2003, 2005.  
- **SLA for customer response**: Auto-reminders at 24h if docs not received. The 20h+ gaps are customer wait time.

**For Resource-related issues**  
- **Manager_Bill capacity**: If he's the sole approver for High claims, add another authorized manager or delegate. His two cases are 48h+ and 77h.  
- **Adjuster calibration**: Compare `Adjuster_Lisa` vs `Adjuster_Mike` doc request rates on similar complexity. Train on what constitutes "enough" info to avoid repeat requests. Case 2005 had 3 requests vs 2003's 2 for same High complexity.  
- **Load balancing**: CSR_Paul had 2 complex cases start within 20 min. Check if workload affects follow-up speed.

**For Measurement**  
1. Track "Customer Wait Time" vs "Internal Processing Time" separately. Most delay here looks external.  
2. Add an attribute `Number_of_Doc_Requests` to flag at-risk cases early. Any case with ≥2 requests will likely exceed 48h.  
3. Segment SLAs by complexity: e.g., Low <4h, Medium <36h, High <72h. Then 2002 would be within SLA but 2003 and 2005 would still breach.

**Bottom line**: The data points to complexity-induced rework as the dominant factor, not region or a single bad resource. Fixing the document collection at submission would cut lead times by ~90% for complex claims.
