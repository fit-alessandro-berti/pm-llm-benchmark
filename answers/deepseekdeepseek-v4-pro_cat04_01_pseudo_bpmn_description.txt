This pseudo-BPMN models an end-to-end order-handling process that begins with a customer request and ends either with a confirmed order (standard or custom) or with a rejection. The process branches early based on the nature of the request, runs parallel checks for standard orders, performs feasibility for custom orders, and then uses a shared approval/rework loop before finalising the invoice and notifying the customer. Below is a stepbystep explanation.

---

### 1. Start and Initial Capture
**Start Event  Task A: ōReceive Customer Requestö**  
The process is initiated when a customer request (an order or an enquiry) is received, for example via a web portal, email, or phone. Task A captures the request details, including the **request type** (Standard or Custom), customer information, product specifics, and quantities.

---

### 2. Routing Based on Request Type
**XOR Gateway: ōCheck Request Typeö**  
An exclusive (XOR) decision gateway evaluates the `Request Type` attribute. Exactly one outgoing path is taken:

- **Standard**  proceed to basic validation and parallel resource checks.
- **Custom**  proceed to a feasibility analysis, because a customised item requires special engineering or sourcing.

---

### 3. The Standard Request Path
**Task B1: ōPerform Standard Validationö**  
The standard order is checked for completeness and conformity to business rules (e.g., valid product code, allowed customer segment). Basic errors are caught here.

**AND Gateway: ōRun Parallel Checksö**  
An parallel (AND) gateway splits the process into two simultaneous threads. Both must be completed before the path can continue.

- **Task C1: ōCredit Checkö** ¢ verifies the customerÆs creditworthiness (credit limit, payment history).  
- **Task C2: ōInventory Checkö** ¢ checks whether the requested quantity of the standard item is available (or can be replenished in time).

**Join (ōAll Parallel Checks Completedö)**  
The converging AND gateway waits for both C1 and C2 to finish. Only then does the process move on.

**Task D: ōCalculate Delivery Dateö**  
Using the results of the credit and inventory checks, the system (or an agent) computes a realistic delivery date, taking into account stock levels, lead times, and any credit holds.

---

### 4. The Custom Request Path
**Task B2: ōPerform Custom Feasibility Analysisö**  
Engineering, production, or sourcing specialists assess whether the requested customisation can be realised at all ¢ considering technical capability, material availability, and capacity.

**XOR Gateway: ōIs Customization Feasible?ö**  
A binary decision:

- **Yes**  **Task E1: ōPrepare Custom Quotationö** ¢ A tailored quote is created, including price, customisation cost, delivery timeline, and any special terms.
- **No**  **Task E2: ōSend Rejection Noticeö** ¢ The customer is informed that the request cannot be fulfilled, and the process ends immediately (terminal End Event).

Only the ōYesö branch reaches the later approval stage; the ōNoö branch terminates the process without invoice or confirmation.

### 5. Convergence and the Approval Decision
After a standard request finishes **Task D**, or after a feasible custom request finishes **Task E1**, both flows converge into a single **XOR Gateway: ōIs Approval Needed?ö**. This gateway embodies a business rule that decides whether the order must be formally approved by a manager. Typical triggers could be:

- Order value above a threshold.
- Custom orders (always require approval).
- First-time customers, or customers with borderline credit.
- Requested delivery date too aggressive.

Two outcomes emerge:

- **No approval needed**  skip straight to **Task G: ōGenerate Final Invoiceö**.
- **Approval needed**  **Task F: ōObtain Manager Approvalö** (a manual or systemassisted task where a manager reviews the order, the delivery date or the custom quotation).

### 6. Approval Outcome and Rework Loop

- **Yes**  proceed to **Task G: ōGenerate Final Invoiceö**.
- **No**  **Task H: ōRe-evaluate Conditionsö**. This task represents a deliberate adjustment of the parameters that led to the denial ¢ perhaps applying a discount, altering the payment terms, negotiating a longer delivery date, or modifying the customisation scope. The process then **loops back** to an earlier point, but the target depends on the **original request type** (information that must be remembered, e.g., from a process variable):
