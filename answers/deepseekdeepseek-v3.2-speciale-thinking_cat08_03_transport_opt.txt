### 1. Process Discovery and Conformance Checking

#### Preprocessing and Integration

The first step is to unify the heterogeneous data sources into a single **event log** conforming to the XES standard, where each event belongs to a case (e.g., a vehicleday), has a timestamp, an activity name, and can carry additional attributes (driver, package, location, speed, etc.). The integration involves:

- **Alignment of timestamps** – all systems must use the same time zone; if not, apply offsets.
- **Case identification** – define a case as a **vehicleday** (e.g., V12-20241205) because the delivery process is repeated daily. Events from all sources that refer to the same vehicle and same day are merged into that case.
- **Activity labelling** – transform raw events into meaningful process steps. For example:
  - GPS `speed=0` for >3 minutes at a customer location  “Idle at Stop”.
  - Scanner events like “Arrive Customer”  “Start Service”, “Delivery Success”  “Complete Service”, “Depart Customer”  “Leave Stop”.
  - GPSdetected low speed on a road segment  “Traffic Delay”.
  - Maintenance logs  “Scheduled Maintenance” or “Unscheduled Repair”.
- **Dealing with granularity** – GPS points occur every few seconds, whereas scanner events are sparse. We aggregate GPS data into **travel segments** between known stops and derive activities like “Travel from Stop A to Stop B”. This can be achieved by mapping GPS coordinates to road network and clustering stops.
- **Challenges**:
  - **Missing or inconsistent data** (e.g., a scanner event might be missing, or GPS data gaps). Use interpolation or probabilistic matching.
  - **Linking packagelevel events** to the correct stop and case. The log must keep `package_id` to analyse deliveryspecific performance.
  - **Noise** – GPS drift, accidental ignition off/on. Filter using speed thresholds and contextual rules.
  - **Synchronization** – events from different sources may be recorded with slight clock differences; we need to order them correctly.

#### Process Discovery

Using the cleaned event log, apply **process discovery algorithms** (e.g., Heuristics Miner, Inductive Miner) to generate a model of the **actual** endtoend delivery process. The model will include activities such as:

- `Depart Depot`
- `Travel`
- `Arrive at Customer`
- `Service` (with substeps: scanning, handling, delivery success/failure)
- `Idle` (waiting, breaks)
- `Traffic Delay`
- `Vehicle Breakdown`
- `Return Depot`
- `End Shift`

The discovered model can be visualised as a **BPMN or Petri net** and will reveal common paths, parallel branches (e.g., multiple attempts at a stop), loops (e.g., return to depot for more packages), and frequent deviations. It also shows the **frequency of each activity** and **decision points** (e.g., after a failed delivery, the package may be marked for redelivery later).

#### Conformance Checking

The **planned routes** from the dispatch system represent the **expected process**: a predefined sequence of stops with estimated travel and service times. Conformance checking compares the actual event log to this reference model. Techniques like **alignments** or **tokenbased replay** measure fitness, precision, and deviation costs.

We specifically look for:

- **Sequence deviations** – stops visited in a different order than planned, or extra/missing stops (e.g., a driver skipping a stop due to time constraints).
- **Unplanned activities** – “Traffic Delay”, “Unscheduled Stop”, “Maintenance”, “Long Idle” that were not part of the plan.
- **Timing deviations** – actual travel times or service times exceeding planned thresholds (e.g., >120% of planned duration).
- **Resource deviations** – a different driver or vehicle than assigned.

These deviations can be quantified per case, per route, or per driver, and their patterns indicate where the process is most unstable.

### 2. Performance Analysis and Bottleneck Identification

#### Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

From the event log, we can compute:

| KPI | Calculation |
|-----|-------------|
| **OnTime Delivery Rate** | Percentage of deliveries where `Delivery Success` timestamp falls within the customerrequested time window. |
| **Average Service Time per Stop** | Time between `Arrive Customer` and `Depart Customer` for successful deliveries, averaged. |
| **Travel Time vs. Service Time Ratio** | Sum of all travel durations (between stops) divided by sum of all service durations per case. |
| **Fuel Consumption per km / per package** | Estimate fuel usage from distance (GPSderived) and known vehicle fuel economy. |
| **Vehicle Utilization Rate** | Percentage of shift time spent in productive activities (travel + service) vs. idle/break/downtime. |
| **Frequency/Duration of Traffic Delays** | Count of “Traffic Delay” events and total time lost (duration where speed < threshold on a road segment). |
| **Failed Delivery Rate** | Number of `Delivery Failed` events divided by total delivery attempts. |
| **Overtime Occurrence** | Percentage of shifts where `End Shift` is beyond scheduled end time. |

#### Bottleneck Identification

Process mining provides several views to pinpoint bottlenecks:

- **Performanceannotated process maps** – colour activities by average waiting time or total duration. Activities with long waiting times (e.g., “Traffic Delay”, “Idle at Depot”) are bottlenecks.
- **Dotted chart** – plot events over time for many cases; vertical gaps indicate waiting, horizontal gaps indicate processing time. Patterns show if delays occur at certain times of day.
- **Throughput time analysis** – measure endtoend case duration and drill down into stages (e.g., from departure to last delivery). Compare across routes, drivers, vehicle types.
- **Resource workload analysis** – see which drivers or vehicles have the highest load, and whether that correlates with delays.
- **Variant analysis** – group cases by behaviour (e.g., sequences with traffic delays vs. without) and compare KPIs.

Bottlenecks often manifest as:

- **Specific road segments** where “Traffic Delay” is frequent (e.g., near city centre during rush hour).
- **Certain stops** with unusually long service times (apartment complexes, hardtopark locations).
- **Inefficient sequencing** causing backtracking or long empty runs.
- **Driverdependent inefficiencies** – some drivers take longer breaks or have higher service times.
- **Vehicle breakdowns** that disrupt entire days.

Quantify the impact by calculating **extra time spent**, **additional fuel consumed**, and **number of delayed deliveries** attributable to each bottleneck.

### 3. Root Cause Analysis for Inefficiencies

The identified bottlenecks are symptoms; we must find underlying causes. Process mining enables root cause investigation through:

#### Variant Analysis (HighPerforming vs. LowPerforming)

Compare cases (vehicledays) that achieved high ontime delivery and low cost with those that performed poorly. Differences in activity order, resource assignment, or contextual attributes (time of day, driver experience, vehicle age) reveal root causes.

#### Decision Mining

Apply decision tree or regression analysis on event data to discover which factors influence outcomes like service time, traffic delay duration, or delivery success. For instance, service time may depend on:
- Package type (size/weight)
- Location type (residential vs. commercial)
- Time of day (morning vs. afternoon)
- Driver experience (tenure)

#### Correlation with External Data

Enrich the log with weather data, traffic indices, or public events to see if delays correlate with external conditions.

#### Dwell Time Analysis

For each stop, analyse the time between `Arrive Customer` and `Depart Customer`. Investigate outliers: are they caused by parking search, building access, or customer interaction? GPS data near the stop can indicate circling behaviour.

#### Failure Pattern Analysis

Group failed deliveries by reason (customer not home, address incorrect, etc.) and examine patterns: do they cluster in certain areas, times, or drivers? This can point to issues with timewindow assignment or address verification.

#### Maintenance Event Correlation

Correlate vehicle breakdowns with prior usage: high mileage without maintenance, frequent harsh braking (from GPS acceleration), or overloading. Use survival analysis to predict failure risk.

Based on typical lastmile challenges, the root causes may be:

- **Suboptimal route planning** – static routes ignore daily variations in traffic and order volume.
- **Inaccurate travel time estimations** – dispatch uses average speeds that are unrealistic for certain times of day.
- **Traffic congestion patterns** – recurring delays on specific roads at specific times.
- **High variability in service time** – due to parking availability, building layout, or customer interaction.
- **Vehicle breakdowns** – insufficient preventive maintenance leads to midshift failures.
- **Driver behaviour differences** – some drivers are less efficient, need training.
- **Failed deliveries** – often because customers are not home; poor time window management or notification is a cause.

Process mining validates these by showing, for example, that routes with more dynamic adjustments have lower deviations, or that service time outliers correlate with specific location attributes.

### 4. DataDriven Optimization Strategies

#### Strategy 1: Dynamic Routing Adjustments Based on RealTime Traffic and Historical Performance

- **Targeted inefficiency**: Long travel times due to traffic congestion and static route plans.
- **Root cause**: Static routes cannot adapt to daily variations; travel time estimates are outdated.
- **Process mining insights**: 
  - Historical analysis reveals which road segments are consistently congested and at what times.
  - Conformance checking shows large positive deviations in travel time for certain legs.
  - Variant analysis identifies that drivers who deviate from the planned route (e.g., taking alternative roads) often achieve better ontime performance.
- **Implementation**: Use a **dynamic routing engine** that, at the start of a shift and periodically during the day, reoptimises the remaining stops using realtime traffic data and historically learned travel times. The optimisation respects constraints (time windows, capacity, working hours). Drivers receive updated sequences on their handheld devices.
- **Expected impacts**: 
  - Reduced travel time  lower fuel consumption per package.
  - Higher ontime delivery rate.
  - Decreased driver overtime.

#### Strategy 2: Optimising Delivery Territories and Route Sequences Using Clustering and Performance Data

- **Targeted inefficiency**: Unbalanced workloads, excessive travel distances, and inefficient stop ordering.
- **Root cause**: Territories are designed manually without considering actual delivery density, leading to some vehicles covering too large an area while others are underutilised.
- **Process mining insights**:
  - Process discovery shows frequent backtracking and long empty runs.
  - Performance analysis indicates that certain routes consistently exceed planned duration, while others finish early.
  - Geographic analysis of stop locations reveals natural clusters that could be assigned to a single vehicle.
- **Implementation**: Apply **cluster analysis** (e.g., kmeans, DBSCAN) on historical delivery locations, weighted by package volume and time windows, to create balanced delivery zones. Within each zone, use **TSPlike algorithms** to generate optimal stop sequences based on historical travel times (including timeofday variations). Reassign zones periodically (e.g., quarterly) to adapt to changing demand.
- **Expected impacts**:
  - Reduced average travel distance per package  lower fuel consumption.
  - More balanced vehicle utilisation  fewer over and underloaded vehicles.
  - Improved ability to meet time windows due to shorter routes.

#### Strategy 3: Predictive Maintenance Scheduling Based on Vehicle Usage Patterns

- **Targeted inefficiency**: Unscheduled breakdowns that cause delays, increase repair costs, and reduce vehicle availability.
- **Root cause**: Reactive maintenance; vehicles are serviced only after failure or at fixed intervals, ignoring actual wear.
- **Process mining insights**:
  - Maintenance logs integrated with vehicle sensor data (speed, ignition cycles, load) show correlations between usage patterns (e.g., high idle time, harsh braking) and subsequent failures.
  - Process mining can identify “nearmiss” events (e.g., engine warning light) that often precede a breakdown.
- **Implementation**: Build a **predictive maintenance model** using machine learning on historical vehicle event data (including GPSderived metrics like mileage, engine hours, number of stops/starts, and load indicators from the dispatch system). Schedule maintenance when the model indicates high risk of failure, preferably during offpeak times or overnight. Provide realtime alerts to drivers for immediate action (e.g., low tyre pressure).
- **Expected impacts**:
  - Fewer midshift breakdowns  less disruption to delivery schedules.
  - Lower repair costs through early intervention.
  - Improved vehicle availability and reduced overtime due to fewer unexpected delays.

### 5. Considering Operational Constraints and Monitoring

#### Accounting for Operational Constraints

All proposed strategies must respect the company’s operational boundaries:

- **Driver working hours** – legal limits and break requirements. Dynamic routing and territory optimisation must ensure that the total estimated shift time (including breaks) does not exceed allowed hours. If overtime is unavoidable, the system should flag it early.
- **Vehicle capacity** – each van has a maximum number of parcels and weight limit. Cluster assignments and daily load plans must stay within these limits.
- **Customer time windows** – deliveries must be attempted within promised windows. The optimisation algorithms treat these as hard or soft constraints depending on business policy.
- **Realworld feasibility** – routes must account for oneway streets, parking restrictions, and access limitations. GPS data can help identify problematic locations.

The process mining insights will inform the constraints: for example, historical service time distributions can be used to set realistic time allowances per stop.

#### Continuous Monitoring Plan

After implementation, set up **process mining dashboards** that continuously ingest new event data and provide near realtime insights. Key elements:

- **Daily KPI dashboard**: Ontime delivery rate, average travel time per km, fuel efficiency, vehicle utilisation, failed delivery rate, overtime hours.
- **Process conformance view**: Alignments between actual routes and optimised plans, highlighting deviations and their reasons.
- **Bottleneck heatmap**: Map showing traffic delays and long service stops, updated daily.
- **Driver/vehicle performance**: Compare individual performance against benchmarks; identify outliers needing coaching.
- **Predictive maintenance alerts**: Display vehicles approaching maintenance thresholds.

These dashboards allow management to quickly spot new inefficiencies, verify that improvements are sustained, and adapt strategies as demand evolves. Additionally, periodic deepdive process mining analyses (e.g., quarterly) can uncover emerging patterns and further optimisation opportunities.
