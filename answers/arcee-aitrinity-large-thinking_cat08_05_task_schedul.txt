## **2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies**

### **Bottleneck Identification & Quantification**
- **Critical Path Analysis:** Identify the machine(s) with the highest average queue time or the longest average flow time multiplier.
- **Resource Contention Heatmap:** Visualize periods where multiple jobs compete for the same bottleneck machine (e.g., CUT-01).
- **Impact on Throughput:** Calculate the bottleneck's throughput as a fraction of total job volume; if < 100%, it's starving downstream.

### **Poor Prioritization Evidence**
- **Due Date Violation Analysis:** Compare the sequence of jobs *released* to a bottleneck vs. their due dates. If high-priority (urgent) jobs arrive after low-priority ones and get processed later, it indicates FIFO overridingEDD.
- **Waiting Time by Priority:** Calculate average queue time for "High" vs. "Medium/Low" priority jobs. If high-priority jobs wait longer, prioritization is broken.

### **Suboptimal Sequencing & Setup Waste**
- **Setup Time Inefficiency Index:** Compare *actual* average setup time to *optimal* (minimum possible) setup time based on part family clustering.
- **Sequence Pattern Mining:** Identify frequently occurring job sequences that lead to long setups (e.g., Steel  Aluminum  Steel on the same machine).
- **Batching Opportunity Analysis:** Count instances where two similar jobs (same part family) were processed consecutively but with a long setup in between (missed opportunity).

### **Starvation & Bullwhip Effect**
- **Downstream Idle Time Correlation:** Check if upstream bottlenecks cause downstream machines (e.g., GRIND-05) to have excessive idle time despite available queue slots.
- **WIP Variability Upstream:** Measure standard deviation of WIP at the first operation vs. later operations; increasing variance indicates bullwhip effect from scheduling variability.

### **Process Mining Evidence**

- **Resource Load Chart:** Identify periods of 100% utilization (overload) alternating with 0% (starvation) on the same machine珦ign of poor load leveling.

- **Low Utilization Periods:** If tardiness occurs even when overall utilization is < 70%, the issue is likely scheduling logic (e.g., poor sequencing).
- **High Variability Periods:** If delays correlate with high load or many changeovers, it may be a capacity issue.

### **Root Causes from Process Mining Insights**
1. **Static Dispatching Rules:** The current mix of FIFO/EDD doesn't account for:
   - **Sequence-dependent setups** (treating all jobs as independent).
   - **Downstream availability** (releasing jobs to a machine that will be idle soon).
   - **Dynamic priorities** (not adjusting for hot jobs mid-schedule).
2. **Lack of Real-Time Visibility:** Dispatchers can't see:
   - Live queue lengths at all work centers.
   - Upcoming maintenance or breakdowns.
   - Progress of jobs on the critical path.
3. **Inaccurate Time Estimates:** Planned durations vs. actual show high variance (e.g., Cutting: 60 min planned vs. 66.4 min actual). This leads to:
   - Overly optimistic schedules.
   - Poor due date promises.
4. **Setup Time Mismanagement:** No systematic approach to minimize setups; setups are treated as fixed overhead rather than a scheduling variable.
5. **Poor Coordination:** Work centers operate in silos; no mechanism to reserve capacity for urgent jobs or to level load across the shop.

## **4. Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies**

### **Strategy 1: Dynamic, Multi-Criteria Dispatching with Setup-Aware Scoring**
**Core Logic:** At each work center, assign a dynamic score to each waiting job based on:
```
Score = w1 * (Due Date Proximity) + w2 * (Remaining Processing Time) + w3 * (Setup Time Savings) + w4 * (Downstream Availability)
```
- **Due Date Proximity:** `(Due Date - Today) / (Total Flow Time Estimate)`炥rioritize jobs with tight windows relative to their estimated flow time.
- **Remaining Processing Time:** Shorter jobs first (to reduce WIP and improve responsiveness).
- **Setup Time Savings:** Negative weight for long setups; favor sequences that minimize changeover time (using the setup matrix from Section 1).
- **Downstream Availability:** Check if the next operation has capacity; avoid releasing jobs to a machine that will cause downstream blocking.

**Process Mining Input:**
- Historical distribution of remaining processing times by job type.
- Setup time matrix between part families.
- Average queue times at downstream machines.

**Addresses:**
- Poor prioritization (weights due dates and remaining time).
- Suboptimal sequencing (explicit setup savings term).
- Starvation (downstream availability check).

**Expected Impact:**
- 20-30% reduction in average tardiness.
- 15% reduction in average flow time.
- Better high-priority job adherence.

Generate thousands of schedule scenarios, then select the schedule that optimizes a **risk-adjusted objective function** (e.g., minimize expected tardiness + 2 * std(tardiness)).

**Addresses:**

### **Strategy 3: Bottleneck-Centric Setup Optimization (Sequencing & Batching)**
**Core Logic:** Focus optimization on the **bottleneck machine(s)** identified in Section 2. Use a **genetic algorithm or tabu search** to sequence jobs at the bottleneck, minimizing total setup time while respecting due dates and priorities.

**Process Mining Input:**
- Bottleneck identification and its utilization/congestion patterns.
- Setup time matrix between all part families processed at the bottleneck.

**Algorithm:**
1. Cluster all jobs waiting for the bottleneck into part families.
2. Generate sequences that group same-family jobs together.

4. Select sequence with best trade-off (e.g., minimize weighted sum of setup time and criticality).

**Addresses:**
- Excessive setup times (direct optimization).
