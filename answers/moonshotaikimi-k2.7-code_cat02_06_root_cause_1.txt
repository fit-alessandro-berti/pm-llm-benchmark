## 1. Case cycle times – which tickets are delayed?

| Case | Receive  Close | Receive  Resolve | Notable? |
|------|----------------|-------------------|----------|
| **101** | 2 h 15 min | 2 h 00 min | Fast |
| **102** | **25 h 10 min** | **24 h 55 min** | **Significantly delayed** |
| **103** | 1 h 20 min | 1 h 05 min | Fast |
| **104** | **24 h 10 min** | **23 h 40 min** | **Significantly delayed** |
| **105** | **49 h 05 min** | **48 h 35 min** | **Very severely delayed** |

The average case duration is roughly **20.4 hours**, but the median is pulled up by the slow cases. Cases **102, 104, and especially 105** stand out; 105 is more than **20× slower** than the fastest case (103).

## 2. Root causes of the long cycle times

The delays are **not** mainly caused by the activities themselves taking a long time. They are caused by **long idle waiting periods between activities**, handoffs, and escalation paths.

### A. Presence of escalation strongly correlates with delay
- **Escalated cases:** **102** (25 h 10 min) and **105** (49 h 05 min).
- **Non-escalated fast cases:** **101** (2 h 15 min) and **103** (1 h 20 min).
- **Escalation adds handoff delays:** in 102, after escalation it still waits **2.5 hours** before investigation starts; in 105, after escalation it waits **28 hours** before the second investigation.

### B. Long pre-investigation / pre-escalation queueing
- **Case 104** has no escalation, but waits **3.5 hours** after assignment before investigation begins.
- **Case 102** waits **2.5 hours** after assignment before escalation.
- In contrast, fast cases 101 and 103 start investigation within **40 minutes** or less of assignment.

This suggests a **Level1 capacity bottleneck** or an overloaded triage/assignment queue.

### C. Cross-day / weekend idle time
All delayed cases include an overnight gap before resolution:

| Case | Key idle gap | Length |
|------|--------------|--------|
| 102 | Investigate  Resolve | ~19 hours |
| 104 | Investigate  Resolve | ~19 hours |
| 105 | Investigate  Escalate  Investigate | 28 hours |
| 105 | Second Investigate  Resolve | ~19 hours |

Because **March 1, 2024 was a Friday**, these tickets sit idle across **Saturday/Sunday**, indicating limited or no weekend coverage for Level2 and possibly for resolution/closure.

### D. Rework after escalation
**Case 105** is unique: it is investigated, then escalated, then investigated again. This rework, combined with the 28-hour wait before the second investigation, is a major reason it is the slowest case.

### E. Waiting time dominates the cycle
For the slow cases, the actual work time is small compared with waiting time:

- **Case 102:** ~95% of the cycle is waiting.
- **Case 104:** ~93% of the cycle is waiting.
- **Case 105:** ~97% of the cycle is waiting.

So the process bottleneck is **queueing and handoff delays**, not slow execution of individual tasks.

---

## 3. Insights and recommendations

1. **Set per-handoff SLAs**  
   Define maximum wait times for each transition, especially:
   - Receive  Triage
   - Assign  Investigate
   - Assign  Escalation
   - Escalation  Level2 investigation
   - Investigate  Resolve

2. **Reduce escalation delays**  
   - Route tickets directly to the right specialist when escalation criteria are met.
   - Introduce an escalation acknowledgment target (e.g., Level2 picks up within 1 hour).
   - Use “swarming” or consultative support so Level1 can resolve complex issues without a formal handoff.

3. **Address Level1 capacity**  
   Case 104 shows that even without escalation, a ticket can wait **3.5 hours** before investigation. Monitor queue length and staffing during peak intake periods.

4. **Provide after-hours / weekend coverage**  
   The ~19-hour overnight gaps strongly suggest the process stops on Friday evening and resumes on Saturday or Sunday. Consider:
   - Weekend on-call Level2 coverage for escalated tickets.
   - Clear customer communication that non-urgent tickets will resume next business day.

5. **Empower Level1 agents**  
   More knowledge base articles, decision trees, and diagnostic tools could prevent unnecessary escalations, reducing cases like 102 and 105.

6. **Distinguish working time from waiting time in reporting**  
   Future event logs should capture **start/end timestamps for each activity**, not just completion timestamps, so you can precisely separate processing time from idle time.

### Bottom line
The performance issue is **not that agents work slowly**; it is that **tickets wait too long in queues, handoffs, and across non-working hours**. Targeting escalation flow, Level1 capacity, and weekend coverage will have the biggest impact on reducing cycle times.
