Yes. It appears to be a **BPMN-style swimlane process diagram** showing how a company prepares a shipment for dispatch.

## Overall structure
The process is divided into **three swimlanes**:
- **Logistics**
- **Computer shop Secretary**
- **Warehouse**

It starts at **“Ship goods”** and ends at **“Shipment prepared.”**

## Main flow
1. **Ship goods** starts the process.
2. A **parallel split** happens:
   - One branch goes to the **Secretary**
   - Another goes to the **Warehouse**

## Secretary lane activities
3. **Clarify shipment method**
4. Decision: **“Special handling?”**
   - **Yes**:
     - **Get 3 offers from logistic companies**
     - **Select logistic company and place order**
   - **No**:
     - **Check if insurance is required**
       - Uses **Order details** and **Customer information**
       - If insurance is necessary:
         - **Insure parcel**
       - In all cases:
         - **Write package label**

These two secretary-side branches later rejoin.

## Warehouse lane activities
5. **Package goods**
6. There is a timing/escalation element around **48 hours**:
   - If delayed that long, **Notify customer about delay**
7. That branch rejoins the warehouse flow.

## Final synchronization
8. The process waits until:
   - the **secretary-side shipping arrangements** are finished, and
   - the **warehouse packaging** is finished.
9. Then:
   - **Fill out Shipment paperwork**
   - **Move package to pickup area**
   - End at **Shipment prepared**

## What the diagram conveys
It shows that preparing a shipment involves:
- deciding shipping method,
- handling special logistics or insurance if needed,
- labeling and possibly insuring the package,
- packaging in the warehouse,
- notifying the customer if packaging is delayed,
- and finally completing paperwork and moving the package for pickup.
