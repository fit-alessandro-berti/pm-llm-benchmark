

# Temporal Profile Anomaly Analysis for Insurance Claim Handling

## 1. Identified Anomalies

### Anomaly 1: R  P (Receive to Approve) — Rigid Approval Timing
- **Average:** ~25 hours | **STDEV:** 1 hour
- The standard deviation is disproportionately small relative to the average. In a realistic claims process, the time from receipt to approval should vary significantly depending on claim complexity, documentation completeness, and adjuster workload. A near-constant 25-hour window suggests an artificial or batch-scheduled approval mechanism rather than genuine case-by-case evaluation.

### Anomaly 2: P  N (Approve to Notify) — Excessive Notification Delay
- **Average:** 7 days | **STDEV:** 2 days
- After a claim is approved, notifying the customer should be a swift, largely automated step. A 7-day average with wide variance suggests a severe bottleneck — possibly manual notification workflows, resource shortages, or systematic deprioritization of the notification step.

### Anomaly 3: A  C (Assign to Close) — Premature Closure
- **Average:** 2 hours | **STDEV:** 1 hour
- Closing a claim within 2 hours of adjuster assignment leaves insufficient time for evaluation, approval, and notification. This pattern implies that a subset of claims is being closed without completing all intermediate process steps — possibly representing rejected, duplicate, or withdrawn claims that bypass the standard workflow.

### Anomaly 4: E  N (Evaluate to Notify) — Suspiciously Rapid Transition
- **Average:** 5 minutes | **STDEV:** 1 minute
- A 5-minute gap between evaluation and customer notification, with almost no variance, suggests either an automated system trigger that fires notification immediately upon evaluation completion, or that the approval step (P) is being skipped entirely for certain claims. Either scenario warrants investigation.

---

## 2. Hypotheses

| Anomaly | Hypothesis |
|---------|-----------|
| **R  P rigid timing** | Approvals may be processed in a nightly or daily batch job that runs at a fixed time (e.g., 1:00 AM), producing a near-constant delay regardless of when the claim was received. Alternatively, an automated rule engine may approve certain low-risk claims on a fixed schedule. |
| **P  N long delay** | Notification may depend on a manual step — perhaps generating a letter or waiting for a specific team to process outbound communications. Regional staffing imbalances or weekend/holiday effects could amplify the delay. The high STDEV suggests some claims are notified quickly (possibly digitally) while others wait for physical mail or manual follow-up. |
| **A  C premature closure** | Certain claim types (e.g., duplicates, below-threshold amounts, or claims missing required documents) may be closed immediately by the assigned adjuster without evaluation or approval. This could also indicate fraudulent fast-tracking or adjuster gaming of performance metrics. |
| **E  N instant transition** | An automated notification system may fire immediately when an evaluation record is saved, bypassing the approval step. This could be intentional for denied claims (where no approval is needed) or a system misconfiguration that skips required authorization. |

## 3. Verification Queries

### 3.1 Claims with Abnormally Rigid R  P Timing

This query identifies claims where the Receive-to-Approve interval clusters tightly around the 25-hour mark, and checks whether these follow a fixed daily schedule:

```sql
SELECT
    ce_r.claim_id,
    ce_r.timestamp AS receive_time,
    ce_p.timestamp AS approve_time,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ce_p.timestamp - ce_r.timestamp)) AS duration_seconds,
    EXTRACT(HOUR FROM ce_p.timestamp) AS approve_hour,
    c.claim_type,
    c.claim_amount
FROM claim_events ce_r
JOIN claim_events ce_p
    ON ce_r.claim_id = ce_p.claim_id
    AND ce_r.activity = 'R'
    AND ce_p.activity = 'P'
JOIN claims c
    ON c.claim_id = ce_r.claim_id
WHERE ABS(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ce_p.timestamp - ce_r.timestamp)) - 90000) <= 3600
ORDER BY approve_hour, ce_r.claim_id;
```

*If approvals cluster at a specific hour of the day, the batch-processing hypothesis is confirmed.*

### 3.2 Claims Where Approval to Notification Exceeds Reasonable Bounds

```sql
WITH pn_times AS (
    SELECT
        ce_p.claim_id,
        ce_p.timestamp AS approve_time,
        ce_n.timestamp AS notify_time,
        EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ce_n.timestamp - ce_p.timestamp)) AS pn_duration_seconds,
        ce_p.resource AS approver,
        ce_n.resource AS notifier
    FROM claim_events ce_p
    JOIN claim_events ce_n
        ON ce_p.claim_id = ce_n.claim_id
        AND ce_p.activity = 'P'
        AND ce_n.activity = 'N'
)
SELECT
    pn.*,
    c.claim_type,
    a.region,
    a.name AS adjuster_name
FROM pn_times pn
JOIN claims c ON c.claim_id = pn.claim_id
LEFT JOIN adjusters a ON a.name = pn.approver OR a.name = pn.notifier
WHERE pn.pn_duration_seconds > (604800 + 2 * 172800)   -- Beyond AVG + 2*STDEV (~11 days)
   OR pn.pn_duration_seconds < (604800 - 2 * 172800)   -- Below AVG - 2*STDEV (~3 days)
ORDER BY pn.pn_duration_seconds DESC;
```

### 3.3 Correlate Long P  N Delays by Region and Claim Type

```sql
SELECT
    a.region,
    c.claim_type,
    COUNT(*) AS claim_count,
    AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ce_n.timestamp - ce_p.timestamp))) AS avg_pn_seconds,
    STDDEV(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ce_n.timestamp - ce_p.timestamp))) AS stdev_pn_seconds,
    MAX(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ce_n.timestamp - ce_p.timestamp))) AS max_pn_seconds
FROM claim_events ce_p
JOIN claim_events ce_n
    ON ce_p.claim_id = ce_n.claim_id
    AND ce_p.activity = 'P'
    AND ce_n.activity = 'N'
JOIN claims c ON c.claim_id = ce_p.claim_id
LEFT JOIN adjusters a ON a.name = ce_p.resource
GROUP BY a.region, c.claim_type
ORDER BY avg_pn_seconds DESC;
```

### 3.4 Claims Closed Prematurely After Assignment (Skipping E, P, N)

```sql
SELECT
    ce_a.claim_id,
    ce_a.timestamp AS assign_time,
    ce_c.timestamp AS close_time,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ce_c.timestamp - ce_a.timestamp)) / 3600.0 AS hours_to_close,
    ce_a.resource AS assigned_adjuster,
    c.claim_type,
    c.claim_amount
FROM claim_events ce_a
JOIN claim_events ce_c
    ON ce_a.claim_id = ce_c.claim_id
    AND ce_a.activity = 'A'
    AND ce_c.activity = 'C'
JOIN claims c ON c.claim_id = ce_a.claim_id
WHERE NOT EXISTS (
    SELECT 1 FROM claim_events ce_e
    WHERE ce_e.claim_id = ce_a.claim_id AND ce_e.activity = 'E'
      AND ce_e.timestamp BETWEEN ce_a.timestamp AND ce_c.timestamp
)
AND EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ce_c.timestamp - ce_a.timestamp)) < 10800  -- Less than 3 hours
ORDER BY hours_to_close ASC;
```

### 3.5 Adjuster-Level Breakdown of Premature Closures

```sql
SELECT
    ce_a.resource AS adjuster,
    COUNT(*) AS premature_close_count,
    AVG(c.claim_amount) AS avg_claim_amount,
    ARRAY_AGG(DISTINCT c.claim_type) AS claim_types
FROM claim_events ce_a
JOIN claim_events ce_c
    ON ce_a.claim_id = ce_c.claim_id
    AND ce_a.activity = 'A'
    AND ce_c.activity = 'C'
JOIN claims c ON c.claim_id = ce_a.claim_id
WHERE EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ce_c.timestamp - ce_a.timestamp)) < 10800
AND NOT EXISTS (
    SELECT 1 FROM claim_events
    WHERE claim_id = ce_a.claim_id
      AND activity IN ('E', 'P')
      AND timestamp BETWEEN ce_a.timestamp AND ce_c.timestamp
)
GROUP BY ce_a.resource
ORDER BY premature_close_count DESC;
```

*A single adjuster with a disproportionate count of premature closures would indicate either a specialized role (handling invalid claims) or potential misconduct.*

### 3.6 Claims Where E  N Occurs Without an Intervening Approval Step

```sql
SELECT
    ce_e.claim_id,
    ce_e.timestamp AS evaluate_time,
    ce_n.timestamp AS notify_time,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ce_n.timestamp - ce_e.timestamp)) AS en_duration_seconds,
    c.claim_type,
    c.claim_amount,
    ce_e.resource AS evaluator,
    CASE
        WHEN EXISTS (
            SELECT 1 FROM claim_events ce_p
            WHERE ce_p.claim_id = ce_e.claim_id
              AND ce_p.activity = 'P'
              AND ce_p.timestamp BETWEEN ce_e.timestamp AND ce_n.timestamp
        ) THEN 'Yes'
        ELSE 'No'
    END AS approval_present
FROM claim_events ce_e
JOIN claim_events ce_n
    ON ce_e.claim_id = ce_n.claim_id
    AND ce_e.activity = 'E'
    AND ce_n.activity = 'N'
JOIN claims c ON c.claim_id = ce_e.claim_id
WHERE EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ce_n.timestamp - ce_e.timestamp)) < 600  -- Under 10 minutes
ORDER BY en_duration_seconds ASC;
```

*Claims where `approval_present = 'No'` and the E  N gap is under 5 minutes are strong candidates for a skipped authorization step.*

### 3.7 Full Process Duration Outlier Detection Across All Activity Pairs

```sql
WITH activity_pairs AS (
    SELECT
        e1.claim_id,
        e1.activity AS activity_from,
        e2.activity AS activity_to,
        EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (e2.timestamp - e1.timestamp)) AS duration_seconds
    FROM claim_events e1
    JOIN claim_events e2
        ON e1.claim_id = e2.claim_id
        AND e2.timestamp > e1.timestamp
    WHERE (e1.activity, e2.activity) IN (
        ('R','A'), ('R','E'), ('R','P'), ('A','C'),
        ('E','N'), ('E','C'), ('P','N'), ('N','C')
    )
),
stats AS (
    SELECT
        activity_from,
        activity_to,
        AVG(duration_seconds) AS avg_dur,
        STDDEV(duration_seconds) AS stdev_dur
    FROM activity_pairs
    GROUP BY activity_from, activity_to
)
SELECT
    ap.claim_id,
    ap.activity_from,
    ap.activity_to,
    ap.duration_seconds,
    s.avg_dur,
    s.stdev_dur,
    CASE
        WHEN s.stdev_dur > 0
        THEN (ap.duration_seconds - s.avg_dur) / s.stdev_dur
        ELSE NULL
    END AS z_score
FROM activity_pairs ap
JOIN stats s
    ON ap.activity_from = s.activity_from
    AND ap.activity_to = s.activity_to
WHERE s.stdev_dur > 0
  AND ABS((ap.duration_seconds - s.avg_dur) / s.stdev_dur) > 2
ORDER BY ABS((ap.duration_seconds - s.avg_dur) / s.stdev_dur) DESC;
```

*This general-purpose query computes Z-scores for every claim across all tracked activity pairs, surfacing the most extreme deviations for further investigation. Adjusting the threshold from 2 to a higher ZETA value narrows the focus to the most egregious outliers.*
