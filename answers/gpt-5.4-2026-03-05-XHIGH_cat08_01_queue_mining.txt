Below is the approach I would take as a healthcare process analyst.

**First, a practical note:** before any queue mining, I would validate the event log semantics. In clinics, a `START` timestamp can sometimes mean “chart opened” rather than true patient contact, and a test `COMPLETE` can mean “sample collected” rather than “result available.” I would also, if possible, enrich the event log with:

- patient arrival / check-in time,
- scheduled appointment time,
- staff rosters and room calendars,
- order placed / result available timestamps for diagnostics.

That enrichment is especially important for measuring the **front-desk queue** and for separating **avoidable delay** from **planned/scheduled waiting**.

---

## 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

### How I would calculate waiting times

With start and complete timestamps, I can compute two core time components for each activity instance:

- **Service time** = `COMPLETE(activity) - START(activity)`
- **Waiting / queue time** = time between the moment the next activity is ready to begin and the moment it actually starts

In the simplest sequential case, for a patient going from activity A to activity B:

- **Wait from A to B** = `START(B) - COMPLETE(A)`

Example: if Registration completes at 09:08:45 and Nurse Assessment starts at 09:15:20, the patient waited **6 min 35 sec** between those two steps.

### Important nuance: “enabled time” vs just “previous completion”

In rigorous process mining, the next activity should be considered ready only when all required prerequisites are complete.

So the better definition is:

- **Queue time for activity B** = `START(B) - ENABLED_TIME(B)`

Where `ENABLED_TIME(B)` is:
- the previous activity’s completion in a simple serial path, or
- the **latest completion** of all required predecessor steps in a more complex path.

This matters in healthcare. For example, if a doctor review requires both a nurse assessment and an ECG, the doctor consultation is not truly “waiting in queue” until both are done.

### What “waiting time” means here

In this clinic context, I would use two related measures:

1. **Operational queue time**  
   Time the patient is ready for the next step, but the step has not started.

2. **Patient-experienced idle time**  
   Any non-service time between stages, even if part of it is planned.

Both matter:
- management wants to reduce operational waste,
- patients care about total waiting, regardless of cause.

### Special case: waiting before the first activity

To measure **waiting for registration**, the clinic needs a true arrival/check-in timestamp.  
If the log starts at `Registration START`, that captures registration service time, **not** the pre-registration queue.

### Handling real-world complications

I would explicitly handle:

- **Parallel activities**: do not treat overlapping steps as negative queue time
- **Missing/duplicate timestamps**
- **Very small transfer gaps**: optionally classify as transit/setup rather than meaningful queue
- **Compound resources**: split “Tech X / Room 3” into staff and room/equipment where possible
- **Specialty variants**: analyze shared steps clinic-wide and specialty-specific steps separately

---

### Key queue metrics to calculate

For each queue/transition (e.g., Registration  Nurse, Nurse  Doctor, Doctor  ECG, ECG  Check-out), I would calculate:

- **Average wait time**
- **Median wait time**  
  Often more reliable than mean because wait times are usually right-skewed
- **Maximum wait time**  
  Useful for outlier review, but not enough on its own
- **90th / 95th percentile wait time**  
  Critical for patient experience because complaints are often driven by the tail
- **Queue frequency**  
  Number / percentage of visits that encounter that queue
- **Total wait minutes contributed by that queue**  
  `sum of all wait time across all cases`
- **% of cases above a threshold/SLA**  
  Example: % waiting more than X minutes for nurse/doctor, segmented by urgency
- **Queue length over time**  
  Number of patients waiting for a given activity at each point in time
- **Peak queue length and time spent above threshold**
- **Share of total visit spent waiting**  
  `total waiting / total visit duration`
- **End-to-end visit duration**
- **Touch-time ratio**  
  `sum of service times / total visit duration`

I would calculate all of these:
- overall,
- by specialty,
- by patient type (new vs follow-up),
- by urgency,
- by day of week,
- by hour/session,
- by resource (doctor, nurse, clerk, tech, room, equipment).

### How I would identify the most critical queues

I would **not** rank queues by average wait alone. A queue can have a long mean but affect few patients, while a shorter queue may affect almost everyone.

I would prioritize using a combination of:

1. **Total wait burden**  
   Which queues consume the most total patient time?
2. **Tail severity**  
   Which have the worst p90/p95 waits?
3. **Frequency**  
   How many patients are affected?
4. **Clinical importance**  
   Are urgent patients impacted?
5. **Contribution to total visit duration**  
   Does this queue dominate the door-to-door experience?
6. **Downstream impact**  
   Does it delay many later steps or create room/resource blocking?
7. **Segment impact**  
   Is it especially bad for new patients, certain specialties, or shared diagnostics?

A practical prioritization rule is:

- first identify the queues causing the highest **total lost minutes**,
- then overlay **clinical risk** and **patient segment impact**.

That usually gives a better intervention list than “longest average wait” alone.

## 2. Root Cause Analysis

Once the queues are located, the main question becomes:

**Are patients waiting because the next step is not available, not ready, or not coordinated?**

### A. Resource bottlenecks

Possible causes:
- too few clerks/nurses/doctors/techs at peak times,
- exam room shortages,
- shared diagnostic equipment constraints,
- poor alignment between staffing and demand.

How process mining helps:
- **Resource utilization analysis**: busy time by staff/room/equipment
- **Time-based bottleneck analysis**: identify when queues build up
- **Queue length over time**: show demand exceeding capacity in specific windows
- **Performance by resource**: identify specific providers/rooms/pools causing delay

Interpretation:
- **High wait + high utilization** usually suggests a true capacity bottleneck
- if a resource pool is near saturation during variable demand, queues grow quickly

### B. Activity dependencies and handovers

Possible causes:
- manual handoffs between nurse and doctor,
- patients waiting for room assignment,
- diagnostics completed but next team not notified,
- results or documentation needed before proceeding.

How process mining helps:
- **Handover analysis / organizational mining**
- **Lag analysis between COMPLETE of one activity and START of the next**
- **Conformance checking** against intended clinic flow
- **Variant analysis** to see where handoffs differ

Interpretation:
- **High wait while the next resource is not actually busy** points to a coordination problem, not a capacity problem

### C. Variability in service times

Possible causes:
- new patients take much longer than follow-ups,
- some doctors or nurses have highly variable durations,
- some specialties have long-tail consultations,
- diagnostic durations vary widely.

How process mining helps:
- compare **service time distributions**, not just averages
- segment by:
  - patient type,
  - urgency,
  - specialty,
  - provider,
  - time of day
- identify activities/resources with high variance or long tails

Interpretation:
- **Moderate average load + very high variability** can still create severe queues
- if p90 is far above median, variability is often a major driver

### D. Appointment scheduling policies

Possible causes:
- patients booked in waves at the top of the hour,
- equal slot lengths for unequal visit types,
- overbooking without good no-show logic,
- no buffer for urgent add-ons,
- diagnostic demand not synchronized with clinic schedules.

How process mining helps:
- **arrival and start-time heatmaps**
- compare scheduled time vs actual start where available
- identify clustering at specific times
- analyze no-show / late-arrival patterns if scheduling data can be linked

Interpretation:
- queues concentrated at certain times often reflect **template design**, not raw staffing shortage

### E. Patient arrival patterns

Possible causes:
- morning surges,
- Monday effects,
- early arrivals creating front-desk congestion,
- urgent walk-ins disrupting planned flow.

How process mining helps:
- time-series of case starts and queue formation
- segmentation by day/time/urgency
- analysis of priority preemption effects

Interpretation:
- if normal patients wait longer immediately after urgent arrivals, the queue discipline is influencing performance—which may be appropriate clinically, but must be measured explicitly

### F. Differences by patient type, urgency, specialty, and process variant

Possible causes:
- new patients need longer registration, assessment, and consultation
- some specialties routinely trigger diagnostics
- urgent patients skip steps or preempt routine cases
- rework loops or extra reviews lengthen visits

How process mining helps:
- **Variant analysis**: compare common visit paths
- **Trace clustering**: compare long-stay vs short-stay visits
- **Performance DFG / process maps** by segment
- identify whether the long queues are concentrated in specific pathways

Interpretation:
- solving “the average queue” may miss the real problem if only certain segments are delayed

### G. Batching and hidden prerequisites

Possible causes:
- diagnostics started in batches,
- checkout processed in bursts,
- lab result availability not captured in the current log,
- room cleanup or documentation creating hidden delay.

How process mining helps:
- identify **start-time clustering**
- compare service time to waiting time
- investigate outlier timelines
- if available, add more event types (order placed, result ready, room ready)

Interpretation:
- **High waits with short actual service times** often indicate batching or coordination waste

### Process mining techniques I would use beyond basic queue calculation

- **Performance process maps / directly-follows graphs**
- **Bottleneck analysis**
- **Resource analysis / utilization**
- **Variant analysis**
- **Organizational / handover mining**
- **Conformance checking**
- **Time-of-day / calendar analysis**
- **Outlier case review**
- If richer room/equipment data exists: **object-centric process mining**

A very useful heuristic is:

- **High wait + high utilization**  capacity issue
- **High wait + low utilization**  coordination/policy issue
- **High p90 vs median**  variability / batching / priority insertions
- **Time-specific wait spikes**  scheduling mismatch
- **Segment-specific delays**  case-mix / pathway design issue

## 3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

Before rollout, I would use the mined data to build a **discrete-event simulation / digital twin** of the clinic. That lets the clinic test changes before implementation and estimate whether a fix reduces waits or merely shifts the bottleneck elsewhere.

Below are four strong strategies; the clinic should implement the ones that match the diagnosed root causes.

---

### Strategy 1: Align capacity to peak demand with flexible staffing and room allocation

**Targets**
- registration queue
- nurse assessment queue
- shared diagnostics queues
- checkout queue during peak discharge periods

**Root cause addressed**
- resource bottlenecks caused by mismatch between staffing/room availability and actual demand pattern

**How data supports it**
- queue length heatmaps by 15-minute interval
- resource utilization by staff/room/equipment
- evidence that waits spike during specific windows while other times are underloaded

**Concrete actions**
- move clerk/nurse/tech schedules to match peak arrival waves
- stagger breaks/lunches
- cross-train staff to provide flex coverage
- reassign underused rooms during peak sessions
- create short peak-time flex capacity instead of adding full-day headcount

**Potential impact**
- a realistic pilot target is often **15–30% reduction** in the affected queue’s mean and p90 wait
- can often be done with **minimal added cost** if based on reallocation rather than new hires

---

### Strategy 2: Redesign appointment templates using actual service-time distributions

**Targets**
- nurse-to-doctor wait
- doctor consultation backlog
- post-consult diagnostic queues

**Root cause addressed**
- appointment bunching, incorrect slot lengths, lack of urgent buffers, and poor synchronization with downstream services

**How data supports it**
- new vs follow-up visits show different service times
- some specialties have much longer consult distributions
- arrivals cluster at the same times
- queue spikes follow clinic sessions predictably

**Concrete actions**
- stop booking many patients on the hour
- use different slot lengths for new, follow-up, and complex patients
- reserve controlled urgent/add-on slots
- align diagnostic slots with provider sessions where demand is predictable
- use selective overbooking only where no-show rates genuinely justify it

**Potential impact**
- often **10–25% reduction** in doctor waiting and less end-of-session spillover
- major improvement usually occurs in **p90 wait** and schedule stability, not just the mean

---

### Strategy 3: Segment and simplify flow for predictable patient cohorts

**Targets**
- registration queue
- doctor-to-diagnostic queue
- checkout queue
- overall visit duration for repeatable pathways

**Root cause addressed**
- one-size-fits-all flow, unnecessary sequential steps, and excessive manual admin for low-complexity visits

**How data supports it**
- variant analysis may show a large group of follow-up patients with short, standardized visits
- some specialties may have predictable diagnostics for selected cohorts
- registration/check-out may be high-frequency but low-complexity bottlenecks

**Concrete actions**
- digital pre-registration and insurance verification
- pre-visit questionnaires/history updates
- express path for eligible follow-up patients
- protocol-driven pre-tests for clinically appropriate cohorts (e.g., standing-order diagnostics)
- e-checkout / online follow-up booking for simple cases

**Potential impact**
- for eligible cohorts, **20–40% shorter visit duration** is plausible
- overall clinic impact depends on how much volume those cohorts represent
- also frees staff time for complex/new patients

**Healthcare caution**
- only use protocol-based prework where it is clinically approved and safe

---

### Strategy 4: Improve real-time coordination and handovers

**Targets**
- gaps between registration and nurse
- nurse and doctor
- doctor and diagnostics
- diagnostics and checkout

**Root cause addressed**
- coordination failures rather than pure capacity shortage

**How data supports it**
- long waits even when the next resource is not fully utilized
- high variability between teams or sessions
- idle downstream capacity while patients remain waiting

**Concrete actions**
- real-time queue dashboard for supervisors/frontline staff
- automatic alerts when a predecessor activity completes
- “next best patient” pull logic for rooms/resources
- escalation rules when waits exceed thresholds
- flow coordinator during peak sessions
- if rooming is the constraint, use more deliberate just-in-time room assignment

**Potential impact**
- often stronger on variability than on mean:
  **10–20% reduction** in p90 waits is a reasonable pilot target
- especially useful for reducing extreme delays that drive complaints

---

### Optional additional tactic if diagnostics are batched

If the logs show that ECG/X-ray/lab starts occur in large bursts while service times are short, I would also consider:

- smaller, more frequent batches,
- reserved urgent slots,
- demand-triggered staffing for shared diagnostics.

That can materially reduce doctor-to-diagnostic wait without adding major cost.

## 4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints

Each intervention has trade-offs.

### Likely trade-offs

**1. Capacity realignment**
- may improve peak waits but create weaker off-peak coverage
- cross-training can burden staff
- scope-of-practice or union constraints may limit flexibility

**2. Appointment redesign**
- longer slots for new/complex patients may reduce nominal appointment capacity
- urgent buffers can look “underused” on quiet days
- overbooking can backfire if no-shows fall

**3. Fast tracks / segmented flow**
- can create perceived unfairness if not communicated well
- digital tools may disadvantage elderly or low-digital-literacy patients
- protocol-driven pre-tests risk unnecessary testing if governance is weak

**4. Real-time orchestration**
- requires change management and system integration
- can create alert fatigue
- staff may feel pressured to rush patients if metrics are used poorly

**5. Local optimization risk**
- solving one queue can shift congestion downstream  
  e.g., faster registration may simply enlarge the nurse queue

### Balancing conflicting objectives

I would balance performance using a **multi-objective scorecard**, not a single wait metric.

**Non-negotiable guardrails**
- no deterioration in urgent access
- no decline in clinical quality or documentation quality
- no unsafe reduction in time for appropriate assessment
- no major rise in staff overtime/burnout

**Optimization principle**
- reduce **avoidable** waiting and variability first
- do not chase near-zero waits at any cost; in variable healthcare systems that usually requires expensive excess capacity
- preserve some slack in high-variability, clinically critical steps

**Practical approach**
1. prioritize low-cost / cost-neutral changes first
2. simulate scenarios before rollout
3. pilot in one specialty or session
4. measure both benefits and side effects
5. scale only when guardrails hold

One important healthcare trade-off is **continuity vs speed**. For some follow-up patients, routing to the next available clinician may reduce queue time, but it may also reduce continuity of care. That choice should be selective and clinically governed.

## 5. Measuring Success

### KPIs to track after implementation

I would use three groups of KPIs.

### A. Flow and queue KPIs
- **Average, median, p90/p95 wait** for:
  - registration
  - nurse assessment
  - doctor consultation
  - diagnostics
  - checkout
- **Total visit duration** (door-to-door if arrival exists)
- **Total non-value-added time per visit**
- **Touch-time ratio** = service time / total visit duration
- **% of visits within agreed wait thresholds**
- **Time to first clinical contact**
- **Peak queue length** and **time above threshold**

### B. Operational KPIs
- **Resource utilization** by staff/room/equipment
- **Throughput**: patients completed per clinic session/day
- **On-time start rate** for consultations
- **Session overrun / overtime**
- **Cost per visit** or incremental labor hours per 100 visits
- **Cost per minute of wait reduced** for intervention evaluation

### C. Patient experience and quality guardrails
- **Patient satisfaction / complaint rate related to waiting**
- **Visit abandonment / rescheduling due to delay**
- **Rework loops** or unexpected repeat steps
- **Clinical quality guardrails**, such as:
  - missed or delayed follow-up tasks,
  - documentation completeness,
  - unsafe return visits/callbacks,
  - same-day completion rate of intended care pathway

All KPIs should be reported:
- overall,
- by specialty,
- by patient type,
- by urgency,
- by time of day/day of week.

That prevents an aggregate improvement from hiding a worse experience for a high-risk group.

---

### Ongoing monitoring using the same event log

The same event structure can support continuous monitoring.

I would set up:

- **daily/weekly queue dashboards**
- **process performance maps** refreshed regularly
- **control charts** for key queue KPIs
- **alerts** when p90 wait or queue length crosses thresholds
- **monthly re-mining** of variants and bottlenecks

### Monitoring approach

1. **Baseline**  
   Use the last six months to define pre-intervention performance.

2. **Pilot comparison**  
   Compare pilot sessions/specialties against baseline and, if possible, against control groups.

3. **Case-mix adjustment**  
   Control for changes in urgency, patient type, and specialty mix.

4. **Trend monitoring**  
   Watch whether improvements are sustained or whether a new bottleneck appears.

5. **Closed-loop improvement**  
   Re-run root cause analysis whenever:
   - p90 waits rise,
   - queue location shifts,
   - quality/cost guardrails worsen.

If rollout is phased by specialty or clinic area, I would ideally use a **before/after plus control-group** design (or a stepped rollout) so management can distinguish real improvement from seasonal variation.

---

## Closing view

The core value of queue mining here is not just showing **where** patients wait, but distinguishing **why** they wait:

- capacity shortage,
- scheduling mismatch,
- high service-time variability,
- handover failure,
- or clinically necessary dependency.

That distinction is what allows the clinic to reduce waiting time, shorten overall visits, improve satisfaction, and do it in a cost-conscious way.
