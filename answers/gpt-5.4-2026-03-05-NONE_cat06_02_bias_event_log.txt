Bias appears in the **score adjustment logic**, especially where **community affiliation** and possibly **local residency** act as favorable signals unrelated to core creditworthiness.

### Where the bias shows up
The clearest pattern is:

- Cases **C001** and **C004** are both:
  - `LocalResident = TRUE`
  - affiliated with **Highland Civic Darts Club**
  - given **+10 (Community)**

By contrast:

- **C002** has a stronger base score than C001 (`720`) but no community affiliation and gets **0 adjustment**
- **C003** and **C005**, both `LocalResident = FALSE` and no community group, also get **0 adjustment**

### How the bias operates
The process appears to reward people for belonging to a specific kind of local community network. That creates an advantage for applicants who are:

- socially connected
- members of recognized community organizations
- likely local insiders

This is problematic because **community-group membership is not a neutral financial variable**. It may correlate with protected or socially stratified characteristics such as:

- socioeconomic status
- ethnicity or religion
- age
- disability
- length of residence
- migration status
- social class

Similarly, `LocalResident` may function as a proxy for geography-based favoritism, benefiting established local applicants over newcomers or people living outside the area.

### Evidence from outcomes
The strongest example is:

- **C004**: base score `690`, receives `+10`, becomes `700`, and is **Approved**
- Without that adjustment, this case likely would not have crossed the approval threshold

Meanwhile:

- **C003**: base score `715`, no adjustment, **Rejected**

That suggests the adjustment may be more influential than underlying creditworthiness alone. A person with a lower initial score but the “right” affiliation gets approved, while another with a higher score but no such affiliation is denied.

### Which groups are favored
Favored applicants appear to be those with:

- membership in a recognized **community group**
- possibly **local resident** status
- access to social institutions that the system values

Disfavored applicants appear to be those who:

- have **no community affiliation**
- are **non-local**
- may be equally or more creditworthy but lack the social/geographic markers that trigger bonus points

### Fairness implications
This creates **systematic inequity** because final decisions are not based solely on financial risk. Instead, they are influenced by social belonging and geography.

Potential harms include:

- **Exclusion of equally qualified applicants** who are not part of favored networks
- **Indirect discrimination** if community membership or residency proxies for protected traits
- **Entrenchment of existing privilege**, since people already integrated into local civic structures gain easier access to approval
- **Reduced transparency**, because the “+10 Community” bonus looks small but may be decisive

### Bottom line
The bias manifests through a **preferential score adjustment for community affiliation**, likely reinforced by **local residency**. This favors socially embedded local applicants and disadvantages people without those affiliations, even when their actual creditworthiness is similar or better. As a result, the process risks being unfair, inequitable, and potentially discriminatory by using social and geographic proxies rather than purely relevant credit factors.
