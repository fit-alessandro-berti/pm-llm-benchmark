### 1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics

To move beyond surface-level observations, I will employ **Process Mining** to transform the raw MES event logs into a transparent, mathematical model of the shop floor.

*   **Reconstruction of Job Flow:** I will use **Discovery Algorithms** (like Inductive Miner) to reconstruct the "As-Is" process model. This will visualize the actual paths jobs take, revealing deviations from the intended routing (e.g., jobs skipping inspection or returning for rework), which is critical in a high-mix environment.
*   **Quantifying KPIs via Process Mining:**
    *   **Lead Time & Makespan:** I will calculate the time delta between `Job Released` and the final `Task End` for every `Case ID`. By plotting the distribution (histogram) of these times, we can identify if lead times are "stable" or "highly erratic."
    *   **Queue (Waiting) Times:** I will measure the interval between a `Queue Entry` event and the subsequent `Task Start` event. This isolates "non-value-added" time, pinpointing exactly where WIP accumulates.
    *   **Resource Utilization:** Using the timestamps of `Task Start` and `Task End`, I will calculate the ratio of *Productive Time* (actual task duration) to *Total Time* (Productive + Setup + Idle + Breakdown). This identifies whether a machine is a bottleneck due to high load or high setup frequency.
    *   **Sequence-Dependent Setup Analysis:** This requires **Sequence Mining**. I will extract pairs of consecutive jobs on the same machine (e.g., $Job_A \rightarrow Job_B$). By correlating the `Setup Start` and `Setup End` timestamps with the properties of $Job_A$ and $Job_B$, I can build a **Setup Time Matrix** that quantifies the time penalty for every possible job transition.
    *   **Schedule Adherence & Tardiness:** I will calculate the "Tardiness Magnitude" per job: $\max(0, \text{Actual Completion} - \text{Due Date})$. I will then perform **Conformance Checking** to see if tardy jobs follow a specific "path" (e.g., do all late jobs pass through the CNC Milling station?).
    *   **Disruption Impact:** I will use **Correlation Analysis** to measure the "ripple effect." When a `Breakdown Start` event occurs, I will track the subsequent increase in `Queue Entry` times and the delay in `Task End` times for downstream processes.

### 2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies

By applying the metrics above, I will look for specific patterns of inefficiency:

*   **The Bottleneck Congestion Path:** If process mining shows high utilization on `MILL-03` and simultaneously shows massive `Queue Entry` durations for all jobs requiring milling, `MILL-03` is a structural bottleneck. This bottleneck dictates the throughput of the entire plant.
*   **Priority Inversion:** Through **Variant Analysis**, I will compare the flow of "High Priority" jobs against "Medium Priority" jobs. If the "High Priority" variant shows similar or higher queue times than others, it proves the current EDD (Earliest Due Date) rule is being "bullied" by sheer volume or poor sequencing.
*   **Setup "Death Spirals":** I will look for sequences where the cumulative setup time consumes a disproportionate percentage of the shift. If a machine spends 40% of its time in `Setup Start $\rightarrow$ Setup End`, the pathology is **suboptimal sequencing**—the shop is likely picking jobs based on due date without considering the setup penalty.
*   **Downstream Starvation:** By analyzing the "inter-arrival time" of tasks at downstream machines, I can identify if a machine is idling because an upstream machine is struggling with long setups or frequent breakdowns, creating a "pulsing" effect in WIP.

### 3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness

I will differentiate between **Capacity Issues** (the machine can't do it) and **Logic Issues** (the machine is doing the wrong thing) using the following logic:

*   **Logic vs. Capacity:** If the total *Productive Time* required by the current order book exceeds the total *Available Machine Hours*, the issue is **Capacity**. If productive time is sufficient but *Tardiness* and *WIP* are high, the issue is **Scheduling Logic** (inefficient setups or poor prioritization).
*   **Estimation Error:** I will perform a **Residual Analysis** by comparing `Task Duration (Planned)` vs. `Task Duration (Actual)`. If the actuals are consistently higher, the root cause is "Planning Fallacy"—the schedules are built on unrealistic data, making them inherently fragile.
*   **Reactive vs. Proactive Management:** By analyzing the time elapsed between a `Breakdown Start` and a change in the queue/schedule, I can determine if the lack of real-time responsiveness is a primary driver of disruption.
*   **The Complexity Trap:** In a high-mix shop, the root cause is often the failure to account for **Complexity-induced Variability**. If certain job types (e.g., "Heat Treatment" combos) always lead to higher variance in lead times, the current static rules are too "blind" to handle this complexity.

### 4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies

I propose the following three strategies to replace the current local dispatching rules:

#### Strategy 1: Dynamic Multi-Factor Dispatching (DM-FD)
*   **Core Logic:** Instead of a single rule (like EDD), each work center uses a **Composite Priority Index (CPI)** calculated in real-time.
*   **Formula Concept:** $CPI = (w_1 \cdot \text{Urgency}) + (w_2 \cdot \text{Setup Penalty}) + (w_3 \cdot \text{Downstream Pressure})$.
    *   *Urgency:* Time remaining until due date.
    *   *Setup Penalty:* The time required to set up for this job based on the *current* machine state (mined from historical matrices).
    *   *Downstream Pressure:* The current queue length at the next station.
*   **Data Usage:** Uses real-time MES queue lengths and the mined Setup Time Matrix.
*   **Expected Impact:** Reduces both tardiness (via Urgency) and setup time (via Setup Penalty), stabilizing lead times.

#### Strategy 2: Stochastic Predictive Scheduling (SPS)
*   **Core Logic:** Moving from deterministic to **probabilistic scheduling**. Instead of scheduling a task for "60 minutes," the system uses the $P_{90}$ (90th percentile) value from the mined historical distribution for that specific task/operator/machine combination.
*   **Data Usage:** Uses the probability density functions (PDFs) of task durations mined from the event logs.
*   **Expected Impact:** Significantly increases the **reliability of completion estimates** provided to customers and builds "intelligent buffers" that prevent minor delays from cascading into major tardiness.

#### Strategy 3: Intelligent Setup-Minimizing Batching (ISB)
*   **Core Logic:** For bottleneck machines, the scheduler implements a "Look-Ahead" window. When a machine finishes a task, the algorithm scans the next $N$ jobs in the queue and selects a job that minimizes the *transition setup time*, even if it isn't the absolute next in line by due date (within a defined "slack" limit).
*   **Data Usage:** Uses the sequence-dependent setup matrix to find "low-cost" transitions.
*   **Expected Impact:** Maximizes machine utilization and reduces WIP by increasing the "velocity" of the bottleneck.

To ensure these strategies don't fail in the real world, I will implement a two-stage validation process:

*   **Comparison:** Strategies will be ranked based on their ability to minimize the "Cost of Tardiness" and "WIP Carrying Cost."

**Stage 2: Continuous Improvement Loop (Closed-Loop Process Mining)**
Once a strategy is deployed, it enters a continuous feedback loop:
1.  **Monitor:** Ongoing process mining compares the *actual* execution to the *predicted* schedule.
2.  **Detect Drift:** If the "Conformance" between the planned and actual flow drops (e.g., because a new type of material is causing longer setup times), the system flags a **"Model Drift."**
3.  **Adapt:** The system automatically triggers a re-calculation of the weights ($w_1, w_2, w_3$) in the DM-FD strategy or updates the Setup Matrix, ensuring the scheduling logic evolves alongside the shop's changing dynamics.
