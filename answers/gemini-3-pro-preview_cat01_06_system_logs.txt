### Logic Explanation

To transform the raw system logs into this event log, the following logic was applied:

**1. Case Identification Strategy (Object-Centric)**
The raw log shows the user multitasking between different applications. If we treated the entire session as one "case," the process map would be a "spaghetti" mess of unrelated activities.
Instead, I identified **Cases based on the specific Work Item (Document or Context)**.
*   **Document1:** Represents the creation of a new document.
*   **Quarterly_Report:** Represents the editing of an existing report. Note that this case starts at the beginning of the log, goes dormant while the user works on other things, and resumes at the end.
*   **Email_AnnualMeeting:** Represents the handling of a specific email thread.
*   **Budget_2024:** Represents the financial update task.
*   **Report_Draft_PDF:** Represents a review task.

**2. Activity Abstraction**
Raw system actions were mapped to standardized business process activities to ensure readability:
*   **FOCUS/SWITCH:** These were generally interpreted as `Open Document` or `Resume Editing`. Explicit "SWITCH" events were removed as they act as transitions between cases, not activities *within* a case.
*   **TYPING:** Mapped to `Edit Content` (Word/Email) or `Update Financial Data` (Excel).
*   **CLICK:** Mapped to specific intents based on the UI element clicked (e.g., `Compose Reply`, `Send Email`).
*   **SCROLL:** Mapped to `Review Content` or `Review Document`.
*   **HIGHLIGHT:** Mapped to `Annotate Document`.
*   **SAVE/CLOSE:** Mapped directly to `Save` and `Close` activities.

**3. Handling Multitasking**
Because the user switches windows, the timestamps in the final event log are chronological, but the **Case IDs interleaved**. This is essential for process mining; it allows a tool to separate the "Email Process" from the "Word Document Process" and analyze them individually, even though they happened concurrently in real-time.
