Here is a comprehensive process mining approach to analyzing and optimizing TechSolve Solutions’ resource assignment practices.

### 1. Analyzing Resource Behavior and Assignment Patterns

To understand the dynamics of the service desk, we must move beyond simple process flows and focus on the "Resource Perspective" within the process mining tool.

**Metrics for Performance and Behavior:**
*   **Workload Distribution:** We will calculate the **Active Time** vs. **Idle Time** for each agent. By visualizing the workload over time (e.g., a Gantt chart resource view), we can identify agents who are consistently overloaded (working 100+% of capacity) versus those with significant idle periods.
*   **Activity Processing Times:** We will analyze the **Average Handling Time (AHT)** and **Cycle Time** per agent. Crucially, we will compare these times against ticket categories. If Agent A takes 4 hours to resolve a "Password Reset" while Agent B takes 10 minutes, this indicates a proficiency disparity.
*   **First-Call Resolution (FCR) Rate:** Specifically for L1 agents, we will measure the percentage of tickets marked "Resolved" directly versus those followed by an "Escalate" activity. A low FCR rate suggests a need for training or better initial routing.
*   **Skill Utilization:** By comparing the `Agent Skills` attribute with the `Ticket Category` and `Required Skill` attributes, we can calculate a **Skill Match Rate**. This tells us how often agents are working on tickets that actually require their specific documented skills.

**Process Mining Techniques for Assignment Patterns:**
*   **Resource Activity Matrix:** This heatmap will show which agents perform which activities most frequently. We expect to see L1 agents clustered around "Triage" and "Basic Troubleshooting," and L2/L3 agents clustered around specific technical tasks. Deviations (e.g., an L3 agent frequently performing password resets) highlight misallocation.
*   **Social Network Analysis (SNA) - Handover of Work:** We will map the flow of tickets between resources. A "Handover" occurs when a ticket is assigned or escalated from one agent to another.
    *   *The Real vs. Intended Logic:* The intended logic is a tiered escalation (L1 -> L2 -> L3). The SNA might reveal a "Mesh" network where tickets bounce laterally between L2 agents (reassignments) or even bounce back from L2 to L1 (downward escalation), indicating confusion over ownership.
*   **Role Discovery:** Using clustering algorithms on the resource activity profiles, we can discover *de facto* roles. We might find that "L1" is too broad and actually consists of two distinct groups: "Generalist Triage" and "Junior Specialists," suggesting a need to refine the organizational structure.

**Skill Utilization Analysis:**
We will filter for activities where `Required Skill` is present (e.g., `Database-SQL`). We will then check the `Agent Skills` of the assigned resource.
*   **Specialist Misuse:** We will specifically look for high-skill agents (e.g., L3 with `Security-IAM`) performing tasks that have a low complexity score or do not require that skill. This quantifies the "highly skilled specialists doing low-level tasks" issue.

### 2. Identifying Resource-Related Bottlenecks and Issues

Using the metrics above, we will drill down into specific inefficiencies.

**Pinpointing Specific Problems:**
*   **Skill Availability Bottlenecks:** We will overlay `Required Skill` on the case duration. If all tickets requiring `Networking-Firewall` show a long "Wait Time" between `Assign L2` and `Work L2 Start`, it indicates a shortage of agents with that specific skill or that those agents are blocked by other work.
*   **Delays from Reassignments:** We will calculate the "Time to Reassign" for every ticket with a `Reassign` event. We will quantify the average delay added to the total cycle time specifically by these loops.
*   **Incorrect Initial Assignments:** We will analyze the `Assign L1` activity. If a ticket is assigned to an L1 agent but immediately triggers an `Escalate` activity (short processing time), it indicates the dispatcher (or round-robin logic) failed to match the ticket's complexity to the right agent.
*   **Underperforming/Overloaded Agents:**
    *   *Overloaded:* Agents with consistently high "Work in Progress" (WIP) counts who contribute to SLA breaches.
    *   *Underperforming:* Agents with low FCR rates and high reassignment rates, suggesting they may lack the skills they are credited with.

**Quantifying Impact:**
*   **Reassignment Impact:** *Calculation:* `(Avg Cycle Time of cases with >1 Reassignment) - (Avg Cycle Time of cases with 0 Reassignments)`. This gives management a dollar value for the time wasted.
*   **SLA Correlation:** We will create a scatter plot or correlation matrix comparing the `Number of Reassignments` against `SLA Breach` (Boolean). We expect to see a strong positive correlation, proving that fixing assignment logic directly improves SLA compliance.

### 3. Root Cause Analysis for Assignment Inefficiencies

Once the *where* and *how much* are established, we must determine the *why*.

**Potential Root Causes:**
*   **Deficient Assignment Rules:** The "round-robin" logic ignores context. A P2 Network ticket might be assigned to an L1 agent whose strength is Access Management simply because they are next in the queue.
*   **Inaccurate Skill Profiles:** Agents may be tagged with a skill in the HR system (e.g., "OS-WindowsServer") but never actually handle those tickets, or conversely, handle them frequently but the tag is missing.
*   **Poor Initial Triage:** The `Ticket Category` chosen at creation might be wrong. If `Required Skill` is updated later (e.g., changed from "Software-App" to "Database-SQL" during L1 analysis), it forces a reassignment.
*   **L1 Empowerment:** If the "Decision Mining" reveals that L1 agents escalate `App-CRM` tickets 90% of the time, it implies a training gap. They treat the ticket as a "black box" and pass it on immediately.

**Using Variant Analysis and Decision Mining:**
*   **Variant Analysis:** We will compare the "Top 1 Most Frequent Variant" (the happy path) against "Variants containing Reassignments." We will look for attributes in the reassignment variants, such as specific agents, specific priorities, or times of day.
*   **Decision Mining:** We will build a decision tree for the `Escalate L2` activity.
    *   *Hypothesis:* The tree might reveal that if `Ticket Priority` = P2 AND `Agent Skills` does *not* contain `Networking`, the probability of escalation is 95%.
    *   *Insight:* This confirms that the lack of a specific skill is the driver for escalation, validating the need for skill-based routing.

### 4. Developing Data-Driven Resource Assignment Strategies

Based on the analysis, here are three distinct strategies to optimize TechSolve’s operations:

**Strategy 1: Proficiency-Weighted Skill-Based Routing**
*   **Issue Addressed:** Skill mismatches, incorrect initial assignments, and specialists doing general work.
*   **Process Mining Insight:** Using the Resource Activity Matrix and historical resolution times, we calculate a "Proficiency Score" for every Agent+Skill combination (e.g., Agent B12 solves App-CRM tickets 2x faster than the L2 average).
*   **Data Required:** `Agent Skills`, `Required Skill`, `Activity Duration`, `Ticket Category`.
*   **Implementation:** Replace round-robin with an algorithm that matches the `Required Skill` to the agent with the highest `Proficiency Score` who currently has the lowest workload.
*   **Expected Benefits:** Increased First-Call Resolution, reduced need for reassignments, and faster AHT due to skills matching.

**Strategy 2: Workload-Aware Dynamic Queuing**
*   **Issue Addressed:** Uneven workload and SLA breaches caused by agents being overloaded while others are idle.
*   **Process Mining Insight:** The workload analysis likely shows that "Availability" (Agent is logged in) does not equal "Capacity" (Agent is drowning in WIP).
*   **Data Required:** Real-time event stream of `Work Start` and `Work End`, plus average `Activity Duration` per agent type.
*   **Implementation:** Implement a weighted queue system. Before assigning a ticket, the system checks the agent's current "Active Case Count" and their "Average Remaining Time" for active cases. It assigns to the agent with the lowest estimated time to clearance, rather than the agent who has been idle the longest.
*   **Expected Benefits:** Balanced utilization across the team, reduced wait times for customers, and fewer SLA breaches during peak hours.

**Strategy 3: Predictive Triage and "Smart" Escalation Guardrails**
*   **Issue Addressed:** Poor initial categorization and unnecessary escalations overwhelming L2/L3.
*   **Process Mining Insight:** Decision mining reveals that certain ticket attributes (e.g., "Keywords in Description: Firewall," "Priority: P2") historically result in an L2 escalation >95% of the time.
*   **Data Required:** Historical event logs, ticket descriptions (text mining), final resolution tier.
*   **Implementation:**
    1.  **Ingestion:** When a ticket is created, a predictive model classifies the "Likely Required Tier" and "Likely Skill."
    2.  **Routing:** Tickets with a >90% probability of requiring L2 are routed directly to the L2 pool, bypassing L1 entirely.
    3.  **Guardrails:** Conversely, if an L1 agent tries to escalate a ticket that the model predicts they *should* be able to solve (based on their skill profile), the system prompts them with a Knowledge Base article or requires a specific reason code before escalating.
*   **Expected Benefits:** Significant reduction in "tunneling" (unnecessary L1 hops), protection of L2/L3 capacity for true complex issues, and improved L1 efficiency.
