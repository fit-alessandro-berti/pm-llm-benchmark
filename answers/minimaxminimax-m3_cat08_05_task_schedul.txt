# Strategic Process Mining and Advanced Scheduling Framework for Precision Parts Inc.

Precision Parts Inc. operates in a complex, high-mix environment where traditional local dispatching rules fail to optimize global objectives. By leveraging the granular data captured within their Manufacturing Execution System (MES), we can transition from reactive scheduling to a proactive, data-driven orchestration of the shop floor. The following outlines a comprehensive methodology using process mining (PM) to analyze, diagnose, and solve their operational challenges.

---

## 1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics

The MES event logs are the ground truth of the shop floor's actual behavior. The first phase involves reconstructing the *as-is* processes and quantifying performance using specialized process mining techniques.

### 1.1 Reconstructing Job Flows and Task Execution
*   **Process Discovery:** Algorithms like the **Alpha algorithm**, **Inductive Visual Miner**, or **Heuristic Miner** will be used to construct a process model from the event logs. While the general flow (Cutting -> Milling -> ...) might be known, PM will reveal actual variants, parallelisms, loops (rework), and hidden paths.
*   **Dotted Chart Analysis:** This is crucial for visualizing the temporal dynamics. Plotting cases (jobs) on the X-axis and timestamps on the Y-axis, color-coded by activity or resource, will immediately expose bottlenecks (long horizontal lines indicating waiting), batching behavior, and the ripple effect of disruptions.
*   **Resource Interaction & Social Network Analysis:** We will build networks showing the handover of work between resources (machines/operators). This reveals informal dependencies, operator-machine specialization, and the actual workflow structure versus the planned routing.

### 1.2 Quantifying Key Performance Metrics
Specific PM techniques and metrics will be applied:

*   **Job Flow Time, Lead Time, and Makespan:**
    *   **Technique:** Case duration analysis. Filter by job priority, due date, or release period.
    *   **Metrics:** Average, median, and percentile (e.g., 95th) distributions of:
        *   **Lead Time:** Time from `Job Released` to final `Task End`.
        *   **Flow Time:** Time from first `Queue Entry` to final `Task End`.
        *   **Makespan:** Total time to complete a batch of jobs.
*   **Task Waiting Times (Queue Times):**
    *   **Technique:** Activity-level performance analysis. For each machine, calculate the time between a case's `Queue Entry` event and its `Task Start` event for the corresponding activity.
    *   **Visualization:** Heatmaps of machines vs. activities, or box plots of waiting times per machine. This pinpoints work centers acting as " parking lots."
*   **Resource Utilization (Machine and Operator):**
    *   **Technique:** Resource performance analysis, often visualized using a **resource heatmap** or timeline.
    *   **Metrics:** Percentage of logged time spent in:
        *   **Productive Processing:** `Task Start` to `Task End`.
        *   **Setup:** `Setup Start` to `Setup End`.
        *   **Idle/Starved:** Time available but no task being processed.
        *   **Blocked/Unavailable:** Including `Breakdown` events.
*   **Sequence-Dependent Setup Times:**
    *   **Technique:** This requires correlating consecutive tasks on the same resource.
        1.  For each machine, extract the sequence of cases it processed.
        2.  Link each `Setup Start` event to the immediately preceding case on that machine (e.g., via the `Notes: Previous job: JOB-6998` field or by sequence logic).
        3.  Calculate the **actual setup duration** (from `Setup Start` to `Setup End`).
        4.  **Analysis:** Build a **setup time matrix** where rows are the previous job (or job type/properties) and columns are the next job. This matrix, often modeled via regression or machine learning using job attributes (material, geometry, priority), is critical for advanced scheduling.
*   **Schedule Adherence and Tardiness:**
    *   **Technique:** Temporal performance analysis comparing completion times to due dates.
    *   **Metrics:**
        *   **Tardiness:** `max(0, Completion Time - Due Date)`.
        *   **Lateness:** `Completion Time - Due Date` (can be negative for early).
        *   **Tardy Job Ratio:** Percentage of jobs completed after their due date.
        *   **Due Date Satisfaction Index (DSI):** A weighted measure. Distribution analysis (histograms) will show the magnitude and frequency of delays.
*   **Impact of Disruptions:**
    *   **Technique:** **Comparative process analysis** and **performance spectrum analysis**.
    *   **Methodology:** Create two cohorts of jobs: those that were active during a `Breakdown Start` or `Priority Change` event and those that were not. Compare their flow times, waiting times at affected resources, and tardiness. Use **root cause analysis** views to trace delays back to a specific disruption event.

## 2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies

The performance analysis will provide hard evidence to diagnose specific, systemic failures in the current scheduling approach.

### 2.1 Bottleneck Resources and Throughput Impact
*   **Diagnosis:** We will identify **structural bottlenecks** (machines with consistently high utilization >90% and long queues) and **dynamic bottlenecks** (resources that become critical under certain load conditions).
*   **Evidence from PM:** High utilization, high waiting time queues *upstream* of the machine, and a "funneling" effect in the process model where cases converge and wait. The impact on throughput is quantified by the degree to which the system's overall throughput rate is constrained by this resource's capacity.

### 2.2 Poor Task Prioritization
*   **Diagnosis:** The current rules (FCFS/EDD) are likely prioritizing ease of execution over global objectives.
*   **Evidence from PM:** **Variant Analysis** comparing "On-Time" vs. "Late" jobs. We will check:
    *   Do jobs with `High` or `Urgent` priority frequently have long waiting times?
    *   Do jobs with earlier due dates get consistently pushed behind jobs with later due dates?
    *   **Resource Contention Analysis:** During periods of high load, does the sequence of processing at bottlenecks favor jobs with laxer due dates?

### 2.3 Suboptimal Sequencing and High Setup Overhead
*   **Diagnosis:** Jobs are likely scheduled in a sequence that maximizes setup times (e.g., alternating between vastly different job types).
*   **Evidence from PM:** The **setup time matrix** will show highly variable setup times for the same resource. Analysis of resource timelines will reveal "rainbow" patterns (constant changeovers) versus periods of "batches" (repeated similar jobs with shorter setups). High overall setup-to-processing time ratio is a key indicator.

### 2.4 Downstream Starvation and Upstream Blocking
*   **Diagnosis:** Imbalanced scheduling leads to a stop-go effect.
*   **Evidence from PM:**
    *   **Starvation:** Resources downstream of a bottleneck will show periodic **idle time** directly correlated with the upstream bottleneck's busy periods.
    *   **Blocking:** Resources upstream of a bottleneck may show tasks completing but no subsequent queue entry for a downstream machine, indicating no available buffer space or the job is waiting for a specific machine. **Dotted charts** are ideal for visualizing this synchronization failure.

### 2.5 WIP Accumulation and the Bullwhip Effect
*   **Diagnosis:** High WIP indicates poor flow.
*   **Evidence from PM:** High average **queue times** at multiple work centers. The bullwhip effect is visible as increasing variance in WIP levels and queue lengths as we move downstream from the release point, often triggered by scheduling variability (e.g., releasing a batch of urgent jobs).

## 3. Root Cause Analysis: Scheduling Logic vs. Inherent Variability

The next critical step is to differentiate between problems that scheduling can fix and those that require capacity investment or process standardization.

### 3.1 Limitations of Static Dispatching Rules
*   **Root Cause:** Rules like FCFS/EDD are myopic. They do not consider the *global* state of the system, the impact on downstream resources, or the cost of a setup. They are purely reactive.
*   **PM Insight:** Conformance checking can quantify how often the current actual sequence violates simple logical rules (e.g., a high-priority job was processed after a medium-priority one despite both being available). This isolates the *rule failure* component.

### 3.2 Lack of Real-Time Shop Floor Visibility
*   **Root Cause:** Decisions are made in isolation at each work center.
*   **PM Insight:** Analysis of **information handover**. The event log will show that the "decision" (task start) is based only on the local queue, not on system-wide data. A clear gap exists between the available data (queues, statuses) and its use in sequencing.

### 3.3 Inaccurate Duration and Setup Estimates
*   **Root Cause:** Plans are based on averages, ignoring variability.
*   **PM Insight:** **Distribution Analysis** of `Task Duration (Actual)` vs. `Task Duration (Planned)`. If actual durations are consistently higher or more variable (high coefficient of variation) than planned, the schedule is built on a flawed foundation. **Conformance checking** on timing can quantify this planning accuracy gap.

### 3.4 Ineffective Handling of Disruptions
*   **Root Cause:** No formal strategy to re-optimize after a breakdown or hot job.
*   **PM Insight:** **Performance comparison** of jobs routed through the system on a "normal" day vs. a day with multiple disruptions. The disproportionate increase in tardiness and WIP during disruption periods shows the lack of resilience.

### 3.5 Differentiating Logic from Capacity Issues
This is a key analytical step:
*   **Capacity Limitation Evidence:** A resource shows >95% utilization *consistently*, with a queue that never fully drains, and long waiting times that are *independent* of the job mix or priority. The bottleneck is the physical limit.
*   **Scheduling Logic Evidence:** A resource shows moderate utilization (~70-80%) but high waiting times. This indicates jobs are being held or sequenced poorly. Alternatively, a highly utilized resource might have waiting times that are *not* correlated with its utilization, pointing to poor release control or sequencing decisions.
*   **PM Tool:** **Bottleneck analysis algorithms** (e.g., based on active period analysis) and **causal net analysis** help attribute delays to either a resource being fully occupied or being idle while work waits.

## 4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies

Based on the diagnostics, we propose three synergistic strategies, moving from enhanced local rules to holistic, predictive systems.

### **Strategy 1: Multi-Factor Dynamic Dispatching (MF-DD) with Setup Awareness**

*   **Core Logic:** Replace static rules at each work center with a composite priority index calculated for each waiting job. The index is a weighted sum of normalized factors, with weights optimized using historical data.
*   **Data/PM Insights Used:**
    *   **Critical Ratio (CR):** `(Due Date - Current Time) / Remaining Processing Time`. From historical flow time distributions per job type.
    *   **Job Priority:** Directly from the log.
    *   **Downstream Load Factor:** The estimated queue length and utilization of the *next* required machine(s) for this job, derived from real-time process mining dashboards.
    *   **Sequence-Dependent Setup Cost:** For the current machine, calculate the estimated setup time to switch from the last completed job to this candidate job, using the **setup time matrix** mined from history.
*   **Addressing Pathologies:** Directly tackles poor prioritization and suboptimal sequencing. It prevents jobs from being "stuck" behind ones that will cause a long setup.
*   **Expected KPI Impact:** **Significant reduction in tardiness** (20-40%) and **setup time overhead** (10-20%), leading to lower WIP and more stable flow.

### **Strategy 2: Predictive Scheduling & Bottleneck Anticipation**

*   **Core Logic:** Use a rolling horizon scheduler (e.g., for the next 4-8 hours) that creates a tentative schedule for bottleneck resources and critical paths. It uses probabilistic models instead of point estimates.
*   **Data/PM Insights Used:**
    *   **Task Duration Distributions:** Mine the log for distributions (e.g., log-normal, Weibull) of actual task durations, segmented by machine, operator, and job attributes (complexity, material). This allows for calculating "P80" durations (80% probability of completion).
    *   **Predictive Maintenance Signals:** While not in the log, we can derive **breakdown frequency and MTBF (Mean Time Between Failures)** per machine from history. Incorporate a stochastic "downtime probability" into the schedule.
    *   **Disruption History:** Use the frequency of urgent hot jobs and priority changes to model their expected impact.
*   **Addressing Pathologies:** Counteracts inaccurate estimates and improves response to disruptions. The scheduler can flag jobs with a high probability of tardiness *before* they become late and suggest proactive resequencing.
*   **Expected KPI Impact:** **Improved due date reliability** (predictable lead times), **reduced WIP** by preventing over-promising and over-loading, and **better machine utilization** by planning for inevitable downtime.

### **Strategy 3: Intelligent Batching & Sequencing Optimization at Bottlenecks**

*   **Core Logic:** This is a specialized tactic for the top 1-2 identified bottleneck resources. It moves beyond a simple queue to an **optimized batch scheduler**.
*   **Data/PM Insights Used:**
    *   **Setup Time Matrix:** The mined matrix is the central input.
    *   **Job Similarity Clustering:** Use job attributes (material, geometry, tooling required) to cluster jobs into families with inherently short intra-family setup times.
    *   **Bottleneck Utilization:** Real-time data on the bottleneck's status.
*   **Addressing Pathologies:** Directly minimizes the **total setup time overhead** at the critical constraint, which is often the largest source of lost capacity. It stabilizes flow at the bottleneck, reducing its variability and downstream starvation.
*   **Mechanism:** Periodically (e.g., every 2 hours), the system groups waiting jobs for the bottleneck into optimal sequences or batches, using heuristics like **Shortest Processing Time (SPT) within similar families** or a **genetic algorithm** for smaller sets. It may even decide to hold a less similar job briefly to wait for a more compatible one, if the setup savings outweigh the waiting cost.
*   **Expected KPI Impact:** **Dramatic increase in bottleneck effective capacity** (5-15% more productive hours), which is the primary lever for increasing overall throughput and reducing lead times.

## 5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement

Before live deployment, strategies must be rigorously tested, and a feedback loop must be established for ongoing adaptation.

### 5.1 Discrete-Event Simulation (DES) for Strategy Validation
A DES model of the entire job shop will be built, **parameterized entirely by data derived from process mining**.

*   **Parameterization Examples:**
    *   **Task Times:** Use the fitted distributions (e.g., Weibull) from the log, not just averages.
    *   **Routing:** Use the discovered process model and variant probabilities.
    *   **Breakdowns:** Use historical frequency and repair time distributions.
    *   **Arrivals:** Model the pattern of new job releases and urgent hot jobs.
    *   **Setup Times:** Implement the mined **sequence-dependent setup matrix** as a lookup function in the simulation.
*   **Scenarios to Test:**
    *   **Baseline:** Current FCFS/EDD logic.
    *   **Strategy Performance:** Each proposed strategy in isolation and combined.
    *   **Stress Tests:**
        *   **High Load:** Increase arrival rate by 20-30%.
        *   **High Disruption:** Simulate a week with 2 major breakdowns/day and 3 hot jobs/day.
        *   **Bottleneck Failure:** Simulate the top bottleneck being unavailable for a full shift.
*   **Evaluation:** Compare strategies on **tardiness, WIP, lead time, and utilization** in the simulated environment. This provides a risk-free, evidence-based selection of the best strategy.

### 5.2 Framework for Continuous Monitoring and Adaptation
The work does not end with deployment. A continuous improvement cycle is required.

1.  **Real-Time KPI Dashboard:** Implement process mining dashboards that monitor the same KPIs analyzed historically (tardiness, queue time, setup ratio) in real-time.
2.  **Drift Detection:**
    *   **Process Drift:** Use conformance checking to detect if the actual process starts to deviate from the desired process model (e.g., new rework loops, unexpected paths).
    *   **Performance Drift:** Automatically alert when a KPI (e.g., average tardiness) exceeds a control limit (e.g., 2 standard deviations from the post-implementation baseline).
    *   **Concept Drift in Models:** Periodically re-mine the **task duration distributions** and **setup time matrices**. Machine learning models can detect when their prediction accuracy is degrading.
3.  **Feedback and Re-optimization:**
    *   The monitoring system triggers a review.
    *   A/B testing framework: Run a modified version of the scheduling strategy (e.g., with new weights in the MF-DD index) on a subset of jobs or work centers.
    *   The cycle repeats: **Analyze -> Diagnose -> Adjust Strategy -> Monitor Impact**. This creates a learning organization that continuously refines its scheduling intelligence based on the most current data.

By implementing this framework, Precision Parts Inc. will transform its scheduling from a source of chronic disruption into a competitive advantage, achieving predictable lead times, high on-time delivery, and optimal use of its critical resources.
