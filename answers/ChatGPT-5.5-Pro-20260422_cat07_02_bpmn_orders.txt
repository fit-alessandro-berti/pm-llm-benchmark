The visualization is a **BPMN-style order-fulfillment process**. It shows how an order moves across four departments: **Sales**, **Production**, **Procurement**, and **Warehouse**.

Solid arrows show the internal sequence of work inside a department. Dashed arrows show messages or handoffs between departments.

## Overall story

The process begins in **Sales**, where an order is checked and customer data is verified. Sales then sends the order details to both **Production** and **Procurement**. Production plans and manufactures the product, but it must wait until Procurement confirms that the required materials are acceptable. Once the product is complete, the **Warehouse** sends it to the customer. After Sales receives confirmation that the product was sent, Sales drafts the invoice.

## Step-by-step explanation

### 1. Sales starts the process

The process begins with a timer-like start event labeled **“Monday morning.”**

Sales then enters a subprocess called **“Check customer’s order.”** Inside that subprocess:

1. Sales checks the order for completeness.
2. Sales checks whether the customer is already in the database.
3. An exclusive gateway decides what happens next:

   * If the **customer is already in the database**, the process continues.
   * If the **customer is not in the database**, Sales adds the customer to the database first.
4. The two paths merge again and the customer-order check is complete.

The database icon represents the customer database used or updated by Sales.

### 2. Sales sends the order details

After the order and customer information are validated, Sales performs **“Send order details.”**

This sends order information to two other departments:

* **Production**, so production can start planning.
* **Procurement**, so materials can be ordered and checked.

Those are shown by dashed message flows labeled **“Order details.”**

### 3. Production receives the order

In the **Production** lane, the process starts when **order details arrive**.

Production then reaches a parallel gateway, marked with a **plus sign**. This means multiple things need to happen before production can continue.

Production does two things in parallel:

1. **Plan production**
2. Wait for confirmation from Procurement that the **material is not faulty**

After production planning is done and the material confirmation has arrived, the paths join again at another parallel gateway. Only then can Production proceed to **Manufacture product**.

Once manufacturing is complete, Production sends a **“Product complete”** message to the Warehouse.

### 4. Procurement orders and checks materials

In the **Procurement** lane, the process also begins when **order details arrive**.

Procurement enters a subprocess called **“Material Compensation Subprocess.”**

Inside it:

1. Procurement orders materials.
2. There is a compensation mechanism connected to **“Return material.”**

The compensation symbol indicates that if something goes wrong later, the ordered material can be returned as a compensating action.

After materials are ordered, Procurement performs **“Check materials.”**

There are two implied outcomes:

* If the material is acceptable, Procurement sends a **“Material not faulty”** message to Production.
* If the material is faulty or the order cannot continue, Procurement triggers cancellation/compensation behavior, shown by the path toward **“Order cancellation.”**

The diagram suggests that faulty material can lead to returning materials and cancelling the order, although the cancellation’s effect on the other lanes is not fully expanded in the picture.

### 5. Warehouse sends the product

The **Warehouse** lane starts when it receives the **“Product complete”** message from Production.

Warehouse then performs **“Send product.”**

After the product is sent, Warehouse emits a **“Product sent”** message.

### 6. Sales drafts the invoice

Back in **Sales**, the process waits for the **“Product sent”** message.

Once Sales receives confirmation that the product was sent, Sales performs **“Draft invoice.”**

The process then ends.

## Key BPMN elements shown

* **Swimlanes**: Sales, Production, Procurement, and Warehouse show responsibility.
* **Rounded rectangles**: Tasks or subprocesses, such as “Plan production” or “Check customer’s order.”
* **Circles**: Events, such as start events, message events, and end events.
* **Envelope icons**: Message events, meaning something is sent or received.
* **X gateway**: Exclusive decision, used for the customer-database check.
* **Plus gateway**: Parallel split or join, used in Production.
* **Cylinder**: Database/data store.
* **Dashed arrows**: Messages between departments.
* **Compensation marker**: Indicates an undo/cleanup action, such as returning materials if procurement fails.

In plain terms, this is an order-processing workflow where Sales validates the order, Production builds the product, Procurement supplies and checks materials, Warehouse ships the product, and Sales invoices after shipment.
