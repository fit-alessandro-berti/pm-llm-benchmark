**Explanation of Logic**

I grouped events into five cases based on the primary document/window the user was actively working with, as these represent distinct logical units of work (per the guidance on case identification). The cases are:

- **Prepare_Quarterly_Report**: All interactions with Quarterly_Report.docx (initial focus + final drafting session).
- **Draft_Document1**: The complete lifecycle of Document1.docx (initial drafting, information gathering pause, return to incorporate budget data, and closure).
- **Handle_Annual_Meeting_Email**: All Chrome activities related to the email thread.
- **Review_Report_Draft_PDF**: All Acrobat activities on the PDF.
- **Update_Budget_2024**: All Excel activities on the budget file.

**Activity naming** was derived by elevating raw system actions into meaningful, analyst-friendly process steps while preserving context:
- FOCUS  "Open [Document]" or "Return to [Document]"
- TYPING  Content-specific names (e.g., "Draft Introduction", "Insert Budget Reference")
- SAVE/CLOSE  Standardized "Save Document" / "Close Document"
- SWITCH  Transition activities assigned to the *target* case
- CLICK/SCROLL/HIGHLIGHT  Specific actions like "Open Annual Meeting Email", "Highlight Key Findings"

This approach creates a coherent narrative of a multitasking user who starts a draft, gathers supporting information from email/PDF/Excel, completes the draft, and then works on the quarterly report. The resulting event log is suitable for standard process mining tools (e.g., to analyze task durations, switches between work items, or bottlenecks in document preparation).
