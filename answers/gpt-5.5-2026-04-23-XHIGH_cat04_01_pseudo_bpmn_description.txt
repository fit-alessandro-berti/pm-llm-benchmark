## Overall interpretation

The pseudo-BPMN describes a **customer request/order handling process**. A request is received, classified as either **standard** or **custom**, processed according to its type, optionally routed for management approval, and then either finalized with an invoice and confirmation or rejected/reworked depending on decisions made along the way.

The main logic is:

1. Receive the customer request.
2. Determine whether it is a **standard** or **custom** request.
3. Process it using the appropriate path.
4. If the request can proceed, determine whether approval is needed.
5. If approved, or if no approval is required, generate the invoice.
6. Send confirmation to the customer.
7. End the process.

---

## Step-by-step explanation

### 1. Start Event

The process begins when a customer submits a request.

This could be a request for a product, service, quotation, delivery, order, or customized solution.

---

### 2. Task A: “Receive Customer Request”

This task represents the intake of the customer request.

Typical activities may include:

- Recording the customer’s details.
- Capturing the requested product or service.
- Checking whether all required information has been provided.
- Creating a request or order record.
- Identifying whether the request appears to be standard or custom.

The output of this task is a registered customer request ready for classification.

---

## First gateway: Check Request Type

### Gateway: XOR — “Check Request Type”

This is an **exclusive gateway**, also called an **XOR gateway**.

It means that **only one path can be taken** based on the request type:

- If the request is **standard**, the process follows the standard request path.
- If the request is **custom**, the process follows the custom request path.

The two paths are mutually exclusive.

---

# Standard request path

A standard request is one that can be handled using predefined rules, catalog items, standard pricing, normal delivery options, and routine validation procedures.

---

### Task B1: “Perform Standard Validation”

This task checks whether the standard request meets predefined business rules.

Examples of standard validation include:

- Is the requested item or service available in the catalog?
- Is the customer eligible to place the request?
- Are required fields complete?
- Are standard terms and conditions applicable?
- Is the requested quantity within allowed limits?

If the request passes this validation, the process continues to operational checks.

---

## Parallel gateway: Run Parallel Checks

### Gateway: AND — “Run Parallel Checks”

This is a **parallel gateway**, also called an **AND gateway**.

It means that multiple tasks are started at the same time. In this case, two checks are performed in parallel:

- Credit Check
- Inventory Check

Both checks must be completed before the process can continue.

---

### Task C1: “Credit Check”

This task evaluates the customer’s financial or credit status.

It may involve checking:

- Payment history.
- Outstanding balances.
- Credit limit.
- Risk profile.
- Whether advance payment is required.

The result may influence whether approval is needed later.

---

### Task C2: “Inventory Check”

This task verifies whether the requested product or capacity is available.

It may involve checking:

- Stock availability.
- Warehouse capacity.
- Production capacity.
- Lead times.
- Substitute item availability.

The result helps determine whether the request can be fulfilled and when delivery can happen.

---

### AND Join: “All Parallel Checks Completed”

The parallel paths converge at an **AND join**.

This means the process waits until **both** the credit check and inventory check are completed.

Only after both checks are finished can the process move forward.

---

### Task D: “Calculate Delivery Date”

After validation, credit checking, and inventory checking, the business calculates a delivery or fulfillment date.

This date may depend on:

- Stock availability.
- Production or preparation time.
- Shipping lead time.
- Customer location.
- Operational capacity.
- Any credit-related restrictions.

After this task, the standard path is ready to join the common downstream process.

---

# Custom request path

A custom request is one that cannot be handled entirely through standard procedures. It may require special pricing, design, engineering, configuration, production planning, or commercial review.

---

### Task B2: “Perform Custom Feasibility Analysis”

This task determines whether the requested customization can realistically be provided.

The analysis may consider:

- Technical feasibility.
- Required materials.
- Labor or engineering effort.
- Production capability.
- Delivery constraints.
- Cost impact.
- Compliance or contractual issues.
- Strategic or commercial acceptability.

The result is used by the next decision gateway.

---

## Feasibility gateway

### Gateway: XOR — “Is Customization Feasible?”

This is another **exclusive gateway**.

Only one path is taken:

- If customization is feasible, proceed to prepare a custom quotation.
- If customization is not feasible, reject the request.

---

### If Yes: Task E1 — “Prepare Custom Quotation”

If the custom request is feasible, the business prepares a tailored quotation.

This quotation may include:

- Custom pricing.
- Special delivery timeline.
- Scope of work.
- Technical assumptions.
- Required approvals.
- Payment terms.
- Validity period of the quote.
- Special risks or exclusions.

After the quotation is prepared, the custom path joins the common approval/finalization path.

---

### If No: Task E2 — “Send Rejection Notice”

If the customization is not feasible, the business sends a rejection notice to the customer.

This notice may explain:

- That the request cannot be fulfilled.
- Why it is not feasible.
- Possible alternatives, if available.
- Whether the customer can submit a modified request.

After the rejection notice is sent, the process ends.

This path does **not** proceed to approval, invoicing, or final confirmation because the request has been declined.

---

# Convergence after standard or custom processing

The standard and custom paths converge after their respective preparatory work is complete.

The converging points are:

- Standard path: after Task D, “Calculate Delivery Date”.
- Custom path: after Task E1, “Prepare Custom Quotation”.

At this point, the request is considered ready for the next decision: whether approval is required.

In BPMN terms, this would usually be modeled as an **exclusive merge**, because only one of the two paths — standard or custom — was active.

---

## Approval decision

### Gateway: XOR — “Is Approval Needed?”

This exclusive gateway determines whether the request must be reviewed by a manager before final invoicing.

Only one path is taken:

- If approval is needed, the process goes to manager approval.
- If approval is not needed, the process proceeds directly to invoice generation.

Approval may be required because of factors such as:

- High order value.
- Large discount.
- Custom pricing.
- Credit risk.
- Non-standard delivery commitment.
- Low inventory or capacity conflict.
- Contractual exception.
- Special customer terms.
- Margin below threshold.
- Policy or compliance requirement.

---

# Approval path

### If Yes: Task F — “Obtain Manager Approval”

The request is sent to a manager or authorized approver.

The manager reviews the request, including things like:

- Pricing.
- Delivery date.
- Customer credit risk.
- Feasibility.
- Profitability.
- Operational impact.
- Policy compliance.

The manager then decides whether to approve the request.

---

## Approval result gateway

### Gateway: XOR — “Is Approval Granted?”

This exclusive gateway determines what happens after the manager reviews the request.

There are two possible outcomes:

- Approval granted.
- Approval not granted.

---

### If Yes: Task G — “Generate Final Invoice”

If the manager approves the request, the process proceeds to invoice generation.

At this point, the business has accepted the request under the proposed conditions.

---

### If No: Task H — “Re-evaluate Conditions”

If approval is not granted, the request is not immediately finalized. Instead, it goes into a rework step.

This task involves reviewing and adjusting the request conditions.

Possible rework actions include:

- Revising price.
- Changing discount level.
- Adjusting delivery date.
- Modifying quantity.
- Updating payment terms.
- Reducing customization scope.
- Checking alternatives.
- Reworking the quotation.
- Escalating or renegotiating internally.

After re-evaluation, the process loops back to the appropriate earlier task depending on the original request type.

---

## Loop-back behavior

The loop depends on whether the request was standard or custom.

### For standard requests

The process loops back to:

> Task D: “Calculate Delivery Date”

This suggests that, after re-evaluation, the standard request conditions may need to be recalculated.

For example:

- A delivery date may need to be changed.
- Inventory allocation may need to be reconsidered.
- Payment or credit constraints may affect timing.
- The order terms may be revised.

After recalculating the delivery date, the process again reaches the approval-needed gateway.

The request may then:

- Go for approval again.
- Skip approval if the revised conditions no longer require it.
- Be rejected or reworked again, depending on the model’s additional rules.

### For custom requests

The process loops back to:

> Task E1: “Prepare Custom Quotation”

This means the custom quote must be revised.

For example:

- The price may be adjusted.
- The customization scope may be reduced.
- Delivery timing may be changed.
- Special conditions may be added.
- The quotation may be made commercially acceptable.

After the revised quotation is prepared, the process again moves to the approval-needed gateway.

This loop can continue until the request is approved, no longer requires approval, or is otherwise terminated by a business rule not shown in the diagram.

---

# No-approval path

### If No Approval Needed: Task G — “Generate Final Invoice”

If the request does not require managerial approval, the process moves directly to final invoice generation.

This would apply when the request falls within normal business rules, such as:

- Standard pricing.
- Normal delivery timeline.
- Acceptable credit status.
- No exceptional discount.
- No unusual customization risk.
- Order value below approval threshold.

---

## Task G: “Generate Final Invoice”

This task creates the final invoice for the customer.

The invoice may include:

- Product or service details.
- Quantity.
- Price.
- Taxes.
- Discounts.
- Payment terms.
- Delivery date or service date.
- Customer account information.

Both approved requests and requests that do not require approval converge here.

---

## Task I: “Send Confirmation to Customer”

After the invoice is generated, the customer receives confirmation.

This confirmation may include:

- Order confirmation.
- Final invoice.
- Delivery date.
- Payment instructions.
- Terms and conditions.
- Contact information for follow-up.

This is the final customer-facing communication for accepted requests.

---

### End Event

The process ends after confirmation is sent to the customer.

For rejected custom requests, the process ends earlier after the rejection notice is sent.

---

# Summary of gateway meanings

| Gateway | Type | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Check Request Type | XOR | Choose either standard or custom path |
| Run Parallel Checks | AND | Start credit check and inventory check in parallel |
| All Parallel Checks Completed | AND Join | Wait until both parallel checks are complete |
| Is Customization Feasible? | XOR | Continue with quotation if feasible; reject if not |
| Is Approval Needed? | XOR | Either request approval or go directly to invoice |
| Is Approval Granted? | XOR | Proceed to invoice if approved; rework if rejected |

---

# Main differences between standard and custom requests

| Area | Standard Request | Custom Request |
|---|---|---|
| Nature | Routine, predefined, catalog-based | Non-standard, tailored, requires analysis |
| Initial processing | Standard validation | Custom feasibility analysis |
| Operational checks | Credit and inventory checks run in parallel | Feasibility analysis determines whether it can proceed |
| If not viable | No explicit rejection path shown in the pseudo-BPMN | Infeasible customization leads to rejection notice |
| Output before approval | Calculated delivery date | Custom quotation |
| Rework loop | Returns to delivery date calculation | Returns to custom quotation preparation |

---

# When approvals and rework are triggered

Approval is triggered when the process reaches the “Is Approval Needed?” gateway and the request meets criteria requiring review.

Examples include:

- The request is high value.
- The customer has credit concerns.
- Inventory is constrained.
- A special discount is requested.
- Delivery terms are unusual.
- The custom quotation has risk or low margin.
- The request falls outside normal business rules.

Rework is triggered when:

1. Approval is required.
2. The manager reviews the request.
3. The manager does not grant approval.

The process then moves to “Re-evaluate Conditions” and loops back to the appropriate task:

- Standard request: back to delivery date calculation.
- Custom request: back to custom quotation preparation.

---

## Important modeling note

The pseudo-BPMN is logically understandable, but in a formal BPMN diagram you may want to make the convergence and loop-back points explicit.

For example:

- Add an explicit XOR merge before “Is Approval Needed?” to combine the standard and custom feasible paths.
- Add a routing gateway after “Re-evaluate Conditions” to decide whether to loop back to Task D or Task E1.
- Consider adding a maximum number of rework cycles or an escalation/cancellation path to avoid an indefinite loop.
- Consider adding explicit outcomes for failed credit or inventory checks if those checks can cause rejection or rework.

Overall, the model represents a structured process for handling both routine and customized customer requests, with appropriate validation, feasibility analysis, parallel checking, approval control, rework loops, and final customer communication.
