**1.  What a “normative” HiretoRetire workflow looks like**

A typical hiring process contains the following *mandatory* activities in a strict order  

1. **Post_Job_Ad** –the vacancy is advertised.  
2. **Screen_Candidates** –the received applications are screened.  
3. **Conduct_Interviews** –the screened candidates are interviewed.  
4. **Make_Hiring_Decision** –the interview results are evaluated and a hiring decision is taken.  
5. **Onboard_Employee** (the selected candidate is welcomed, paperwork, training, etc.).  
6. **Add_to_Payroll** –the new employee is entered into the payroll system.  
7. **Close_Case** –the hiring case is archived.

In addition, the following logical constraints are normally enforced:

* **Screen  Interview** –you never interview a candidate that has not been screened.  
* **Interview  Decide** –the hiring decision must be based on the interview.  
* **Decide  Onboard** –onboarding only starts after a *positive* decision.  
* **Onboard  Payroll  Close** –the payroll entry and the case closure are performed once the employee has been onboarded.  

Any model that allows any of these essential steps to be **omitted**, **reordered**, or **executed in parallel** in a way that breaks the above chain is regarded as *anomalous*.

## 2.  Analysis of the two POWL models

### 2.1  Model1 (StrictPartialOrder)

```text
Post  Screen  Decide
            
            Interview
            
          Onboard  Payroll  Close
```

**What the model actually says**

* After **Screen** both **Decide** and **Interview** become enabled, but there is **no edge** from **Interview** to **Decide** (nor from **Decide** to **Interview**).  
* Consequently **Decide** may be taken **before**, **after**, or **simultaneously** with **Interview**.  
* The three final activities (**Onboard**, **Payroll**, **Close**) are *mandatory* and are ordered **Decide  Onboard  Payroll  Close**.  

**Anomalies**

| # | Anomaly | Severity | Why it matters |
|---|---------|----------|-----------------|
| 1 | **No precedence** between **Interview** and **Make_Hiring_Decision**. The decision can be made *without* having interviewed the candidate. | **Severe** | The core of hiring is to interview before deciding. Allowing a decision without an interview defeats the purpose of the process. |
| 2 | **Parallel execution** of **Interview** and **Decide** (they are not ordered). This makes it possible to start onboarding while the interview is still in progress. | **ModerateSevere** | Outoforder execution can lead to a hired candidate that has never been interviewed. |
| 3 | No explicit **rejection** branch after the decision. The model always proceeds to onboarding, even if the decision is “reject”. | **Moderate** | In practice a negative decision should stop the hiring chain; the model forces an (unrealistic) “always hire” path. |
| 4 | The model **does** contain *all* the required activities (Post, Screen, Interview, Decide, Onboard, Payroll, Close) and the final three are correctly ordered. | **Low** | The structure is close to the desired workflow; only the ordering of the early steps is broken. |

**Overall impression** – Model1 contains the full set of activities and enforces the crucial **postdecision** part of the workflow (Onboard  Payroll  Close). The problem lies in the **predecision** part: interview may be missing or happen after the decision.

### 2.2  Model2 (Partial Order + LOOP + XOR)

```text
Post  Screen
Post  Interview  Decide  (LOOP: Onboard | skip)  (XOR: Payroll | skip)  Close
```

**What the model actually says**

* **Post** directly triggers both **Screen** and **Interview** (they are independent).  
* **Decide** has only one incoming edge – from **Interview** – so a decision **cannot** be taken unless an interview has occurred, but **Screen** is **not** required before the interview.  
* The **LOOP** operator contains **Onboard** and a **skip** (silent) transition, meaning **Onboard** may be executed repeatedly *or* omitted entirely.  
* The **XOR** operator contains **Payroll** and a **skip**, meaning **Payroll** may be **skipped**.  

**Anomalies**

| # | Anomaly | Severity | Why it matters |
|---|---------|----------|-----------------|
| 1 | **Screening can be bypassed** – the edge `Post  Interview` allows an interview **without** a preceding screen. | **Severe** | Unscreened applicants should never be interviewed; this violates the most basic precondition of a hiring process. |
| 2 | **No enforcement that Screen precedes Interview** – Screen and Interview are independent branches from Post. | **Severe** | Same as above – the model permits interviewing unscreened candidates. |
| 3 | **Onboarding is optional** (LOOP with *skip*). The employee may be “hired” but never onboarded. | **Severe** | Onboarding is a legal and administrative necessity; skipping it renders the hiring incomplete. |
| 4 | **Payroll is optional** (XOR with *skip*). The model permits closing a case without ever adding the employee to payroll. | **Severe** | Payroll processing is a mandatory step for any real employee; omitting it breaks the “HiretoRetire” chain. |
| 5 | **Loop may cause repeated onboarding** – the model allows arbitrary repetitions of Onboard (e.g., reonboarding, training cycles). | **Moderate** | In most hiring scenarios onboarding occurs exactly once; repeated execution is unusual but not necessarily wrong. |
| 6 | The decision can be taken **only after Interview**, which is good, but **Screen** may be missing – the decision may be based on an unscreened interview. | **Severe** | Same root cause as (1). |

**Overall impression** – Model2 contains many *optional* branches and allows the most critical steps (**Screen**, **Onboard**, **Payroll**) to be omitted. The presence of *skip* transitions makes the workflow fundamentally incomplete.

## 3.  Which model is “more normative”?

| Criterion | Model1 | Model2 |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| **All mandatory activities present?** | Yes (Post, Screen, Interview, Decide, Onboard, Payroll, Close) | Yes, but **optional** (Screen, Onboard, Payroll can be skipped) |
| **Ordering of core steps (Post  Screen  Interview  Decide  Onboard  Payroll  Close)** | *Post  Screen* enforced; *Interview  Decide* **not** enforced; *Decide  Onboard  Payroll  Close* enforced | *Post  Interview  Decide* enforced; **Screen** can be omitted; **Onboard** and **Payroll** optional |
| **Possibility to skip a critical step** | No (all steps are mandatory, only ordering is wrong) | Yes (Screen, Onboard, Payroll can be skipped) |
| **Severity of deviations** | One severe ordering issue (Interview  Decide) | Multiple severe issues (screen bypass, optional onboarding, optional payroll) |
| **Clarity of process intent** | Clear flow with a single linear chain (once the ordering is fixed) | Ambiguous – the XOR/LOOP make the intended business logic unclear |

**Conclusion:** **Model1 is the more normative of the two.**  

* It contains *all* the essential activities and forces them to be executed (the model does not contain any “skip” or XOR that would allow omission).  
* The final part of the workflow (Decide  Onboard  Payroll  Close) respects the required sequence.  
* The only real defect is the missing precedence edge **Interview  Decide** (and the resulting parallel execution of Interview and Decide). This is a **single, relativelyeasytofix ordering error**.  

In contrast, **Model2** suffers from **multiple severe anomalies** that directly violate the fundamental business rules of a HiretoRetire process:

* Screening can be completely bypassed, which undermines the very first quality gate.  
* Onboarding and payroll – the two steps that actually turn a candidate into a paid employee – are optional, meaning the model can terminate without having hired anyone.  

Thus, even though Model1 still requires a small correction (adding the edge `Interview  Decide`), it is far closer to a **normative** HiretoRetire workflow than Model2. The anomalies in Model2 fundamentally break the integrity of the process, whereas the anomaly in Model1 is a matter of missing ordering constraints that can be corrected without removing any required activities.
