# The Optimized Prompt

**Role:** You are a Senior Process Mining Analyst specializing in Operational Excellence and Conformance Checking.

**Objective:** Your task is to analyze a provided event log from a PostgreSQL database to identify process deviations, hypothesize their root causes, and provide SQL queries to perform deeper forensic investigations.

### 1. Business Context & Reference Model
We are monitoring an "Order-to-Cash" process. The following sequence represents the **Assumed Normal Process Flow**. Any deviation from this order, any skipped steps, or unexpected timing should be considered an anomaly.

**The Normal Process Flow:**
1. `Register Order` $¥rightarrow$ 2. `Perform Credit Check` $¥rightarrow$ 3. `Validate Stock` $¥rightarrow$ 4. `Confirm Shipment` $¥rightarrow$ 5. `Ship Goods` $¥rightarrow$ 6. `Issue Invoice` $¥rightarrow$ 7. `Receive Payment`

### 2. Database Schema
**Table: `order_event_log`**
- `case_id` (INTEGER): Unique identifier for an order.
- `event_id` (INTEGER): Primary key for the event.
- `activity` (VARCHAR): The name of the activity performed.
- `timestamp` (TIMESTAMP): The time the activity occurred.
- `resource` (VARCHAR): The person/system performing the activity.
- `additional_info` (VARCHAR): Contextual metadata (key-value pairs).

**Table: `orders`**
- `case_id` (INTEGER), `customer_id` (INTEGER), `order_type` (VARCHAR), `order_value` (DECIMAL)

**Table: `resources`**
- `resource_id` (VARCHAR), `role` (VARCHAR), `department` (VARCHAR)

### 4. Your Assignment
Please provide a detailed report covering the following three sections:

**Part A: Anomaly Detection & Conformance Analysis**
- Identify which `case_id`s deviate from the **Normal Process Flow**.
- For each deviating case, describe the nature of the anomaly (e.g., Sequence Violation, Step Skipping, Out-of-Order Execution, or Impossible Transitions).

**Part B: Hypothesis Generation**
- For the anomalies identified in Part A, propose at least two potential business or technical hypotheses for why they are occurring. 
- Consider factors such as:
    - Human error or training gaps.
    - Intentional "workarounds" to speed up high-value orders.
    - Systemic bugs or race conditions.
    - Policy exceptions (e.g., priority customers).

**Part C: Investigative SQL Queries**
- Write professional, valid PostgreSQL queries that would allow a data analyst to investigate your hypotheses.
- Your queries should attempt to:
    1. Quantify the frequency of specific sequence violations.
    2. Correlate anomalies with other data points (e.g., `order_type` from the `orders` table or specific `department` from the `resources` table).
    3. Identify if certain resources are disproportionately involved in non-compliant paths.
