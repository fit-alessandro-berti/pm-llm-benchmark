### How the Bias Manifests

1. **Score Adjustment as a “Perk”**  
   - In GroupB, the `ScoreAdjustment` column explicitly adds **+10 points** when the applicant belongs to a designated community group (`Highland Civic Darts Club`).  
   - This adjustment is *absent* in GroupA. Consequently, an applicant in GroupB can *outscore* a baseline applicant in GroupA even when the raw preliminary score is lower.

2. **ThresholdCrossing Effect**  
   - The decision system likely uses a *hard cutoff* (e.g., 720Approved).  
   - In P002 (protected) the raw score is **710**, below the cutoff  **Rejected**.  
   - In U002 (unprotected) the raw score is also **710**, but **no boost** is applied, leading to the same “Rejected” outcome.  
   - However, when a boost is applied (U001, U003), the adjusted score **exceeds** the cutoff, guaranteeing **Approval** regardless of any other merit considerations.

3. **Systemic Favoritism Toward Community Affiliation**  
   - The mere presence of a `CommunityGroup` label triggers the boost, implying that *membership* in that group confers an *unearned advantage*.  
   - This is a form of *indirect discrimination*: the protected status (e.g., race, gender, etc.) is not directly encoded, but the communitygroup attribute is used as a proxy that advantage a particular socially salient group.

4. **Process Sparseness and Differences**  
   - In GroupA, many activities are labelled with “None” for `CommunityGroup`, and `ScoreAdjustment` stays at 0 throughout.  
   - In GroupB, the same activity (PreliminaryScoring, ManualReview) now carries **“+10 (Community Boost)”** tags, signalling that the pipeline *recognizes* and *modifies* the score based on that attribute.  
   - The extra label creates a **different operational path**: the system must verify the boost, may route to a different reviewer, or may log additional audit steps. This can lead to *differentlatent* processing times and possibly more lenient final decisions.

## Why This Matters

- **Systemic bias is not just “human prejudice”;** it can be encoded in procedural rules (e.g., “add +10 points for community group membership”).  
- Because the boost is applied *automatically* when a certain `CommunityGroup` is present, the bias becomes *structural*: every applicant tagged with that group receives an *instant* advantage, irrespective of other qualifications.  
- The result is a **disproportionate approval rate** for the unprotected cohort, especially when the baseline score would otherwise be insufficient. This skews downstream outcomes (e.g., allocation of resources, benefits, or services) in a way that favors the community group.
