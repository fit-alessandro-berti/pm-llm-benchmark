### **1. Identification of Cases with Longer Resolution Times**

- **Case 105** has the longest cycle time—nearly **2 full days**.

---

### **2. Root Causes of Performance Issues**

We examine process flow and timing patterns across these delayed cases:

#### **A. Presence of Escalations**
- Cases **102** and **105** include the **“Escalate to Level-2 Agent”** step.
- Both show extended durations post-escalation:
  - **Case 102**: Escalated at 11:30; investigation begins only at 14:00 (**2.5-hour gap**).
  - **Case 105**: Escalated at 10:00; investigation starts only at **14:00 the next day**—a **28-hour delay**.

>  **Insight**: Escalation clearly correlates with longer resolution times, but the primary bottleneck isn't the escalation decision itself—it's the **waiting time before the specialized team begins work**.

#### **B. Long Waiting Times Between Activities**
We identify key transition delays:

| Transition | Case 102 | Case 105 |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Escalate  Investigate | 2.5 hrs | **28 hrs** |
| Assign  Investigate | — | **31 hrs** (09:00  next day 14:00) |

Even for **Case 104** (no escalation), we observe:
- **Assign to Level-1** at 09:30  **Investigate Issue** at 13:00 (**3.5-hour gap**)
- Then **Resolve Ticket** occurs only at 08:00 the next day (**19-hour gap**)

>  **Insight**: Bottlenecks exist not just in escalation handling, but also in **agent availability or handoff efficiency**, particularly between assignment and investigation.

#### **C. Resource Allocation Timing**
All escalations occur during business hours (e.g., 10:00–11:30 on 2024-03-01), yet the next investigative action happens much later (next day). This suggests:
- Level-2 agents may not be available to pick up escalated tickets immediately.
- There may be **no SLA or automated trigger** to prioritize escalated cases.
- Possible **batch processing** behavior (e.g., handling escalations only at start of next shift).

---

### **3. Explanations and Recommendations**

#### **Why These Factors Increase Cycle Time**
- **Escalation introduces a secondary queue**, and if not managed actively, it becomes a black hole in the process.
- **Idle waiting post-escalation** indicates misalignment between triage decisions and downstream capacity.
- Even without escalation (e.g., Case 104), **slow progression from assignment to investigation** reveals underutilized Level-1 bandwidth or unclear prioritization.

#### **Recommendations to Reduce Cycle Time**

1. **Implement Escalated Case SLAs**  
   - Define maximum wait times after escalation (e.g., Level-2 must begin investigation within 2 hours).
   - Use automated alerts or ticket routing to ensure immediate visibility.

2. **Improve Handoff Protocols**  
   - Introduce **real-time assignment notifications** aligned with agent shift schedules.
   - Monitor **"Assign  Investigate"** duration as a key performance metric.

3. **Analyze Triage Accuracy**  
   - In Case 105, escalation occurred just 10 minutes after investigation began—suggesting **premature or incorrect Level-1 assignment**.
   - Refine triage criteria to reduce unnecessary escalations, which consume specialized resources.

4. **Enable Parallel Workflows**  
   - Allow Level-2 teams to begin preliminary analysis during triage if early indicators suggest escalation.
   - Reduce serial dependencies where possible.

5. **Monitor Queue Backlogs**  
   - Track case volumes and agent load at Level-2 to anticipate bottlenecks.
   - Use historical data to forecast escalation probability and pre-allocate resources.

---

### **Conclusion**

The primary driver of prolonged resolution times is **delayed action following escalation**, compounded by inefficient transitions between process stages—even in non-escalated cases. While escalation is sometimes necessary, its impact can be mitigated through proactive resource management, clearer SLAs, and tighter operational controls. Addressing these bottlenecks will significantly reduce average cycle times and improve customer satisfaction.
