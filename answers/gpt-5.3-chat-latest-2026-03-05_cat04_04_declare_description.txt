The DECLARE model describes a flexible but rulegoverned loan approval process. Instead of prescribing one fixed sequence, it defines constraints that any valid execution of the process must satisfy. In practice, the process would unfold roughly as follows.

The process always begins with the intake of a loan request. The init constraint requires that Receive_Application is the first activity, and the existence constraint ensures that this step must occur in every case. This reflects the obvious operational reality: a loan process only starts once the bank receives a customer𠏋 application.

Immediately after receiving the application, the institution must perform a Preliminary_Credit_Check. The response constraint from Receive_Application to Preliminary_Credit_Check guarantees that once an application is received, a credit check will eventually happen. Additionally, the exactly_one constraint enforces that this credit check occurs exactly once. This prevents redundant credit inquiries (which could negatively affect the applicant𠏋 credit score) and ensures that risk evaluation is consistently performed.

After the credit check, the process proceeds toward document collection. The succession constraint between Preliminary_Credit_Check and Gather_Additional_Documents ensures two things: whenever the credit check occurs, document gathering must follow at some point, and document gathering cannot happen unless the credit check has already taken place. In practical terms, the bank first determines whether the application is plausible enough to continue before asking the applicant for supporting documentation such as pay stubs or identification.

The existence constraint on Gather_Additional_Documents ensures that documentation is always collected at least once. The coexistence rule linking Gather_Additional_Documents and Authorize_Contract_Terms means that if documents are gathered, the authorization of contract terms must also appear in the process (and vice versa). This models the real-world fact that a bank cannot authorize loan terms without supporting documentation.

Next comes the preparation and verification of the loan offer. After documentation, the process can move to Quality_Assurance_Review. The altsuccession constraint between Gather_Additional_Documents and Quality_Assurance_Review ensures that every documentation step is eventually followed by a review before another documentation step can occur again. This prevents the organization from repeatedly requesting documents without evaluating them.

Once the review occurs, the chainsuccession constraint requires that Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package happens immediately afterward. This reflects a practical workflow: after quality or compliance officers verify the application materials, the loan terms and documents are assembled right away.

Another rule銄esponded_existence珦tates that whenever the loan offer package is assembled, a Quality_Assurance_Review must occur somewhere in the process. This reinforces the idea that internal checks are mandatory before progressing further.

Following the preparation of the loan offer, the process moves toward authorization. The precedence constraint ensures that Quality_Assurance_Review must happen before Authorize_Contract_Terms. This represents a compliance safeguard: no contract terms can be formally approved unless they have been reviewed.

There is also a chainprecedence relationship stating that Authorize_Contract_Terms must be directly preceded by Preliminary_Credit_Check. Operationally, this indicates that the final authorization step must rely on the validated credit assessment and cannot occur without it.

Once the loan terms are authorized and the package has been assembled, funds can be disbursed. The chainresponse constraint ensures that Transfer_Funds occurs immediately after Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package. In practice, this models the transition from final preparation of the offer to the operational act of disbursing money.

Customer communication is tightly controlled. The altresponse constraint specifies that each Transfer_Funds must eventually be followed by Notify_Customer, ensuring the borrower is informed about the transaction. The altprecedence rule requires that every Transfer_Funds must have been preceded by Notify_Customer in an alternating pattern, ensuring consistent communication before subsequent transfers if multiple disbursements occur.

Several constraints prevent undesirable behavior. The absence constraint forbids the activity Proceed_Without_Compliance, representing a prohibited shortcut where regulatory checks would be bypassed. The noncoexistence constraint between Transfer_Funds and Receive_Application ensures they cannot occur in the same case in certain contexts, preventing illogical situations where funds would be transferred during the intake stage.

The nonsuccession rule prohibits Notify_Customer from being followed by Preliminary_Credit_Check. This reflects the expectation that once customers are notified of the outcome, the institution should not restart internal evaluation steps. The nonchainsuccession constraint ensures that Authorize_Contract_Terms is never immediately followed by Notify_Customer, implying that other operational actions (such as fund transfer or processing tasks) must occur before notification.

Taken together, these rules encode important real-world motivations. Risk management is addressed by enforcing a mandatory credit check and structured document verification. Regulatory compliance is reflected in the required quality assurance review and the prohibition of bypassing compliance procedures. Operational efficiency is supported by chain constraints that ensure certain tasks follow immediately after others, minimizing delays. Customer satisfaction is ensured by requiring notification after key outcomes such as fund transfer.

If these constraints were violated, significant problems could occur. Skipping the credit check or document gathering would expose the lender to fraud or default risk. Authorizing contract terms without a prior quality review could lead to regulatory violations or incorrect loan conditions. Transferring funds before authorization or documentation could create financial loss or legal liability. Not notifying customers after fund transfer would damage trust and lead to service complaints. Finally, allowing compliance to be bypassed could result in severe regulatory penalties and reputational damage.

Overall, the DECLARE model captures a realistic loan approval workflow: starting with application intake, moving through risk evaluation and documentation, passing through internal quality checks and authorization, and concluding with fund disbursement and customer communication, all while ensuring strict compliance and risk controls.
