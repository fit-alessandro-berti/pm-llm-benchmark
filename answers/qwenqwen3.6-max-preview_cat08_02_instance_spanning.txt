### Differentiating Within-Instance vs. Between-Instance Waiting Time
* **Within-Instance Wait:** Delays inherent to a single case (e.g., picker travel time, system processing, manual verification). Identified by analyzing activity duration distributions per case without cross-case resource overlap. Process mining reveals this as consistent, case-internal idle time between `START` and `COMPLETE` events.
* **Between-Instance Wait:** Delays caused by inter-case dependencies. Identified via:
  * **Resource Contention Wait:** Queue mining matches case request timestamps with resource release timestamps. If `Case A` requests `Station C2` at 10:20 but `Station C2` is occupied by `Case B` until 10:28, the 8-minute gap is classified as resource contention wait.
  * **Synchronization Wait (Batching):** Detected when multiple cases complete upstream steps but remain idle until a batch trigger fires. Process mining flags this as clustered idle time across multiple cases sharing a region/batch attribute.
  * **Regulatory/Policy Wait:** Identified when cases are ready to proceed but are held because a global counter (e.g., hazardous concurrent limit) is maxed out. This appears as synchronized queuing across unrelated cases with the `Hazardous Material` flag.

By tagging each idle interval with its root cause (resource, sync, policy, or internal), we can build a **Wait Time Attribution Model** that quantifies exactly how much delay stems from instance-spanning constraints vs. internal process inefficiencies.

## 2. Analyzing Constraint Interactions

Instance-spanning constraints rarely operate in isolation. Their interactions create compound bottlenecks and non-linear delay patterns:

* **Express Priority × Cold-Packing Scarcity:** Express orders requiring cold-packing will preempt or jump the queue for a highly constrained resource (5 stations). This creates **priority starvation** for standard cold-chain orders, disproportionately inflating their cycle times and increasing spoilage risk. Frequent preemption also introduces context-switching overhead, reducing effective station throughput.
* **Batching × Hazardous Regulatory Cap:** If a regional batch accumulates multiple hazardous orders, the facility-wide cap (10 in Packing/QC) may be triggered. Non-hazardous orders destined for the same region will then wait unnecessarily for the batch, even though they aren't subject to the cap. Conversely, aggressive batching can cause hazardous orders to arrive at Packing/QC in surges, repeatedly hitting the regulatory ceiling and causing facility-wide flow restrictions.
* **Priority × Batching:** Express orders may bypass batching or force early batch dispatch to meet SLAs. While this protects express delivery times, it reduces route optimization efficiency for standard orders, increasing shipping costs and potentially delaying standard label generation due to fragmented batch cycles.
* **Cold-Packing × Hazardous Limits:** Orders requiring both cold-packing and hazardous handling consume two constrained dimensions simultaneously. They occupy limited cold stations while counting toward the hazardous concurrency cap, creating a **dual-constraint bottleneck** that amplifies queueing delays upstream.

### Why Understanding Interactions is Crucial
Optimizing constraints in isolation leads to **sub-optimization and policy conflict**. For example, adding more cold-packing capacity without adjusting hazardous flow control could increase cap breaches. Similarly, tightening batch triggers to reduce wait times might fragment shipments and increase logistics costs. A holistic, interaction-aware model ensures trade-offs are explicitly managed, prevents bottleneck shifting, and enables strategies that harmonize competing constraints rather than exacerbating them.
