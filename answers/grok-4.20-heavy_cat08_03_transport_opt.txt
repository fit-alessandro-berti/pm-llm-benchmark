**1. Process Discovery and Conformance Checking**

To create a cohesive event log, first define the **case notion** as Vehicle-Day (e.g., V12-20241205). This represents a complete delivery tour and allows end-to-end analysis of a driver's shift. Integrate the sources by joining on common keys: Timestamp (normalized to a single timezone/format), Vehicle ID, Driver ID, and Location (for spatial linking). Scanner events provide discrete delivery milestones ("Arrive Customer", "Delivery Success/Failed", "Depart Customer"), GPS data supplies frequent updates on movement, speed, and status (aggregated into higher-level activities such as "Travel", "Traffic Delay", or "Idle"), dispatch data adds planned activities and time windows as attributes, and maintenance logs insert "Unscheduled Stop" or "Maintenance" events.

Standardize activity names (e.g., map various GPS low-speed readings to "Traffic Delay" or "Idle") and sort events chronologically within each case. Package ID can serve as a secondary trace for per-delivery views when needed. Use tools such as PM4Py, Celonis, or Disco for ETL.

**Challenges** include:
- Granularity mismatch (high-frequency GPS vs. sparse scanner events) requiring aggregation or filtering to avoid overly complex "spaghetti" models.
- Timestamp synchronization and missing data (e.g., unscanned deliveries or GPS gaps).
- Event correlation across systems.
- Choosing the right abstraction level—too fine-grained leads to unreadable models; too coarse loses insights into delays.

For **process discovery**, apply the Inductive Miner (or Fuzzy Miner for noisy data) to the event log. These algorithms produce a sound process model that captures the actual flow: Start Shift  Route Assigned  Depart Depot  repeated (Travel  Arrive Customer  [Delivery Success or Failed]  Depart Customer)  possible branches for Unscheduled Stop/Maintenance  Arrive Depot  End Shift. The model visualizes frequencies (e.g., how often failed deliveries occur), loops (re-attempts or returns to depot), and parallel or optional activities (maintenance during route). Animation on the map reveals typical sequences and outliers.

**Conformance checking** involves creating a normative model from the dispatch system's planned routes and time windows, then using alignments (preferred over token-based replay for complex logs) or multi-alignments to quantify fitness. Key deviations to detect:
- **Insertions**: Unplanned activities like "Traffic Delay", "Low Speed Detected", "Unscheduled Stop", or extra idling.
- **Skips or wrong order**: Deliveries out of planned sequence, missed stops, or deviations from optimal routing.
- **Timing/performance deviations**: Actual travel or service times significantly exceeding planned estimates.
- **Case-level violations**: Tours ending in overtime or with incomplete deliveries.

Metrics such as fitness score, precision, and generalization help quantify how much the actual process deviates from the ideal, highlighting systemic issues like poor route planning in certain urban zones.

**2. Performance Analysis and Bottleneck Identification**

Relevant **KPIs** derivable from the enriched event log include:
- **On-Time Delivery (OTD) Rate**: Percentage of "Delivery Success" timestamps falling within the customer time window from the dispatch system.
- **Average Time per Delivery Stop (Service Time)**: Difference between "Arrive Customer" and "Depart Customer" timestamps.
- **Travel Time vs. Service Time Ratio**: Aggregated travel durations (derived from GPS depart/arrive or speed/location changes) divided by service times per case.
- **Failed Delivery Rate**: Proportion of "Delivery Failed" events.
- **Tour Duration and Overtime**: Time from "Depart Depot" to "Arrive Depot" and "End Shift" vs. planned or standard hours.
- **Idle/Low-Speed Time Percentage**: Aggregated time spent at low speed or stopped (from GPS) as a proxy for fuel inefficiency or traffic.
- **Vehicle Utilization**: Active delivery/travel time as a percentage of total shift time.
- **Delay Frequency/Duration**: Count and length of "Low Speed Detected" or "Unscheduled Stop" events, potentially correlated with location.

These are calculated by replaying the event log on the discovered model or using direct queries on the log (e.g., via Python/pandas or process mining software).

**Techniques** for bottleneck identification include performance-enhanced process maps (annotate activities and arcs with mean/median durations, waiting times, or costs) and bottleneck detection algorithms that highlight activities or handoffs with the highest sojourn times or lowest throughput. Replay the log on the model with animation to see where cases "pile up." Use process cubes or filtering by attributes (time of day, driver ID, vehicle ID, location clusters derived from lat/lon via geospatial analysis) to slice the data. For example, dotted charts or heatmaps can reveal traffic bottlenecks in specific urban corridors during rush hours, or drivers with consistently longer service times at certain stop types (e.g., due to parking difficulties). Impact is quantified by comparing KPI deltas between filtered subsets (e.g., tours with vs. without early maintenance stops) and through contribution analysis (how much a bottleneck adds to overall tour delay or fuel proxy).

**3. Root Cause Analysis for Inefficiencies**

Process mining moves beyond "where" to "why" through attribute-enriched analysis. Potential root causes visible in the data include suboptimal static route planning (frequent sequence deviations or travel time overruns), inaccurate travel time estimates (systematic underestimation in certain zones), recurring traffic congestion patterns (clusters of low-speed events at specific times/locations), high variability in customer service times (long dwell times linked to parking or customer interaction), vehicle breakdowns during shifts (unscheduled stops correlated with high prior utilization or specific routes), driver behavior differences (performance variance across Driver IDs), and failed deliveries creating rework loops (extra travel and next-day re-deliveries).

**Specific analyses** include:
- **Variant analysis**: Cluster or compare "happy path" cases (high OTD, low duration) against problematic variants using sequence similarity. This reveals if certain route sequences or stop orders correlate with delays.
- **Decision mining/decision trees**: On branching points (e.g., after "Arrive Customer") to see which attributes (time of day, customer type, driver) lead to longer times or failures.
- **Correlation and statistical analysis**: Link GPS-derived traffic events to overall case duration; analyze dwell time distributions by location or time; use social network analysis on drivers to identify performance influencers.
- **Process cube slicing**: Multi-dimensional views (by driver × route area × weekday) to isolate effects, such as certain drivers handling failed deliveries better or specific vehicles showing higher maintenance rates after high-idle periods.

These techniques validate hypotheses directly from the integrated log without requiring extensive additional data collection initially.

**4. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies**

**Strategy 1: Dynamic Routing Adjustments Using Discovered Traffic Hotspots**  
Targets traffic delays and suboptimal static routes. Root cause: Inaccurate static travel time estimations and lack of real-time adaptation to recurring congestion (identified via frequent "Low Speed Detected" clusters in the process map). Process mining support: Performance analysis and location-filtered variants show specific corridors and times where actual travel time exceeds planned by >30%, plus common deviation paths. Integrate these insights into routing software to trigger rerouting or buffer times when approaching known hotspots. Expected impacts: Reduce travel time and idle percentage (lowering fuel costs), improve OTD rate by 10-20%, and decrease tour duration variability.

**Strategy 2: Pattern-Based Time Window Optimization and Customer Notification to Reduce Failed Deliveries**  
Targets high failed delivery rates and the resulting rework loops. Root cause: Poor alignment of scheduled windows with actual customer availability patterns and high service time variability. PM support: Variant analysis shows failed delivery cases have significantly longer overall durations due to loops; decision mining reveals strong correlations with specific times of day, customer zones, or stop numbers (later stops more prone to failure). Use insights to adjust default windows per zone/customer type and implement pre-delivery notifications or SMS confirmations for high-risk stops. Expected impacts: Lower failed delivery rate, reduce rework travel time, improve first-attempt success and OTD, with direct cost savings from fewer re-deliveries.

**Strategy 3: Predictive Maintenance Scheduling Based on Usage and Event Patterns**  
Targets unscheduled stops and associated downtime/repair costs. Root cause: Reactive rather than proactive maintenance, linked to high utilization patterns or preceding event sequences (e.g., frequent idling or specific speed profiles). PM support: Correlating maintenance events with prior GPS/maintenance log patterns, case performance metrics (vehicles with more unscheduled stops show higher overall tour delays), and resource analysis per vehicle. Build predictive models triggered by cumulative km, event sequences, or early warning signals identified in the log. Expected impacts: Decrease frequency and duration of unscheduled stops, improve vehicle utilization and availability, reduce maintenance costs and overtime, indirectly boosting OTD through more reliable fleet.

**5. Considering Operational Constraints and Monitoring**

All strategies must respect operational constraints. Driver working hours are enforced using "Start Shift" and "End Shift" timestamps (avoiding optimizations that push cases into overtime). Vehicle capacity constraints come from dispatch data (number of packages/stops). Customer time windows are treated as hard or soft constraints in any new routing or scheduling algorithms. Use constrained optimization or simulation (e.g., extending PM with what-if analysis in tools like Celonis or custom models) that only propose changes within feasible bounds derived from historical compliant cases. For dynamic routing, incorporate real-time vehicle status and remaining capacity.

For **continuous monitoring**, implement process mining dashboards (e.g., in Celonis, UiPath Process Mining, or PM4Py-based custom views) that track:
- Core KPIs (OTD rate, failed delivery rate, tour duration, idle time %) with trend lines and alerts for deviations beyond thresholds.
- Conformance metrics (fitness score over time) and automated highlighting of new or increasing deviations.
- Bottleneck heatmaps and variant explorer to detect emerging issues (e.g., new traffic patterns or driver-specific problems).
- Dimensional drill-downs (by route, driver, time period) and process map comparisons (before/after changes).

Set up weekly conformance and performance reports, with automated alerts for KPI drops or new high-frequency variants. This creates a feedback loop to refine strategies, ensure sustainability, and quickly identify unintended side effects (e.g., new bottlenecks created by territory changes).

This comprehensive process mining approach transforms Speedy Parcels' multi-source event data into actionable insights, directly linking operational inefficiencies to data-driven improvements in punctuality and cost.
