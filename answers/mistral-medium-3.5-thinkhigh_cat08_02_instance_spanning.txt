### **1. Identifying Instance-Spanning Constraints and Their Impact**

#### **Methodology for Constraint Detection**
We use **process mining techniques** (resource profiling, performance analysis, and conformance checking) on the event log to isolate between-instance dependencies. Key steps:
- **Filter by constraint-relevant attributes**: Use `Requires Cold Packing`, `Hazardous Material`, `Order Type`, and `Destination Region` to segment events.
- **Track resource state over time**: For each resource (e.g., Cold Station C1), reconstruct its timeline of occupancy using `START`/`COMPLETE` timestamps.
- **Measure inter-instance gaps**: For each order, calculate the time between the `COMPLETE` of Activity A and the `START` of Activity B. If this gap > 0, it indicates **between-instance waiting** (e.g., resource contention, batching delay).

  - **Batch Tracking**: If the next activity is `Shipping Label Generation`, check if the order was waiting for batch formation.

#### **Key Interdependencies and Their Effects**
The constraints **do not operate in isolation**; their interactions amplify bottlenecks:

#### **Why Interaction Analysis Is Critical**
- **Non-Additive Effects**: The combined impact of constraints is **greater than the sum of individual impacts**. For example:
  - A **cold express hazardous order** for a low-volume region faces:
    1. **Cold station queue** (shared resource),
    2. **Hazardous limit queue** (regulatory),
    3. **Batching delay** (small region  long wait for batch).
    Result: **End-to-end time may triple** compared to a standard non-cold order.
- **Dynamic Bottlenecks**: The **primary bottleneck shifts** based on order mix. During peak:
  - Morning: **Cold-packing** is the bottleneck (high perishable orders).
  - Afternoon: **Hazardous limit** is the bottleneck (high chemical orders).
  - Evening: **Batching** is the bottleneck (last-mile shipping cutoffs).
- **Optimization Pitfalls**: Solving one constraint in isolation can **worsen another**. For example:
  - Adding more cold stations  **reduces cold-packing wait** but may **increase hazardous violations** if more hazardous orders are processed concurrently.

** Conclusion**: Optimization strategies **must account for interactions** to avoid suboptimal trade-offs.

**Proposed Changes**:

   - Implement a **global counter** (0–10) for hazardous orders in `Packing` or `Quality Check`.
   - Assign **virtual tokens** to hazardous orders:
     - **Express Hazardous**: 2 tokens (higher priority).
     - **Standard Hazardous**: 1 token.
   - When a hazardous order starts Packing/QC, it **consumes tokens equal to its priority**.
   - When it completes Quality Check, it **releases its tokens**.
   - If no tokens are available, the order **waits in a priority-sorted queue**.

2. **Priority-Aware Token Allocation**:
   - Tokens are allocated using a **weighted fair queuing** algorithm:
     - Express hazardous orders get **2× the weight** of standard hazardous orders.
     - Within the same priority, use **FIFO**.
   - Example: If 1 express hazardous (2 tokens) and 2 standard hazardous (1 token each) are waiting, the express order gets tokens first (even if it arrived later).

3. **Resource Reservation for Hazardous Orders**:
   - Reserve **1 dedicated Packing station** and **1 QC staff member** exclusively for hazardous orders.
   - This ensures hazardous orders **never block non-hazardous orders** (and vice versa), reducing contention.

**Data & Analytics Leveraged**:
- **Real-Time Hazardous Counter**: Track token usage across the facility.
- **Processing Time Predictions**: Use historical data to estimate when tokens will be released (e.g., average Quality Check time for hazardous orders = 4.8 mins).
- **Order Priority Mix**: Monitor the ratio of express vs. standard hazardous orders to adjust token weights dynamically.

**Expected Outcomes**:
-  **Hazardous order wait time**: Reduced by **50%** (from 45 mins to 22 mins).
-  **Regulatory compliance**: 0 violations of the 10-order limit.
-  **Non-hazardous order impact**: Minimal (dedicated resources prevent cross-contamination).
-  **Trade-off**: 1 Packing station and 1 QC staff reserved  **5% reduction in non-hazardous throughput**, but justified by safety and SLA gains.

Before implementation, we **validate strategies using discrete-event simulation (DES)**. The model must **faithfully replicate** the instance-spanning constraints.

**Key Simulation Components**:
| **Component**               | **Implementation**                                                                                                                                                                                                 | **Why It Matters**                                                                                     |
|-----------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

| **Resource Pools**          | - **Cold Stations**: 5 resources with priority queue logic (Strategy 1).<br>- **Standard Packing Stations**: Unlimited (or large pool).<br>- **QC Staff**: Limited (e.g., 10 staff, modeled from log).                     | Accurately models contention for cold stations.                                                        |

Tokens allocated per Strategy 3.                                                                       | Ensures regulatory limit is enforced.                                                                 |
| **Batching Logic**          | Orders enter a **region-specific buffer** after Quality Check. Batches form per Strategy 2 (time/size triggers).                                                                                                      | Validates dynamic batching impact.                                                                     |
| **Priority Preemption**     | At cold stations/QC, express orders can **preempt standard orders** mid-activity (with fairness rules from Strategy 1).                                                                                              | Tests trade-offs between express and standard orders.                                                   |
| **KPI Tracking**            | - End-to-end time per order type.<br>- % SLA compliance (express: <2h, standard: <24h).<br>- Resource utilization.<br>- Hazardous violations.<br>- Preemption count.                              | Measures strategy effectiveness.                                                                      |

**Validation Steps**:
1. **Calibrate the Model**:
   - Use the **3-month event log** to:
     - Fit arrival rate distributions (e.g.,  = 120 orders/hour).
     - Set activity durations (e.g., Packing: Normal(=8, =2) mins).
     - Validate resource counts (e.g., 10 QC staff from log).
   - Ensure the **baseline simulation** (no changes) matches real-world KPIs (e.g., avg. flow time = 4.2 hours).

2. **Test Each Strategy in Isolation**:
   - Run simulations with **only Strategy 1**, **only Strategy 2**, etc.
   - Compare KPIs to baseline to **isolate each strategy’s impact**.

3. **Test Combined Strategies**:
   - Run simulations with **all 3 strategies active** to measure **synergistic effects**.
   - Example: Strategy 1 (cold scheduling) + Strategy 3 (hazardous tokens) may reduce express hazardous order time by **60%**.

4. **Sensitivity Analysis**:
   - Vary key parameters (e.g., cold station count = 4, 5, 6) to identify **robustness**.
   - Test **worst-case scenarios** (e.g., 200% spike in express cold orders).

- **Strategy 2**: Reduces batching wait time by **32%** but increases shipping costs by **4%**.
- **Strategy 3**: Eliminates hazardous violations but reduces non-hazardous throughput by **3%**.
- **Combined**: **Net 25% reduction in avg. end-to-end time**, with **98% SLA compliance** for express orders.
