### 1. Identifying Instance-Spanning Constraints and Their Impact

To optimize the fulfillment process, we must first quantify the bottlenecks caused by dependencies between orders. Standard process mining focuses on single-case flow; here, we must employ **instance-spanning analysis** techniques to detect interactions across cases.

**A. Methodology for Identification and Quantification:**

*   **Shared Cold-Packing Stations (Resource Contention):**
    *   *Technique:* Filter the event log for activities where `Requires Cold Packing = TRUE`. Reconstruct resource usage traces for the 5 Cold Stations. Perform **Resource Utilization Analysis** and **Waiting Time Decomposition**.
    *   *Metric:* Calculate `Cold_Station_Wait_Time = Start_Packing_Cold - Complete_Picking`. Compare this against `Standard_Wait_Time` for non-cold orders. Calculate **Resource Contention Index**: Ratio of waiting time for cold orders vs. standard orders.
    *   *Impact:* High contention indicates that the 5 stations are a bottleneck, causing queuing that delays perishable goods specifically.

*   **Shipping Batches (Batching Delays):**
    *   *Technique:* The log contains `Shipping Label Gen` with `System (Batch B1)`. We can group cases by `Batch ID`. If batch IDs are missing, we use **Batch Reconstruction** based on `Destination Region` and timestamp proximity at the `Shipping Label Gen` step. Analyze the gap between `Complete_QualityCheck` and `Complete_ShippingLabelGen`.
    *   *Metric:* **Batch Latency**: `Complete_ShippingLabelGen - Complete_QualityCheck`. Also, **Batch Formation Delay**: Time elapsed from the first order in a batch completing QC to the batch being triggered.
    *   *Impact:* High batch latency indicates orders are sitting idle waiting for other orders to arrive, directly inflating cycle times.

*   **Priority Order Handling (Preemption/Interruption):**
    *   *Technique:* Analyze resource usage traces for `Packing` and `QC`. Detect **Resource Occupancy Overlaps** or **Interruption Patterns**. If a Standard order starts, and an Express order starts on the same resource shortly after, and the Standard order's completion time extends beyond its expected duration (or shows a gap), this indicates preemption.
    *   *Metric:* **Priority Interference Time**: Duration a standard order is paused or delayed due to an express arrival. **Express Acceleration Gain**: Reduction in express cycle time due to preemption vs. standard queue.
    *   *Impact:* Quantifies the "cost" of priority handling. Excessive interference may degrade standard order SLAs and increase rework or staff stress.

*   **Regulatory Compliance (Hazardous Limits):**
    *   *Technique:* Perform **Conformance Checking** against the constraint rule: `Count(Active_Hazardous_in_Packing_or_QC) <= 10`. Reconstruct the state of the system over time. For every timestamp, count orders with `Hazardous = TRUE` that have `Start` but no `Complete` for Packing or QC.
    *   *Metric:* **Violation Frequency**: Number of times the limit is breached. **Hazardous Blockage Time**: Time orders spend waiting because the limit is reached. **Throughput Reduction**: Difference in flow rate when the limit is active vs. inactive.
    *   *Impact:* Identifies if the regulatory limit is causing systemic blocking or if it is only a rare edge case.

**B. Differentiating Within-Instance vs. Between-Instance Waiting:**

*   **Within-Instance Factors:** Measured by **Processing Time** (`Complete_Activity - Start_Activity`). If a Packing activity takes 20 minutes, that is within-instance variance due to complexity or worker speed.
*   **Between-Instance Factors:** Measured by **Waiting Time** (`Start_Activity - Complete_Previous_Activity`).
    *   *Attribution:* To attribute waiting to specific constraints, we use **Attribute-based Filtering**:
        *   *Resource Wait:* Waiting occurs, but the resource is occupied by another order (detected via resource trace overlap).
        *   *Batch Wait:* Waiting occurs, and the order is part of a batch where other orders are still in `Item Picking` or `Packing`.
        *   *Priority Wait:* Waiting occurs, and a high-priority order arrives at the resource during this period.
        *   *Constraint Wait:* Waiting occurs, and the Hazardous count is at the limit.

### 2. Analyzing Constraint Interactions

Constraints do not act in isolation; they compound delays. Understanding interactions is vital to avoid "local optima" that worsen global performance.

*   **Priority + Cold-Packing Contention:**
    *   *Interaction:* An Express order requiring cold packing creates a "double whammy." It competes for the scarce resource *and* demands immediate service. If a Standard order is using a Cold station, the Express order preempts it. This increases the completion time of the Standard order significantly.
    *   *Risk:* If preemption is frequent, Standard orders may miss their SLAs, leading to customer churn. The system may also suffer from "thrashing" if handovers are slow.

*   **Batching + Hazardous Limits:**
    *   *Interaction:* Batching groups orders by region. If a region has a high volume of Hazardous orders, a batch might form containing many hazardous items. However, the global limit of 10 applies to *all* regions.
    *   *Risk:* A batch might be "ready" for shipping label generation, but if the facility is near the hazardous limit, these orders cannot proceed to Packing/QC efficiently. This creates a **Deadlock-like scenario** where batching logic pushes orders forward, but safety constraints block them, causing massive backlogs in the batch buffer.

*   **Priority + Batching:**
    *   *Interaction:* Express orders may be stuck waiting for a batch to form if the batching logic is rigid.
    *   *Risk:* If the batching window is long to optimize route density, Express orders suffer unacceptable delays. The interaction suggests that Express orders should either bypass batching or use dynamic, shorter batching windows.

*   **Cold + Hazardous:**
    *   *Interaction:* Perishable goods might sometimes be hazardous (e.g., certain chemicals). If so, these orders face both resource scarcity (Cold stations) and regulatory limits.
    *   *Risk:* These "High-Risk" orders could be the primary drivers of delay. Analysis must check for correlation between `Requires Cold` and `Hazardous`.

    *   **Express Batch Bypass:** Express orders are exempt from regional batching. They trigger immediate label generation upon QC completion.
    *   **Hazardous Safety Gate:** Before a batch is released for Packing, the system checks the current Hazardous concurrency. If releasing the batch would push the count >10, the batch is split or delayed, and non-hazardous orders in the batch are allowed to proceed.

*   *Expected Outcome:* Reduced batch latency for 80% of orders; Express SLA compliance restored; Hazardous violations eliminated without stopping non-hazardous flow.

**Strategy 2: Predictive Cold-Packing Resource Allocation & Queue Management**
*   *Constraints Addressed:* Shared Cold-Packing, Priority Handling.
*   *Proposed Changes:*

*   *Expected Outcome:* Higher Cold station utilization; Reduced waiting time for Express orders; Smoother flow for Standard orders by reducing erratic preemptions.

**Strategy 3: Hazardous Flow Control via Staggered Release and Parallel QC**
*   *Constraints Addressed:* Hazardous Limits, Throughput.
*   *Proposed Changes:*
    *   **Staggered Release Mechanism:** Instead of blocking orders at the Packing step, introduce a "Hazardous Buffer" zone before Packing. Orders enter this buffer and are released into Packing/QC only when the global count drops below 9, ensuring a smooth stream rather than a hard stop.

    *   **Batching Decoupling for Hazardous:** Hazardous orders are excluded from regional batching and processed individually to ensure they can be managed against the global limit without batch interference.
*   *Data Leverage:* Analyze `Hazardous Blockage Time` to size the buffer. Analyze `QC Duration` variance to optimize sequencing.
*   *Expected Outcome:* Elimination of blocking due to hazardous limits; Increased throughput of hazardous goods; Compliance guaranteed by design.
