**1. Process Discovery and Conformance Checking**

To create a cohesive event log, integrate the four data sources by using **Vehicle-Day** (e.g., V12-20241205) as the primary case identifier, as it naturally represents a complete operational trace from shift start to end. Standardize timestamps to a common UTC format and synchronize them using vehicle GPS as the temporal backbone. Map raw events to standardized activities:

- Driver/Scanner events become high-level milestones (e.g., "Start Shift", "Arrive Customer", "Delivery Success", "Delivery Failed", "Depart Customer", "End Shift").
- GPS data is abstracted into derived activities (e.g., "Travel Segment", "Low-Speed/Idle Period", "Unscheduled Stop") using speed thresholds, location clustering, and ignition status.
- Dispatch data supplies attributes (planned sequence, time windows, package details) and reference activities (e.g., "Route Assigned").
- Maintenance logs are linked via timestamps and location to create "Unscheduled Maintenance" or "Repair" activities.

Preprocessing steps include: deduplication of near-simultaneous events, imputation of missing locations via interpolation, noise filtering (e.g., GPS jumps), and enrichment with derived attributes (e.g., dwell time = Depart – Arrive, distance between stops). Challenges include: temporal misalignment across systems (scanner events may lag GPS), incomplete traces (some GPS pings lack driver context), high-volume GPS data requiring aggregation to avoid explosion of micro-events, privacy/GDPR concerns with location data, and multi-granularity (continuous GPS vs. discrete scanner events). Tools like Python (pandas + pm4py) or commercial platforms (Celonis, UiPath Process Mining) can handle ETL.

For process discovery, apply algorithms suited to noisy, high-volume logistics data such as the **Inductive Miner** (for sound, block-structured models) or **Heuristics Miner** (tolerant of infrequent behavior). Discover the end-to-end model from "Start Shift" to "End Shift," treating the trace as a sequence of depot departure  travel segments  customer interactions (Arrive  Success/Failed  Depart)  potential deviations (Unscheduled Stop, Low Speed)  depot return. Visualize as a BPMN or Petri net showing parallel travel and service activities, loops for multiple stops, and deviation paths. Include performance annotations (e.g., average durations on arcs). Filter by attributes (driver, vehicle, day type) to reveal route-specific variants.

Conformance checking compares the discovered "as-is" model against the planned routes from the dispatch system (treated as a normative model or directly as a reference sequence per case). Use **alignments** (optimal deviation paths) or token replay to compute fitness (how much observed behavior fits the plan) and precision (how much the plan is followed). Key deviation types to quantify:
- Sequence deviations: Deliveries executed out of planned order or skipped stops.
- Timing deviations: Actual arrival outside customer windows or excessive dwell times.
- Unplanned activities: Unscheduled stops, maintenance events, or extra travel segments.
- Frequency of failed deliveries leading to re-routing loops.
- Overall: Low fitness on high-traffic days or specific drivers. This reveals systemic gaps between planning assumptions and reality (e.g., the snippet shows a failed delivery at Stop 5 and an unscheduled stop at 11:05, neither likely in the 35-stop plan).

**2. Performance Analysis and Bottleneck Identification**

Relevant KPIs, calculated directly from the enriched event log:

- **On-Time Delivery Rate**: Percentage of "Delivery Success" events where arrival timestamp falls within the dispatch time window. Filter by package or customer.
- **Average Time per Delivery Stop**: Mean (Depart Customer – Arrive Customer) across all successful/failed attempts.
- **Travel Time vs. Service Time Ratio**: Aggregate GPS-derived travel segments vs. total dwell time per case or route; also compute idle time (speed = 0, ignition on).
- **Fuel Consumption Proxy per km/package**: Correlate low-speed/idle durations and distance (from GPS) with known vehicle efficiency data; track "Unscheduled Stop" frequency.
- **Vehicle Utilization Rate**: (Productive time: travel + service) / (Shift duration: End Shift – Start Shift), excluding overtime.
- **Frequency/Duration of Traffic Delays**: Count and total duration of "Low Speed Detected" events below a threshold (e.g., <10 km/h outside stops), segmented by location clusters.
- **Rate of Failed Deliveries**: (Failed attempts / Total attempts) per route, driver, or time window; track re-delivery attempts.

Bottleneck identification uses process mining performance analysis: activity duration histograms, performance spectrum (time between consecutive activities), and waiting-time views. In tools, filter the log by time-of-day, route clusters (via location), driver ID, or vehicle type to isolate hotspots. For example, the snippet's 08:10 low-speed event and 11:05 unscheduled stop would surface as high waiting time between travel segments. Bottlenecks often appear as:
- Specific routes or time windows with elevated travel-to-service ratios (traffic or parking).
- High-variability dwell times at certain customer locations (customer interaction or access issues).
- Driver-specific patterns (e.g., longer stops for some drivers).
- Time-of-day effects (morning rush vs. midday).

Quantify impact via bottleneck cost (extra hours * labor/fuel rate) or throughput loss (delayed cases vs. planned). Multi-perspective mining (e.g., combining GPS speed with scanner events) reveals whether delays are in travel, service, or deviation handling.

**3. Root Cause Analysis for Inefficiencies**

Beyond surface bottlenecks, root causes are validated through targeted analyses:

- **Suboptimal route planning**: Variant analysis comparing high- vs. low-performing traces (e.g., on-time routes vs. those with overtime). High performers often show tighter sequencing; low performers reveal static plans ignoring historical congestion.
- **Inaccurate travel time estimations**: Correlation analysis (Pearson/Spearman) between planned vs. actual segment durations, enriched with external traffic data or GPS speed aggregates. Decision mining can build rules (e.g., "if location cluster X and hour Y, add 25% buffer").
- **Traffic congestion patterns**: Overlay low-speed events on maps derived from GPS; use process variant comparison to quantify delay contribution to overall cycle time.
- **High variability in service time**: Dwell-time analysis and box plots per customer cluster or stop type. High variance often indicates inconsistent customer access or driver practices.
- **Vehicle breakdowns/maintenance**: Link maintenance log events to preceding/following activities; measure added duration and frequency of "Unscheduled Stop" traces. Predictive correlation with mileage or prior low-speed patterns.
- **Driver behavior/skill differences**: Social network mining or driver-attribute filtering in variants. Compare models for top vs. bottom quartile drivers on the same routes.
- **Failed delivery impact**: Trace analysis of cases with "Delivery Failed"  count re-attempts or added travel. Correlate with time windows and customer attributes.

These analyses (variant comparison, correlation, decision trees on log attributes) move from "where" to "why," e.g., confirming that the snippet's failed delivery at Stop 5 and engine warning stop stem from poor sequencing and unpredicted maintenance rather than isolated incidents.

**4. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies**

**Strategy 1: Dynamic route adjustment with real-time traffic feedback loops.**  
Targets traffic delays and static planning gaps. Root cause: inaccurate estimations and lack of adaptation. Supported by discovered frequent delay paths and low-speed hotspots from GPS. Integrate PM insights into a routing engine that replans mid-shift when actual travel deviates >15% from discovered norms. Expected impacts: +10-20% on-time delivery, 8-12% fuel reduction via avoided congestion, lower overtime.

**Strategy 2: Historical performance-based territory and sequence optimization.**  
Targets inefficient stop ordering and high failed-delivery rates. Root cause: planning ignores actual dwell variability and driver/route performance. Use PM variant analysis to extract "best-practice" sequences (high on-time, low failed) and cluster customers by historical service times. Re-optimize territories quarterly using these as inputs to a vehicle routing problem solver. Expected impacts: 15-25% reduction in average stops per route, improved vehicle utilization, 20%+ drop in failed attempts.

**Strategy 3: Predictive maintenance scheduling combined with customer communication protocols for failed deliveries.**  
Targets unscheduled stops and re-delivery costs. Root cause: reactive maintenance and poor time-window/customer-availability alignment. Leverage maintenance log correlations and failed-delivery patterns to build predictive models (e.g., flag vehicles after X km + prior low-speed events). Pair with automated pre-delivery notifications triggered by historical failure clusters. Expected impacts: 30-50% fewer in-shift breakdowns, 15-25% higher first-attempt success, reduced maintenance downtime and fuel waste from idle stops.

**5. Considering Operational Constraints and Monitoring**

All strategies must incorporate hard constraints during optimization: maximum driver hours (enforce via shift-end timestamps), vehicle capacity (package counts from dispatch), and customer time windows (use as hard/soft constraints in routing models). For dynamic adjustments, use constrained solvers (e.g., OR-Tools or custom ILP) that respect remaining shift time and load. Failed-delivery re-attempts are queued only within legal hours.

For continuous monitoring, deploy process mining dashboards updated daily or in near-real-time from the integrated log:
- Core views: KPI trend lines (on-time rate, utilization, fuel proxy), conformance fitness over time, top deviation variants (with drill-down to specific cases/drivers), bottleneck heatmaps (time/location), and driver/route performance matrices.
- Alerts for emerging issues: automatic detection of fitness drops below threshold, new high-impact variants, or rising failed-delivery rates.
- Sustainability checks: A/B testing of optimizations (compare pre/post periods), periodic full re-discovery to validate model changes, and root-cause re-analysis every quarter. Track leading indicators (e.g., early deviation frequency) to prevent regression. This closed-loop approach ensures optimizations remain effective as traffic, customer behavior, or fleet composition evolves.
