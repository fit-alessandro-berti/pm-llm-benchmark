### 1. Case 1002: Breakdown of Internal Controls & Payment Risks

This case exhibits the most severe violations, effectively bypassing all major checks and balances in the P2P process.

**The Anomalies:**
*   **Purchase Order (PO) issued before Approval:** The "Issue Purchase Order" activity occurs on Feb 1, but the "Approve Purchase Requisition" does not occur until Feb 3.
*   **Payment released before goods are received:** "Release Payment" occurs on Feb 3, but "Receive Goods" does not happen until Feb 4.
*   **Payment released before Invoice Matching:** "Release Payment" occurs on Feb 3, but "Match Invoice to PO" occurs on Feb 5.
*   **Payment released before Purchase Requisition Approval:** The payment is released while the purchase requisition is still pending approval.

**Why this is problematic:**
*   **Bypass of Authorization:** Issuing a PO before the requisition is approved defeats the purpose of a segregation of duties. A buyer could create a requisition for themselves and immediately issue an order without a manager reviewing it.
*   **Financial Loss (Theft/Fraud):** Releasing payment *before* goods are received is extremely high-risk. If the vendor never ships the goods or ships defective goods, the company has already paid and will face significant difficulty recovering the funds.
*   **Uselessness of Verification:** Matching the invoice to the PO and Goods Receipt *after* payment is a purely administrative exercise with no financial control value. The "Three-Way Match" is designed to ensure a payment is only made when the documents agree; doing it retroactively leaves the company vulnerable to overpayments or double invoicing during the payment window.

### 2. Case 1003: Premature Payment

This case follows a sequential order but violates the financial principle of "payment upon verification."

**The Anomalies:**
*   **Payment released before goods received:** "Release Payment" occurs on Feb 8, while "Receive Goods" does not happen until Feb 10.
*   **Payment released before Invoice Received:** "Release Payment" occurs on Feb 8, but "Vendor Invoice Received" is not recorded until Feb 9.
*   **Matching occurs post-payment:** "Match Invoice to PO" happens on Feb 11, three days after the payment was released.

**Why this is problematic:**
*   **Lack of Leverage:** Once a payment is released, the company loses leverage over the vendor. If the goods arrive damaged or not at all, the company must chase a refund rather than simply withholding payment.
*   **Cash Flow Risk:** Paying without an invoice is unusual and can lead to accounting discrepancies. The payment amount might not match the final invoice amount, leading to under or overpayment.

### 3. Case 1004: Receipt Before Order

While less financially risky than Case 1002, this case violates the compliance and traceability standards of procurement.

**The Anomalies:**
*   **Goods Received before PO Issued:** "Receive Goods" occurs on Feb 6 at 09:00, but "Issue Purchase Order" occurs on Feb 6 at 09:15.

**Why this is problematic:**
*   **"Maverick" Buying:** Receiving goods without a Purchase Order suggests that an employee negotiated with a vendor and requested goods without following the formal procurement procedure. This is often called "maverick spend."
*   **System & Compliance Errors:** In most ERP systems, it is technically impossible to record a Goods Receipt without a valid Purchase Order number to reference. If this happened, it likely involved manual overrides or entering a "dummy" PO retroactively.
*   **Price and Quality Verification:** Issuing a PO *after* goods are received means the negotiation of price and terms happened after the commitment to buy. The company may have accepted goods at a higher price than standard or without agreed-upon terms.
