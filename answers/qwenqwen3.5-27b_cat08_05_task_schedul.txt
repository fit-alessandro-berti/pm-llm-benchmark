# Strategic Process Optimization Plan for Precision Parts Inc.

**To:** Senior Management, Precision Parts Inc.
**From:** Senior Operations Analyst
**Subject:** Data-Driven Scheduling Optimization via Process Mining and Advanced Analytics

## 1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics

To move from reactive dispatching to proactive optimization, we must first establish a digital twin of the "as-is" operations using the available MES event logs. Process mining will transform raw logs into actionable process models and performance metrics.

### **Reconstructing the Actual Flow**
Using **Control Flow Mining** algorithms (e.g., Inductive Miner or Heuristic Miner), we will reconstruct the actual routing of jobs. Unlike the theoretical BOM/routing, this reveals the *actual* path, including rework loops, skipped steps, or alternative machine usage.
*   **Log Parsing:** We will parse the `Timestamp`, `Case ID`, and `Activity` columns to build a case-centric event log.
*   **Resource Mapping:** We will map `Resource (Machine ID)` to activities to identify which machines handle which operations and their actual capacity usage.

### **Key Metrics and Techniques**

*   **Job Flow Times & Lead Times:**
    *   **Technique:** *Performance Mining.* Calculate the difference between the `Job Released` timestamp and the final `Task End` timestamp for each `Case ID`.
    *   **Metric:** *Cycle Time Distribution* (Mean, Median, 90th percentile). We will analyze the variance to understand lead time unpredictability.
    *   **Example:** For JOB-7001, the log shows a planned duration of 60 mins vs. actual 66.4 mins. Aggregating this across all jobs reveals the systemic "planning fallacy."

*   **Task Waiting Times (Queue Times):**
    *   **Technique:** *Inter-Task Latency Analysis.* Calculate the time gap between `Task End` (Operation $i$) and `Task Start` (Operation $i+1$).
    *   **Metric:** *Average Queue Time per Work Center.* High queue times at specific machines (e.g., MILL-03 in the snippet) indicate bottlenecks.
    *   **Differentiation:** Distinguish between *Queue Time* (waiting for machine) and *Wait Time* (waiting for material/operator).

*   **Resource Utilization:**
    *   **Technique:** *Resource Utilization Mining.*
    *   **Calculation:** $\frac{\text{Sum(Task Duration + Setup Duration)}}{\text{Total Shift Time}}$.
    *   **Granularity:** Break down utilization into **Productive Time**, **Setup Time**, and **Idle Time**.
    *   **Insight:** If `CUT-01` shows 90% utilization but 40% of that is Setup Time, the machine is busy but inefficient.

*   **Sequence-Dependent Setup Times:**
    *   **Technique:** *Transition Mining.* We will analyze the `Setup Required` flag and the `Notes` column (e.g., "Previous job: JOB-6998").
    *   **Matrix Construction:** Build a **Setup Time Matrix** where rows are `Previous Job Type` and columns are `Current Job Type`.
    *   **Metric:** Average setup time for transition $A \rightarrow B$ vs. $A \rightarrow C$. This quantifies the penalty of poor sequencing.

*   **Schedule Adherence and Tardiness:**
    *   **Technique:** *Conformance Checking.* Compare `Order Due Date` against `Task End` timestamp.
    *   **Metric:** *Tardiness Rate* (% of jobs late) and *Average Tardiness* (days late).
    *   **Slack Analysis:** Calculate `Slack = Due Date - Current Time - Remaining Processing Time`. Negative slack indicates a critical delay.

*   **Impact of Disruptions:**
    *   **Technique:** *Root Cause Correlation.* Link `Breakdown Start` events to downstream `Task Start` delays.
    *   **Metric:** *Recovery Time.* How long does it take for the schedule to return to normal after a disruption?
    *   **Priority Impact:** Analyze `Priority Change` events (e.g., JOB-7005) to measure the "displacement cost" (how many other jobs were delayed to accommodate the hot job).

## 2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies

Based on the metrics derived above, we will diagnose specific pathologies inherent in the current local dispatching rules.

### **Identified Pathologies & Evidence**

1.  **Bottleneck Starvation and Saturation:**
    *   **Diagnosis:** A specific machine (e.g., a CNC Mill) operates at >95% utilization while upstream machines sit idle due to lack of buffer space, or downstream machines starve because the bottleneck is blocked.
    *   **Process Mining Evidence:** *Queue Depth Heatmap.* Visualizing queue length over time will show persistent queues at the bottleneck. *Resource Contention Analysis* will show frequent "Waiting for resource" events (like JOB-7001 at MILL-03) specifically at this node.

2.  **Setup Time Inflation (The "Setup Trap"):**
    *   **Diagnosis:** The current FCFS/EDD rules ignore sequence dependencies. Processing a dissimilar job after a complex one incurs high setup costs, inflating lead times without adding value.
    *   **Process Mining Evidence:** *Transition Frequency vs. Setup Cost.* We will likely find that high-frequency transitions between dissimilar job families (e.g., Heat Treatment $\rightarrow$ Cutting) correlate with higher-than-average setup durations (like the 23.5 min actual vs. 20 min planned for JOB-7001).

3.  **Priority Paradox:**
    *   **Diagnosis:** High-priority "Hot Jobs" are inserted into the queue without checking downstream availability, causing them to wait at non-bottleneck stations while the bottleneck remains busy with low-priority work.
    *   **Process Mining Evidence:** *Variant Analysis.* Compare the process paths of "High Priority" jobs vs. "Medium Priority" jobs. If High Priority jobs show longer *Total Wait Times* than Medium Priority jobs, the system is prioritizing local rules over global due dates.

4.  **WIP Bullwhip Effect:**
    *   **Diagnosis:** Variability in upstream processing times (e.g., actual vs. planned) is amplified downstream, causing erratic inventory levels.
    *   **Process Mining Evidence:** *Throughput Variance Analysis.* Plot WIP inventory levels at each stage over time. High variance in WIP between stations indicates poor synchronization and lack of buffering strategies.

5.  **Reactive Disruption Handling:**
    *   **Diagnosis:** Breakdowns trigger ad-hoc rescheduling rather than optimized re-sequencing, leading to cascading delays.
    *   **Process Mining Evidence:** *Causal Link Analysis.* Correlate `Breakdown Start` events with a spike in `Tardiness` events in the following 24-48 hours. If the correlation is strong and recovery is slow, the resilience strategy is weak.

## 3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness

Process mining allows us to distinguish between *process logic failures* and *resource capacity constraints*.

1.  **Limitations of Static Dispatching Rules:**
    *   **Root Cause:** Local rules (FCFS, EDD) optimize for the current machine, not the shop floor. They treat all jobs as identical regarding setup and downstream load.
    *   **Mining Differentiation:** If utilization is low but tardiness is high, the root cause is **Logic**. If utilization is 100% and tardiness is high, the root cause is **Capacity**.

2.  **Lack of Real-Time Visibility:**
    *   **Root Cause:** Operators schedule based on what is physically in front of them, unaware of a bottleneck 5 stations down that is already backed up.
    *   **Mining Differentiation:** *Flow Deviation Analysis.* If jobs frequently wait at Station B while Station C (downstream) is idle, it indicates a lack of information flow or synchronization.

3.  **Inaccurate Estimations:**
    *   **Root Cause:** The log shows `Task Duration (Planned)` vs. `Task Duration (Actual)` discrepancies (e.g., 60 vs. 66.4 mins). Schedules built on optimistic averages fail when variance occurs.
    *   **Mining Differentiation:** *Variance Analysis.* Calculate the coefficient of variation ($CV = \sigma / \mu$) for task times. High $CV$ indicates that the planning engine is unreliable, necessitating stochastic rather than deterministic scheduling.

4.  **Ineffective Handling of Sequence-Dependent Setups:**
    *   **Root Cause:** Setups are treated as fixed constants rather than dynamic variables dependent on the previous job.
    *   **Mining Differentiation:** *Setup Time Distribution.* If the standard deviation of setup times is high, it proves that sequencing matters. If the scheduler ignores this, it is a logic flaw.

5.  **Poor Coordination & Disruption Response:**
    *   **Root Cause:** No global re-optimization trigger when a breakdown occurs.
    *   **Mining Differentiation:** *Recovery Time Analysis.* If the system takes 4 hours to recover from a 30-minute breakdown, the rescheduling logic is the bottleneck, not the machine repair.

## 4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies

We propose three distinct strategies that leverage the mined data to transition from static dispatching to dynamic, intelligent scheduling.

### **Strategy 1: Adaptive Priority Scoring with Setup Awareness (APSSA)**
*   **Core Logic:** A dynamic dispatching rule that calculates a "Score" for every job in the queue at a specific machine. The job with the highest score is selected.
*   **Formula Concept:** $Score = w_1(Due\ Date\ Slack) + w_2(Priority\ Weight) + w_3(Setup\ Penalty) + w_4(Downstream\ Load)$.
*   **Process Mining Input:**
    *   **Setup Penalty:** Derived from the *Setup Time Matrix*. If Job A is next, the setup cost for Job B is retrieved from historical data.
    *   **Downstream Load:** Derived from *Real-time Queue Depth Mining*. If the next machine is congested, the score is lowered to prevent WIP pile-up.
*   **Addressing Pathologies:** Directly attacks **Setup Inflation** and **Bottleneck Saturation**. It prevents high-priority jobs from clogging non-bottlenecks if the downstream path is blocked.
*   **Expected Impact:** 15-20% reduction in total setup time; 10% improvement in On-Time Delivery (OTD).

### **Strategy 2: Predictive Constraint-Based Scheduling (PCBS)**
*   **Core Logic:** Instead of just dispatching, we generate a finite horizon schedule (e.g., next 24 hours) using Machine Learning predictions for task durations.
*   **Mechanism:**
    *   **ML Model:** Train a regression model (e.g., Random Forest) using features like `Job Complexity`, `Operator ID`, and `Machine ID` to predict `Actual Duration`.
    *   **Constraint:** The scheduler respects the predicted duration and known maintenance windows (derived from `Breakdown Start` logs).
*   **Process Mining Input:**
    *   **Duration Distributions:** Use the `Task Duration (Actual)` log to build probabilistic profiles for each task type.
    *   **Breakdown Probability:** Use `Breakdown Start` logs to predict machine availability probabilities.
*   **Addressing Pathologies:** Mitigates **Unpredictable Lead Times** and **Schedule Adherence** issues by accounting for variance and maintenance risks proactively.
*   **Expected Impact:** 25% improvement in Lead Time accuracy; reduced WIP due to better synchronization.

### **Strategy 3: Bottleneck-Centric Sequencing Optimization (BCSO)**
*   **Core Logic:** Apply "Theory of Constraints" principles. Only the bottleneck machines use complex optimization; non-bottlenecks use simpler rules to feed the bottleneck.
*   **Mechanism:**
    *   **Identification:** Use mining to identify the top 20% of machines causing 80% of delays.
    *   **Optimization:** On these machines, solve a Traveling Salesperson Problem (TSP) variant to minimize total setup time for the current queue.
    *   **Batching:** Group jobs with similar attributes (e.g., same material thickness) to minimize setups.
*   **Process Mining Input:**
    *   **Bottleneck Identification:** Derived from *Queue Depth* and *Utilization* metrics.
    *   **Similarity Clustering:** Cluster jobs based on setup requirements to create optimal batches.
*   **Addressing Pathologies:** Maximizes **Resource Utilization** at critical points and reduces **WIP** by ensuring the bottleneck is never starved.
*   **Expected Impact:** 10-15% increase in overall throughput; significant reduction in machine changeover costs.
