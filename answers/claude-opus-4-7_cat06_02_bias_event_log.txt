# Bias Analysis of the Loan Approval Process

## Where Bias Manifests

The bias in this process is concentrated at the **PreliminaryScoring** stage, where a **+10 "Community" adjustment** is applied to applicants affiliated with the **Highland Civic Darts Club**. This adjustment then propagates through ManualReview and FinalDecision, materially changing outcomes.

### Key Evidence from the Log

| Case | Resident | Community | Raw Score | Adjusted | Decision |
|------|----------|-----------|-----------|----------|----------|
| C001 | TRUE | Darts Club | 710 | **720** | Approved |
| C002 | TRUE | None | 720 | 720 | Approved |
| C003 | FALSE | None | 715 | 715 | **Rejected** |
| C004 | TRUE | Darts Club | 690 | **700** | Approved |
| C005 | FALSE | None | 740 | 740 | Approved |

## How the Bias Operates

### 1. Community Affiliation Boost (Direct Bias)
- **C004** had a raw score of **690** — the lowest in the dataset — yet was **Approved** after receiving the +10 Darts Club bonus (final 700).
- **C003**, a non-resident with a higher raw score of **715**, was **Rejected**.
- A non-member with a 690 score would almost certainly have been rejected, meaning **club membership functionally substituted for creditworthiness**.

### 2. Geographic/Residency Correlation (Indirect/Proxy Bias)
- Every Darts Club member in the log is also a **LocalResident = TRUE**.
- The "community" boost therefore doubles as a **locality premium**, disadvantaging non-local applicants who, by definition, cannot join a local civic group.
- This is a classic **proxy discrimination** pattern: a seemingly neutral attribute (club membership) encodes a protected or sensitive one (geography/origin).

### 3. Inconsistent Treatment at Similar Risk Levels
- C001 (710  720, Approved) and C003 (715, Rejected) had **essentially identical underlying creditworthiness**. The deciding factor was affiliation, not risk.
- This violates the fairness principle of **"similarly situated applicants should be treated similarly."**

## Fairness and Equity Implications

1. **Arbitrary advantage**: Membership in a specific private social club (darts) has no demonstrable causal link to credit risk. Using it as a scoring factor is arbitrary and lacks actuarial justification.

2. **Exclusionary by design**: People who are new to the area, belong to different cultural/social groups, work non-traditional hours, or simply don't play darts are structurally excluded from the adjustment — regardless of their financial behavior.

3. **Potential disparate impact**: If Darts Club membership correlates with demographic attributes (age, ethnicity, socioeconomic background, gender — darts clubs historically skew in particular directions), the process could produce **disparate impact** on protected classes, raising legal concerns under fair lending regulations (e.g., ECOA, Fair Housing Act, or equivalents).

4. **Manual Review does not correct it**: Underwriters receive the *already-adjusted* score (720 instead of 710), anchoring their judgment to an inflated baseline. The bias is laundered through a human checkpoint without being flagged.

5. **Opacity to applicants**: Rejected applicants like C003 would have no visibility into the fact that a +10 community adjustment — not their own financial profile — was the decisive factor.

## Recommendations

- **Remove the community adjustment** unless it can be statistically validated as a genuine, non-proxy predictor of repayment, and even then only if it does not produce disparate impact.
- **Audit the correlation** between Darts Club membership and protected demographic attributes.
- **Present unadjusted scores to underwriters** to prevent anchoring.
- **Log and disclose** any non-financial adjustments so they can be reviewed and challenged.
- **Test counterfactual outcomes**: would each decision be the same if community affiliation were stripped? For C004, the answer is clearly no.

### Bottom Line
The process rewards **social belonging and geographic rootedness** rather than creditworthiness. Applicants like C003 — who may be equally or more creditworthy — are systematically disadvantaged for characteristics unrelated to their ability to repay. This is a textbook case of **process-embedded bias**, where fairness is undermined not by a single bad actor but by a structural rule applied uniformly.
