## 2. Attribute-based root cause analysis

### A. Complexity is the strongest indicator of longer lead time

There is a clear relationship between claim complexity, document requests, and total duration.

| Complexity | Cases | Avg. Duration | Pattern |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Low | 2001, 2004 | ~1h 28m | No document requests, fast completion |
| Medium | 2002 | 25h 55m | One document request, crosses into next day |
| High | 2003, 2005 | ~62h 43m | Multiple document requests, multi-day completion |

High-complexity claims require repeated **Request Additional Documents** activities, which appear to be the main driver of delay.

For example:

- **Case 2003**, High complexity, has two document requests and takes **48h 20m**.
- **Case 2005**, High complexity, has three document requests and takes **77h 05m**.

The number of document requests correlates strongly with duration:

| # Document Requests | Cases | Duration Pattern |
|---:|---|---|
| 0 | 2001, 2004 | ~1.5 hours |
| 1 | 2002 | ~26 hours |
| 2 | 2003 | ~48 hours |
| 3 | 2005 | ~77 hours |

### Likely root cause

High-complexity claims are probably being submitted with incomplete or insufficient supporting evidence, causing repeated back-and-forth between the insurer and claimant. Each additional document cycle adds significant waiting time.

---

### B. Region B appears to have worse performance for non-low-complexity cases

Region comparison:

| Region | Cases | Avg. Duration | Observation |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| A | 2001, 2003 | ~24h 55m | Low case fast, high case delayed |
| B | 2002, 2004, 2005 | ~34h 48m | Contains the longest case, 2005 |

Region alone is not the only root cause, because **Case 2004**, a low-complexity Region B case, is completed quickly in **1h 25m**.

However, Region B seems to experience worse delays when complexity increases:

- Region A high-complexity case 2003: **48h 20m**
- Region B high-complexity case 2005: **77h 05m**

So the issue may be specifically related to **high- or medium-complexity claims in Region B**.

### Possible explanations

Region B may have:

- Stricter documentation requirements.
- More manual checks.
- Slower customer or third-party response times.
- Higher workload or staffing constraints.
- Less efficient handling of complex claims.
- Regional compliance rules that require additional verification.

### C. Resource patterns suggest possible bottlenecks, but they are partly confounded with complexity

#### Adjusters

| Adjuster | Cases | Observation |
|---|---|---|
| Adjuster_Mike | 2001, 2003 | Fast on low-complexity case, delayed on high-complexity case |
| Adjuster_Lisa | 2002, 2004, 2005 | Fast on low-complexity case, delayed on medium/high cases |

The data does not prove that either adjuster is individually causing delays. Both adjusters handle fast cases when the claim is low-complexity.

However, **Adjuster_Lisa handles Region B cases**, including the most delayed case, **2005**. This may indicate either:

- A Region B issue,
- A high-complexity handling issue,
- A workload issue for Adjuster_Lisa,
- Or a combination of these.

#### Managers

| Manager | Cases | Observation |
|---|---|---|
| Manager_Ann | 2001, 2002, 2004 | Mostly low/medium cases; low cases approved quickly |
| Manager_Bill | 2003, 2005 | Handles high-complexity cases; both are long-running |

Manager_Bill is associated with the two high-complexity cases, both of which are delayed. This may not mean Manager_Bill is the root cause; he may simply be assigned the more difficult claims.

Still, there are notable approval delays after the final document request:

- Case 2003: final document request to approval = **23h**
- Case 2005: final document request to approval = **19h**

This suggests that the **high-complexity approval queue** may be a bottleneck.

#### Finance resources

Payment is generally not the largest cause of delay, but there are some delays after approval:

- Case 2003: Approve to Pay = **17h**
- Case 2005: Approve to Pay = **4h**

For high-complexity claims, payment may require additional checks, larger payout authorization, or may be affected by batch/payment cutoff times.

## 3. Main root causes

### Root Cause 1: High-complexity claims require repeated document collection

The strongest pattern is:

> Higher complexity  more document requests  longer case duration.

Cases 2003 and 2005 are delayed mainly because they go through multiple rounds of document requests before approval.

### Root Cause 2: Region B may amplify delays for complex claims

Region B’s low-complexity case is fast, so Region B is not universally slow. But Region B’s high-complexity case, 2005, is the longest case in the log.

This suggests Region B may have problems specifically with complex claims, such as heavier documentation requirements, staffing constraints, or slower document turnaround.

### Root Cause 3: High-complexity approval may be a bottleneck

Both high-complexity cases are approved by Manager_Bill and have long waits before approval. This may indicate that complex claims wait for specialized managerial review.

### Root Cause 4: Document request process may be inefficient

The repeated document request pattern suggests that the insurer may not be asking for all required documents at once. Instead, documents are requested in multiple rounds, which extends the process over several days.

## 4. Recommendations to mitigate the issues

### A. Improve upfront document validation

Introduce a more rigorous document checklist at claim submission, especially for medium- and high-complexity claims.

Possible actions:

- Require mandatory documents before allowing claim submission.
- Use a digital claim portal with automated completeness checks.
- Provide region- and claim-type-specific documentation checklists.
- Use guided forms that tell customers exactly what is missing.

This would reduce the number of downstream document requests.

---

### B. Consolidate document requests

Instead of requesting additional documents multiple times, adjusters should perform a complete missing-document review and send one consolidated request.

Suggested control:

- Before sending a document request, require adjusters to complete a checklist.
- Add peer review for high-complexity document requests.
- Track the number of document-request cycles per claim as a KPI.

Target metric:

> Reduce high-complexity claims from 2–3 document request cycles to 1 cycle where possible.

---

### C. Create a dedicated high-complexity claims path

High-complexity cases should not follow the same operational model as simple claims.

Possible actions:

- Assign high-complexity claims to a specialized team.
- Introduce early triage after submission.
- Assign a case owner responsible for coordinating documents, approval, and payment.
- Set stricter internal SLAs for high-complexity cases.

---

### D. Investigate Region B handling of complex claims

Because Region B contains the most delayed case, the insurer should examine whether Region B has special issues.

Questions to investigate:

- Does Region B have more regulatory or documentation requirements?
- Is Adjuster_Lisa overloaded?
- Are customers in Region B slower to respond?
- Are there local process differences compared with Region A?
- Are document requests in Region B less complete or less clear?

If Region A has better practices for high-complexity claims, those practices could be standardized across Region B.

---

### E. Reduce approval bottlenecks for high-complexity cases

Since Manager_Bill handles the high-complexity cases, the company should check whether the high-complexity approval queue is overloaded.

Possible mitigations:

- Add backup approvers for high-complexity claims.
- Define approval SLAs after final document receipt.
- Use automated risk scoring to pre-prioritize approvals.
- Allow preliminary review before all documents are finalized.

---

### F. Monitor payment delays for approved high-complexity claims

Payment is not the primary bottleneck, but high-complexity cases show longer payment times than simple cases.

Possible actions:

- Pre-validate payment details before approval.
- Trigger finance preparation once approval is likely.
- Review payment cutoff times and batch-processing rules.
- Automate payment authorization for approved claims below certain risk thresholds.

---

## Conclusion

The main performance issue is not the basic claim-handling flow itself. Low-complexity claims in both regions are completed quickly. The delays arise primarily when claims are **medium or high complexity**, especially when they require **one or more additional document requests**.

The most severe case is **2005**, a high-complexity Region B claim handled through three document request cycles, taking **77h 05m**. The likely root causes are repeated documentation loops, possible Region B-specific handling delays, and bottlenecks in high-complexity approval.
