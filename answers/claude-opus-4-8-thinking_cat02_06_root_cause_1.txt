# Customer Support Ticket Resolution — Performance Analysis

| Case ID | Start | End | Total Cycle Time | Escalated? |
|---------|-------|-----|------------------|------------|
| 101 | 03-01 08:00 | 03-01 10:15 | **2h 15m** | No |

**Outliers:** Cases **102, 104, and 105** take dramatically longer than the fast baseline cases (101 and 103, both resolved within a few hours the same day). Case **105** is by far the worst (~49 hours, more than 20× the fastest case).

---

## 2. Root-Cause Analysis

Breaking each slow case down into its inter-activity gaps reveals the bottlenecks:

### Case 105 (worst — 49h)
- Investigate  **Escalate to Level-2** at 10:00
- Escalate  **second Investigate** at next day **14:00**  **~28-hour wait** for Level-2 to pick up the ticket.
- Investigate  Resolve  another **~19-hour gap** spanning overnight.
- Note: the issue is investigated **twice** (rework loop) — Level-1 work was essentially discarded after escalation.

### Case 102 (~25h)
- Assign  **Escalate to Level-2** (11:30): escalation occurred ~2.5h after assignment.
- Escalate  Investigate: 2.5h wait.
- Investigate (14:00)  Resolve (next day 09:00): **~19-hour overnight gap** — Level-2 investigation stalled across a non-working period.

### Case 104 (~24h — *no escalation*)
- Receive (08:20)  Triage (09:00): 40-min triage delay (longer than fast cases’ ~5–10 min).
- Investigate (13:00)  Resolve (next day 08:00): **~19-hour gap** even without escalation.
- This shows delays are **not solely caused by escalation** — long waits before/within investigation occur in standard cases too.

### Fast cases (101, 103)
- Tight, consistent gaps (5–60 min) between every step; no escalation; no overnight handoffs.

---

## Key Patterns Identified

1. **Escalation to Level-2 adds large queue/handoff delays.** In both escalated cases (102, 105) the longest waits occur *after* escalation, suggesting Level-2 is a constrained/queued resource.
2. **Overnight / off-hours gaps dominate the cycle time.** The ~19–28h delays consistently occur when a ticket sits idle between activities across a day boundary — indicating limited after-hours staffing and tickets parked in queues overnight.
3. **Rework loop in Case 105** (Investigate performed twice) — work done before escalation is repeated, wasting effort and time.
4. **Investigation wait is a shared bottleneck**, present even in the non-escalated slow case (104), pointing to limited investigation capacity, not just escalation.

---

## 3. Impact & Recommendations

**How these factors increase cycle time:**
- Escalation handoffs introduce **idle queue time** while waiting for a scarce Level-2 team.
- Tickets crossing day boundaries lose ~16+ hours of clock time per overnight gap.
- Repeated investigation (rework) duplicates effort and resets progress.

**Recommendations:**
- **Set SLAs/alerts on handoff waits**, especially the Escalate  Investigate transition, to prevent tickets idling for a day in the Level-2 queue.
- **Increase Level-2 capacity or add a triage rule** so tickets likely to need Level-2 are routed there early (avoiding redundant Level-1 investigation as in Case 105).
- **Add off-hours coverage or smarter scheduling** to reduce overnight idle gaps, which are the single largest contributor to delays.
- **Improve initial triage accuracy** — Case 105’s escalation after L1 investigation suggests mis-routing. Better upfront classification would cut the rework loop.
- **Measure and monitor inter-activity waiting time (not just total time)** to continuously surface these bottlenecks.

**Bottom line:** The delays in Cases 102, 104, and 105 are driven primarily by **escalation handoff queues and overnight idle waits before investigation/resolution**, compounded by **rework** in the escalated cases. Addressing Level-2 capacity, off-hours coverage, and early routing would yield the biggest reduction in cycle time.
