### Explanation of Transformation Logic

**1. Case Identification (Case ID)**
Cases are defined by the **work object** (document, spreadsheet, PDF, or email thread) rather than by application or time window. This follows a document-centric process mining paradigm, which is natural for knowledge-work logs. Each distinct file or email subject becomes its own process instance:
- `DOC001_Document1` and `DOC002_QuarterlyReport` represent two separate Word documents.
- `EMAIL001_AnnualMeeting` represents the lifecycle of handling one specific email.
- `PDF001_ReportDraft` and `XLS001_Budget2024` represent review and data-update tasks, respectively.

Events are grouped by the `Window` attribute (e.g., *Document1.docx*, *Email - Inbox* with the specific email action). This allows the discovered process model for each case to show a realistic object lifecycle: *Open  Edit  (potentially interrupt)  Resume  Save  Close*.

**2. Activity Naming**
Low-level system actions are translated into **semantic, high-level process activities**:
- **TYPING** is abstracted to `Edit Content` for documents and spreadsheets, and to `Compose Reply` for email, reflecting the user's intent rather than the keystroke mechanics.
- **FOCUS / SWITCH-TO** events are mapped to `Open Document/Spreadsheet` on first encounter and `Resume Document/Spreadsheet` when the user returns after an interruption. This makes multitasking and context-switching explicit in the process model.
- **SAVE / CLOSE** retain their obvious business meanings.
- **SCROLL** is elevated to `Read Email` or `Review Document`, representing the cognitive action of consuming information.
- **HIGHLIGHT** becomes `Annotate Document`.
- **CLICK** actions with explicit intents (e.g., *Reply*, *Send*, *Open Email*) are mapped directly to those business activities.

**3. Handling of Interleaved Cases**
The user frequently switches between cases (e.g., leaving Document1 to check email, then a PDF, then Excel, before returning). Rather than forcing a single linear session case, the log preserves these as **parallel, interleaved process instances**. The `SWITCH` events are assigned to the *target* case as opening/resuming activities, and the source case simply exhibits a natural time gap until the user returns. This design is analyst-friendly because it allows process mining algorithms to discover both the intra-case flow (e.g., how a document is edited) and the cross-case dependencies (e.g., Document1 cannot be finalized until the Budget spreadsheet is updated).
