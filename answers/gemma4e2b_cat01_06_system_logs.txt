## Explanation of Logic and Grouping

The transformation involved grouping sequential low-level actions into higher-level business activities (cases) based on the context, application usage, and the logical flow of the user's intent.

### 1. Case Identification Logic:

Three distinct process instances (cases) were identified based on the specific documents and tasks the user was engaged in:

*   **Case 1: Document 1 Processing:** This case groups all activities related to working with `Document1.docx` and the related communication tasks (switching to email and viewing the PDF report). This reflects a single, multi-application task session.
*   **Case 2: Budget Calculation:** This case is isolated because it strictly involves interaction with `Budget_2024.xlsx` (Excel), focusing solely on data input and saving, representing a focused calculation task.
*   **Case 3: Quarterly Report Drafting:** This case captures the activities related to working on the final output document, `Quarterly_Report.docx`, including drafting and final saving.

### 2. Activity Naming Logic:

Raw actions (like `TYPING`, `FOCUS`, `CLICK`) were translated into descriptive, business-oriented activities:

*   **Focus/Typing/Save:** These were grouped into actions like "Drafting" and "Saving" to indicate the overall goal of the interaction, rather than just the mechanical act of typing.
*   **Switching/Clicking:** These were contextualized as "Email Communication" or "Reviewing PDF," reflecting the *purpose* of the application switch, not just the technical command.
*   **Coherence:** The order of events within each case (e.g., drafting followed by saving, or data input followed by saving) maintains the chronological and logical flow of the user's workflow, making the resulting log coherent for process analysis.
