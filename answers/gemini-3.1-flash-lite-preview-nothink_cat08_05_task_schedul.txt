This analysis provides a blueprint for transforming Precision Parts Inc. from a reactive job shop to a data-driven, proactive manufacturing environment using Process Mining (PM) and Advanced Planning and Scheduling (APS) logic.

---

### 1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance
I will ingest the MES logs into a process mining tool (e.g., Celonis, ProM, or Python-based `pm4py`) to create a digital twin of the shop floor.

*   **Reconstruction:** I will map `Case ID` to the process flow and `Resource` to the machine. By creating a transition matrix, I can visualize the actual versus planned process paths.
*   **Quantifying KPIs:**
    *   **Flow/Lead Time & Makespan:** Using the delta between `Job Released` and `Task End (Final)`, I will calculate probability density functions (PDFs) of lead times.
    *   **Queue Times:** I will calculate the delta between `Queue Entry` and `Setup Start` to pinpoint where jobs "wither" on the floor.
    *   **Resource Utilization:** By aggregating `Task Start` to `Task End` duration versus total available calendar time, I will segment time into: *Productive*, *Idle*, *Setup*, and *Breakdown*.
    *   **Sequence-Dependent Setup Times (SDST):** I will create a correlation matrix of `Task Duration (Setup)` grouped by `(Prev_Job_Type, Current_Job_Type)`. This quantifies the "changeover penalty" for specific sequences.
    *   **Tardiness:** By subtracting `Task End` from `Order Due Date`, I will generate a tardiness distribution to identify chronic vs. acute delays.

### 2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies
*   **Bottleneck Identification:** I will use the **"Time-in-State" analysis**. If `MILL-03` has a high frequency of "Waiting for Resource" events upstream and a high "Idle" count downstream, it is a classic bottleneck.
*   **Variant Analysis:** I will perform a **conformance check** comparing "On-time" vs. "Late" job paths. Often, late jobs share a common "pathology": they get stuck behind "Hot Jobs," or they pass through a bottleneck during a known period of high contention.
*   **WIP Bullwhip:** By analyzing the arrival rate vs. the departure rate at each work center, I can identify nodes where scheduling variability causes inventory "bloat," creating a feedback loop that leads to longer lead times.

### 3. Root Cause Analysis
*   **Static vs. Dynamic:** I will use **Log Replay** to test whether the current FCFS/EDD rules were actually applied. I expect to see that local, greedy decisions (e.g., taking the easiest job next) cause massive global tardiness.
*   **Capacity vs. Logic:** If throughput is high but tardiness remains, the issue is **scheduling logic** (poor sequencing). If machines have high utilization but queues are consistently large, it is a **capacity limitation** requiring investment or process re-engineering.
*   **Disruptions:** I will perform a **correlation analysis** between "Breakdown Events" and "Tardiness Spikes" to see if the system recovers efficiently or if one breakdown creates a "ripple effect" (starvation) throughout the shop.

### 4. Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies
I propose migrating from local dispatching to a **Global-Dynamic Scheduling Framework**:

*   **Strategy 1: Multi-Factor Heuristic Dispatching:**
    *   *Logic:* Instead of FCFS, replace it with a **Weighted Shortest Processing Time + Apparent Tardiness Cost (WSPT+ATC)** rule.
    *   *PM Insight:* Use the historical setup time matrix as a weight in the dispatching decision. If two jobs are both due soon, the one requiring the shortest setup on the current machine configuration is prioritized.
*   **Strategy 2: Predictive "Due Date Quoting" & Buffer Management:**
    *   *Logic:* Use regression models trained on historical task durations (e.g., `Job Type + Machine + Operator = Actual Duration`) to provide realistic completion estimates.
    *   *PM Insight:* Use the probability distribution of past `Actual vs. Planned` durations to add "Dynamic Buffers" to the schedule, protecting the due date from variance.
*   **Strategy 3: Setup-Aware Batching at Bottlenecks:**
    *   *Logic:* Force grouping of similar jobs at critical machines.
    *   *PM Insight:* Use sequence mining to identify the "optimal families" of jobs that minimize setup times, even if this means delaying a "non-family" job slightly to maintain throughput.

### 5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement
*   **Discrete-Event Simulation (DES):** Before deployment, I will build a DES model (e.g., AnyLogic or SimPy) using the PDFs derived from the MES logs.
    *   *Scenarios:* I will run "Stress Tests" (e.g., increasing order arrival rate by 20%, simulating machine failures at the 95th percentile, and introducing a sudden influx of "Hot Jobs").
    *   *Metric:* Compare the "Area Under the Curve" for WIP and the "Tardiness Variance" across strategies.
*   **Continuous Improvement Loop:**
    *   **Monitoring:** I will implement a "Drift Detection" dashboard. If the average setup time for a specific job transition begins to deviate from the historical mean (perhaps due to tool wear or operator turnover), the scheduling model will automatically trigger an update to its "Setup Time Matrix."
    *   **Closed-Loop:** The MES will feed back the actual performance of the new schedules, which will be re-mined every month to refine the heuristic weights, ensuring the strategy adapts to the evolving shop floor reality.
