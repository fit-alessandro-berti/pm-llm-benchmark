The provided DECLARE model captures a highly structured, compliance-oriented loan approval process, likely inspired by real-world banking or financial lending workflows (e.g., mortgage, personal, or commercial loans). DECLARE is a declarative process mining language that defines constraints on activity occurrences and ordering rather than prescribing a rigid procedural flowchart. This allows flexibility in execution traces while enforcing mandatory rules, non-occurrences, and temporal relationships. #### 1. **Basic Occurrence Constraints**
These ensure certain activities always (or never) happen and control cardinality.

- **Existence**: Receive_Application (A) and Gather_Additional_Documents (C) must occur at least once.  
  In practice: Every loan process starts with an application intake (A), and supplementary documentation (C) is always required (e.g., for anti-money laundering or credit risk assessment). This prevents "ghost" loans without formal entry points.

- **Absence**: Proceed_Without_Compliance (Z) must never occur.  
  In practice: This is a "red flag" activity representing any shortcut that bypasses regulatory checks (e.g., approving without verification). Its prohibition enforces strict compliance with laws like Know Your Customer (KYC), Anti-Money Laundering (AML), or fair lending regulations (e.g., ECOA in the US).

- **Exactly_one**: Preliminary_Credit_Check (B) occurs exactly once per process instance.  
  In practice: A single initial credit pull (soft or hard inquiry) is performed early to avoid unnecessary repeated checks that could harm the applicant's credit score or waste resources. This maintains efficiency while ensuring consistency.

- **Init**: Receive_Application (A) is always the starting activity.  
  In practice: The process formally begins only when an application is received, providing a clear audit trail entry point.

These rules establish a reliable foundation: every valid case has a proper start, mandatory steps, no forbidden shortcuts, and controlled repetition.

#### 2. **Response-Like Constraints (Activation → Target Must Follow Eventually)**
These mandate that if the activation activity occurs, the target must eventually occur later.

- **Response**: Receive_Application (A) → Preliminary_Credit_Check (B)  
  Practical: Application intake always triggers an initial credit assessment—no application sits idle without screening.

- **Responded_existence**: Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package (D) → Quality_Assurance_Review (E)  
  Practical: Once an offer is prepared, it must undergo QA review before proceeding.

- **Altresponse** (alternate response: at least one of several possible targets, but here modeled as single): Transfer_Funds (G) → Notify_Customer (H)  
  Practical: Disbursement is always followed by customer notification (e.g., confirmation of funds transfer).

- **Chainresponse**: Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package (D) → Transfer_Funds (G)  
  Practical: Offer assembly eventually leads directly (in chain sense) to fund disbursement, implying a streamlined path in approved cases.

These ensure forward progress: key milestones trigger required downstream actions, preventing stalled or incomplete processes.

#### 3. **Precedence-Like Constraints (Target Cannot Occur Before Activation)**
These enforce ordering by requiring the activation to happen first.

- **Precedence**: Quality_Assurance_Review (E) → Authorize_Contract_Terms (F)  
  Practical: Contract terms cannot be authorized until QA is complete.

- **Altprecedence**: Notify_Customer (H) → Transfer_Funds (G)  
  Practical: Customers cannot be notified of approval/disburse before funds are actually transferred (prevents false promises).

- **Chainprecedence**: Authorize_Contract_Terms (F) → Preliminary_Credit_Check (B)  
  Practical: Early checks must precede final authorization—logical backward enforcement of early diligence.

These protect against premature actions, ensuring prerequisites are met.

#### 4. **Succession-Like Constraints (Activation → Target and Target → Activation in Sequence)**
Succession combines response and precedence for strict ordering.

- **Succession**: Preliminary_Credit_Check (B) → Gather_Additional_Documents (C)  
  Practical: Credit check directly leads to document gathering (e.g., if initial check flags issues).

- **Altsuccession**: Gather_Additional_Documents (C) → Quality_Assurance_Review (E)  
  Practical: Document collection is strictly followed by QA.

- **Chainsuccession**: Quality_Assurance_Review (E) → Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package (D)  
  Practical: QA directly precedes offer assembly (note direction: E then D).

These create tight sequential chains, reducing parallelism and variability where risks are high.

#### 5. **Coexistence and Non-Coexistence**
- **Coexistence**: Gather_Additional_Documents (C) ↔ Authorize_Contract_Terms (F) (both occur or neither)  
  Practical: In valid traces, document gathering and term authorization always co-occur—reflecting that full documentation is needed for authorization.

- **Noncoexistence**: Transfer_Funds (G) and Receive_Application (A) cannot both occur without proper ordering (but here it's strict prohibition of coexistence in wrong context).  
  Practical: Funds are never transferred in the same case as mere application receipt without intervening steps—prevents immediate disbursement.

#### 6. **Negative Succession Constraints**
These forbid certain orderings.

- **Nonsuccession**: Notify_Customer (H) cannot directly succeed Preliminary_Credit_Check (B)  
  Practical: No notification right after initial check—intermediate steps (documentation, review) are required.

- **Nonchainsuccession**: Authorize_Contract_Terms (F) cannot directly succeed Notify_Customer (H)  
  Practical: Notification doesn't immediately trigger authorization (wrong directionality).

These block shortcuts or illogical sequences.
