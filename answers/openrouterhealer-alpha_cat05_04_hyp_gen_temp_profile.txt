# Analysis of Temporal Profile Anomalies in Insurance Claim Processing

## Identified Anomalies in the Temporal Profile Model

**1. Receive to Approve (RP): Suspiciously Low Standard Deviation**
- **Average time**: 25 hours (90,000 seconds)
- **Standard deviation**: 1 hour (3,600 seconds)
- **Anomaly**: The coefficient of variation (stdev/mean) is only 4%, indicating extremely consistent timing that doesn't align with the natural variability of human-driven processes like claim evaluation.

**2. Approve to Notify (PN): Excessively Long and Variable Delay**
- **Average time**: 7 days (604,800 seconds)
- **Standard deviation**: 2 days (172,800 seconds)
- **Anomaly**: The standard deviation is 34% of the mean, showing high variability. A 7-day average notification delay after approval is unusually long for customer communications.

**3. Assign to Close (AC): Potentially Premature Closure**
- **Average time**: 2 hours (7,200 seconds)
- **Standard deviation**: 1 hour (3,600 seconds)
- **Anomaly**: Closing claims within 2 hours of assignment suggests either automated closures or bypassing of standard evaluation procedures.

**4. Evaluate to Notify (EN): Unrealistically Fast Transition**
- **Average time**: 5 minutes (300 seconds)
- **Standard deviation**: 1 minute (60 seconds)
- **Anomaly**: A 5-minute window between evaluation completion and customer notification is too quick for proper decision communication, especially if approval is required.

## Hypotheses for Anomalies

### Hypothesis 1: Automated Approval Triggers
The consistent RP timing suggests automated approval rules may be triggering based on:
- Fixed time thresholds (e.g., claims under $X auto-approve after 25 hours)
- Batch processing that runs on a rigid schedule
- Lack of manual review for certain claim categories

### Hypothesis 2: Notification System Bottlenecks
The PN delay indicates:
- Manual notification processes that operate in batches
- Email/SMS system backlogs or scheduling issues
- Dependency on unavailable customer contact information
- Regulatory requirements for approval waiting periods

### Hypothesis 3: Process Skipping or Bypassing
The AC and EN anomalies suggest:
- "Express" claim handling for certain adjusters or regions
- System errors that prematurely close claims
- Adjusters closing claims without completing required steps
- Automated workflows that skip intermediate steps under specific conditions

### Hypothesis 4: Resource Constraints or Misalignment
- Adjusters specializing in certain claim types causing bottlenecks
- Regional differences in staffing affecting processing times
- Seasonal or time-of-day patterns in claim submission and processing

## Verification Approaches Using SQL Queries

### Query 1: Identify Claims with Abnormal RP Timing
```sql
-- Find claims where RP timing deviates significantly from the model
WITH rp_timing AS (
    SELECT 
        c.claim_id,
        c.claim_type,
        c.claim_amount,
        a.name AS adjuster_name,
        a.specialization,
        a.region,
        EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ep.timestamp - er.timestamp)) AS time_seconds,
        (90000 - 2*3600) AS lower_bound,  -- Average minus 2 standard deviations
        (90000 + 2*3600) AS upper_bound   -- Average plus 2 standard deviations
    FROM claims c
    JOIN claim_events er ON c.claim_id = er.claim_id AND er.activity = 'R'
    JOIN claim_events ep ON c.claim_id = ep.claim_id AND ep.activity = 'P'
    LEFT JOIN adjusters a ON ep.resource = a.adjuster_id::TEXT
    WHERE ep.timestamp > er.timestamp
)
SELECT 
    claim_id,
    claim_type,
    claim_amount,
    adjuster_name,
    specialization,
    region,
    time_seconds,
    CASE 
        WHEN time_seconds < lower_bound THEN 'Too Fast'
        WHEN time_seconds > upper_bound THEN 'Too Slow'
        ELSE 'Normal'
    END AS timing_status
FROM rp_timing
WHERE time_seconds < lower_bound OR time_seconds > upper_bound
ORDER BY ABS(time_seconds - 90000) DESC
LIMIT 50;
```

### Query 2: Analyze PN Delays by Resource and Region
```sql
-- Examine extreme PN delays and correlate with adjusters and regions
WITH pn_delays AS (
    SELECT 
        c.claim_id,
        c.claim_type,
        c.claim_amount,
        a.name AS assigner_name,
        a.region AS adjuster_region,
        EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (en.timestamp - ep.timestamp)) / 86400.0 AS days_delay,
        CASE 
            WHEN EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (en.timestamp - ep.timestamp)) / 86400.0 > 14 THEN 'Extreme (>2 weeks)'
            WHEN EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (en.timestamp - ep.timestamp)) / 86400.0 > 7 THEN 'High (1-2 weeks)'
            ELSE 'Normal'
        END AS delay_category
    FROM claims c
    JOIN claim_events ep ON c.claim_id = ep.claim_id AND ep.activity = 'P'
    JOIN claim_events en ON c.claim_id = en.claim_id AND en.activity = 'N'
    LEFT JOIN adjusters a ON ep.resource = a.adjuster_id::TEXT
    WHERE en.timestamp > ep.timestamp
)
SELECT 
    assigner_name,
    adjuster_region,
    claim_type,
    COUNT(*) AS total_claims,
    AVG(days_delay) AS avg_delay_days,
    MAX(days_delay) AS max_delay_days,
    COUNT(CASE WHEN delay_category IN ('Extreme (>2 weeks)', 'High (1-2 weeks)') 
         THEN 1 END) AS delayed_count
FROM pn_delays
GROUP BY assigner_name, adjuster_region, claim_type
HAVING COUNT(CASE WHEN delay_category IN ('Extreme (>2 weeks)', 'High (1-2 weeks)') 
             THEN 1 END) > 0
ORDER BY delayed_count DESC
LIMIT 25;
```

### Query 3: Investigate Premature Claim Closures (AC)
```sql
-- Identify claims closed quickly after assignment, potentially skipping evaluation
WITH ac_closures AS (
    SELECT 
        c.claim_id,
        c.claim_type,
        c.claim_amount,
        a.name AS adjuster_name,
        a.specialization,
        a.region,
        EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ec.timestamp - ea.timestamp)) / 3600.0 AS hours_to_close,
        EXISTS (
            SELECT 1 FROM claim_events ee 
            WHERE ee.claim_id = c.claim_id 
            AND ee.activity = 'E' 
            AND ee.timestamp BETWEEN ea.timestamp AND ec.timestamp
        ) AS has_evaluation,
        EXISTS (
            SELECT 1 FROM claim_events ep 
            WHERE ep.claim_id = c.claim_id 
            AND ep.activity = 'P' 
            AND ep.timestamp BETWEEN ea.timestamp AND ec.timestamp
        ) AS has_approval
    FROM claims c
    JOIN claim_events ea ON c.claim_id = ea.claim_id AND ea.activity = 'A'
    JOIN claim_events ec ON c.claim_id = ec.claim_id AND ec.activity = 'C'
    LEFT JOIN adjusters a ON ea.resource = a.adjuster_id::TEXT
    WHERE ec.timestamp > ea.timestamp
    AND EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (ec.timestamp - ea.timestamp)) / 3600.0 <= 4  -- Closed within 4 hours
)
SELECT 
    adjuster_name,
    specialization,
    region,
    claim_type,
    COUNT(*) AS quick_closure_count,
    AVG(claim_amount) AS avg_claim_amount,
    COUNT(CASE WHEN NOT has_evaluation AND NOT has_approval THEN 1 END) AS skipped_both_steps,
    COUNT(CASE WHEN NOT has_evaluation THEN 1 END) AS skipped_evaluation
FROM ac_closures
GROUP BY adjuster_name, specialization, region, claim_type
HAVING COUNT(*) >= 3  -- Focus on patterns with at least 3 occurrences
ORDER BY skipped_both_steps DESC, quick_closure_count DESC;
```

### Query 4: Correlate EN Speed with Missing Steps
```sql
-- Check if 5-minute EN transitions indicate skipped approval steps
WITH en_transitions AS (
    SELECT 
        c.claim_id,
        c.claim_type,
        c.claim_amount,
        a.name AS evaluator_name,
        a.specialization,
        EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (en.timestamp - ee.timestamp)) AS seconds_to_notify,
        EXISTS (
            SELECT 1 FROM claim_events ep 
            WHERE ep.claim_id = c.claim_id 
            AND ep.activity = 'P' 
            AND ep.timestamp BETWEEN ee.timestamp AND en.timestamp
        ) AS has_approval_between,
        EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (en.timestamp - ee.timestamp)) - 300 AS deviation_from_avg  -- Deviation from 5-minute average
    FROM claims c
    JOIN claim_events ee ON c.claim_id = ee.claim_id AND ee.activity = 'E'
    JOIN claim_events en ON c.claim_id = en.claim_id AND en.activity = 'N'
    LEFT JOIN adjusters a ON ee.resource = a.adjuster_id::TEXT
    WHERE en.timestamp > ee.timestamp
    AND EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (en.timestamp - ee.timestamp)) <= 600  -- Within 10 minutes
)
SELECT 
    evaluator_name,
    specialization,
    claim_type,
    COUNT(*) AS fast_transition_count,
    AVG(seconds_to_notify) AS avg_seconds,
    COUNT(CASE WHEN NOT has_approval_between THEN 1 END) AS skipped_approval,
    AVG(deviation_from_avg) AS avg_deviation_seconds,
    COUNT(CASE WHEN has_approval_between THEN 1 END) AS with_approval
FROM en_transitions
GROUP BY evaluator_name, specialization, claim_type
HAVING COUNT(*) >= 3
ORDER BY fast_transition_count DESC;
```

### Query 5: Temporal Pattern Analysis by Time of Day/Day of Week
```sql
-- Analyze if anomalies follow temporal patterns (batch processing indicators)
WITH temporal_patterns AS (
    SELECT 
        EXTRACT(HOUR FROM ep.timestamp) AS approval_hour,
        EXTRACT(DOW FROM ep.timestamp) AS approval_day_of_week,
        COUNT(*) AS approval_count,
        AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (en.timestamp - ep.timestamp))) AS avg_pn_delay_seconds,
        STDDEV(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (en.timestamp - ep.timestamp))) AS stdev_pn_delay_seconds
    FROM claim_events ep
    JOIN claim_events en ON ep.claim_id = en.claim_id 
    WHERE ep.activity = 'P' 
    AND en.activity = 'N'
    AND en.timestamp > ep.timestamp
    GROUP BY EXTRACT(HOUR FROM ep.timestamp), EXTRACT(DOW FROM ep.timestamp)
    HAVING COUNT(*) > 10
)
SELECT 
    approval_hour,
    CASE approval_day_of_week 
        WHEN 0 THEN 'Sunday'
        WHEN 1 THEN 'Monday'
        WHEN 2 THEN 'Tuesday'
        WHEN 3 THEN 'Wednesday'
        WHEN 4 THEN 'Thursday'
        WHEN 5 THEN 'Friday'
        WHEN 6 THEN 'Saturday'
    END AS day_of_week,
    approval_count,
    ROUND(avg_pn_delay_seconds / 3600.0, 2) AS avg_delay_hours,
    ROUND(stdev_pn_delay_seconds / 3600.0, 2) AS stdev_delay_hours
FROM temporal_patterns
ORDER BY approval_day_of_week, approval_hour;
```

These verification approaches will help identify whether the temporal anomalies represent:
- Systemic process design choices
- Resource allocation issues
- Automation gaps or inefficiencies
- Data quality problems
- Regional or categorical variations

The queries are designed to move from identifying individual anomalous claims to uncovering broader patterns that explain the deviations observed in the temporal profile model.
