**The XOR branching introduces subtle bias by creating non-uniform treatment that favors applicants with specific social and geographic ties.**

In the POWL model, after PreliminaryScoring (C), the XOR operator chooses between executing CheckLocalAffiliation (D) or skipping via the silent transition. The model description explicitly states that "Being selected for D leads to a subtle score uplift." This means the process does not apply the same criteria to all applicants: some receive an additional positive adjustment to their score for being local residents and members of "known community groups," while others with similar (or better) financial profiles receive no such boost and proceed directly to ManualReview (E).

The choice of XOR path is not described as random, purely objective, or applied uniformly to everyone. In practice, selection for the D branch is likely triggered by indicators of local residency or community membership. As a result, the process systematically grants an incremental advantage to one subset of applicants based on affiliation rather than strictly on creditworthiness factors (income, credit history, collateral, etc.). This is embedded structurally in the workflow: the partial order routes everyone through the same high-level sequence, but the XOR creates divergent scoring paths that are not equivalent.

### Implications of Favoring a Non-Legally Protected Group

"Local affiliation" and membership in certain community groups are typically not a legally protected class in the same way as race, gender, religion, or national origin under fair lending laws (e.g., ECOA in the US). Nevertheless, granting this group an incremental advantage has several problematic effects on fairness and equity:

- **Undermines merit-based and procedural fairness**: Fairness in lending generally requires that similarly situated applicants (comparable risk profiles) receive consistent treatment. Here, the model allows social connections and insider status to tip borderline cases during scoring and subsequent manual review/final decision. This shifts outcomes away from objective risk assessment toward irrelevant or weakly relevant factors such as geographic longevity or participation in specific networks. Even if community membership correlates modestly with lower default risk (e.g., via social collateral or stability), selectively applying it via an XOR branch rather than as a transparent, uniformly applied variable is inconsistent and non-transparent.

- **Potential as a proxy for protected characteristics (disparate impact risk)**: In many real-world settings, "known community groups" or strong local residency patterns correlate with ethnicity, religion, socioeconomic status, immigration status, or race. Favoring such groups can therefore produce disparate outcomes for outsiders, newcomers, or members of other communities even without explicitly using a protected attribute. Regulators often scrutinize practices that result in statistically different approval rates or terms across demographic groups under the "disparate impact" doctrine. While the model avoids direct use of protected classes, it can still create indirect effects that invite legal or regulatory attention.

- **Impacts on equity and broader societal effects**: Equity seeks to provide comparable opportunity rather than identical inputs. By design, this process gives an unearned edge to established insiders, making it harder for equally qualified outsiders to compete on equal terms. Over time, this can:
  - Perpetuate existing social and economic advantages (those already connected get better access to credit, which aids wealth building).
  - Reduce diversity in the borrower pool and reinforce homogeneous networks.
  - Erode public trust in lending institutions, as decisions appear influenced by favoritism rather than merit.
  - Compound in downstream steps (the uplift affects what reaches ManualReview and FinalDecision).

- **Risks in process mining and automation**: Models like this POWL representation are often discovered from historical event logs. If past decisions already contained human bias or discretionary favoritism, the mined model encodes and propagates it. When used for conformance checking, simulation, or as input to predictive/ML systems, the bias becomes structural and harder to detect. Best practice in responsible process mining would involve auditing such optional/variable steps for fairness, removing or uniformizing non-merit factors, and ensuring all paths are justified by legitimate business needs.

In summary, the XOR construct here is not a neutral control-flow choice. It creates a subtle, selective advantage for a particular non-protected group based on affiliation. While it may appear minor ("subtle score uplift"), its placement after preliminary scoring and before manual review/final decision allows it to influence real outcomes in borderline cases. True fairness and equity in lending require transparent, uniformly applied, and demonstrably relevant criteria. Non-merit-based advantages—however indirect—undermine that goal, reduce accountability, and risk both ethical and practical harms. Process models should be reviewed specifically for such patterns to avoid embedding them into operational systems.
