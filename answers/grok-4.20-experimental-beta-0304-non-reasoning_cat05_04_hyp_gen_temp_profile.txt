**Anomalies Identified in the Temporal Profile**

The temporal profile reveals several statistically suspicious patterns:

- **R  P (Receive to Approve)**: Average of ~25 hours with an extremely low standard deviation (1 hour). This unusually tight clustering suggests approvals are occurring on a near-fixed schedule regardless of claim complexity.
- **P  N (Approve to Notify)**: Average delay of 7 days with high variability (ｱ2 days). This is the longest gap in the process and shows inconsistent customer communication timing.
- **A  C (Assign to Close)**: Only 2 hours on average between assignment and closure. This appears too short to realistically accommodate full evaluation and approval for most claims.
- **E  N (Evaluate to Notify)**: Only 5 minutes on average. This rapid transition is unrealistic for most insurance claims, which typically require more processing time before customer notification.

These patterns deviate from expected insurance claim handling logic, where evaluation and approval should show more natural variation based on claim type, amount, and complexity.

**Hypotheses for These Anomalies**

1. **Batch or Scheduled Processing**: The tight RP timing and low variance may indicate that approvals are processed in nightly batches or follow a rigid calendar (e.g., all approvals happen during a specific daily review window), rather than being driven by actual claim assessment.

2. **System Automation vs. Manual Workarounds**: The very short EN interval suggests either automated notification scripts that fire immediately after evaluation (potentially skipping quality checks) or backdating of timestamps by users.

3. **Premature Closures or Process Bypass**: The short AC duration points to some claims being closed shortly after assignment, possibly through "fast-track" or "low-value" claim shortcuts, or due to incorrect activity logging where intermediate steps (E, P) are skipped or recorded retroactively.

4. **Notification Backlog or Resource Constraints**: The long and highly variable PN gap likely reflects a bottleneck in the notification team, pending approval queues, or cases where customers are only notified after additional internal reviews or when resources become available.

5. **Data Quality Issues**: Some anomalies could stem from timestamp manipulation, bulk updates by administrators, or system integration problems where events are logged out of sequence.

**Verification Approaches Using SQL**

Here are targeted queries to investigate these patterns on the `claim_events` table:

```sql
-- 1. Find claims with suspiciously fast Receive-to-Approve timing (< 12 hours)
SELECT 
    c.claim_id,
    c.claim_type,
    c.claim_amount,
    MIN(CASE WHEN e1.activity = 'R' THEN e1.timestamp END) as receive_time,
    MIN(CASE WHEN e1.activity = 'P' THEN e1.timestamp END) as approve_time,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (MIN(CASE WHEN e1.activity = 'P' THEN e1.timestamp END) - 
                        MIN(CASE WHEN e1.activity = 'R' THEN e1.timestamp END))) as seconds_to_approve
FROM claim_events e1
JOIN claims c ON e1.claim_id = c.claim_id
WHERE e1.activity IN ('R', 'P')
GROUP BY c.claim_id, c.claim_type, c.claim_amount
HAVING EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (MIN(CASE WHEN e1.activity = 'P' THEN e1.timestamp END) - 
                           MIN(CASE WHEN e1.activity = 'R' THEN e1.timestamp END))) < 43200
ORDER BY seconds_to_approve;
```

```sql
-- 2. Identify claims with long delays between Approve and Notify (> 10 days)
SELECT 
    c.claim_id,
    c.claim_type,
    a.name as adjuster,
    a.region,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (MIN(CASE WHEN e2.activity = 'N' THEN e2.timestamp END) - 
                        MIN(CASE WHEN e2.activity = 'P' THEN e2.timestamp END))) as approve_to_notify_seconds,
    COUNT(DISTINCT e2.activity) as activities_between
FROM claim_events e2
JOIN claims c ON e2.claim_id = c.claim_id
LEFT JOIN adjusters a ON e2.resource = a.name
WHERE e2.activity IN ('P', 'N')
GROUP BY c.claim_id, c.claim_type, a.name, a.region
HAVING EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (MIN(CASE WHEN e2.activity = 'N' THEN e2.timestamp END) - 
                           MIN(CASE WHEN e2.activity = 'P' THEN e2.timestamp END))) > 864000
ORDER BY approve_to_notify_seconds DESC;
```

```sql
-- 3. Detect claims closed very quickly after assignment (A to C < 4 hours)
SELECT 
    c.claim_id,
    c.claim_type,
    e.resource,
    MIN(CASE WHEN e.activity = 'A' THEN e.timestamp END) as assign_time,
    MIN(CASE WHEN e.activity = 'C' THEN e.timestamp END) as close_time,
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (MIN(CASE WHEN e.activity = 'C' THEN e.timestamp END) - 
                        MIN(CASE WHEN e.activity = 'A' THEN e.timestamp END))) as seconds_to_close
FROM claim_events e
JOIN claims c ON e.claim_id = c.claim_id
WHERE e.activity IN ('A', 'C')
GROUP BY c.claim_id, c.claim_type, e.resource
HAVING EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (MIN(CASE WHEN e.activity = 'C' THEN e.timestamp END) - 
                           MIN(CASE WHEN e.activity = 'A' THEN e.timestamp END))) < 14400
ORDER BY seconds_to_close;
```

```sql
-- 4. Check for suspiciously fast Evaluate-to-Notify transitions and see resource patterns
SELECT 
    e.resource,
    COUNT(*) as claim_count,
    AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n_time - e_time))) as avg_seconds_e_to_n,
    MIN(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n_time - e_time))) as min_seconds,
    MAX(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n_time - e_time))) as max_seconds
FROM (
    SELECT 
        claim_id,
        resource,
        MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'E' THEN timestamp END) as e_time,
        MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'N' THEN timestamp END) as n_time
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity IN ('E', 'N')
    GROUP BY claim_id, resource
) sub
JOIN claims c ON sub.claim_id = c.claim_id
WHERE n_time IS NOT NULL 
  AND e_time IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY e.resource
HAVING AVG(EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (n_time - e_time))) < 600
ORDER BY avg_seconds_e_to_n;
```

These queries can help isolate specific claims, adjusters, regions, or claim types that contribute most to the observed temporal anomalies.
