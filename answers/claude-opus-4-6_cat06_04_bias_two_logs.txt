# Bias Analysis: Comparing Group A and Group B Event Logs

## Identification of Bias

**Group B's event log exhibits bias Ś specifically, it reveals a systematic advantage granted to certain members of the unprotected group through a "Community Boost" mechanism that is structurally unavailable to Group A (the protected group).**

---

## How the Bias Manifests

### 1. The Community Boost Mechanism

The most critical difference between the two logs lies in the **ScoreAdjustment** column. In Group B, applicants who are members of the **Highland Civic Darts Club** receive a **+10 point "Community Boost"** during the PreliminaryScoring phase. This adjustment is applied by the Scoring Engine Ś an automated resource Ś meaning the bias is **embedded in the system's rules**, not merely the result of individual human discretion.

- **U001**: Preliminary score of 720  adjusted to **730** (Community Boost)
- **U003**: Preliminary score of 695  adjusted to **705** (Community Boost)

In contrast, **every applicant in Group A receives a ScoreAdjustment of 0** across all cases. No member of Group A is affiliated with any community group (`CommunityGroup = None`), and none are local residents (`LocalResident = FALSE`).

### 2. The Interplay of LocalResident and CommunityGroup

These two attributes are **perfectly correlated with group membership** in the data:

| Attribute        | Group A (Protected) | Group B (Unprotected) |
|------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| LocalResident    | Always FALSE       | Always TRUE           |
| CommunityGroup   | Always None        | Sometimes affiliated  |

Group A members are non-local residents with no community group ties. Because the Community Boost is only available to those in recognized community groups Ś groups that appear to be **locally rooted** Ś the mechanism creates a pathway to score enhancement that is **de facto inaccessible** to the protected group. Even if the policy is facially neutral (i.e., it doesn't explicitly reference group membership), its effect is discriminatory because the qualifying criteria (local residency, community group membership) serve as **proxies** for protected group status.

### 3. Concrete Impact on Decisions

The bias directly influences outcomes in at least two observable ways:

**Case comparison Ś P002 vs. U003:**

| Aspect            | P002 (Group A)     | U003 (Group B)       |
|-------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Preliminary Score | 710                | 695                   |
| Score Adjustment  | 0                  | +10                   |
| Final Score       | 710                | 705                   |
| Decision          | **Rejected**       | **Approved**          |

This is the starkest illustration of the bias. **P002 had a higher raw score (710 > 695) yet was rejected, while U003 Ś with a lower underlying creditworthiness Ś was approved** after receiving the community boost. The adjustment did not even bring U003's score up to P002's level (705 < 710), yet U003 was approved. This suggests the community affiliation may also carry a **qualitative signal** during Manual Review that further advantages Group B members beyond the numerical adjustment, or that the approval threshold is being applied inconsistently.

**Case comparison Ś P001 vs. U001:**

Both started at 720. P001 was approved with a score of 720. U001 was also approved but with an inflated score of 730. While the outcome is the same, the boosted score for U001 may yield **better terms** (e.g., lower interest rate, higher credit limit), creating inequality even when both are approved.

### 4. Systematic Pattern

Summarizing outcomes:

| Group   | Cases Approved | Cases Rejected | Boost Applied |
|---------|---------------|----------------|---------------|
| Group A | 2 (P001, P003) | 1 (P002)       | Never         |
| Group B | 2 (U001, U003) | 1 (U002)       | 2 of 3 cases  |

Notably, **U002** Ś the only Group B member **without** a community group affiliation Ś was the one rejected, mirroring Group A's experience. This reinforces that the community boost is the differentiating mechanism.

---

## Conclusion

The bias is **systemic and structural**, encoded into the scoring engine's adjustment rules. It operates through ostensibly neutral criteria Ś community group membership and local residency Ś that function as **proxy variables** correlating with protected group status. The result is that Group A applicants are evaluated solely on their raw preliminary scores, while favored Group B applicants receive score enhancements that can convert what would otherwise be a rejection into an approval. This constitutes **indirect (disparate impact) discrimination**: a facially neutral policy that produces systematically worse outcomes for the protected group.
