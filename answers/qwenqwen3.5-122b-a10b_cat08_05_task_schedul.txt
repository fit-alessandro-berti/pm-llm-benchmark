# Process Mining-Driven Optimization for Precision Parts Inc.

## 1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics

To move beyond the current "black box" of production, we must first reconstruct the digital twin of the shop floor using **Process Mining** on the MES event logs. The objective is to transition from *planned* schedules to *actual* execution data.

### Process Discovery and Flow Reconstruction
Using **Inductive Mining** or **Heuristic Mining** algorithms on the MES logs, we will reconstruct the process model. Unlike standard workflow mining, Job Shop scheduling requires a **Resource-Centric Process Map**.
*   **Technique:** We will treat `Case ID` as the Job and `Resource (Machine ID)` as the activity node.
*   **Log Pre-processing:** We will clean the log to handle the `Queue Entry` and `Task Start/End` events. Specifically, we will calculate the `Wait Time` as the delta between `Task End (Prev Station)` and `Queue Entry (Current Station)`.
*   **Variant Analysis:** We will identify unique process variants (routings). Since this is a job shop, we expect high variability. We will cluster jobs by similar routing paths to identify dominant flow patterns.

### Quantification of Key Metrics
We will derive the following metrics directly from the event log timestamps:

*   **Job Flow Times & Makespan:**
    *   *Calculation:* $T_{finish} - T_{release}$ for each `Case ID`.
    *   *Distribution:* We will plot histograms to identify the tail risk (the 90th or 95th percentile lead time) rather than just the average, which hides the variability causing missed due dates.
*   **Task Waiting Times (Queue Times):**
    *   *Calculation:* $\sum (\text{Task Start}_{i} - \text{Task End}_{i-1})$.
    *   *Analysis:* We will aggregate this by `Resource ID`. High variance in queue times indicates resource contention or erratic upstream processing.
*   **Resource Utilization:**
    *   *Calculation:* $\frac{\sum(\text{Task End} - \text{Task Start})}{\text{Total Shift Duration}}$.
    *   *Decomposition:* Crucially, we will separate `Setup Time` (derived from `Setup Start` to `Setup End`) from `Processing Time`. This allows us to calculate **Net Utilization** (Value-Add) vs. **Gross Utilization** (including setups).
*   **Sequence-Dependent Setup Times:**
    *   *Method:* We will sort logs by `Resource ID` and `Timestamp`. For every transition from Job $A$ to Job $B$ on Machine $M$, we calculate the inter-arrival time ($T_{setup\_start\_B} - T_{task\_end\_A}$).
    *   *Correlation:* Using the `Notes` column (e.g., "Previous job: JOB-6998") and `Order Priority`, we will build a **Setup Transition Matrix**. This quantifies the cost of switching from Material Type X to Material Type Y.
*   **Schedule Adherence & Tardiness:**
    *   *Calculation:* $\text{Actual Finish} - \text{Order Due Date}$.
    *   *Metric:* **Tardiness Rate** (% of late jobs) and **Mean Tardiness** (days late).
    *   *Conformance Checking:* We will compare the actual sequence of tasks against the *intended* routing (if stored in the MES) to detect unauthorized process deviations.
*   **Impact of Disruptions:**
    *   *Filtering:* We will isolate events labeled `Breakdown Start/End` and `Priority Change`.
    *   *Impact Analysis:* We will measure the "Recovery Time"𤪔ow long it took for the system to return to planned throughput after a breakdown㻡nd the "Cascading Delay"𤪔ow many subsequent jobs were delayed due to the interruption.

## 2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies

With the quantitative baseline established, we will use process mining diagnostics to pinpoint specific inefficiencies.

### Identification of Bottlenecks and Capacity Constraints
*   **Evidence:** We will generate **Resource Heatmaps**. A bottleneck is identified not just by high utilization (>85%), but by **Queue Buildup**.
*   *Mining Technique:* **Bottleneck Analysis**. We look for the resource where the `Queue Entry` to `Task Start` wait time is highest and most variable.
*   *Pathology:* If `CUT-01` shows 95% utilization but `MILL-03` shows 40% utilization with high WIP waiting for `MILL-03`, the bottleneck has shifted. If `CUT-01` is consistently the queue point, it is the primary constraint.

### Evidence of Poor Task Prioritization
*   **Evidence:** We will perform a **Variant Comparison** between `Order Priority = High` and `Order Priority = Medium`.
*   *Pathology:* If `High Priority` jobs have a higher average Queue Time than `Medium Priority` jobs at specific resources, the local dispatching rule (e.g., FCFS) is overriding the global priority logic.
*   *Mining Technique:* **Decision Mining**. We analyze the decision point at `Queue Entry`. Did the scheduler select the High Priority job? If the log shows a Medium job starting before a High job that arrived earlier, the rule is being ignored or overridden by setup minimization.

### Suboptimal Sequencing and Setup Inflation
*   **Evidence:** We will analyze the **Setup Transition Matrix**.
*   *Pathology:* If the data shows that Job A $\to$ Job B takes 120 minutes, but Job A $\to$ Job C takes 20 minutes, and the scheduler frequently sequences A $\to$ B $\to$ A (high changeover), this indicates a lack of **Group Technology** (batching similar jobs).
*   *Mining Technique:* **Sequence Mining**. We look for frequent patterns in the `Activity` stream that correlate with high `Setup Duration` columns.

### Starvation and WIP Variability (The Bullwhip Effect)
*   **Evidence:** **WIP Profile Analysis**. We will plot the number of jobs in the system over time.
*   *Pathology:* If upstream machines are running at 100% while downstream machines show "Idle" periods followed by "Rush" periods, this indicates poor synchronization.
*   *Mining Technique:* **Conformance Checking on Flow**. We trace the `Case ID` across resources. If the gap between `Task End (Station 1)` and `Queue Entry (Station 2)` is consistently large, it indicates a release rate that exceeds downstream capacity (over-releasing).

## 3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness

Process mining allows us to distinguish between *systemic capacity issues* and *scheduling logic failures*.

### Limitations of Static Dispatching Rules
*   **Analysis:** We will correlate `Queue Wait Time` with `Shift Time`.
*   **Root Cause:** If wait times spike at the end of shifts or during shift changes, it suggests the current rules (FCFS/EDD) do not account for operator availability or shift handover complexities. Static rules cannot react to the dynamic state of the shop floor (e.g., a machine breaking down at 10:00 AM).
*   **Process Mining Evidence:** High variance in `Task Duration (Actual)` vs. `Task Duration (Planned)` suggests the static rules rely on inaccurate data, causing the schedule to drift immediately upon execution.

### Ineffective Handling of Sequence-Dependent Setups
*   **Analysis:** Regression analysis of `Setup Duration` against `Job Attributes` (e.g., material, size).
*   **Root Cause:** The current system likely treats setup as a constant or ignores it. The log shows `Setup Required: TRUE` and `Previous Job` notes, implying the data exists but isn't used for decision making.
*   **Process Mining Evidence:** A strong correlation between specific job pairs and high setup times indicates that the scheduler is not grouping jobs to minimize changeovers. This leads to "hidden capacity" loss.

### Lack of Real-Time Visibility and Disruption Response
*   **Analysis:** Time-to-Response metrics for `Breakdown Start` events.
*   **Root Cause:** The gap between `Breakdown Start` and the next `Task Start` on that resource (or the next `Task Start` on the affected job) reveals the lag in rescheduling.
*   **Process Mining Evidence:** If `Priority Change` events (Hot Jobs) result in immediate preemption without a ripple analysis, it causes chaos downstream. Mining the logs will show "cascading delays" where one hot job delays five other critical jobs.

### Distinguishing Logic vs. Capacity
*   **Logic Issue:** High WIP + High Queue Times + Low Resource Utilization = **Scheduling Logic Failure** (Jobs released too fast, poor sequencing).
*   **Capacity Issue:** High WIP + High Queue Times + High Resource Utilization = **Capacity Limitation** (Need more machines or overtime).
*   **Process Mining Role:** By overlaying Utilization and Queue Time distributions, we can definitively state whether investing in new machines (Capacity) or new algorithms (Logic) is the correct ROI.

## 4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies

Based on the mining insights, we will implement three distinct strategies.

### Strategy 1: Multi-Criteria Dynamic Dispatching (MCD-Dispatch)
*   **Core Logic:** Replace static FCFS/EDD with a **Weighted Priority Score** calculated in real-time at the moment a machine becomes free.
    *   $Score = (w_1 \cdot \text{SlackTime}) + (w_2 \cdot \text{PriorityWeight}) + (w_3 \cdot \text{SetupPenalty})$
    *   *SlackTime:* (Due Date - Remaining Processing Time).
    *   *SetupPenalty:* Estimated setup time for the candidate job given the *current* last job on the machine.
*   **Process Mining Input:**
    *   **Weights ($w$):** Derived from regression analysis of historical logs. Which factor most strongly correlated with on-time delivery in the past?
    *   **Setup Penalty:** Uses the **Setup Transition Matrix** mined in Point 1 to predict the exact setup cost for Job $X$ after Job $Y$.
*   **Targeted Pathology:** Addresses **Poor Prioritization** and **Suboptimal Sequencing**.
*   **Expected Impact:** Reduces Tardiness by 15-20% by ensuring urgent jobs aren't blocked by long setups.

### Strategy 2: Predictive Scheduling with Digital Shadow
*   **Core Logic:** Move from static schedules to **Model Predictive Control (MPC)**. The schedule is re-optimized (or adjusted) daily or upon disruption.
    *   Uses **Machine Learning** (e.g., Random Forest) to predict `Actual Task Duration` based on `Operator ID`, `Machine ID`, and `Job Complexity` rather than using the `Planned Duration`.
    *   Incorporates **Predictive Maintenance** signals (if available) to reserve capacity for maintenance.
*   **Process Mining Input:**
    *   **Duration Distributions:** Instead of a mean time, the scheduler uses a probability distribution (e.g., Log-Normal) of task durations mined from logs to add dynamic safety buffers.
    *   **Breakdown Probability:** Historical frequency of `Breakdown Start` events per machine to calculate availability risk.
*   **Targeted Pathology:** Addresses **Unpredictable Lead Times** and **Inaccurate Task Duration Estimations**.
*   **Expected Impact:** Increases Lead Time Accuracy (Forecast Error reduction) by 30%, allowing for better customer communication.

### Strategy 3: Bottleneck-Centric Setup Optimization (Group Technology)
*   **Core Logic:** Identify the primary bottleneck resource (e.g., `CUT-01`) via mining. Enforce a **Sequence Optimization** rule specifically for this resource.
    *   Jobs are grouped by material type or setup similarity *before* entering the bottleneck queue.
    *   Non-bottleneck resources follow a "Pull" logic to feed the bottleneck without starving it.
*   **Process Mining Input:**
    *   **Bottleneck Identification:** Confirmed via the Resource Heatmap.
    *   **Setup Clustering:** K-Means clustering on `Job Attributes` to identify which jobs can be processed with minimal setup changeovers.
*   **Targeted Pathology:** Addresses **Inefficient Resource Utilization** and **High WIP** (by smoothing flow to the bottleneck).
*   **Expected Impact:** Reduces total Setup Time at the bottleneck by 25%, effectively increasing capacity without buying new machines.
