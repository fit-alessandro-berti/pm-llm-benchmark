## **Step 2: Root Cause Analysis**

Let’s analyze the **sequence of activities** and **time gaps** between key steps to uncover bottlenecks.

---

###  **Case 105 – The Extreme Delay (49.08 hours)**

| Activity | Timestamp | Time Gap from Previous |
|--------|-----------|------------------------|
| Receive Ticket | 2024-03-01 08:25 | – |
| Triage Ticket | 2024-03-01 08:35 | 10 min |
| Assign to Level-1 Agent | 2024-03-01 09:00 | 25 min |
| Investigate Issue | 2024-03-01 09:10 | 10 min |
| **Escalate to Level-2 Agent** | 2024-03-01 10:00 | **50 min** |
| **Investigate Issue (Level-2)** | 2024-03-02 14:00 | **>28 hours**  |
| Resolve Ticket | 2024-03-03 09:00 | **39 hours** |
| Close Ticket | 2024-03-03 09:30 | 30 min |

####  Key Observations:
- **Massive delay (28+ hours)** between **escalation** and **Level-2 investigation**.
- The escalation happens on **March 1 at 10:00**, but **Level-2 investigation doesn't begin until March 2 at 14:00** — a **full 28-hour gap**.
- This delay is **not due to triage or assignment**, but **post-escalation inactivity**.
- The resolution and closure occur **two full days later**, indicating **no progress during that time**.

####  Potential Causes:
- **Lack of SLA enforcement** after escalation.
- **No automatic notification** to Level-2 team.
- **Delayed handover** due to shift changes, holidays, or poor coordination.
- **No visibility or tracking** of escalated tickets.

## **Insights & Recommendations**

###  **Critical Insight: Escalation is the primary bottleneck**
- All **delayed cases (102, 104, 105)** either:
  - Involve **escalation**, or
  - Experience **long delays after assignment** (Case 104).
- Cases **without escalation (101, 103)** are resolved quickly (< 3 hours).

 **Escalation is not just a step — it’s a process trigger that often breaks down.**

---

###  **Recommendations to Improve Performance**

1. **Implement Escalation SLAs with Automated Alerts**
   - Define **maximum time to respond after escalation** (e.g., 4 hours).
   - Automatically notify Level-2 team via email, Slack, or ticketing system.
   - Escalate further if no action within SLA.

2. **Introduce a Ticket Monitoring Dashboard**
   - Visualize **time spent at each stage** (Triage, Assign, Investigate, Resolve).
   - Flag tickets that exceed **thresholds** (e.g., >2 hours in "Assign" or >12 hours in "Investigate").

3. **Set Internal Time Limits for Key Steps**
   - Enforce: “Investigation must start within 1 hour of assignment.”
   - Use **automated reminders** if a ticket is pending.

4. **Standardize Escalation Workflow**
   - Define clear **handover procedures**: who owns the ticket post-escalation, how to acknowledge.
   - Require **confirmation of receipt** after escalation.

5. **Analyze Patterns of Delay**
   - Check if delays occur during **weekends, holidays, or shift changes**.
   - Adjust staffing or auto-escalate to on-call teams.

6. **Track and Report on "Time to First Response"**
   - Measure time from **Receive  Triage** and **Triage  Assign**.
   - Ensure these are under 15–30 minutes consistently.

7. **Conduct Root Cause Analysis (RCA) for Each Delayed Case**
   - Investigate *why* Case 105 sat idle for 28 hours.
   - Was it a missed alert? No one available? No priority tag?

---

##  **Final Summary**

- **Case 105** has the **most severe delay (49 hours)** due to a **28-hour gap after escalation**.
- **Cases 102 and 104** also show **critical delays** after escalation or assignment, indicating **process breakdowns in handover or task initiation**.
- The **common root cause** is **lack of automated follow-up and accountability** after key transitions.
- **Escalation is the single biggest performance risk** in the process.

###  **Conclusion**

> **The delay in ticket resolution is not due to complexity of the issue, but to process gaps in escalation handling and task progression.**  
> **Immediate action on SLA enforcement, automation, and real-time monitoring will reduce cycle times by 70–90%.**
