# Data-Driven Scheduling Optimization for Precision Parts Inc.
## A Process Mining Approach to Job Shop Scheduling

---

## 1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics

### 1.1 Reconstructing the Actual Process Flow

The MES event log is structured with the essential `(Case ID, Activity, Timestamp, Resource)` tuple required for process mining, enriched with scheduling-critical attributes (setup flags, priorities, due dates, planned vs. actual durations). The reconstruction proceeds in layers:

- **Control-flow discovery at the job level:** Apply the Inductive Miner (infrequent variant) or the Heuristics Miner on the activity dimension (`Cutting  Milling  Lathing  ...`). Because each job has a unique routing, I would partition jobs into **routing families** (via trace clustering using edit-distance on activity sequences) before mining, producing a family-specific process model rather than one "spaghetti" model.
- **Machine-level discovery:** Re-project the log using `Resource (Machine ID)` as the case notion. This produces a *machine-centric* process model (sometimes called a "resource handover" view) showing the **actual sequence of jobs** that each machine processed — the cornerstone for setup-time and utilization analysis.
- **Performance enrichment:** Timestamps of `Queue Entry`, `Setup Start/End`, `Task Start/End` allow decomposition of each task's sojourn time into: **wait + setup + processing + post-processing idle**.

### 1.2 Specific Techniques and Metrics

| KPI Domain | Technique | Metric |
|---|---|---|
| **Flow / lead times** | Case duration analysis; distribution fitting (log-normal, Weibull) | Mean, P50, P90 lead time per routing family; makespan of release cohorts |
| **Queue times** | Timestamp delta between `Queue Entry` and `Setup/Task Start` | Per-machine waiting-time distribution; Little's Law cross-check: $L = \lambda W$ |
| **Utilization** | Resource timeline reconstruction | % time in {Setup, Processing, Idle, Down}; operator vs. machine breakdown |
| **Setup times** | Sequence-pair analysis on machine-centric log | Matrix $S_{ij}$ = mean setup time when job of family *j* follows family *i* |
| **Tardiness** | Compare `Task End` of last operation to `Due Date` | % tardy, mean tardiness, maximum lateness, weighted tardiness (by priority) |
| **Disruption impact** | Event-window analysis around `Breakdown` and `Priority Change` events | Pre/post queue-length delta, downstream propagation depth, lead-time inflation |

### 1.3 Sequence-Dependent Setup Analysis

For each machine, I sort events by `Task Start` time to produce an ordered sequence of jobs. I extract the attribute vector (material type, geometry, tolerance class — typically derivable from the Job master data) of job *n* and *n1*. This yields a dataset:

$$\text{setup\_time}_n = f(\text{features}_{n-1}, \text{features}_n, \text{operator}, \text{machine})$$

Fitting a **regression model** (gradient-boosted trees, e.g., XGBoost) on this dataset yields a predictive setup-time model with feature importance that reveals which transitions drive setup cost. The empirical transition matrix $S_{ij}$ is the descriptive counterpart.

### 1.4 Disruption Impact

Use **change-point detection** on time series of queue length and throughput around `Breakdown Start`/`End` events. Quantify: cascade propagation (how many downstream machines see queue spikes), recovery time, and the tardiness delta for jobs active during the disruption versus comparable unaffected jobs (causal matching).

## 2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies

### 2.1 Bottleneck Identification

- **Active time vs. queue time ratio:** Machines where $\bar{W}_{queue} \gg \bar{T}_{processing}$ are demand bottlenecks.
- **Utilization ceiling analysis:** Machines consistently >85% utilized with growing queues are structural bottlenecks.
- **Throughput-based identification:** The machine with the smallest throughput rate in steady state constrains the whole system (Goldratt).

Process mining's **Performance Spectrum** (Denisov) is particularly powerful here: it visualizes all jobs simultaneously flowing between two activities and makes waiting-time accumulations visible as dense bands.

### 2.2 Poor Prioritization Evidence

Compute for each completed task the **slack at dispatch**: `slack = due_date  now  remaining_work`. If the empirical distribution shows high-priority/low-slack jobs routinely being dispatched *after* lower-priority jobs in the same queue, the FCFS/EDD mix is failing. **Variant analysis** between on-time and tardy cohorts, controlled by routing family, often reveals tardy jobs spent disproportionate time waiting in specific queues — a fingerprint of prioritization failure rather than capacity.

### 2.3 Suboptimal Sequencing

Compare **realized total setup time** on each machine to a counterfactual optimum obtained by solving a small TSP (using the $S_{ij}$ matrix) on the same job set. The gap quantifies sequencing waste. Typical findings: 15–30% of machine time on bottleneck resources is setup, of which a significant fraction is avoidable.

### 2.4 Starvation and Bullwhip

Resource timelines often reveal **synchronized idle periods** immediately downstream of bottleneck completions — evidence that the upstream bottleneck starves downstream machines. WIP variance across workstations over time, visualized as a sawtooth, indicates bullwhip. Compute the **coefficient of variation** of WIP per work center; amplification downstream of variability sources confirms the effect.

### 2.5 Evidence Layer

All pathologies are substantiated by:
- **Bottleneck analysis dashboards** (e.g., in Celonis, Disco, or PM4Py)
- **Variant analysis**: split log into on-time vs. tardy, compare activity durations and waiting times node-by-node with statistical tests (Mann–Whitney U)
- **Social network / handover analysis** for resource contention clusters

## 3. Root Cause Analysis

### 3.1 Root Causes

1. **Myopic dispatching:** FCFS and EDD are locally optimal but globally blind — they ignore downstream load, setup compatibility, and remaining critical-path time.
2. **No real-time visibility:** Operators make dispatch choices with no awareness of queue states elsewhere; the MES records events but does not feed back into decisions.
3. **Static, inaccurate time estimates:** Planned durations (column in the log) deviate systematically from actual — bias can be mined per activity/machine/operator. Planning systems using these estimates inherit the bias.
4. **Sequence-dependent setups treated as fixed:** Scheduling logic likely uses average setup time, not the transition-specific $S_{ij}$.
5. **No coordination mechanism:** No CONWIP, drum-buffer-rope, or kanban between work centers.
6. **Reactive disruption handling:** Breakdowns and hot jobs trigger ad-hoc re-sequencing with no systematic rescheduling policy.

### 3.2 Differentiating Scheduling Logic vs. Capacity vs. Variability

This is the critical diagnostic question. I use a three-test approach:

- **Capacity test:** Compute load ratio $\rho_m = \lambda_m \cdot \bar{p}_m / c_m$ per machine. If $\rho > 1$, no schedule can succeed — it is a capacity problem.
- **Variability test:** For machines with $\rho < 0.85$ but long queues, apply the Kingman formula:
  $$W_q \approx \left(\frac{\rho}{1-\rho}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{C_a^2 + C_s^2}{2}\right) \cdot \bar{p}$$
  If observed $W_q$ matches Kingman's prediction, the queue is driven by **inherent variability**. If it substantially exceeds prediction, **scheduling logic** is the culprit.
- **Counterfactual simulation:** Replay the arrival stream through a simulator with an optimal scheduler; the achievable improvement quantifies scheduling headroom versus capacity headroom.

## 4. Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies

### Strategy 1: Composite Dynamic Dispatching with Learned Weights

**Core logic.** At each machine's dispatch decision, compute a priority index for every queued job:

$$\pi_j = w_1 \cdot \frac{1}{\text{slack}_j^+} + w_2 \cdot \text{priority}_j - w_3 \cdot \hat{S}_{i \to j} + w_4 \cdot \text{DownstreamLoad}(j)^{-1} - w_5 \cdot \widehat{RPT}_j$$

where $\hat{S}_{i\to j}$ is the predicted setup time from current job *i* to candidate *j*, and $\widehat{RPT}_j$ is the expected remaining processing time over the critical path.

**How PM informs it.**
- The setup-time prediction model from §1.3 provides $\hat{S}_{i\to j}$.
- Historical duration distributions provide $\widehat{RPT}$ (not planned estimates).
- Variant analysis (§2.2) identifies which factors most discriminated on-time from tardy jobs — these guide initial weights.
- Weights $w_k$ are **calibrated via simulation optimization** (§5) and periodically re-learned using contextual bandits as the shop evolves.

**Addresses:** myopic dispatching, poor prioritization, sequence-dependent setup blindness, starvation (via downstream load term).

**Expected KPI impact:** 20–35% tardiness reduction, 10–20% setup-time reduction on bottlenecks.

### Strategy 2: Predictive Look-Ahead Scheduling with Uncertainty

**Core logic.** Build a rolling-horizon schedule (e.g., 24-hour window, re-optimized every 30 minutes or on trigger events) using a **stochastic flexible job-shop MILP or constraint-programming model** that consumes as inputs:
- Task duration distributions (not points): use P50 for objective, P85 for safety buffers.
- Machine availability forecasts including **predicted breakdown windows** from a survival model fitted on historical breakdown events (`Resource | Breakdown` event patterns  Weibull failure distribution per machine).
- Setup time predictions $\hat{S}_{i\to j}$.

Run two schedules: a **nominal schedule** (committed operationally) and a **Monte-Carlo replication schedule** that stress-tests against sampled breakdowns and duration noise. Report *completion-time confidence intervals* to customer-facing systems.

**How PM informs it.** Duration distributions are derived per (activity, machine, operator, job-complexity) stratum via event log analysis. Breakdown inter-arrival distributions come from resource-log mining. Routing probabilities (for jobs with variable routings) come from the discovered process model.

**Addresses:** inaccurate estimates, lack of visibility, reactive disruption handling. Enables realistic customer promises.

**Expected KPI impact:** 30–50% improvement in due-date prediction accuracy, 15–25% reduction in lead-time variance.

### Strategy 3: Setup-Aware Bottleneck Sequencing (Intelligent Batching)

**Core logic.** For the identified bottleneck machine(s), replace local dispatch with a **small-horizon sequencing optimizer** that solves an Asymmetric Traveling Salesman Problem with Due Dates (ATSPDD) over the current queue:

$$\min \sum_{n} \left[S_{f(n-1), f(n)} + p_n\right] + \lambda \sum_n \max(0, C_n - d_n)$$

where $f(n)$ is the job family in position *n*, $\lambda$ is the tardiness penalty. Solved via a metaheuristic (tabu search, large-neighborhood search) in seconds.

**Batching rule:** Form opportunistic batches of jobs from the same family *if and only if* doing so does not delay any job beyond its slack minus a safety margin (derived from observed setup-time distribution).

**How PM informs it.** The full setup transition matrix $S_{ij}$ and its confidence intervals, the family definitions (from trace clustering), and the slack distributions all come from the log. Historical analysis reveals which families have the largest asymmetries (e.g., changing from Material A  B is 3× costlier than B  A), which the sequencer exploits.

**Addresses:** wasted setup time, bottleneck throughput loss.

**Expected KPI impact:** 25–40% setup-time reduction on bottlenecks, 10–15% throughput increase, improved downstream flow.

### Integration

The three strategies are complementary: Strategy 2 produces the rolling plan; Strategy 3 governs bottleneck sequencing inside that plan; Strategy 1 handles real-time dispatch at non-bottleneck machines and when the plan is disrupted.

## 5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement

### 5.1 Discrete-Event Simulation Framework

Build a DES model (SimPy, AnyLogic, or Simio) whose parameters are **directly instantiated from process mining outputs**:

| Simulation Input | Source from PM |
|---|---|
| Job arrival process | Inter-arrival distribution from `Job Released` events |
| Routing per job family | Discovered process model + family classifier |
| Task duration distributions | Fitted distributions per (activity, machine, operator) |
| Setup time function | ML model from §1.3 |
| Breakdown process | Weibull fits per machine |
| Operator availability | Shift patterns from log |

**Validation:** Run the simulator under the *baseline* scheduling logic and statistically compare output KPIs (lead time, tardiness, utilization) to observed log KPIs. Acceptable deviation: <5% on primary KPIs. Use K–S tests on flow-time distributions.

### 5.2 Experimental Design

Test each strategy (and combinations) across scenarios:

1. **Normal load** — reproduce a typical month.
2. **High load (110% of historical arrivals)** — stress test.
3. **High disruption** — double breakdown frequency.
4. **Hot-job burst** — inject frequent priority changes.
5. **Product-mix shift** — change routing-family distribution.
6. **Operator absence** — reduced labor capacity.

For each scenario, run 30+ replications per strategy, compare via paired-difference tests on weighted tardiness, mean WIP, and utilization. Visualize via **performance-frontier plots** (tardiness vs. WIP) to reveal Pareto-dominant strategies.

### 5.3 Continuous Monitoring and Adaptation

Deploy the chosen strategy with a closed-loop governance layer:

- **Live PM dashboard** refreshing hourly on the MES event stream: tardiness rate, bottleneck queue depth, setup-time ratio, schedule-adherence index.
- **Concept-drift detection:** Apply statistical drift tests (Page-Hinkley, ADWIN) on setup-time predictions, task-duration distributions, and routing probabilities. Triggering drift  re-train models.
- **Conformance checking:** Continuously compare executed schedules against the planned schedule. Large conformance gaps trigger root-cause drill-downs (new process variant? new product? operator change?).
- **A/B / champion-challenger:** When a new strategy variant is proposed, shadow-run it (decisions logged but not executed) alongside the production scheduler; promote if it outperforms on live data for a validated period.
- **Quarterly re-calibration:** Re-run the full simulation-optimization to update dispatching weights, setup matrices, and duration distributions.

### 5.4 Governance

A formal review cadence — Operations, Scheduling Analyst, Data Engineering — examines the dashboard monthly, reviews drift alerts weekly, and authorizes model updates. This ensures the system remains adaptive rather than degrading into another static rule set.

---

### Summary

Process mining transforms the raw MES log from a passive record into the foundation of a predictive, adaptive scheduling system. By quantifying pathologies (bottlenecks, setup waste, prioritization failures), distinguishing scheduling problems from capacity/variability problems, and feeding rigorously validated models into dynamic dispatching, rolling-horizon optimization, and setup-aware bottleneck sequencing — all validated in simulation before deployment and continuously monitored for drift — Precision Parts Inc. can realistically target 25–40% tardiness reduction, 20–30% WIP reduction, and materially more reliable customer due-date commitments.
