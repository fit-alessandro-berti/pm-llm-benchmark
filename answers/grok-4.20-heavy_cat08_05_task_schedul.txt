**1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics**

Process mining begins with converting the MES event logs into a standardized event log format (e.g., XES or CSV with columns for case ID, activity, timestamp, resource, and attributes like priority, due date, planned/actual durations, and notes). Cases are defined primarily by Job ID for end-to-end flows, with secondary projections onto individual resources (machines) to analyze local execution sequences.

**Process discovery** techniques such as the Inductive Miner (for sound, block-structured models) or Heuristics Miner (robust to noise in real manufacturing data) are applied to reconstruct the *actual* job flows, revealing common and exceptional routings (variants) across operations like Cutting  Milling  Lathing, including loops for rework or quality failures. For machine-specific sequences, the log is filtered by Resource ID, sorted by timestamp, and mined to show task execution order and transitions. Dotted charts visualize the temporal dynamics of job arrivals, processing, and disruptions across the shop floor.

Specific metrics are derived as follows:

- **Job flow times, lead times, and makespan distributions**: Throughput time is calculated as the difference between final completion event and "Job Released" timestamp per case. Distributions (mean, median, 90th percentile, histograms) are generated via performance analysis plugins. Lead time includes waiting and transport; makespan can be analyzed for groups of related jobs.
- **Task waiting times**: Identified by subtracting "Queue Entry" timestamp from "Task Start" (or Setup Start). Aggregated per work center/machine with statistics and visualizations (e.g., heatmaps or performance spectrum) to highlight queue hotspots.
- **Resource utilization**: For each machine/operator, productive time is summed from Setup Start/End and Task Start/End intervals. Idle time is derived from gaps between consecutive tasks on the same resource (accounting for shifts). Utilization = (setup + processing time) / available time. Setup time proportion is isolated separately.
- **Sequence-dependent setup times**: Filter and sort events per machine. For each pair of consecutive jobs, extract attributes of the prior job (material, dimensions, or previous Job ID linked to characteristics) and the current one, then correlate with actual setup duration (Setup End – Setup Start). Decision trees, regression models, or clustering on job features quantify how specific transitions (e.g., steel-to-aluminum vs. same-material) affect setup duration, producing a data-driven setup time matrix per machine.
- **Schedule adherence and tardiness**: Completion timestamp vs. Order Due Date yields tardiness = max(0, completion – due date). Metrics include % tardy jobs, mean/median tardiness, and distribution by priority. Conformance checking compares actual execution against any planned model or due-date constraints.
- **Impact of disruptions**: Filter "Resource" events for breakdowns or "Job" events for priority changes. Analyze affected cases (those in queue or processing near the disruption timestamp) for delta in flow times, waiting times, and tardiness compared to baseline periods using statistical tests or variant analysis before/after the event.

These analyses provide a factual, quantitative baseline of current performance directly from the year-long logs.

**2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies**

Process mining reveals several interconnected pathologies stemming from local dispatching rules:

- **Bottleneck resources**: Machines with highest average queue waiting times, highest utilization (>85-90%), or largest contribution to overall lead time are flagged via bottleneck analysis plugins. Their impact is quantified by how much they inflate end-to-end flow times or cause downstream starvation (measured via correlated idle times on successor resources).
- **Poor task prioritization**: Variant analysis compares process variants and performance of on-time vs. late jobs, and high- vs. low-priority jobs. Evidence includes high-priority or near-due-date jobs experiencing similar or higher waiting times than others, indicating FCFS/EDD rules are overridden by local decisions or not enforced globally.
- **Suboptimal sequencing increasing setup times**: Reconstruction of per-machine sequences shows frequent high-setup transitions. Total observed setup time is compared against a theoretical minimum (obtained by clustering jobs on setup-similarity features and solving as a traveling salesman problem or using batching). High variance in setup times for similar job pairs indicates poor sequencing.
- **Starvation of downstream resources**: Idle time analysis correlated with upstream bottlenecks or disruptions shows periods where flexible machines are starved while WIP piles up elsewhere.
- **Bullwhip effect in WIP**: Time-series analysis of queue lengths and WIP levels (via case counting in active states) reveals high variability and amplification of waves, visible in dotted charts or performance dashboards.

Techniques providing evidence include: bottleneck analysis, comparative process mining (on-time vs. tardy variants), decision mining on what influences dispatch choices, and root-cause analysis linking attributes (priority, disruption flags) to performance outcomes. These directly tie inefficiencies to the lack of holistic, sequence-aware scheduling.

**3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness**

The primary root causes are the limitations of static, local dispatching rules (FCFS mixed with EDD) in a high-variety, dynamic environment with sequence-dependent setups, resource contention, and frequent disruptions. These rules ignore global shop status, downstream load, and setup implications, leading to myopic decisions.

Other causes include:
- Lack of real-time visibility into queue lengths, machine availability, and job progress across the shop.
- Inaccurate or absent use of task duration and setup time estimations (evident when planned vs. actual durations diverge systematically).
- Ineffective handling of sequence-dependent setups, as local rules do not look ahead or group similar jobs.
- Poor inter-work-center coordination, causing upstream decisions to create downstream imbalances.
- Ad-hoc responses to breakdowns and hot jobs that further destabilize the system without predictive buffering.

Process mining differentiates between poor scheduling logic and inherent issues (capacity limits or process variability) through several methods: conformance checking shows deviations from any "ideal" plan due to local rules; performance analysis correlated with attributes reveals whether high waiting times occur even at moderate utilization (pointing to logic/scheduling) versus consistent overload (capacity). Variant analysis and decision mining can quantify how much performance variance is explained by scheduling choices versus job complexity or disruptions. If utilization is below 100% yet tardiness and WIP are high with high setup waste, the evidence points primarily to suboptimal logic rather than pure capacity shortages.

**4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies**

**Strategy 1: Enhanced Multi-Factor Dynamic Dispatching Rules**  
Core logic: At each work center, when a machine becomes free, score all jobs in the queue using a weighted composite rule: score = w1 × (due date slack / remaining processing time) + w2 × (priority factor) + w3 × (-estimated setup time with previous job) + w4 × (downstream load penalty). Select the highest-scoring job. Weights are derived via regression or simulation optimization on historical data.  
Process mining informs it by providing the setup time prediction model (from sequence analysis), bottleneck identification (to increase weight on downstream load for critical machines), and performance factors that most differentiate on-time vs. late jobs.  
It addresses poor prioritization, suboptimal sequencing, and lack of global view. Expected impact: reduced tardiness (by protecting due dates and setups), lower WIP (faster flow), and better utilization at bottlenecks.

**Strategy 2: Predictive Scheduling with Stochastic and ML Models**  
Core logic: Mine empirical distributions and predictive models for task durations (using features like job complexity, operator ID, material) and breakdown probabilities from logs. Generate proactive schedules (e.g., using stochastic programming, simulation-based scheduling, or reinforcement learning policies) that incorporate uncertainty and predict bottlenecks. Re-schedule periodically or upon events (breakdowns, hot jobs) with rolling horizons.  
Process mining supplies the distributions, routing probabilities, setup models, and disruption frequencies. Predictive maintenance insights can be derived by correlating machine event patterns with breakdowns.  
It addresses inaccurate estimations, unpredictable lead times, and disruption handling. Expected impact: more realistic due date promises, proactive bottleneck avoidance, reduced tardiness under variability, and lower overall lead times.

**Strategy 3: Setup Time Optimization via Intelligent Sequencing and Batching**  
Core logic: At bottleneck machines, cluster jobs in the queue (or incoming) based on setup-similarity features mined from historical data. Use optimized sequencing (e.g., genetic algorithm or heuristic for sequence-dependent setup job shop) to minimize total setup within a time window, while still respecting due dates and priorities (via multi-objective optimization). Non-bottleneck machines use simpler rules.  
Process mining directly informs the similarity model and setup time matrix. It targets the specific pathology of excessive setup waste identified in sequence analysis.  
Expected impact: significant reduction in total setup time (10-30% possible), increased effective capacity at bottlenecks, lower lead times, and improved predictability.

These strategies are data-driven, moving from static local rules to dynamic, predictive, and optimization-based approaches.

**5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement**

**Discrete-event simulation (DES)** (implemented in tools like Python SimPy, AnyLogic, or Arena) is parameterized directly from process mining outputs: empirical distributions for inter-arrival times, task durations (planned vs. actual to capture variability), routing probabilities from discovered models, setup time matrices, breakdown frequencies and repair times, and operator availability. The baseline (current dispatching rules) is implemented first and validated against historical KPIs for fidelity.

Proposed strategies are then coded as alternative dispatching/sequencing policies within the simulator. Experiments compare them across scenarios: baseline load, high-load (increased order arrival rate), high-disruption (increased breakdown frequency or hot job rate), and high-variability (wider duration distributions). Key performance indicators measured include mean/max tardiness, % tardy jobs, average WIP, mean lead time, machine utilization, and total setup time. Statistical analysis (multiple replications, confidence intervals) ensures rigorous comparison before any live deployment.

For **continuous monitoring and adaptation**: Implement an ongoing process mining pipeline on streaming MES data (using tools supporting streaming PM). Track the same KPIs in dashboards (tardiness distributions, waiting time trends, utilization, setup waste). Use concept drift detection algorithms to automatically identify shifts in process behavior, routing, or performance (e.g., new bottleneck emerging or setup patterns changing due to new materials). When drift is detected or KPIs degrade beyond thresholds, trigger re-analysis of recent logs to update models (setup matrices, duration predictors, weights in dispatching rules). This creates a closed-loop system where insights continuously refine the scheduling logic.

This integrated approach—deep analysis via process mining, insight-driven strategy design, simulation-based validation, and continuous monitoring—offers a sophisticated path to substantially reduce tardiness, WIP, and lead time variability while improving resource efficiency in Precision Parts Inc.'s complex job shop environment. Implementation should start with pilot application on the most critical bottleneck work centers.
