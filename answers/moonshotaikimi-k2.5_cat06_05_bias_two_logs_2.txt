This creates **indirect discrimination** where a seemingly neutral criterion (association membership) disproportionately disadvantages the protected group.

## Implications for Fairness and Equity

### **Procedural Unfairness**
The process violates **procedural justice** by introducing arbitrary, non-job-related criteria after assessment. Cultural fit should measure alignment with organizational values, not social network membership. The adjustment is applied opaquely during the `CulturalFitCheck` activity by the HR Analyst, suggesting institutionalized rather than random bias.

### **Disparate Impact**
While the policy appears facially neutral (anyone *could* join the association), it creates **disparate impact** against Group A. Since Group A members are non-local residents, they face structural barriers (geographic distance, lack of local social capital, potential exclusionary membership practices) that prevent them from obtaining the association membership required for the boost.

### **Erosion of Equity**
The practice confers **unearned advantage** to candidates with pre-existing privilege. Professional association membership often correlates with socioeconomic status, local social networks, and demographic homogeneity. By embedding this advantage into the scoring algorithm, the organization perpetuates existing inequalities rather than providing equitable opportunity based on qualifications.

### **Legal and Reputational Risk**
This practice exposes the organization to significant legal liability under anti-discrimination frameworks (such as Title VII in the US or the Equality Act in the UK), as residency status and professional network access often correlate with protected characteristics like national origin, race, or socioeconomic background. Furthermore, it risks creating a homogenous workforce that lacks diversity of thought and experience.
