Based on the comparison of the two event logs, **Group B (Unprotected Group)** exhibits the most visible and active manifestation of algorithmic bias. While both groups follow a similar workflow, Group B demonstrates preferential treatment through automated score adjustments that correlate with community membership, creating a disparate impact on final decisions compared to Group A.

Here is the detailed analysis of how this bias manifests:

### 1. The Mechanism of Bias: `ScoreAdjustment` and `CommunityGroup`
The primary driver of bias in this system is the **`ScoreAdjustment`** column, which is conditional on the **`CommunityGroup`** attribute.

*   **Group A (Protected Group):** The `ScoreAdjustment` column consistently shows `N/A` or `0`. There is no mechanism to increase a user's score based on external factors.
*   **Group B (Unprotected Group):** When a user belongs to a specific community (`Highland Civic Darts Club`), the system applies a **+10 (Community Boost)**. This adjustment is applied at the `PreliminaryScoring`, `ManualReview`, and `FinalDecision` stages for those affiliated members.

**Analysis:** The bias manifests as an **Affinity Bias**. The system grants higher credit to individuals based on their association with a specific social group (`Highland Civic Darts Club`) rather than solely on their objective performance metrics (the raw score). Group A members are denied this "community lift," effectively placing them at a disadvantage compared to their counterparts in Group B.

### 2. Disparate Impact: Case Comparisons
The most compelling evidence of bias is found when comparing the final decisions and scores of similar cases across both logs. The system demonstrates that a lower score in Group B can result in approval, while a higher score in Group A results in rejection.

### 3. Influence of Attributes on Systemic Decisions
The bias creates a systemic difference in how decisions are made based on the attributes provided:

*   **`LocalResident`:** While this attribute divides the groups (False vs. True), it does not directly trigger the adjustment. Instead, it serves as a proxy for accessing the `CommunityGroup`.
*   **`CommunityGroup`:** This is the discriminator. In Group A, this column is always `None`. In Group B, it triggers the logic that modifies the score. The bias lies in the fact that belonging to "Highland Civic Darts Club" acts as a privilege modifier, whereas no such club exists for Group A members.
*   **`ScoreAdjustment`:** This column reveals the algorithmic intent. It explicitly adds value based on group identity rather than merit. In the `ManualReview` stage alone, U003 receives an adjustment that P002 does not receive, even though P002 had a higher raw score.

### 4. Conclusion
**Group B exhibits bias in favor of community affiliation.**

The system is not neutral; it actively rewards Group B members who belong to the "Highland Civic Darts Club" by inflating their scores (up to +10 points). This leads to **disparate impact**, where a user with a raw score of 695 (U003) is approved, while a user with a higher raw score of 710 (P002) is rejected.

**Recommendation:** To mitigate this bias, the `ScoreAdjustment` logic should be decoupled from the `CommunityGroup` attribute or standardized across all groups to ensure that approval decisions are based on the objective `PreliminaryScore` rather than external community affiliations.
