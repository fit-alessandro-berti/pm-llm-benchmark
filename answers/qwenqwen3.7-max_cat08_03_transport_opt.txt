As a Process Mining Consultant specializing in logistics, I approach Speedy Parcels' challenges by treating their delivery network not just as a physical operation, but as a complex, data-generating information system. By applying process mining to the integrated event logs, we can transition from reactive problem-solving to proactive, data-driven optimization.

Here is the comprehensive approach to analyzing and optimizing Speedy Parcels' last-mile delivery operations.

---

### 1. Process Discovery and Conformance Checking

#### Data Preprocessing and Integration
The primary challenge in logistics process mining is **data granularity and object multiplicity**. GPS data is high-frequency and continuous, while scanner data is discrete and milestone-based. Furthermore, we have multiple "objects" interacting: Vehicles, Drivers, and Packages.

*   **Integration Strategy:** I would implement an **Object-Centric Process Mining (OCPM)** approach. Instead of forcing a single Case ID, we will model three interconnected case notions: `Vehicle-Day` (for fleet/route analysis), `Package` (for parcel journey analysis), and `Driver-Shift`. 
*   **Data Cleaning:** 
    *   *GPS Data:* Filter out "micro-movements" (GPS drift while parked) and aggregate continuous GPS pings into discrete events (e.g., "Start Travel", "Stop Travel", "Idle > 5 mins").
    *   *Timestamps:* Synchronize all system clocks to a single time zone and handle missing scanner scans by inferring them from GPS geofences (e.g., if GPS shows the van at a customer location for 5 minutes but no scan occurred, flag as "Missing Scan").
*   **Event Log Construction:** Create a unified log where each row represents an event linked to the relevant object IDs (Vehicle, Package, Driver), complete with attributes like location, planned vs. actual times, and vehicle telemetry.

#### Process Discovery
Using algorithms like the **Inductive Miner** (which handles noise and loops well, crucial for delivery routes with multiple stops), I would generate a **Directly-Follows Graph (DFG)** and a **Petri Net** for the `Vehicle-Day` case.
*   **Visualization:** The model will visualize the "Happy Path" (Depot Departure $\rightarrow$ Travel $\rightarrow$ Arrive Customer $\rightarrow$ Deliver $\rightarrow$ Depart $\rightarrow$ ... $\rightarrow$ Depot Return). 
*   **Deviations:** It will explicitly map out loops and deviations, such as "Delivery Failed $\rightarrow$ Travel $\rightarrow$ Next Customer" or "Travel $\rightarrow$ Unscheduled Stop (Maintenance) $\rightarrow$ Travel".

#### Conformance Checking
I would use **Alignments** (the most accurate conformance technique) to compare the actual event log against the *planned* routes from the Dispatch System.
*   **Sequence Deviations:** Did the driver skip a stop? Did they reorder the stops (e.g., doing Stop 5 before Stop 3)? 
*   **Timing Deviations:** Compare planned arrival times with actual "Arrive Customer" timestamps to identify time-window violations.
*   **Resource/Activity Deviations:** Identify unplanned activities (e.g., "Unscheduled Stop", "Low Speed Detected" for extended periods) that do not exist in the planned dispatch model.

### 2. Performance Analysis and Bottleneck Identification

#### Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)
KPIs will be calculated directly from the time and attributes within the event log:
*   **On-Time Delivery (OTD) Rate:** % of "Delivery Success" events occurring within the customer's requested time window (from Dispatch data).
*   **Stop Service Time (Dwell Time):** `Depart Customer` timestamp minus `Arrive Customer` timestamp.
*   **Travel Time vs. Service Time Ratio:** Total time in "Moving" status vs. total time in "Service/Idle at stop" status.
*   **Failed Delivery Rate:** Count of "Delivery Failed" events divided by total attempted deliveries.
*   **Vehicle Utilization Rate:** Time spent "Moving" divided by total "Shift Time" (`End Shift` - `Start Shift`).
*   **Fuel Efficiency Proxy:** Total distance traveled (calculated from GPS coordinates) divided by successful deliveries, penalized by time spent "Idling" or in "Low Speed" (traffic).

#### Bottleneck Identification Techniques
*   **Performance Overlay:** I will overlay the DFG with performance metrics, coloring activities and transitions based on average duration. This instantly highlights if bottlenecks are in *travel* (red transitions between stops) or *service* (red nodes at "Arrive Customer").
*   **Geospatial Process Mining:** By mapping "Arrive Customer" to "Delivery Success" dwell times onto a city map, we can identify geographic bottlenecks. For example, specific high-density apartment complexes or downtown zones with severe parking shortages will show up as "hotspots" for high service times.
*   **Animation:** Using token animation, we can visualize the flow of vehicles over the day to see where "traffic jams" of tokens occur (e.g., all vehicles bottlenecking at the depot loading bay between 07:00 and 08:00).

### 3. Root Cause Analysis for Inefficiencies

Identifying *where* a delay happens is only half the battle; we must understand *why*. 

*   **Suboptimal Routing & Travel Time Estimation:** If conformance checking shows high sequence deviations (drivers reordering stops), it indicates the dispatch system's static routing is illogical or its travel time estimates are wrong. *Analysis:* Compare planned travel times vs. actual travel times between specific coordinate pairs to calibrate the routing engine.
*   **Traffic Congestion vs. Driver Behavior:** "Low Speed Detected" could be traffic or a distracted driver. *Analysis:* Correlate GPS speed data with external historical traffic APIs and time-of-day. If low speed happens at 08:30 on a main artery, it's traffic. If it happens at 14:00 on an empty road, it's driver behavior.
*   **Service Time Variability:** High dwell times at customer locations. *Analysis:* Use **Decision Mining** (classification) to predict high service times based on attributes: package weight, customer type (B2B vs B2C), building type, and driver experience. 
*   **Failed Deliveries:** *Analysis:* Perform **Variant Analysis** on failed deliveries. Are they clustered in specific time windows (e.g., 09:00-11:00 when people are at work)? Are they assigned to specific drivers who don't call ahead?
*   **Vehicle Breakdowns:** *Analysis:* Correlate "Unscheduled Stops" and "Engine Warning" events with vehicle age, mileage, and time since last "Scheduled Maintenance" to identify if maintenance intervals are too sparse.

### 4. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

Based on the anticipated insights, I propose the following three concrete strategies:

#### Strategy 1: Dynamic, AI-Enhanced Micro-Routing and Time Window Management
*   **Target Inefficiency:** Late deliveries, high travel times, and high sequence deviations (drivers ignoring planned routes).
*   **Root Cause:** Static routing algorithms that use theoretical travel times and fail to account for real-world micro-conditions (parking, walking time, real-time traffic).
*   **Process Mining Support:** Geospatial process mining reveals actual "parking-to-door" walk times. Conformance checking proves drivers are manually reordering stops to be more efficient.
*   **Action:** Feed the actual, empirically derived travel and service times from the process mining models back into the Dispatch System's routing engine. Implement dynamic routing that adjusts in real-time based on live GPS traffic data. Introduce "soft" time windows with automated SMS customer updates if a delay is predicted.
*   **Expected Impact:** Increase in OTD rate by 15-20%, reduction in total distance traveled (fuel savings), and decreased driver frustration.

#### Strategy 2: Geospatial Service-Time Profiling and "Drop-Point" Optimization
*   **Target Inefficiency:** High and highly variable Stop Service Times (Dwell Time), leading to driver overtime and reduced vehicle utilization.
*   **Root Cause:** Drivers spending excessive time searching for parking in dense urban areas or navigating large apartment complexes.
*   **Process Mining Support:** Performance overlays and geospatial mapping highlight specific urban zones where the "Arrive Customer" to "Delivery Success" transition takes >10 minutes (compared to the 3-minute average in suburbs).
*   **Action:** For identified high-dwell-time urban hotspots, shift from door-to-door delivery to centralized "Drop-Points" (parcel lockers or local retail partners). For remaining door-to-door deliveries in these zones, update the routing algorithm to allocate a realistic 10-minute service time buffer, preventing cascading delays for subsequent stops.
*   **Expected Impact:** Reduction in average stop service time in urban zones by 40%, reduction in driver overtime costs, and more predictable route completion times.

#### Strategy 3: Predictive Maintenance and Fleet Health Triage
*   **Target Inefficiency:** Unscheduled vehicle breakdowns, high emergency repair costs, and disrupted delivery routes.
*   **Root Cause:** Reactive maintenance schedules based purely on calendar time or basic mileage, ignoring actual vehicle wear-and-tear (e.g., frequent idling, stop-and-go urban driving).
*   **Process Mining Support:** Analysis of "Unscheduled Stop" and "Low Speed/High RPM" (engine strain) events correlated with vehicle IDs and maintenance logs reveals that vehicles doing dense urban routes break down 30% faster than those on suburban highway routes, despite similar mileage.
*   **Action:** Transition to a predictive maintenance model. Calculate an "Engine Strain Score" for each vehicle based on GPS idling time, stop-start frequency, and speed variance. Trigger maintenance alerts based on this strain score rather than just odometer readings.
*   **Expected Impact:** 25% reduction in unscheduled breakdowns, extended vehicle lifespan, lower emergency towing/repair costs, and higher overall fleet availability.

### 5. Considering Operational Constraints and Monitoring

#### Accounting for Operational Constraints
Optimization cannot exist in a vacuum; it must respect hard and soft constraints:
*   **Driver Working Hours:** The dynamic routing engine (Strategy 1) must have hard constraints programmed for legal driving limits (e.g., max 9 hours driving, mandatory breaks). The process mining model will monitor `End Shift` - `Start Shift` to ensure compliance.
*   **Vehicle Capacities:** Dispatch integration must ensure that volumetric and weight constraints are strictly enforced before routes are finalized.
*   **Customer Time Windows:** Time windows will be treated as hard constraints for premium B2B clients, but as soft constraints (with automated customer communication) for standard B2C deliveries to allow the algorithm flexibility to optimize fuel and time.

#### Continuous Monitoring Plan
To ensure sustainability, Speedy Parcels will implement a **Process Mining Center of Excellence (CoE)** dashboarding strategy:

1.  **Operational Dashboard (Real-Time):** 
    *   *Metrics:* Live vehicle locations, current OTD probability, active alerts for "Unscheduled Stops" or "Idle > 15 mins".
    *   *Use:* Dispatchers use this for immediate, intra-day interventions (e.g., rerouting a driver who is stuck in an accident).
2.  **Tactical PM Dashboard (Weekly/Monthly):**
    *   *Metrics:* Conformance fitness scores (are drivers following the new optimized routes?), average service time per zone, failed delivery rates, and predictive maintenance alerts.
    *   *Views:* Live DFGs filtered by depot or vehicle type; variant analysis comparing top 10% vs bottom 10% performing drivers.
    *   *Use:* Fleet managers use this to identify training needs, adjust routing parameters, and schedule maintenance.
3.  **Feedback Loop:** 
    *   Every month, the empirical data from the tactical dashboard is fed back into the Dispatch System's machine learning models to continuously refine travel time and service time estimations, ensuring the system learns and adapts to seasonal changes (e.g., holiday rush, winter weather).
