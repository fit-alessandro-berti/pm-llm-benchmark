**Analysis of Cases with Performance Issues (Long Durations)**

I calculated approximate end-to-end durations (Submit Claim  Close Claim) for all cases:

- **Case 2001**: ~1.5 hours (Low complexity, Region A)
- **Case 2002**: ~26 hours (Medium complexity, Region B)
- **Case 2003**: ~48.3 hours (High complexity, Region A)
- **Case 2004**: ~1.4 hours (Low complexity, Region B)
- **Case 2005**: ~77 hours (High complexity, Region B)

**Cases with significant performance issues**: 2002, 2003, and especially 2005. Low-complexity cases (2001, 2004) complete quickly on the same day with no delays. Medium and High complexity cases take 1–3+ days.

**Key correlations with attributes (Resource, Region, Complexity)**

- **Complexity is the strongest driver**: 
  - Low complexity: 0 "Request Additional Documents" activities  same-day completion.
  - Medium (2002): 1 request  overnight/multi-day delay.
  - High (2003 & 2005): 2–3 repeated requests  multi-day spans with long waiting periods between requests and approval.
  - High-complexity claims clearly trigger iterative back-and-forth with the claimant, creating cumulative delays (waiting for document submission).

- **Region**: Mixed but secondary signal. Both regions handle low-complexity claims quickly. Region B's high-complexity case (2005) took notably longer than Region A's (2003), and Region B had more activity on April 1 (three cases started). This may indicate higher workload or slightly slower processing in B, but the sample is small.

- **Resources**: Notable patterns in high/medium cases.
  - Adjuster_Lisa (Region B) handled three overlapping claims on April 1 (2002, 2004, 2005) and performed all the repeated document requests in B. This suggests possible overload, leading to spaced-out follow-ups.
  - Adjuster_Mike (Region A) handled two claims but with fewer overlaps.
  - Approvals for high-complexity cases used Manager_Bill (later timestamps: 16:00 on day 2 or 10:00 on day 4), while Manager_Ann handled quicker low/medium approvals. This points to Bill possibly being a bottleneck or high-complexity approvals simply requiring more review time.
  - Finance and CSR resources show less clear impact.

**Explanations for why these attributes contribute to performance issues**

Higher Complexity naturally requires more verification, but the process design (sequential "Request Additional Documents" activities) turns this into repeated cycles of waiting for customer responses, rather than collecting everything upfront. This is the primary root cause of long lead times. Resource contention amplifies it—e.g., one adjuster juggling multiple claims may not follow up aggressively, and specialized approvers (like Manager_Bill) for complex cases introduce further delays. Region B's slightly worse outcomes for high-complexity claims likely stem from workload imbalance (more concurrent cases for Lisa) rather than inherent regional differences.

**Suggestions to mitigate these root causes**

- **Address Complexity**: Improve the "Evaluate Claim" step with standardized checklists or decision-support tools to identify *all* required documents upfront for High/Medium claims. This reduces iterations. Consider enhanced customer portals or automated reminders for faster document submission.
- **Resource balancing and specialization**: Distribute workload more evenly (avoid overloading single adjusters like Lisa on busy days). Create dedicated high-complexity teams or fast-track approvers to reduce bottlenecks with Manager_Bill. Monitor resource utilization by region.
- **Process improvements**: Explore parallelizing document requests or adding SLA timers on responses. Use process mining on larger logs to confirm these patterns statistically. Pilot better upfront guidance during "Submit Claim" (e.g., complexity-based document checklists) to prevent downstream requests.
- **Further analysis**: With only five cases, these are strong hypotheses. On a full event log, calculate exact waiting times between activities, aggregate by attribute (e.g., average duration per Complexity/Region/Resource), and look for bottlenecks using throughput or bottleneck analysis.

This points to a combination of inherent claim complexity and how the process/resources handle it, rather than one single factor. The main opportunity is reducing document-request loops for complex claims.
