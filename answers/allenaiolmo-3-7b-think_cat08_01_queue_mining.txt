### **1. Queue Identification and Characterization**  
**Queue Identification and Metrics:**  
- **Waiting Time Calculation:** For each patient, compute the time between the **completion** of one activity and the **start** of the next consecutive activity. For example, the wait time between *Registration* (completed at 09:08:45) and *Nurse Assessment* (started at 09:15:20) is **6 minutes**.  
- **Key Metrics:**  
  - **Average Waiting Time:** Overall mean delay across all queues.  
  - **Median Waiting Time:** Identifies the "typical" wait experienced by half the patients.  
  - **90th Percentile Waiting Time:** Highlights the worst-case delay (critical for prioritizing urgent issues).  
  - **Queue Frequency:** Number of patients experiencing waits exceeding a threshold (e.g., >10 minutes).  
  - **Max Waiting Time:** Identifies extreme outliers (e.g., a 45-minute wait due to a delayed blood test).  

**Critical Queues Identification:**  
- Prioritize queues with the **highest average waiting time**, especially those involving **high-priority patients** (e.g., urgent cases or follow-ups).  
- Use **patient type** as a filter: For instance, if 40% of urgent patients wait >15 minutes for nurse assessments, this queue is a top priority.  
- **Justification:** Queues with long tails (high percentiles) or frequent occurrences of extended waits are most impactful on patient satisfaction and operational efficiency.

### **2. Root Cause Analysis**  
**Potential Root Causes:**  
1. **Resource Bottlenecks:**  
   - **Analysis:** Use **resource consumption analysis** to track staff/room utilization. For example, if Nurse 1 is assigned to 3 patients in 1 hour (vs. a target of 1.5), this indicates overloading.  
   - **Process Mining Tools:** Gantt charts or resource timeline visualizations can expose overbooked resources.  

2. **Activity Dependencies & Handovers:**  
   - **Analysis:** Identify delays in handovers between activities (e.g., nurse-to-doctor transitions). If nurse assessments often finish late, it delays subsequent steps like doctor consultations.  
   - **Technique:** **Dependency analysis** to map activity sequences and detect delays in prerequisite completion.  

3. **Variability in Service Times:**  
   - **Analysis:** Use **variant analysis** to identify activities with high variability (e.g., blood tests taking 10–30 minutes). Unpredictable durations create bottlenecks downstream.  
   - **Example:** If ECG tests have a 20-minute average but 10-minute standard deviation, some patients experience delays due to extended testing.  

4. **Scheduling Policies:**  
   - **Analysis:** Correlate appointment start times with activity durations. For instance, if appointments are scheduled every 15 minutes but a nurse assessment takes 20 minutes, this causes cascading waits.  
   - **Data:** Compare scheduled vs. actual activity durations to identify mismatches.  

5. **Patient Type & Urgency:**  
   - **Analysis:** Segment data by patient type (e.g., new vs. follow-up) and urgency. If urgent patients are queued behind non-urgent ones, this violates fairness and efficiency.

### **4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints**  
- **Cost vs. Efficiency:** Dynamic resource allocation may increase short-term costs but reduce long-term labor costs. Prioritize strategies with the best cost-benefit ratio (e.g., parallelization is low-cost).  
- **Staff Workload:** Redesigning workflows may redistribute tasks (e.g., nurses handling registration) but could strain staff if not balanced. Use workload analysis to ensure fairness.  
- **Care Quality:** Avoid over-scheduling (e.g., compressing time between activities) that risks rushing care. Monitor KPIs like "time spent per activity" to prevent compromising quality.
