### 1. Identification of Performance Issues (Cycle Time Analysis)

By calculating the total cycle time (time from "Receive Ticket" to "Close Ticket") for each case, we can clearly identify which tickets suffered from significant delays.

**Conclusion:** While Cases 101 and 103 were resolved within a standard workday window (indicative of "Happy Path" performance), **Cases 102, 104, and 105** represent significant performance outliers. Specifically, **Case 105** took nearly two full days to resolve, indicating a severe bottleneck.

---

### 2. Root Cause Analysis

Upon examining the timestamps between activities (the "soak time" or waiting time), three primary factors emerge as drivers for these delays:

#### A. The "Level-2 Escalation" Bottleneck (Primary Cause)
The strongest correlation with delay exists in cases involving escalations.
*   **Case 105 (The Critical Example):** After the ticket was escalated at **10:00 on Day 1**, it sat idle for **28 hours** before a Level-2 Agent began investigating it at **14:00 on Day 2**.
*   **Implication:** There appears to be a massive backlog, lack of visibility, or resource shortage within the Level-2 support team. Tickets entering this queue essentially stop moving for over a day.

#### B. Excessive Pre-Investigation Waiting Times
Even before investigation begins, tickets are experiencing long idle periods.
*   **Case 104:** After being assigned to an agent at **09:30**, the ticket waited **3.5 hours** (until **13:00**) for the investigation to begin.
*   **Case 102:** There was a **2.5-hour** gap between assignment and escalation/investigation.
*   **Implication:** Level-1 agents may be overloaded, or there is no immediate "pickup" protocol when a ticket enters their queue.

#### C. Extended Investigation Durations
Once the investigation phase begins in delayed cases, the active work time often spans overnight periods or multiple days.
*   **Case 102:** Investigation ran from **14:00 Day 1** to **09:00 Day 2** (19 hours).
*   **Case 104:** Investigation ran from **13:00 Day 1** to **08:00 Day 2** (19 hours).
*   **Implication:** These issues are likely complex, requiring external information, or are being paused at the end of the workday and resumed the next morning, adding "idle overnight" time to the total clock time.

---

### 3. Insights and Recommendations

Based on these factors, here is how the delays are occurring and how to fix them:

**The "Why":**
The process is failing primarily during **handovers**. The standard flow (Receive $\rightarrow$ Triage $\rightarrow$ Assign $\rightarrow$ Investigate $\rightarrow$ Resolve) works well when handled by a single agent (as seen in Cases 101 & 103). However, once a ticket requires **Escalation (to Level-2)** or sits in a queue too long **(Pre-Investigation)**, the cycle time explodes due to lack of urgency or availability in the subsequent team.

**Recommendations:**

1.  **Implement SLA for Level-2 Acknowledgement:**
    *   *Problem:* Case 105 waited 28 hours after escalation.
    *   *Fix:* Enforce a strict Service Level Agreement (SLA) requiring Level-2 agents to *acknowledge* or *begin* investigation within 4 hours of escalation. If the team is consistently missing this, they need more headcount.

2.  **Review Level-1 Workload Distribution:**
    *   *Problem:* Case 104 waited 3.5 hours just to be looked at after assignment.
    *   *Fix:* Investigate why the assigned agent in Case 104 could not pick up the ticket until 13:00. If the agent was busy, the ticket should have been reassigned to an available pool rather than sitting idle.

3.  **Analyze "Complexity" Triggers:**
    *   *Problem:* Cases 102 and 104 required long investigation times spanning days.
    *   *Fix:* Determine if these specific issues required waiting on third parties (e.g., customers, vendors). If so, the process should include a specific "Waiting for Customer" status so the internal clock pauses, distinguishing between *agent delay* and *external dependency*.
