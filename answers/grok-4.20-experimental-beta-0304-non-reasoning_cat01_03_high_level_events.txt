**High-Level Process Steps**

After analyzing the event sequences, I identified **5 distinct high-level process steps** that consistently appear in both cases (A1 and B2). These groupings are based on:

- Logical manufacturing workflow phases
- Temporal clustering
- Resource similarity
- Functional coherence (what the group achieves as a whole)

### 1. Material Preparation
**Low-level events included:**
- Retrieve raw metal sheet
- Scan barcode
- Place metal sheet on table
- Align metal sheet
- Preheat metal sheet

**Rationale:**  
These events form the initial setup phase. They involve acquiring the raw material, verifying its identity (traceability), positioning it correctly on the work surface, and preparing it thermally for subsequent operations. All occur within the first ~20–25 seconds and are prerequisites for any physical transformation of the part.

### 2. Welding / Assembly
**Low-level events included:**
- Pick up welding tool
- Weld corner A
- Weld corner B

**Rationale:**  
This is the core joining/assembly phase. It begins with tool acquisition and consists of the actual welding operations. These events are performed consecutively by the same resource (Operator B) using the same tool and represent the primary value-adding transformation step (joining the material).

### 3. Weld Quality Inspection
**Low-level events included:**
- Measure weld integrity

**Rationale:**  
This is a dedicated quality assurance step immediately following welding. It uses automated sensing (Quality Sensor #1) to evaluate the critical output of the previous step. It is temporally and logically distinct — a verification gate before proceeding to surface treatment.

### 4. Surface Coating
**Low-level events included:**
- Apply protective coating
- Dry coating

**Rationale:**  
These two events form a clear surface treatment phase. The coating application and subsequent drying are tightly coupled (same Coating Unit and Heating Unit usage) and serve the same purpose: protecting the welded part. They occur after weld inspection and before final verification.

### 5. Final Visual Inspection
**Low-level events included:**
- Visual check

**Rationale:**  
This is the final manual quality gate performed by a different operator (Operator C). It serves as the last human verification step before the product moves to the next stage (packaging, shipping, etc.).

### Structured Output

**Proposed High-Level Activity Model:**

| High-Level Activity          | Low-Level Events                                      | Primary Resources              | Purpose                              |
|-----------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Material Preparation        | Retrieve, Scan, Place, Align, Preheat                 | Operator A, Robot Arm #2, Heating Unit #1 | Prepare and validate raw material   |
| Welding / Assembly          | Pick up welding tool, Weld corner A, Weld corner B    | Operator B                     | Join material (core transformation) |
| Weld Quality Inspection     | Measure weld integrity                                | Quality Sensor #1              | Verify weld strength                 |
| Surface Coating             | Apply protective coating, Dry coating                 | Coating Unit #3, Heating Unit #2 | Apply protective surface treatment  |
| Final Visual Inspection     | Visual check                                          | Operator C                     | Final human quality approval        |

---

**Key Observations:**
- The process is highly standardized (same sequence and similar durations across cases).
- There is a clear alternation between manual, robotic, and automated sensor steps.
- Quality checks are embedded at critical points (after welding and at the end).
- The grouping reduces ~12 low-level events down to 5 meaningful process stages, providing a much clearer view of the manufacturing workflow.

This high-level model can now be used for process mining, bottleneck analysis, cycle time calculation per stage, or operator performance monitoring.
