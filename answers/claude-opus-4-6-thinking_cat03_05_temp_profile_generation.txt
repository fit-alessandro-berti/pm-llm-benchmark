## Reasoning

The process follows the general sequence:
**SS  OP  RC  QI  CA  PT  PK  WS  DT  AS**

I estimated realistic elapsed times based on supply-chain domain knowledge (supplier lead times, manufacturing durations, warehousing delays, shipping logistics, etc.), then accumulated those times for non-adjacent pairs. Standard deviations grow for pairs farther apart, reflecting compounding variability.

## Key Design Decisions

| Aspect | Rationale |
|---|---|
| **OP  RC (~10 days)** | Supplier lead time dominates; high-tech components often ship internationally |
| **WS  DT (~5 days ± 3 days)** | Warehousing dwell time is highly variable (batch shipping, demand-driven) |
| **DT  AS (~20 days ± 10 days)** | After-sales triggers are inherently unpredictable |
| **Growing STDEV for distant pairs** | Variability compounds across multiple intermediate steps |
| **Short spans (PTPK, PKWS)** | In-factory handoffs are tightly controlled, low variance |

## Anomaly Detection Example

With **ZETA = 2**, for the pair `('OP', 'RC')` (avg=864000s, stdev=172800s):
- **Lower bound**: 864000  2×172800 = **518400s** (~6 days)
- **Upper bound**: 864000 + 2×172800 = **1209600s** (~14 days)

A component delivery arriving in **3 days** or taking **18 days** would both be flagged as temporal deviations — the former suggesting an unusual expedited shipment, the latter a supply disruption.
