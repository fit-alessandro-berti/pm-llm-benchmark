The DECLARE model describes a highly controlled loan application process in which certain activities must happen, some must happen in a specific order, and others are explicitly forbidden. In practical terms, the process is designed to ensure that a loan is not approved, offered, funded, or communicated to the customer without proper intake, credit assessment, documentation, quality review, and authorization.

At a high level, the intended process looks like this:

1. The customer submits a loan application.
2. The application is received by the lender.
3. A preliminary credit check is performed.
4. Additional documents are gathered from the customer.
5. The loan terms are reviewed and authorized.
6. A quality assurance review is completed.
7. The loan offer package is assembled.
8. Funds are transferred to the customer.
9. The customer is notified.

However, because DECLARE models are constraint-based rather than strict flowcharts, they do not always prescribe one single sequence. Instead, they define rules that every valid process execution must satisfy. These rules collectively shape a compliant and logically ordered loan approval process.

---

## 1. The process must begin with receiving the application

The model includes:

```python
'init': {
    'Receive_Application': {'support': 1.0, 'confidence': 1.0}
}
```

This means that every valid case must start with:

**Receive_Application**

In practical terms, the lender cannot begin processing a loan case with a credit check, document collection, quality review, or fund transfer. The process begins only once the customer’s application has been formally received.

This is important because the application creates the official record of the customer’s request. It contains identity information, requested loan amount, purpose of the loan, income details, consent declarations, and other data needed to begin the approval process.

If this step were skipped or another activity happened first, the lender might be acting without a valid customer request or without documented consent. That could create regulatory, privacy, and operational problems.

```python
'existence': {
```

This means that in every valid process instance:

- **Receive_Application** must occur at least once.
- **Gather_Additional_Documents** must occur at least once.

The requirement to receive the application is obvious: the lender needs a formal application to process.

The requirement to gather additional documents suggests that the process always requires supporting evidence. This could include pay stubs, bank statements, tax returns, proof of identity, proof of address, or business records, depending on the type of loan.

In real-world lending, documentation is essential for:

- Verifying the applicant’s identity.
- Confirming income and employment.
- Preventing fraud.
- Meeting know-your-customer and anti-money-laundering obligations.
- Supporting credit risk analysis.
- Creating an auditable approval record.

If the lender omitted this step, it might approve loans based on incomplete or unverified information, increasing the risk of fraud, defaults, regulatory breaches, and customer disputes.

This forbidden activity represents a compliance violation, such as bypassing mandatory checks, ignoring documentation requirements, approving a loan without authorization, or disbursing funds without proper review.

In practical terms, this constraint says that the process must never intentionally bypass mandatory compliance protocols.

The real-world motivation is clear: lending is heavily regulated. Financial institutions must follow rules related to fair lending, affordability, identity verification, anti-money laundering, responsible lending, data privacy, and internal risk controls.

If this forbidden activity occurred, the consequences could include:

- Regulatory penalties.

## 4. The preliminary credit check occurs exactly once

This means:

**Preliminary_Credit_Check must occur once and only once.**

The credit check is mandatory, but it should not be repeated multiple times within the same process instance.

In real-world terms, this may reflect a controlled initial screening step. The lender checks whether the applicant broadly qualifies before investing further effort in document collection, offer preparation, or contract authorization.

The “exactly once” rule may also help avoid:

- Duplicate credit bureau inquiries.
- Inconsistent risk scores.
- Customer dissatisfaction.
- Increased processing costs.
- Confusion over which credit result is authoritative.

If the credit check were omitted, the lender might proceed without understanding the applicant’s credit risk. If it were repeated unnecessarily, it could negatively affect customer experience, create audit confusion, or lead to inconsistent decision-making.

## 5. Receiving the application must be followed by a preliminary credit check

The model includes:

```python
'response': {

        'confidence': 1.0,
        'target': 'Preliminary_Credit_Check'
    }
}
```

This means:

Whenever **Receive_Application** occurs, **Preliminary_Credit_Check** must eventually occur afterward.

So, after receiving the customer’s loan application, the lender must perform the initial credit assessment.

In practical terms, the lender should not let an application sit indefinitely without a credit review. The application triggers the underwriting workflow.

This rule supports:

- Risk management.
- Timely application processing.
- Consistent treatment of applicants.
- Early identification of ineligible or high-risk applications.

If the application were received but no credit check followed, the process would be incomplete and potentially unfair to the customer. The customer may wait for a decision that never comes, and the lender would not have fulfilled its operational responsibilities.

## 6. Preliminary credit check and document gathering are linked by succession

The model includes:

```python
'succession': {
    'Preliminary_Credit_Check': {
        'support': 1.0,
        'confidence': 1.0,
        'target': 'Gather_Additional_Documents'
    }
}
```

Succession combines two ideas:

1. If **Preliminary_Credit_Check** occurs, then **Gather_Additional_Documents** must occur afterward.
2. **Gather_Additional_Documents** can only occur if **Preliminary_Credit_Check** occurred before it.

In practical terms, the lender first performs the preliminary credit check, and only then requests or gathers additional documentation.

This is logical because the credit check may determine what documents are required. For example:

- A lower credit score may require proof of additional income.
- A self-employed applicant may need tax returns.
- A large loan amount may require enhanced documentation.
- A borderline application may require additional verification.

This rule supports operational efficiency. The lender avoids requesting unnecessary documents from customers before confirming that the application passes an initial screening.

If documents were gathered before the credit check, the lender might waste time and inconvenience the customer unnecessarily. If documents were not gathered after the credit check, the lender may lack evidence needed for final approval.

Coexistence means:

If **Gather_Additional_Documents** occurs, then **Authorize_Contract_Terms** must also occur, and vice versa.

Because the model already requires **Gather_Additional_Documents**, this effectively implies that **Authorize_Contract_Terms** must also occur in every valid case.

In practical terms, once documentation has been gathered, the process must reach the point where loan terms are formally authorized or finalized.

This suggests that collected documents are used to support the decision and finalization of contract terms. The lender should not collect sensitive customer documents without continuing toward a formal authorization decision.

The real-world motivation includes:

- Ensuring collected documents are used for a legitimate processing purpose.
- Preventing incomplete or abandoned cases.
- Making sure loan terms are formally approved before funding.
- Maintaining an auditable decision trail.

If the lender gathered documents but never authorized terms, the process would be incomplete and possibly frustrating to the customer. If the lender authorized terms without gathering documents, it could approve the loan on insufficient evidence.

## 8. Authorizing contract terms must be preceded by a preliminary credit check

The model includes:

```python
'chainprecedence': {
    'Authorize_Contract_Terms': {
        'support': 1.0,
        'confidence': 1.0,
        'target': 'Preliminary_Credit_Check'
    }
}
```

Chain precedence means that whenever **Authorize_Contract_Terms** occurs, it must be immediately preceded by **Preliminary_Credit_Check**.

In practical terms, the model says the final authorization of contract terms should occur directly after the preliminary credit check.

This is a very strict constraint. It ensures that no unrelated activity occurs between the credit check and contract authorization.

The motivation could be to ensure that the authorization decision is based directly on the most recent credit check result, without intervening steps that might introduce inconsistency or outdated information.

However, this constraint creates some tension with the earlier rule that a preliminary credit check must be followed by document gathering. In a real loan process, one might normally expect:

1. Credit check.
2. Document gathering.
3. Verification/underwriting.
4. Authorization of terms.

But the chain precedence rule requires:

1. Preliminary_Credit_Check.
2. Authorize_Contract_Terms.

This means that, under the strict interpretation of the DECLARE model, authorization must happen immediately after the preliminary credit check. The model therefore describes a somewhat idealized or possibly simplified process, rather than a fully realistic underwriting sequence.

If authorization occurred without being immediately preceded by the credit check, the lender might approve contract terms based on stale, incomplete, or unverified information.

## 9. Quality assurance review must occur before authorization

The model includes:

```python
'precedence': {
    'Quality_Assurance_Review': {
        'support': 1.0,
        'confidence': 1.0,
        'target': 'Authorize_Contract_Terms'
    }
}
```

Interpreting this according to the usual DECLARE convention, this means:

**Authorize_Contract_Terms** may occur only if **Quality_Assurance_Review** occurred before it.

In practical terms, the lender must complete a quality assurance review before formally authorizing the loan terms.

The quality assurance review likely checks whether:

- The offer complies with internal policy.
- The interest rate and repayment terms are correct.
- Required documents are present.
- The customer’s eligibility has been properly evaluated.
- Regulatory disclosures are complete.
- The file is ready for final approval.

This rule is important for compliance and operational quality. It prevents the organization from authorizing terms that contain errors, missing disclosures, policy exceptions, or compliance defects.

If authorization happened before QA review, the lender could finalize incorrect or noncompliant terms. This might lead to legal disputes, customer harm, rework, or regulatory findings.

There is also a possible tension here with the chain precedence rule discussed above. If authorization must be immediately preceded by the preliminary credit check, but also must be preceded by a QA review, then the model may be difficult or impossible to satisfy unless the QA review happens before the immediately preceding credit check. That would be unusual in a real-world process. This illustrates how DECLARE constraints can sometimes interact in ways that require careful validation.

Also, each QA review must be preceded by document gathering in the corresponding alternating pattern.

In practical terms, after documents are gathered, the next phase must include a quality assurance review before another round of document collection is allowed.

This supports disciplined case handling. The lender does not repeatedly collect documents without reviewing what has already been received.

The real-world motivation includes:

- Reducing customer burden.
- Avoiding duplicate document requests.
- Ensuring documents are checked promptly.
- Improving process efficiency.
- Maintaining a clear audit trail.

If the lender kept requesting documents without QA review, the customer experience would suffer and the process could become chaotic. If QA review occurred without gathered documents, the review would have little basis.

- The destination account.
- The repayment start date.
- Any next steps.
- How to contact support.

If funds were transferred without notification, the customer might be unaware of repayment obligations or might not notice an incorrect transfer. That could lead to disputes, missed payments, or customer complaints.

## 16. Transfer of funds must not coexist with receiving the application

The model includes:

```python
'noncoexistence': {
    'Transfer_Funds': {
        'support': 1.0,
        'confidence': 1.0,
        'target': 'Receive_Application'
    }
}
```

Noncoexistence normally means that the two activities must not both occur in the same case.

This creates a major contradiction in the model, because:

- **Receive_Application** is mandatory and must be the initial activity.
- **Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package** must be immediately followed by **Transfer_Funds**.
- The described loan process expects funds to be transferred after approval.

If interpreted strictly, the noncoexistence constraint says that a case containing **Receive_Application** cannot also contain **Transfer_Funds**. Since receiving the application is mandatory, this would mean funds can never be transferred in a valid case.

That conflicts with the intended end-to-end loan disbursement process.

In a realistic model, this constraint might have been intended to prevent **Transfer_Funds** from happening too early, for example before receiving an application. But noncoexistence is stronger than that: it forbids the two from appearing together at all.

A better constraint for that intention would be a precedence rule requiring **Receive_Application** before **Transfer_Funds**, not noncoexistence.

If this constraint were enforced literally, no valid process could both start with an application and end with disbursement. The process would be blocked from completing.

# A possible practical execution path

Taking the intended business logic into account, a reasonable practical process might be:

1. **Receive_Application**  
   The customer submits a loan application, and the lender formally records it.

2. **Preliminary_Credit_Check**  
   The lender performs an initial creditworthiness assessment.

3. **Gather_Additional_Documents**  
   The lender requests and receives supporting documentation from the customer.

4. **Quality_Assurance_Review**  
   The file is reviewed for completeness, accuracy, policy compliance, and regulatory compliance.

5. **Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package**  
   The approved terms and supporting documents are assembled into a formal loan offer package.

6. **Transfer_Funds**  
   The approved loan amount is disbursed to the customer’s account.

7. **Notify_Customer**  
   The customer is informed that the funds have been transferred and is given relevant next steps.

However, the model also contains constraints suggesting that **Authorize_Contract_Terms** must occur, and that it must be tightly connected to the credit check and QA review. A more business-realistic path including authorization would be:

1. **Receive_Application**
2. **Preliminary_Credit_Check**
3. **Gather_Additional_Documents**
4. **Quality_Assurance_Review**
5. **Authorize_Contract_Terms**
6. **Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package**
7. **Transfer_Funds**
8. **Notify_Customer**

But this business-realistic sequence may not satisfy all strict DECLARE constraints exactly, especially the chain precedence constraint requiring **Authorize_Contract_Terms** to be immediately preceded by **Preliminary_Credit_Check**, and the chain succession constraint requiring **Quality_Assurance_Review** to be immediately followed by **Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package**.

So the intended process is clear, but the formal model appears to contain some inconsistencies or overly strict constraints.

# How the constraints support compliance and control

Overall, the constraints are designed to enforce several important principles.

## Regulatory compliance

The model forbids proceeding without compliance and requires key review steps before finalization. This helps ensure the lender follows legal and internal rules before issuing a loan.

## Fraud prevention

Requiring a formal application, a credit check, and supporting documentation reduces the risk of identity fraud, income misrepresentation, or unauthorized borrowing.

## Risk management

The preliminary credit check and document gathering help the lender evaluate whether the applicant can repay the loan. Contract terms should be authorized only after appropriate evaluation.

## Operational consistency

Rules such as “exactly one credit check” and chained relationships between QA, offer assembly, and funding create a consistent process. Staff cannot arbitrarily skip or reorder critical activities.

## Auditability

The model creates a traceable sequence of events. If regulators or internal auditors review a case, they can see whether required steps occurred in the expected order.

## Customer satisfaction

The model ensures that customers are notified after funds are transferred and that the process begins properly with their application. It also reduces the risk of premature or incorrect communication.

---

# Consequences of violating the prescribed order

If activities occur out of order or mandatory steps are omitted, the consequences could be significant.

For example:

- If **Preliminary_Credit_Check** is skipped, the lender may approve a high-risk borrower without proper assessment.
- If **Gather_Additional_Documents** is omitted, the lender may lack proof of income, identity, or eligibility.
- If **Quality_Assurance_Review** is skipped, the offer may contain compliance errors or incorrect terms.
- If **Authorize_Contract_Terms** is omitted, the loan may be funded without formal approval.
- If **Transfer_Funds** happens too early, money may be disbursed before the loan is validly approved.
- If **Notify_Customer** occurs before funds are transferred, the customer may be misled.
- If **Notify_Customer** never occurs, the customer may not know the loan status or repayment obligations.
- If **Proceed_Without_Compliance** occurs, the lender may face regulatory enforcement, financial penalties, or reputational harm.

In a real financial institution, these failures could lead to loan losses, customer complaints, legal disputes, audit findings, or regulator intervention.

# Important note about model consistency

Although the business intent is understandable, the DECLARE model contains some constraints that may conflict with an ordinary end-to-end loan process.

The most important issue is:

```python
'noncoexistence': {
    'Transfer_Funds': {
        'target': 'Receive_Application'
    }
}
```

Since **Receive_Application** is mandatory and must start the case, this constraint forbids **Transfer_Funds** from occurring in the same case. That contradicts the requested process description from application to disbursement.

There are also possible ordering tensions involving:

- **Authorize_Contract_Terms**
- **Preliminary_Credit_Check**
- **Quality_Assurance_Review**
- **Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package**

In particular, the model simultaneously suggests that authorization depends on QA review, but also that authorization must be immediately preceded by the preliminary credit check. Meanwhile, QA review must be immediately followed by offer package assembly.

Therefore, the model should be reviewed if it is intended to represent a fully executable loan process. The intended business rules are sensible, but some DECLARE constraints may need adjustment.

For example, the noncoexistence rule between **Transfer_Funds** and **Receive_Application** could be replaced by a precedence rule saying:

```python
Transfer_Funds requires prior Receive_Application
```

That would better express the desired rule that funds cannot be transferred before an application exists.

In summary, the DECLARE model represents a controlled loan approval process that begins with receiving the application, requires a credit check and document collection, uses quality assurance and authorization to ensure the loan terms are valid, then proceeds to offer preparation, fund transfer, and customer notification. The constraints are motivated by compliance, fraud prevention, risk management, auditability, operational discipline, and customer communication. However, some constraints appear overly restrictive or contradictory, so the model should be refined if it is meant to allow a complete process from application to final disbursement.
