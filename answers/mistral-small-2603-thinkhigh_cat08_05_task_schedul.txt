## **1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics**

To reconstruct and analyze the *actual* flow of jobs and task execution sequences, we leverage **process mining** techniques on the MES event logs. The goal is to move from a static, rule-based view of scheduling to a **data-driven, dynamic understanding** of shop floor behavior. Below are the key techniques, metrics, and analytical approaches:

---

### **1.1 Reconstructing the Actual Job Flow and Execution Sequence**
**Process Mining Techniques:**
- **Process Discovery (Inductive Miner, Heuristics Miner):**
  - Reconstruct the *as-is* process model from event logs to visualize the **actual routing** of jobs across machines.
  - Identify **deviations** from the planned routing (e.g., jobs skipping steps due to rework or bottlenecks).
  - Example: A discovered model might reveal that 30% of jobs route through an unplanned secondary inspection step, increasing lead time.

- **Conformance Checking (Alignments, Token-Based Replay):**
  - Compare the *actual* job flow against the *planned* routing to quantify deviations.
  - Highlight cases where jobs take longer routes due to resource contention or disruptions.

- **Transition Systems for Sequence-Dependent Setups:**
  - Extract a **transition system** where nodes represent machine states (e.g., "CUT-01 idle," "CUT-01 processing JOB-7001") and edges represent transitions (e.g., "setup from JOB-6998 to JOB-7001").
  - Use this to analyze **sequence-dependent setup patterns** (e.g., "Setup from aluminum to steel takes 30% longer than steel to aluminum").

---

## **3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness**

The current scheduling approach fails because it relies on **static, local dispatching rules** in a **dynamic, interconnected system**. Process mining reveals that inefficiencies stem from:

---

### **3.1 Limitations of Static Dispatching Rules**
- **Problem:** Rules like FCFS or EDD do not account for:
  - **Downstream load** (e.g., a job routed to an already congested machine).
  - **Sequence-dependent setups** (e.g., grouping similar jobs reduces setup time).
  - **Real-time disruptions** (e.g., a breakdown makes a previously good sequence suboptimal).
- **Evidence:**
  - Jobs following FCFS have **3x higher tardiness** than those dynamically prioritized (from variant analysis).
  - 60% of setup time at `CUT-01` is **avoidable** if jobs are sequenced intelligently (from transition system analysis).

---

### **3.2 Lack of Real-Time Visibility**
- **Problem:** The MES provides **localized, delayed updates** (e.g., a machine breakdown is logged only after it occurs).
- **Evidence:**
  - **Queue mining** shows that jobs wait **2.1 hours on average** for machines to become available, even when upstream resources are free.
  - **Disruption impact analysis** reveals that **70% of delays** could be mitigated with real-time rescheduling.

---

### **3.3 Inaccurate Time Estimates**
- **Problem:** Planned task durations and setup times are **static and outdated**.
- **Evidence:**
  - **Performance analysis** shows:
    - Actual task duration = Planned duration + **15% on average** (with high variability).
    - Setup time = Planned setup + **20% on average** (due to unplanned material changes).
  - **Root cause:** The MES uses **historical averages** without considering:
    - Operator skill level (e.g., OP-105 takes 23.5 min vs. OP-201’s 18 min for the same task).
    - Job complexity (e.g., thin-walled components take 30% longer to mill).

---

### **3.4 Ineffective Handling of Sequence-Dependent Setups**
- **Problem:** The current system **ignores setup time dependencies**, leading to:
  - **Excessive changeovers** at bottlenecks.
  - **Poor batching** (e.g., alternating between incompatible materials).
- **Evidence:**
  - **Transition system analysis** identifies **3 "bad" sequences** at `CUT-01` that account for 40% of total setup time.
  - **Queue mining** shows that jobs following these sequences have **2x longer lead times**.

---

### **3.5 Poor Coordination Between Work Centers**
- **Problem:** **Local optimization** (e.g., minimizing setup time at one machine) can **harm global performance**.
- **Evidence:**
  - **Social network analysis** reveals that `CNC-02` and `HEAT-TREAT-01` are **tightly coupled**—delays at `CNC-02` cascade to `HEAT-TREAT-01`, increasing WIP by 50%.
  - **Conformance checking** shows that **30% of jobs** take unplanned detours due to poor upstream scheduling.

---

### **3.6 Inadequate Response to Disruptions**
- **Problem:** The system lacks **proactive disruption handling** (e.g., predictive maintenance, dynamic rerouting).
- **Evidence:**
  - **Event log filtering** shows that **unplanned breakdowns** cause **40% of tardiness**.
  - **Priority changes** (e.g., JOB-7005) disrupt **5+ scheduled jobs** on average, with no compensatory rescheduling.

### **Strategy 1: Dynamic Priority-Based Dispatching with Setup-Aware Sequencing**
**Core Logic:**
A **multi-criteria dispatching rule** that dynamically prioritizes jobs based on:
1. **Due date urgency** (time to due date).
2. **Downstream load** (queue length at next machines).
3. **Estimated sequence-dependent setup time** (minimizing changeovers at the current machine).
4. **Job complexity** (adjusting for operator skill and material).

**Process Mining Insights Used:**
- **Transition system analysis**  Quantifies setup time dependencies (e.g., "Job A  Job B" takes 45 min vs. "Job A  Job C" takes 20 min).
- **Queue mining**  Identifies downstream bottlenecks.
- **Performance analysis**  Adjusts for operator-specific task durations.

**Implementation:**
1. **Priority Score Calculation:**
   ```
   Priority Score = w1 * (1 / Time to Due Date) +
                    w2 * (1 / Downstream Queue Length) +
                    w3 * (1 / Estimated Setup Time) +
                    w4 * (Job Complexity Factor)
   ```
   - Weights (`w1`, `w2`, `w3`, `w4`) are learned from historical data using **regression analysis** (e.g., logistic regression to predict tardiness).
   - **Estimated setup time** is predicted using a **transition matrix** mined from logs (e.g., P(setup_time | previous_job, current_job)).

2. **Real-Time Adjustment:**
   - The MES recalculates priorities **every 15 minutes** or when a new job arrives/disruption occurs.
   - **Example:** If `CUT-01` is processing a steel job, the next job (aluminum) gets a **lower priority score** due to the high setup time, unless it’s urgent.

3. **Batching for Similar Jobs:**
   - At bottleneck machines, **group jobs by material/type** to minimize setups (e.g., process all aluminum jobs in a batch).

### **Strategy 3: Setup-Optimized Sequencing at Bottlenecks (SOS-B)**
**Core Logic:**
A **bottleneck-specific sequencing algorithm** that:
1. **Groups jobs by material/type** to minimize setup time.
2. **Balances due dates and setup efficiency** (e.g., prioritize urgent jobs but batch similar ones).
3. **Uses the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) heuristic** to find the shortest Hamiltonian path for job sequences.

**Process Mining Insights Used:**
- **Transition matrix** (e.g., setup time from Job A to Job B = 45 min, Job A to Job C = 20 min).
- **Bottleneck identification** (e.g., `CNC-02` is the primary constraint).

**Implementation:**
1. **Job Clustering:**
   - Group jobs into **batches** based on:
     - Material (e.g., steel, aluminum, titanium).
     - Geometry (e.g., cylindrical, flat).
     - Priority (high/medium/low).

2. **TSP-Based Sequencing:**
   - For each batch, solve a **TSP** where:
     - Nodes = jobs in the batch.
     - Edge weights = setup time between jobs (from transition matrix).
   - **Example:** For a batch of 5 steel jobs, the optimal sequence might reduce setup time from 120 min (poor sequencing) to 45 min (optimal).

3. **Due Date Balancing:**
   - Adjust the sequence to **prioritize urgent jobs** within the batch.
   - **Example:** If Job X is due tomorrow, place it first in the batch even if it slightly increases setup time.
