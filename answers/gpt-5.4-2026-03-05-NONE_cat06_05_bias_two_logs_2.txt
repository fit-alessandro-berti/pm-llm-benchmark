### Why Group B is biased
Group B applies a **special +5 cultural fit adjustment** to candidates who belong to **LocalBusinessLeadersClub**. That means candidates are not being evaluated only on job-relevant performance, but also on **membership in a particular professional association**.

This creates **systematic preferential treatment** for some applicants:
- **U001**: Cultural fit goes from **60 to 65**, and the candidate is **Hired**
- **U003**: Cultural fit goes from **58 to 63**, and the candidate is **Hired**

By contrast, Group A candidates receive **no comparable adjustment**, even when their underlying scores are similar or stronger.

### How the bias manifests
The bias appears as:
- **Unequal scoring rules** across groups
- A **non-job-related advantage** tied to social/professional affiliation
- A mechanism that can favor applicants with access to local networks, which may correlate with privilege or non-protected characteristics

This is a form of **disparate treatment**, because one set of candidates receives an explicit bonus based on affiliation status.

### Evidence from the logs
Compare:
- **P002**: Skill 78, Personality 75, Cultural Fit 60, **Not Hired**
- **U001**: Skill 80, Personality 72, Cultural Fit 60  **65 after boost**, **Hired**

Also:
- **U003**: Skill 75, Personality 65, Cultural Fit 58  **63 after boost**, **Hired**

Without the boost, U003 appears weaker than some Group A candidates who were not given any equivalent advantage.

### Fairness and equity implications
This practice undermines fairness because:
- Candidates are not judged by the same standards
- Access to a specific association may reflect **network privilege**, not merit
- Protected-group candidates may be indirectly disadvantaged if they are less likely to belong to that association

So even if the rule does not explicitly mention a protected trait, it can still produce **indirect discrimination** or **structural unfairness**.

### Conclusion
**Group B exhibits bias** because it gives certain candidates an explicit score boost based on association membership, creating an unfair advantage in hiring outcomes. A fairer process would use **consistent, job-relevant criteria** for all candidates and avoid adjustments based on social or professional affiliations unless they are clearly validated as necessary for job performance.
