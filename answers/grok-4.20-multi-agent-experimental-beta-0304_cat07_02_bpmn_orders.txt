**The visualization is a BPMN (Business Process Model and Notation) diagram** showing an end-to-end business process for handling a customer order in a manufacturing or order-fulfillment company. It uses a **swimlane (pool/lane) layout** with four horizontal lanes representing different organizational units/departments: **Sales** (top), **Production**, **Procurement**, and **Warehouse** (bottom).

### Overall Structure and Symbols
- Standard BPMN elements are used: 
  - Circles for **start/end events** (e.g., "Monday morning" start event, "Product sent" end event).
  - Rounded rectangles for **tasks/activities** (e.g., "Check order for completeness", "Manufacture product").
  - Diamonds for **decision gateways** (e.g., "Check if customer is already in the database?", material quality checks).
  - Arrows for sequence flow within lanes.
  - Dashed arrows/lines for **message flows** or data objects (e.g., "Order details" flowing down from Sales to Production).
  - A **subprocess** box in Procurement labeled "Material Compensation Subprocess".
  - Document icons for outputs like "Draft invoice".
- There are cross-lane interactions (message flows) showing how information, orders, materials, and products move between departments. A dashed vertical "Order details" line connects the top lane downward.

### Lane-by-Lane Flow
**Sales Lane (Top):**
- Begins with a start event ("Monday morning" / incoming customer order).
- Tasks: "Check order for completeness"  decision gateway ("Check if customer is already in the database?").
- If the customer is new: task "Add customer to the database" (database icon).
- Proceeds to "Send order details" (message flow down to Production) and "Draft invoice" (with document icon).
- Later steps involve invoice handling and an end event related to "Payment sent" or "Customer invoice."

**Production Lane:**
- Triggered by "Order details arrive" (from Sales).
- Involves decision points and tasks such as "Plan production"  "Manufacture product"  "Product ready."
- Includes material-related activities/gateways and a dashed line for "Material is ready" or similar.
- Output flows (message/product ready) downward to Procurement (if materials needed) and/or Warehouse.

**Procurement Lane:**
- Contains a collapsed or expanded **"Material Compensation Subprocess"**.
- Triggered by material/order needs (e.g., "Order materials arrive").
- Tasks include "Order materials," "Check materials," "Return material" (with loops labeled "01"/"02" or Q1/Q2 for iterations or quantities).
- Decision gateways for material quality/availability and "Order confirmation."
- Handles returning or compensating materials when issues arise.

**Warehouse Lane (Bottom):**
- Receives "Product arrives" or "Product ready" from Production.
- Task: "Send product."
- Decision gateway and final end event "Product sent."

### High-Level Process Summary
The diagram models an **order-to-delivery (or order-to-cash)** process: a customer order is received and validated in Sales, details are passed to Production for planning and manufacturing (with Procurement handling any material shortages or returns via a dedicated subprocess), and the finished product is shipped from the Warehouse. It highlights decision points, data flows (order details, invoices, materials), and inter-departmental handoffs.

This type of diagram is commonly used in business process management (BPM), process improvement, or system design to visualize responsibilities, potential loops (e.g., material checks), and optimization opportunities.
