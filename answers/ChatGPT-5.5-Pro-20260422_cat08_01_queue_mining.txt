## 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

The clinic’s event log is well suited for queue mining because it contains both **START** and **COMPLETE** lifecycle timestamps. In process mining terms, this allows us to separate **service time**—the time spent actively performing an activity—from **waiting time**—the time a case is not being processed or is waiting for the next resource or condition to become available. This distinction is central because reducing total visit duration usually requires attacking the idle gaps, not simply asking clinicians to work faster. Process mining is commonly used in healthcare to analyze diagnostic, treatment, and organizational processes from logged execution data, but healthcare pathways require special care because they are variable and patient-centered. ([ScienceDirect][1])

### How to calculate waiting times

First, reconstruct each patient visit as an ordered sequence of **activity instances**:

`Case ID | Activity | Activity instance number | Start timestamp | Complete timestamp | Resource | Patient type | Urgency | Specialty`

For each activity instance:

[
\text{Service Time}_i = \text{Complete Timestamp}_i - \text{Start Timestamp}_i
]

For two consecutive activities in the same patient visit:

[
\text{Waiting Time}*{i \rightarrow i+1} = \text{Start Timestamp}*{i+1} - \text{Complete Timestamp}_i
]

Example:

* Registration completes at 09:08:45.
* Nurse Assessment starts at 09:15:20.
* Waiting time from Registration to Nurse Assessment = **6 minutes 35 seconds**.

In this context, a “queue” is not just a physical line of people. It is any repeated transition where patients frequently spend time between the completion of one stage and the start of the next, for example:

* Registration → Nurse Assessment
* Nurse Assessment → Doctor Consultation
* Doctor Consultation → ECG
* ECG → Specialist Review
* Diagnostic Test → Doctor Review
* Doctor Consultation → Check-out

A key caveat: with only start and complete timestamps, the measured gap is best called **inter-activity waiting/idle time** unless the clinic also logs “patient ready,” “test ordered,” “room requested,” or “resource assigned” events. The gap may include true queueing, walking time, paperwork, waiting for a room, waiting for results, or clinical dependency time. Literature on waiting-time analysis notes that different notions of waiting time exist, including idle time, time between activities, and resource-contention waiting time, so the clinic should define its measure explicitly before making decisions. ([Springer][2])

### Data preparation steps

Before calculating queues, I would do the following:

1. **Pair START and COMPLETE records** by Case ID, Activity, Resource, and activity occurrence number. This matters because the same activity may occur more than once, such as “Doctor Review” before and after diagnostics.

2. **Remove or flag data-quality issues**, including missing START or COMPLETE timestamps, negative durations, duplicate events, impossible overlaps, and clock inconsistencies between systems.

3. **Create an activity-instance table** with one row per executed activity, rather than one row per lifecycle event.

4. **Sort each case chronologically** and identify the direct-follow transitions between activities.

5. **Handle parallelism carefully.** If Blood Test and ECG happen in parallel, the waiting time into the next shared activity should not be double-counted. For parallel branches, distinguish:

   * queue time before each branch,
   * synchronization time before the next common step,
   * true idle time where the patient is waiting with no active work happening.

### Queue metrics to calculate

For each queue transition, such as `Nurse Assessment → Doctor Consultation`, I would calculate:

| Metric                                            | Why it matters                                                                                |
| ------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Average waiting time**                          | Useful for overall burden, but sensitive to outliers.                                         |
| **Median waiting time**                           | Represents the typical patient experience.                                                    |
| **75th, 90th, and 95th percentile waiting time**  | Captures the experience of patients who wait unusually long. Very important for satisfaction. |
| **Maximum waiting time**                          | Useful for incident review, but should not drive decisions alone.                             |
| **Standard deviation / coefficient of variation** | Shows instability and unpredictability.                                                       |
| **Queue frequency**                               | Number and percentage of visits passing through this queue.                                   |
| **Total waiting burden**                          | Sum of all waiting minutes in that queue; often better than average alone.                    |
| **Percentage exceeding threshold**                | For example, percentage of patients waiting more than 15, 30, or 45 minutes.                  |
| **Patient-type-specific wait**                    | New vs. follow-up, urgent vs. normal, specialty, age group if available.                      |
| **Time-of-day / day-of-week wait profile**        | Identifies morning peaks, lunch gaps, evening spillover, and specific clinic sessions.        |
| **Resource-specific wait**                        | For example, waits before Dr. Smith vs. Dr. Lee, Room 3 vs. Room 4, ECG Tech X vs. Tech Y.    |
| **Contribution to total visit duration**          | Shows which queues most increase overall length of stay.                                      |

I would also calculate **service-time metrics** for each activity because queueing problems often emerge from mismatches between arrival rates, service times, and resource availability. Service time can be computed directly from START and COMPLETE timestamps when those events are reliably paired. 

### How to identify the most critical queues

The most critical queues should not be selected only by the longest average wait. I would rank queues using a multi-factor priority score:

[
\text{Queue Priority Score} =
w_1(\text{Total Waiting Burden}) +
w_2(\text{P90 Wait}) +
w_3(%\text{Above SLA}) +
w_4(\text{Volume}) +
w_5(\text{Clinical/Satisfaction Impact})
]

A queue is critical if it has one or more of the following characteristics:

1. **High total waiting burden**
   Example: A 12-minute wait affecting 8,000 patients may matter more than a 45-minute wait affecting 40 patients.

2. **High tail waiting time**
   A queue with a moderate average but a very high 90th percentile causes poor patient experience and unpredictability.

3. **High frequency**
   Registration → Nurse Assessment and Nurse Assessment → Doctor Consultation may affect most patients.

4. **Impact on sensitive groups**
   If urgent patients, elderly patients, new patients, or high-complexity patients wait disproportionately, the queue deserves priority even if the overall average is acceptable.

5. **Strong effect on total visit duration**
   A queue that explains 30–40% of total visit time is a prime improvement target.

6. **Downstream amplification**
   A delay after registration may propagate into nurse, doctor, diagnostic, and check-out delays.

A useful first output would be a **process map with performance overlays**, where each transition arrow is colored or weighted by median wait, P90 wait, and total waiting burden. That makes bottlenecks visually obvious.

## 2. Root Cause Analysis

Once the major queues are identified, the next question is: **why are patients waiting there?** Queue mining tells us *where* the delays occur; root cause analysis explains *what causes them*.

### A. Resource bottlenecks

Potential causes:

* Too few clerks during peak arrival times.
* Nurse staffing does not match appointment inflow.
* Doctors start sessions late or have consultation durations longer than scheduled slots.
* Diagnostic equipment, rooms, or technicians are over-utilized.
* Rooms are available but staff are not, or vice versa.
* Lunch breaks, shift changes, and part-time schedules create capacity valleys.
* Some resources are overloaded while others are underused.

How to analyze this:

* Calculate utilization by resource, room, equipment, hour, day, and specialty.
* Compare **arrival rate into a queue** with **start rate of the next activity**.
* Reconstruct queue length over time: how many patients are waiting for Nurse Assessment, Doctor Consultation, ECG, X-Ray, etc.
* Identify whether waiting increases when resource workload is high.
* Compare service times by resource to detect large differences.
* Check whether high waiting periods coincide with off-duty periods, lunch breaks, or room unavailability.

Waiting-time research explicitly identifies resource contention and resource unavailability as common causes of waiting; in healthcare, this can manifest as patients waiting for the next doctor or clinical resource to become available. ([Springer][2])

### B. Activity dependencies and handovers

Potential causes:

* Nurse completes assessment, but the doctor is not notified that the patient is ready.
* Doctor needs lab or ECG results before continuing, but tests are ordered late.
* Diagnostic results are complete but not reviewed promptly.
* Patients wait because rooms are not cleaned or turned over.
* Check-out cannot proceed until physician documentation, billing codes, or follow-up orders are complete.
* Patients are physically moved between departments without coordinated handoff.

How to analyze this:

* Look for long gaps between activity completion and next activity start when the next resource appears available.
* Analyze handover pairs: Nurse → Doctor, Doctor → Diagnostic, Diagnostic → Doctor Review, Doctor → Check-out.
* Compare waits by resource pair, for example Nurse 1 → Dr. Smith versus Nurse 2 → Dr. Smith.
* Check whether delays are longer after certain activities, suggesting information handoff problems rather than capacity problems.
* Use conformance checking to see whether the expected care pathway is followed or whether patients take detours.

### C. Variability in service times

Potential causes:

* New patients take longer to register and assess.
* Some specialties have highly variable consultation lengths.
* Urgent patients interrupt normal flow.
* Certain diagnostic tests have variable setup or turnaround times.
* Complex patients require additional documentation or coordination.
* Some doctors consistently run over scheduled slot length.

How to analyze this:

* Calculate service-time distributions by activity, patient type, specialty, urgency, resource, and time of day.
* Compare scheduled slot length with actual service time.
* Identify activities with high coefficient of variation.
* Analyze whether long service times are followed by growing queue lengths.
* Segment Doctor Consultation by specialty because Cardiology, Orthopedics, Dermatology, and Internal Medicine may have very different duration profiles.

### D. Appointment scheduling policies

Potential causes:

* Too many patients arrive at the same clock time, such as 09:00.
* New and follow-up patients are mixed without accounting for different service times.
* Overbooking is applied uniformly instead of based on no-show probability.
* Diagnostic-heavy patients are scheduled without coordinating test capacity.
* Multiple doctors send patients to the same ECG or X-Ray resource at the same time.
* The first appointment block starts before doctors or rooms are actually ready.

How to analyze this:

* Compare scheduled appointment time, actual arrival time, registration start, nurse start, and doctor start.
* Plot arrivals by 5-, 10-, and 15-minute intervals.
* Identify appointment waves.
* Compare waits for early arrivals, on-time arrivals, and late arrivals.
* Estimate no-show and walk-in patterns by specialty and patient type.
* Check whether backlog begins early and persists throughout the session.

### E. Patient arrival patterns

Potential causes:

* Patients arrive early in batches.
* Public transport, parking, or check-in instructions create arrival clustering.
* Walk-ins and urgent patients disrupt scheduled flow.
* Morning clinics experience more demand than afternoon clinics.
* Monday or post-holiday demand is higher.

How to analyze this:

* Build arrival heatmaps by day of week and hour.
* Compare arrival patterns with staffing rosters.
* Separate scheduled arrivals, walk-ins, urgent add-ons, and late arrivals if the data allows.
* Identify when queue length first becomes unstable.

### F. Differences by patient type or urgency

Potential causes:

* New patients require longer registration and nurse assessment.
* Follow-up patients may be clinically simple but still pass through the full pathway.
* Urgent patients may be prioritized, increasing waits for normal patients.
* Some specialties require diagnostics more often.
* Multispecialty visits create more handoffs and synchronization delays.

How to analyze this:

* Perform variant analysis by patient type, urgency, and specialty.
* Compare average and P90 wait times by group.
* Identify whether specific groups have systematically longer total visit duration.
* Analyze whether urgent patients are actually prioritized and whether that prioritization creates excessive waits elsewhere.

### Process mining techniques to use

Beyond basic queue calculations, I would use:

1. **Process discovery with performance overlay**
   Shows actual patient pathways and highlights high-wait transitions.

2. **Variant analysis**
   Compares common pathways, such as:

   * New Cardiology patient with ECG
   * Follow-up Cardiology patient without diagnostic test
   * Urgent patient with Specialist Review
   * Normal patient with Blood Test and Check-out

3. **Bottleneck analysis**
   Identifies transitions and activities with the largest waiting contribution.

4. **Resource analysis**
   Examines staff workload, utilization, service times, handover patterns, and resource-specific queues.

5. **Conformance checking**
   Compares actual flows against intended clinic protocols.

6. **Queue-length reconstruction**
   Estimates how many patients are waiting for each activity over time.

7. **Predictive waiting-time modeling**
   Uses features such as time of day, patient type, urgency, specialty, resource, current queue length, and previous activity duration to predict high-risk waits.

8. **Discrete-event simulation**
   Tests proposed interventions before deployment, such as adding a nurse for two peak hours, staggering appointments, or pre-ordering ECGs.

## 3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

Below are five concrete strategies. The clinic would not necessarily implement all at once; I would pilot the highest-value interventions based on the queue analysis.

---

### Strategy 1: Peak-time resource reallocation and flexible staffing

**Target queues**

* Registration → Nurse Assessment
* Nurse Assessment → Doctor Consultation
* Doctor Consultation → Diagnostic Test
* Diagnostic Test → Doctor Review
* Doctor Consultation → Check-out

**Root cause addressed**

Capacity does not match demand at specific times. The clinic may not need more total staff; it may need staff in different places or at different times.

**Data support**

This strategy is supported if the event log shows:

* Queue length rises sharply during specific time windows.
* Waiting times spike during morning check-in, lunch coverage, or late afternoon.
* Certain resources have high utilization while others are idle.
* Patients wait even though later in the day capacity is underused.
* The same queues recur on specific days or clinic sessions.

**Concrete actions**

* Add a floating clerk from 08:30–10:30 if registration queues peak then.
* Shift one nurse’s start time earlier if Nurse Assessment backlog begins before doctors start.
* Add a part-time diagnostic technician during Cardiology-heavy sessions.
* Use cross-trained staff for check-out during peak discharge waves.
* Create a “flow coordinator” role during high-volume sessions to move patients to the next available resource.

**Expected impact**

The impact should be estimated using simulation on the six-month log. A practical target would be:

* Reduce P90 Registration → Nurse Assessment wait by **20–40%** during peak windows.
* Reduce average total visit duration for affected sessions by **10–20%**.
* Reduce overtime by smoothing end-of-day backlog.

The exact percentage should come from a scenario simulation using the clinic’s observed arrivals, service times, and resource calendars.

---

### Strategy 2: Redesign appointment templates to level-load demand

**Target queues**

* Registration → Nurse Assessment
* Nurse Assessment → Doctor Consultation
* Doctor Consultation → ECG/X-Ray/Blood Test
* Diagnostic Test → Specialist Review

**Root cause addressed**

Patient arrivals and service needs are clustered in ways that overload downstream resources.

**Data support**

This strategy is supported if the event log shows:

* Many patients are scheduled or arrive at the same time.
* New patients have much longer service times than follow-ups.
* Diagnostic-heavy specialties send patients to ECG, X-Ray, or lab in concentrated waves.
* Doctor waits are low early in the session but grow progressively through the day.
* Late patients disrupt the queue disproportionately.

**Concrete actions**

* Stagger appointment arrivals in smaller intervals rather than scheduling many patients at the top of the hour.
* Use different slot lengths for New vs. Follow-up patients based on observed service-time distributions.
* Reserve diagnostic capacity for specialties with predictable testing needs.
* Avoid scheduling multiple diagnostic-heavy new patients back-to-back.
* Reserve a small number of protected urgent slots to prevent urgent add-ons from disrupting the entire session.
* Use historical no-show probabilities to overbook selectively rather than uniformly.

**Expected impact**

If queue mining shows that arrival batching is the main driver, level-loading can reduce both average and tail waits without adding major cost. A reasonable pilot target would be:

* Reduce P90 Nurse Assessment → Doctor Consultation wait by **15–30%**.
* Reduce session-end delays by **20% or more**.
* Improve on-time doctor starts for later appointments.

This should be validated by comparing matched sessions before and after the appointment-template change.

---

### Strategy 3: Pre-visit digital intake and express registration/check-out

**Target queues**

* Arrival → Registration
* Registration → Nurse Assessment
* Doctor Consultation → Check-out

**Root cause addressed**

Administrative activities create unnecessary service time and variability, especially for new patients or patients with insurance, demographic, consent, or referral updates.

**Data support**

This strategy is supported if the data shows:

* New patients have much longer registration service times.
* Registration waits are highest when new-patient volume is high.
* Check-out queues spike after doctor sessions end.
* Many check-out activities are short but delayed because they occur in a batch.
* Administrative queues contribute significantly to total visit duration.

**Concrete actions**

* Send digital forms before the visit.
* Pre-verify insurance and referrals before arrival.
* Allow returning patients with no changes to use express check-in.
* Create mobile or kiosk check-in for simple visits.
* Enable electronic follow-up scheduling and payment after the visit.
* Separate complex registration from quick check-in.

**Expected impact**

This strategy reduces both queue time and service time. A useful operational calculation is:

[
\text{Staff time saved per day} =
\text{Patients per day} \times \text{Minutes removed per patient}
]

For example, removing just **3 minutes** from registration for **150 patients per day** frees **450 minutes**, or **7.5 staff-hours** of administrative capacity per day. That does not automatically translate one-for-one into reduced waits, but it materially increases effective capacity.

A realistic pilot target:

* Reduce average registration service time by **20–35%** for eligible patients.
* Reduce Registration → Nurse Assessment waiting by **10–25%**.
* Reduce check-out queues by shifting simple transactions to digital channels.

---

### Strategy 4: Parallelize diagnostics where clinically appropriate

**Target queues**

* Doctor Consultation → ECG Test
* Doctor Consultation → Blood Test
* Doctor Consultation → X-Ray
* Diagnostic Test → Doctor Review
* Doctor Consultation → Specialist Review

**Root cause addressed**

The current flow may be too sequential. Patients may wait for a doctor, then wait for diagnostics, then wait again for the doctor to review results.

**Data support**

This strategy is supported if variant analysis shows:

* Patients requiring diagnostics have much longer total visit duration.
* Diagnostic waits are predictable for certain specialties or patient types.
* ECG, blood tests, or X-Ray are frequently ordered after the first doctor consultation.
* Test capacity is underused before doctors begin ordering tests, then overwhelmed later.
* Doctor Review waits are long after diagnostics complete.

**Concrete actions**

* For predefined clinical protocols, order likely diagnostics during or immediately after Nurse Assessment.
* For Cardiology patients likely to need ECG, route to ECG before doctor consultation when clinically appropriate.
* Create pre-consult diagnostic slots for certain visit types.
* Notify doctors automatically when results are ready.
* Use standing orders only where clinically approved and safe.
* For patients who need multiple diagnostics, coordinate tests as a bundle rather than serially.

**Expected impact**

The goal is not to eliminate diagnostic work but to move it into time that would otherwise be idle. For diagnostic-heavy patient variants, a pilot might aim to:

* Reduce total visit duration by **15–25%**.
* Reduce Doctor Consultation → ECG wait by **25–50%** if ECG demand can be smoothed.
* Reduce repeat doctor-review delays by using result-ready notifications.

This strategy must be clinically governed because unnecessary testing would increase cost and may reduce care quality.

---

### Strategy 5: Real-time patient-flow dashboard and handoff alerts

**Target queues**

* Nurse Assessment → Doctor Consultation
* Diagnostic Test → Doctor Review
* Doctor Consultation → Check-out
* Any transition where the patient is ready but no one “owns” the next step

**Root cause addressed**

Some waiting is caused not by insufficient capacity, but by lack of visibility and weak handoffs.

**Data support**

This strategy is supported if the event log shows:

* Long waits occur even when the next resource is not fully utilized.
* Certain handover pairs have much longer delays than others.
* Diagnostic results are completed but review starts much later.
* Check-out waits depend on missing documentation or uncommunicated orders.
* Patients are repeatedly delayed after activities that require staff coordination.

**Concrete actions**

* Create a real-time queue dashboard by clinic area and specialty.
* Mark patient states: “registered,” “ready for nurse,” “ready for doctor,” “test complete,” “ready for review,” “ready for check-out.”
* Trigger alerts when a patient exceeds a waiting threshold.
* Use escalation rules, for example:

  * Nurse wait > 15 minutes → charge nurse notified.
  * Doctor wait > 25 minutes → rooming coordinator notified.
  * Diagnostic result complete > 10 minutes without review → physician/team alert.
* Assign ownership of each transition.

**Expected impact**

This strategy is low-cost relative to adding permanent staff. It is especially useful when root cause analysis shows handoff latency rather than pure capacity shortage. A reasonable pilot target:

* Reduce handoff-related waits by **10–20%**.
* Reduce extreme waits by improving response to outliers.
* Improve patient communication because staff can see where each patient is stuck.

## 4. Trade-offs and Constraints

### A. Bottleneck shifting

Improving registration may simply move the queue to Nurse Assessment. Improving Nurse Assessment may move the queue to doctors. Adding ECG capacity may create more waiting for Doctor Review.

**How to manage it:**
Use end-to-end simulation and monitor total visit duration, not just one queue. Every intervention should be tested for downstream effects.

### B. Cost versus waiting-time reduction

Adding staff can reduce waits, but permanent staffing increases cost. The best approach is often targeted, time-limited capacity rather than blanket staffing increases.

Examples:

* Add a floating clerk only during Monday morning peaks.
* Shift staff schedules rather than hiring.
* Use cross-trained staff instead of dedicated extra staff.
* Use digital intake to reduce administrative load before adding headcount.

### C. Staff workload and burnout

Reducing patient waits by pushing staff harder is not sustainable. Shorter waits should come from better flow, not unsafe compression of clinical work.

Guardrails:

* Monitor overtime.
* Monitor missed breaks.
* Monitor consultation duration distributions.
* Monitor documentation completion.
* Include staff feedback in pilots.

### D. Care quality risk

Some strategies may unintentionally reduce quality:

* Shortening consultations may miss clinical issues.
* Pre-ordering diagnostics may increase unnecessary testing.
* Fast-tracking some patients may create perceived unfairness.
* Digital check-in may disadvantage patients with limited digital access.
* Excessive standardization may not fit complex cases.

Guardrails:

* Do not set targets that pressure clinicians to reduce clinically necessary time.
* Track clinical quality indicators alongside waiting time.
* Use protocol-based pre-ordering only where medically approved.
* Keep assisted check-in options available.

### E. Fairness and prioritization

Urgent patients should be prioritized, but frequent urgent add-ons may increase waits for normal patients. Conversely, strict first-come-first-served logic may be clinically inappropriate.

**How to balance:**
Use explicit queue classes:

* Urgent clinical priority
* Scheduled appointment priority
* Simple follow-up fast track
* Complex new-patient pathway
* Diagnostic-heavy pathway

The goal is transparent, clinically justified prioritization.

### F. Utilization versus responsiveness

A clinic operating at extremely high utilization will almost always experience queues. Some slack is necessary for responsiveness, urgent cases, and variation.

**How to balance:**
Set resource-utilization targets by function. For highly variable resources such as doctors and diagnostic equipment, do not aim for 100% utilization. Instead, use simulation to find the point where wait-time reduction is worth the cost of additional slack.

### G. Choosing among conflicting objectives

I would use a multi-objective decision framework:

| Objective          | Example measure                                                                   |
| ------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Reduce waiting     | P90 wait by queue, total visit duration                                           |
| Control cost       | Staff hours, overtime, cost per visit                                             |
| Maintain quality   | Consultation duration, documentation quality, follow-up safety indicators         |
| Protect staff      | Workload, utilization, overtime, missed breaks                                    |
| Improve experience | Satisfaction, complaints, perceived wait                                          |
| Preserve equity    | Wait-time differences by patient type, urgency, specialty, age group if available |

The best intervention is not necessarily the one with the lowest average wait. It is the one that improves patient experience materially while staying within staffing, budget, quality, and safety constraints.

## 5. Measuring Success

### Core KPIs after implementation

I would track KPIs at three levels: queue-level, visit-level, and system-level.

### A. Queue-level KPIs

For each major transition:

* Average waiting time
* Median waiting time
* P90 and P95 waiting time
* Percentage of patients waiting longer than SLA
* Number of patients experiencing excessive waits
* Total waiting burden in minutes
* Queue length by time of day
* Time from patient-ready to next activity start, if patient-ready events are added
* Waiting time by patient type, urgency, specialty, and resource

Example SLA thresholds:

* Registration wait: ≤ 10 minutes
* Nurse wait after registration: ≤ 15 minutes
* Doctor wait after nurse assessment: ≤ 20 or 30 minutes, depending on specialty
* Diagnostic wait: ≤ 20 minutes
* Check-out wait: ≤ 10 minutes

These thresholds should be validated with clinic leadership and patient-experience expectations.

### B. Visit-level KPIs

* Total visit duration, also called lead time or flow time
* Median and P90 total visit duration
* Percentage of visits completed within target duration
* Total idle time per visit
* Percentage of total visit time spent waiting
* Number of handoffs per visit
* Number of diagnostic loops or repeat reviews
* Visits with rework, missing documentation, or repeated activities
* Left-without-being-seen rate, if applicable
* Appointment start delay

Lead time or flow time is the total time from case creation to case completion; this should be measured alongside service time and waiting time to avoid optimizing one stage while worsening the overall visit. ([Process Mining][3])

### C. Resource and operational KPIs

* Staff utilization by role, resource, hour, and day
* Room and equipment utilization
* Overtime hours
* Missed or delayed breaks
* Patients served per staff hour
* Cost per visit
* End-of-session overrun
* No-show and late-arrival rates
* Diagnostic equipment downtime
* Documentation completion time

### D. Patient-experience KPIs

* Patient satisfaction score
* Net Promoter Score, if used
* Complaints related to waiting
* Perceived wait versus actual wait
* Percentage of patients receiving delay communication
* Satisfaction by patient type and specialty

### E. Quality and safety KPIs

* Consultation duration distribution
* Unplanned return visits
* Escalations or adverse events, if available
* Diagnostic rework
* Missed follow-up orders
* Documentation completeness
* Clinician-reported quality concerns

### Ongoing process monitoring

The clinic should use the same event-log structure to build a continuous monitoring loop:

1. **Daily or weekly event-log refresh**
   Pull new START and COMPLETE events from registration, EHR, diagnostic, rooming, and billing systems.

2. **Automated queue calculation**
   Recompute waiting times for each transition.

3. **Dashboard by queue and patient segment**
   Show current performance versus baseline.

4. **Control charts**
   Track whether changes are statistically meaningful or just normal variation.

5. **Before-after comparison**
   Compare pre-intervention and post-intervention periods, adjusted for volume, case mix, specialty, weekday, and seasonality.

6. **Drill-down capability**
   Allow managers to filter by doctor, nurse, room, patient type, urgency, specialty, and time of day.

7. **Alerting**
   Trigger alerts when P90 wait, queue length, or excessive-wait rate crosses a threshold.

8. **Monthly improvement review**
   Review whether the bottleneck moved elsewhere, whether costs rose, and whether quality indicators stayed stable.

9. **Simulation recalibration**
   Update the simulation model with new service-time and arrival distributions after each intervention.

10. **Data-quality monitoring**
    Track missing timestamps, unmatched START/COMPLETE records, duplicate events, and inconsistent resource logging.

## Practical end-to-end approach

A strong implementation roadmap would look like this:

1. **Build a clean activity-instance log** from START and COMPLETE events.
2. **Calculate service times and transition waiting times** for every visit.
3. **Create a process map with waiting-time overlays.**
4. **Rank critical queues** using total waiting burden, P90 wait, SLA breaches, volume, and patient impact.
5. **Segment the problem** by specialty, patient type, urgency, time of day, day of week, and resource.
6. **Diagnose root causes** using resource analysis, variant analysis, bottleneck analysis, and queue-length reconstruction.
7. **Test improvement scenarios** through discrete-event simulation before changing operations.
8. **Pilot targeted interventions** such as peak-time staffing, appointment-template redesign, digital intake, diagnostic parallelization, and handoff dashboards.
9. **Measure post-implementation KPIs** against baseline.
10. **Continue monitoring** to prevent bottlenecks from reappearing or shifting unnoticed.

The key principle is that the clinic should not merely ask, “Where is the longest wait?” It should ask, “Which waits contribute most to total patient burden, why do they occur, which patients are most affected, and which intervention reduces them without harming cost, staff workload, or care quality?”
