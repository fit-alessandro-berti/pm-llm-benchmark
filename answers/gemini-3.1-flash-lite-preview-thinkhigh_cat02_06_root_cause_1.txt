*   **Lack of Queue Prioritization:**
    *   The delay in Case 105 indicates that once a ticket is escalated, it may be sitting in an unmonitored or overloaded queue, as it took over 24 hours just to begin the investigation phase after the initial escalation.

### 3. Insights and Recommendations

**Insights:**
*   **Escalation Bottleneck:** The process is highly inefficient once it leaves the Level-1 stage. There is a lack of SLAs (Service Level Agreements) for Level-2 response times, leading to tickets "stalling" after escalation.
*   **Assignment Latency:** Even for non-escalated tickets (Case 104), there is significant downtime between the assignment of a ticket and the start of the investigation. This suggests that agents might be overwhelmed or that the triage process is not effectively matching ticket volume to agent capacity.

**Recommendations:**
1.  **Implement Escalation SLAs:** Introduce a mandatory time-to-acknowledge threshold for Level-2 agents. If a ticket is escalated, it must be picked up within a specific timeframe (e.g., 60 minutes) to prevent the "stalling" seen in Case 105.
2.  **Automated Load Balancing:** The delays between "Assign" and "Investigate" suggest that agents are manually picking up tickets or are overloaded. Implementing a "pull" system or a smarter distribution algorithm could ensure that tickets don't sit idle for 3+ hours after being assigned.
3.  **Intermediate Status Monitoring:** Introduce a status of "Awaiting Assignment" or "Awaiting Level-2 Review." This would allow management to visualize exactly where tickets are idling and identify which departments or teams are causing the longest wait times.
4.  **Triage Refinement:** Review why Case 104 and 102 required so much time post-assignment. It is possible that the "Triage" phase is not correctly identifying the complexity of the issue, leading to "mismatched" assignments that then require late escalations or delays.
