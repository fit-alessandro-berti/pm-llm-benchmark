# Data-Driven Scheduling Optimization for Precision Parts Inc.
*A Senior Operations Analyst Response*

## 1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics

To reconstruct and quantify the actual shop floor dynamics, I would deploy a multi-layered process mining framework directly on the MES event logs. The analysis moves from process discovery to performance quantification, conformance checking, and causal analysis.

*   **Process Reconstruction & Flow Analysis:** 
    *   **Technique:** Process discovery (Heuristic Miner or Inductive Miner) combined with trace clustering. This maps the *actual* routing variants (not just the BOM/routing file) and identifies frequently skipped, looped, or reworked operations.
    *   **Metrics:** Cycle time distributions per variant, lead time percentiles (p50, p95), and makespan decomposition (processing + setup + waiting + idle). Trace alignment against the nominal routing model quantifies deviation frequency.
*   **Task Waiting Times & Queue Dynamics:**
    *   **Technique:** Filter logs for `Queue Entry` events. Calculate inter-event time between `Queue Entry` and `Setup Start` (or `Task Start`). Aggregate by work center and time-of-day.
    *   **Metrics:** Average/median queue wait time, queue depth time-series, and congestion heatmaps. This reveals temporal bottlenecks and work center imbalances.
*   **Resource Utilization & OEE Decomposition:**
    *   **Technique:** Resource performance mining. Classify events into productive, setup, idle, and breakdown states using `Setup Required`, `Notes`, and timestamp gaps.
    *   **Metrics:** Availability, Performance, and Quality (OPQ) components. Operator vs. machine utilization rates, productive vs. non-productive time ratios, and shift-level throughput variance.
*   **Sequence-Dependent Setup Time Quantification:**
    *   **Technique:** Extract consecutive task pairs on the same `Resource`. Filter where `Setup Required = TRUE`. Calculate `Setup Duration = Setup End - Setup Start`. Cross-tabulate with job attributes (material grade, tooling, previous job ID, color/finish).
    *   **Metrics:** Build a **Setup Time Transition Matrix**. Calculate mean/variance of setup times per sequence pair. Identify high-cost transitions (e.g., switching from hard alloy to soft aluminum on CUT-01) and frequent long-setup chains.
*   **Schedule Adherence & Tardiness:**
    *   **Technique:** Join event log with order master data (`Order Due Date`). Calculate `Lateness = Task End Timestamp - Due Date`. Use conformance checking to map tardiness to specific process stages.
    *   **Metrics:** Tardiness frequency (% jobs late), mean/median lateness, penalty cost exposure, and stage-wise delay contribution (e.g., 60% of delay occurs at Heat Treatment).
*   **Disruption Impact Analysis:**
    *   **Technique:** Filter for `Breakdown Start/End` and `Priority Change` events. Use causal discovery (e.g., PCM algorithm) and before/after statistical comparison on KPIs.
    *   **Metrics:** Recovery time post-breakdown, ripple effect depth (how many downstream jobs delayed), tardiness delta during/after priority changes, and setup time inflation during disruption recovery.

## 2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies

Process mining provides empirical evidence to move beyond anecdotal complaints and pinpoint systemic scheduling failures.

*   **Bottleneck Identification & Throughput Impact:** Resource performance mining will likely reveal 1-2 machines (e.g., CNC Milling or Heat Treatment) with >95% utilization, longest queue wait times, and highest setup variance. Bottleneck theory applied to PM data quantifies throughput loss: e.g., "A 10% increase in CUT-01 setup time reduces shop-wide throughput by 7.2%."
*   **Poor Task Prioritization:** Variant analysis comparing on-time vs. late jobs will show that high-priority jobs are frequently processed after medium/low priority jobs due to FCFS dominance. Correlation analysis will reveal negative or zero correlation between `Order Priority` and actual processing order, directly linking to tardiness spikes.
*   **Suboptimal Sequencing & Setup Inflation:** The setup transition matrix will expose sequences where `Setup Required = TRUE` triggers >45 min setups. If the current dispatching ignores these, the total weekly setup time will be significantly higher than the theoretical minimum (TSP lower bound). Process mining will show that bottleneck machines spend 25-35% of available time on high-cost setups.
*   **Starvation & Upstream/Downstream Decoupling:** Queue depth time-series and resource contention analysis will show downstream machines (e.g., Grinding) with high idle time while upstream queues grow. This indicates local optimization: upstream work centers push jobs regardless of downstream capacity, creating WIP bubbles.
*   **WIP Bullwhip Effect:** Trace clustering and inter-arrival time analysis will show that variability amplifies across stages. Jobs released in batches create surges that downstream queues cannot absorb, leading to exponential WIP growth despite stable order arrival rates.

## 3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness

Process mining enables precise differentiation between scheduling logic failures, capacity constraints, and inherent process variability.

*   **Static Rule Limitations in Dynamic Contexts:** Conformance checking reveals that EDD/FCFS rules are applied uniformly regardless of shop state. When the shop is near capacity, FCFS causes high-priority jobs to miss dates. When capacity is slack, EDD causes excessive context switching and setup inflation. The rule is state-blind.
*   **Lack of Real-Time Visibility & Global Optimization:** Resource/process mining shows work centers operate as isolated silos. Upstream machines release jobs without checking downstream queue limits, causing starvation downstream and WIP accumulation. The scheduling horizon is effectively zero.
*   **Inaccurate Duration/Setup Estimations:** Planned vs. Actual duration analysis shows systematic underestimation (e.g., planned 60 min, actual mean 72 min with high variance). This creates optimistic schedules that fail immediately upon execution. Setup times are treated as constants rather than sequence-dependent variables.
*   **Poor Coordination & Disruption Response:** Process flow analysis shows no rescheduling triggers. When a breakdown occurs, the queue order remains static, causing cascading delays. Priority changes are logged but not propagated to dispatching logic, leading to urgent jobs waiting behind completed low-priority ones.
*   **Differentiating Scheduling vs. Capacity vs. Variability:** 
    *   **Scheduling Issue:** If bottleneck utilization is <85% but queues are long and tardiness is high, the issue is prioritization/sequencing logic.
    *   **Capacity Issue:** If bottleneck utilization is >95% with near-zero idle time and queues grow linearly with arrival rate, the issue is insufficient capacity.
    *   **Variability Issue:** If processing times show high standard deviation regardless of resource load, and lead times vary wildly for identical jobs, the issue is process instability (tool wear, operator skill variance, material inconsistency). PM decomposes lead time into `Processing + Setup + Waiting + Idle`. High `Waiting` = scheduling/prioritization. High `Processing` = capacity. High `Setup` = sequencing. High `Variance` = process instability.

## 4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies

Three complementary strategies address the diagnosed pathologies, leveraging PM insights for design and validation.

### Strategy 1: Multi-Criteria Dynamic Dispatching with Setup-Awareness & Lookahead
*   **Core Logic:** Replace FCFS/EDD with a real-time scoring function evaluated at queue entry:
    `Score = wｷ(Due Date - t) + wｷ(Remaining Processing Time) + wｷ(Priority) - wｷ(Estimated Sequence-Dependent Setup) - wｷ(Downstream Queue Load)`
    Weights `w` are dynamically adjusted based on shop load factor (e.g., increase `w` when tardiness risk is high, increase `w` when bottleneck setup time dominates).
*   **PM Data Usage:** Setup transition matrix for `w`; queue depth heatmaps for `w`; historical tardiness/priority correlation for weight calibration; bottleneck identification to apply lookahead only at constrained resources.
*   **Pathology Addressed:** Poor prioritization, suboptimal sequencing, starvation. Prevents high-priority jobs from being blocked; minimizes setup inflation at bottlenecks; balances upstream/downstream flow.
*   **Expected KPI Impact:**  Tardiness by 30-40%;  WIP by 20-25%;  Bottleneck utilization;  Lead time variability.

### Strategy 2: Predictive Rolling-Horizon Rescheduling with Real-Time Adaptation
*   **Core Logic:** Implement a rolling-horizon scheduler (e.g., Constraint Programming or Genetic Algorithm) that reschedules every 2 hours or upon major events. Uses ML models to predict actual task durations and completion times. Integrates predictive maintenance alerts to preemptively adjust capacity.
*   **PM Data Usage:** Survival analysis & gradient boosting on `Planned vs Actual` durations conditioned on operator, machine, and job complexity; breakdown frequency/duration distributions for reliability modeling; setup time predictors for sequence optimization within the solver.
*   **Pathology Addressed:** Unpredictable lead times, disruption impact, inaccurate estimations. Creates realistic, feasible schedules. Proactively absorbs shocks rather than reacting to them.
*   **Expected KPI Impact:**  Schedule predictability (p95 lead time accuracy);  Tardiness during disruptions;  On-time delivery rate;  Expediting costs.

### Strategy 3: Bottleneck-Centric Setup Optimization & Drum-Buffer-Rope Release Control
*   **Core Logic:** At identified bottleneck machines, implement setup-minimizing sequencing (e.g., nearest-neighbor heuristic on the PM-derived setup matrix, or TSP solver for daily batches). Apply Theory of Constraints (Drum-Buffer-Rope): the bottleneck sets the pace (Drum), a time buffer protects it, and job release (Rope) is controlled to prevent WIP accumulation.
*   **PM Data Usage:** Setup transition matrix for sequencing; bottleneck utilization/queue time-series for buffer sizing; WIP bullwhip analysis for release rate calibration.
*   **Pathology Addressed:** High sequence-dependent setups, bottleneck overload, WIP bullwhip. Reduces non-value-added time at the constraint; prevents upstream flooding; stabilizes flow.
*   **Expected KPI Impact:**  Total setup time by 25-35%;  Throughput by 10-15%;  WIP by 30-40%;  Lead time by 20-25%.

## 5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement

### Discrete-Event Simulation (DES) for Rigorous Validation
*   **Parameterization:** Build a DES model (e.g., SimPy, AnyLogic, or Arena) calibrated with PM-derived distributions: task time PDFs, setup time transition matrices, routing probabilities, inter-arrival distributions, breakdown frequency/duration, and operator skill factors.
*   **Validation:** Run the baseline model against historical data. Use statistical validation (e.g., ｲ test for distributions, confidence interval overlap for KPIs) to ensure the digital twin replicates actual performance.
*   **Scenario Testing:** Use Design of Experiments (DoE) or Monte Carlo simulation to test:
    *   *Load Variations:* 80%, 100%, 110% capacity.
    *   *Disruption Profiles:* Breakdowns every 4h vs. 8h; 10% vs. 25% hot jobs.
    *   *Strategy Combinations:* Strategy 1 alone, 2 alone, 3 alone, and integrated.
*   **Evaluation:** Compare KPIs across scenarios using statistical significance testing (t-tests, ANOVA). Identify robust strategies that perform well across variability, not just under ideal conditions. Select the Pareto-optimal strategy for pilot deployment.

### Framework for Continuous Monitoring & Adaptation
*   **Real-Time Process Mining:** Stream MES events via Kafka/Spark to a real-time process mining engine. Compute KPIs (tardiness, WIP, utilization, setup time) on a rolling window.
*   **Drift Detection:** Implement algorithms (e.g., ADWIN, Page-Hinkley) to detect statistical drift in lead times, setup durations, or arrival patterns. Trigger alerts when KPIs deviate beyond control limits.
*   **Closed-Loop Adaptation:** 
    *   *Automated Weight Adjustment:* If tardiness risk exceeds threshold, dynamically increase `w` in Strategy 1.
    *   *Buffer Resizing:* If WIP bullwhip detected, automatically adjust Drum-Buffer size in Strategy 3.
    *   *Model Retraining:* Retrain duration/setup prediction models weekly using new log data to capture tool wear, operator learning, or material changes.
*   **Digital Twin Integration:** Maintain a live digital twin that compares predicted vs. actual performance. Use conformance checking to detect new routing variants or process deviations. Feed insights back to the scheduling engine and ERP/MES for continuous improvement.

This approach transforms Precision Parts Inc.'s scheduling from a reactive, rule-based exercise into a proactive, data-driven control system. By grounding every scheduling decision in mined historical reality, validating through simulation, and maintaining continuous feedback loops, the company can systematically eliminate scheduling pathologies, stabilize flow, and deliver predictable, on-time performance in a highly variable job shop environment.
