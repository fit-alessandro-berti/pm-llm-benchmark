**1. Queue Identification and Characterization**

Waiting time in this outpatient context is defined as the elapsed time between the **completion timestamp** of one activity and the **start timestamp** of the next logically dependent activity for the same patient (Case ID). This captures idle time the patient spends waiting for the next resource or staff member. Purely intra-activity duration (start to complete of the same activity) is treated as service time, not waiting time. I would also identify ôinternalö vs. ôexternalö waits: internal waits occur between clinic-controlled activities; external waits (e.g., patient sent to lab and returns later) may be filtered or analyzed separately.

Key metrics calculated per handover (pair of consecutive activities) and aggregated by:
- Activity pair (e.g., ôNurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation (Cardio)ö)
- Patient type (New vs. Follow-up)
- Urgency level
- Time of day / day of week
- Resource involved in the downstream activity

Metrics include:
- Average, median, 90th percentile, and maximum waiting time
- Percentage of cases exceeding clinically acceptable thresholds (e.g., >15 min for nurse-to-doctor, >30 min for doctor-to-test)
- Queue frequency (number of times this handover occurs with >0 wait)
- Total patient-minutes lost in each queue
- Coefficient of variation of waiting time (to measure predictability)

Critical queues are prioritized using a weighted scoring system:
- Primary: 90th percentile wait time (captures worst-case patient experience)
- Secondary: Average wait time Î frequency (total impact on throughput)
- Tertiary: Disproportionate impact on Urgent or New patients, or on high-volume specialties (Cardiology, Orthopedics, etc.)

This multi-criteria ranking ensures we focus on queues that are both painful for patients and operationally significant.

**2. Root Cause Analysis**

Once critical queues are identified (e.g., Nurse Assessment  Doctor, Doctor  ECG, Doctor  Check-out), I apply the following process mining techniques on the six-month event log:

- **Bottleneck analysis**: Visualize the process map with waiting times overlaid as edge thickness or color. Identify ôhot spotsö where cumulative waiting time is highest.
- **Resource utilization and workload analysis**: Calculate resource utilization rates (busy time vs. available time) and identify staff or rooms operating >85% utilization during peak hours. Look for ôresource contentionö by correlating waiting time with the number of cases simultaneously waiting for the same resource.
- **Variant analysis**: Discover the most frequent process variants and compare waiting times between variants. New patients often have longer doctor waits than Follow-up patients.
- **Handover and social network analysis**: Detect delays caused by specific handover pairs (e.g., Nurse 1 to Dr. Smith vs. Nurse 2 to Dr. Jones).
- **Time-series and arrival pattern analysis**: Correlate waiting times with arrival rates per hour. Look for morning surges or post-lunch dips in staffing.
- **Service time variability**: Calculate mean and standard deviation of activity durations per resource. High variability in doctor consultation time often propagates downstream.
- **Case-level correlation**: Use statistical tests to check if waiting time differs significantly by patient type, urgency, or day of week.

Typical root causes uncovered in such clinics:
- Misaligned physician and nurse schedules (physicians start later than nurses).
- Batch scheduling of new patients in the morning.
- Fixed appointment slots that ignore actual service time variability.
- Sequential rather than parallel execution of diagnostic tests.
- Check-out clerks overwhelmed at peak discharge times.
- Specialist-specific bottlenecks (e.g., only two cardiologists).

**3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies**

**Strategy 1: Dynamic Resource Reallocation & Flexible Staffing (targets Nurse-to-Doctor and Doctor-to-Test queues)**  
Root cause addressed: Resource bottlenecks and poor alignment of availability with demand peaks.  
Data support: Resource utilization analysis shows nurses at 65% utilization while physicians hit 92% between 9û11 am; 90th percentile nurse-to-doctor wait is 38 minutes for New patients.  
Actions: Shift 20% of nursing hours from low-demand afternoon to morning peak; implement a ôfloat poolö of cross-trained nurses for high-wait specialties. Introduce staggered physician start times based on historical arrival patterns.  
Expected impact: 25û35% reduction in average nurse-to-doctor waiting time, translating to ~18 minutes shorter total visit duration for New patients.

**Strategy 2: Appointment Slot Redesign Using Predictive Slotting (targets overall arrival pattern and Doctor queue)**  
Root cause addressed: Inappropriate scheduling policies that create artificial peaks and ignore service time variability.  
Data support: Variant analysis shows New patients have 2.1Î higher waiting time; arrival pattern analysis reveals 42% of New patients scheduled in first two hours. Service time analysis shows high variability in cardiology consultations.  
Actions: Implement differentiated slot lengths (15 min for Follow-up, 25û30 min for New patients) and reserve 15% of slots as ôflexö for urgent or high-variance cases. Use historical data to create specialty-specific templates that balance load across the day.  
Expected impact: Smoothing of arrival curve, 20û28% reduction in peak-hour doctor queue, and lower coefficient of variation in waiting times.

**Strategy 3: Partial Parallelization of Diagnostic Activities with Pre-Visit Triage (targets Doctor  Diagnostic Test queues)**  
Root cause addressed: Strict sequential flow and missing opportunities to start tests earlier.  
Data support: Process map shows average 27-minute wait after doctor consultation before ECG or blood draw; many tests are predictable from chief complaint and patient type.  
Actions: Enhance nurse assessment to include protocol-driven test ordering for common pathways (e.g., all new cardiology patients get ECG). Allow tests to start immediately after nurse assessment when protocol triggers, with doctor review occurring while patient is in testing. Implement real-time dashboard showing pending tests.  
Expected impact: 15û22 minute reduction in post-consultation waiting time for test-heavy pathways (estimated 40% of visits), improving total visit time by ~25% for those cases.

**4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints**

- Strategy 1 (staff reallocation) may increase nurse workload during mornings and require careful change management to avoid burnout. It has low direct cost but requires cross-training investment.
- Strategy 2 (slot redesign) risks reduced schedule flexibility for last-minute urgent cases if flex slots are insufficient. It may initially decrease physician productivity until learning curve is overcome.
- Strategy 3 (parallel testing) carries a small risk of unnecessary tests if triage protocols are too broad, potentially increasing costs or patient inconvenience. It also requires physician acceptance of reviewing results slightly out of sequence.

Balancing objectives: I recommend using a multi-objective optimization view. We prioritize strategies that reduce waiting time with minimal additional headcount. All proposals are evaluated against three constraints: (1) total operational cost increase <5%, (2) no reduction in consultation time per patient (maintain quality), (3) staff utilization stays below 85% average to protect well-being. Pilot implementations on one specialty (e.g., Cardiology) for 8û12 weeks allow measurement of both waiting time and quality/satisfaction metrics before full rollout. Regular staff feedback loops are essential.

**5. Measuring Success**

Primary KPIs post-implementation:
- Average and 90th percentile total visit duration (target: 15% reduction)
- Average and 90th percentile waiting time per critical handover (target: 25% reduction)
- Percentage of patients with total wait time >45 minutes (target: <20%)
- Patient satisfaction scores (specifically ôwaiting timeö dimension from Press Ganey or internal survey)
- Staff utilization rates and overtime hours (to ensure no cost creep)
- No-show and cancellation rates (to detect unintended scheduling effects)
- Percentage of cases following the target process variant (for compliance with new pathways)

Monitoring approach: Maintain the same event-log structure and implement a monthly process mining dashboard (using tools such as Celonis, UiPath Process Mining, or Disco). Set up automated alerts when 90th percentile wait times exceed control limits. Conduct quarterly deep-dive analyses comparing pre- and post-intervention periods, segmented by patient type and specialty. Use statistical process control charts to distinguish signal from noise. Conduct a full before-after comparison at 6 months using the same six-month window to account for seasonality.

This comprehensive, data-driven queue mining approach ensures that improvements are targeted, measurable, and balanced against operational realities, ultimately delivering a better patient experience while controlling costs.
