### 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

#### Defining and Calculating Waiting Time (Queue Time)
Using the event log, we first reconstruct each patientÆs visit as a **case trace**, ordering events by `Timestamp` within each `Case ID`. For any two consecutive activities in a trace, say Activity *A* followed by Activity *B*:

*   **Service Time (Processing Time):** `Timestamp(COMPLETE) û Timestamp(START)` for a given activity. This is the time a resource (staff, room, equipment) is actively engaged with the patient.
*   **Waiting Time (Queue Time):** `Timestamp(START of B) û Timestamp(COMPLETE of A)` for the same `Case ID`.

This inter-stage gap represents the time a patient is idle, waiting for the next resource to become available, for a handover to occur, or for a scheduled slot to begin. We must data-clean negative values (indicating data quality issues or parallel logging) and flag extreme outliers (e.g., >4 hours) that may represent intra-day holds or data errors rather than typical operational queues.

#### Key Metrics for Queue Characterization
For every transition pair (e.g., Registration  Nurse Assessment), we calculate:
*   **Central Tendency:** Average and **median** waiting time. The median is critical in healthcare because wait times are typically right-skewed (a few very long waits distort the mean).
*   **Variability & Tail Risk:** Standard deviation, **90th and 95th percentile** wait times, and maximum wait. High variability indicates an unpredictable patient experience.
*   **Volume & Frequency:** The absolute number of cases experiencing the queue, and the queueÆs **frequency** as a percentage of all visits.
*   **Cumulative Patient Delay:** `Frequency Î Average Wait Time`. This reveals which queues consume the most total patient-hours.
*   **SLA Breach Rate:** The percentage of cases exceeding clinically/administratively defined thresholds (e.g., >15 minutes before Nurse Assessment; >30 minutes before Doctor Consultation).
*   **Stratified Metrics:** All metrics computed separately by `Patient Type` (New vs. Follow-up), `Urgency`, `Specialty`, and `Resource`.

#### Identifying the Most Critical Queues
We prioritize queues using a **multi-criteria scoring matrix**, not just average wait time:
1.  **Pareto Impact:** Rank queues by total cumulative patient delay. The top 20% of transitions typically account for ~80% of wasted patient time.
2.  **Patient Impact Weighting:** A queue before **Doctor Consultation** is weighted more heavily than one before Check-out because it affects perceived quality of care and patient anxiety. Similarly, queues affecting **Urgent** patients are escalated regardless of frequency.

This is restricted to cohorts where the data shows >75% historical probability and clinical governance approves.
*   **Potential Impact:** **Elimination of ~80% of Blood Test/ECG queue times** for eligible patient cohorts; **15û20 minute net reduction in critical path length**; improved consult quality because diagnostic data is available at the moment the physician sees the patient.

### 4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints

#### Trade-offs and Risks
*   **Dynamic Pooling & Staggered Shifts:** Cross-training and staggered shifts incur **training costs** and may face staff resistance due to childcare/commute disruptions. Pooled queues can reduce the personal rapport of a dedicated clerk, potentially affecting patient experience for some demographics. There is a risk of **role confusion** if clerks perform clinical tasks without clear scope boundaries.
*   **Predictive Diagnostic Booking:** Probabilistically reserving ECG/lab slots will inevitably result in **idle diagnostic capacity** when the doctor decides a test is unnecessary. This reduces diagnostic resource utilization (increasing cost per test) and requires an overbooking buffer. It also adds **scheduling complexity** and IT integration costs between the EHR and scheduling systems.
*   **Parallel Pre-Consultation Testing:** Nurse-initiated testing carries **medical-legal risk** if a test is performed without a contemporaneous physician order. It requires robust standing-order protocols. There is a small risk of **duplicate testing** if the physician needs a different panel. It also shifts workload to the nursing front end, potentially creating a new bottleneck at Nurse Assessment if not resourced.

#### Balancing Conflicting Objectives
We use the event log to power a **multi-objective discrete-event simulation (DES)** to find Pareto-optimal solutions before implementation. The simulation evaluates trade-offs between:
*   **Patient Wait Time** (primary goal)
*   **Resource Utilization** (cost control)
*   **Clinical Quality** (hard constraint: minimum consult times, mandatory triage accuracy)

Rather than maximizing resource utilization (which inevitably maximizes queues per queueing theory), we target **optimal utilization** (e.g., 75û80% for front-end staff, 80û85% for doctors) as a deliberate buffer. Cost is controlled by **redeploying existing capacity** (shifting staff times, cross-training, better scheduling) rather than hiring. Care quality is protected by using the event log to define **non-negotiable service time floors** for clinical activitiesùour strategies reduce *waiting*, not *treatment* time.

### 5. Measuring Success

#### Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)
To evaluate post-implementation effectiveness, we define:

**Primary Effectiveness KPIs:**
*   **Queue-Specific Metrics:** Mean, median, and 90th percentile waiting time for each transition (e.g., RegNurse, NurseDoctor, DoctorTest).
*   **System Cycle Time:** End-to-end visit duration (`last COMPLETE` û `first START`), stratified by specialty and patient type.
*   **Patient Experience:** Patient Satisfaction Score (e.g., Likert scale on "waiting time") and Net Promoter Score (NPS).

**Secondary Operational & Financial KPIs:**
*   **Throughput:** Patients served per clinic day per specialty.
*   **Resource Utilization:** Utilization by role (clerks, nurses, doctors, diagnostic techs) to ensure we have not over-corrected into costly idleness.
*   **SLA Breach Rate:** % of visits exceeding threshold wait times per stage.
*   **Left Without Being Seen (LWBS) Rate:** A direct indicator of extreme dissatisfaction.
*   **Cost per Visit:** To ensure operational cost discipline is maintained.

#### Ongoing Monitoring and Sustainability
*   **Automated Queue Mining Pipeline:** Establish a weekly ETL process that loads new event log data into the process mining environment. Re-calculate all queue metrics automatically to detect regression or bottleneck shift.
*   **Statistical Process Control (SPC):** Implement control charts (e.g., Shewhart or CUSUM) for median wait times per critical queue. An alert triggers if a metric crosses an upper control limit for two consecutive weeks.
*   **Real-Time Operational Dashboards:** Feed live timestamp data to a dashboard showing current queue lengths at Registration, Nursing, and Diagnostics, enabling real-time interventions (e.g., calling in a floater nurse).
*   **Before/After & Cohort Comparison:** Use the same event log definitions to compare a 90-day post-implementation window against the baseline, controlling for seasonality. Where possible, use **A/B testing** (e.g., implement Strategy 2 only in Cardiology for one month while keeping Internal Medicine as a control) to isolate causal impact.
*   **Quarterly Variant Re-Analysis:** As patient mixes and diseases change, new variants and queues may emerge. Quarterly reviews ensure the clinic adapts its strategies rather than optimizing for a static historical state.

By embedding queue mining into both the diagnostic *and* continuous monitoring phases, the clinic transitions from reactive complaint management to a proactive, data-driven patient flow operation.
