### 1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics

To move beyond intuition, we first treat the MES event logs as a "digital twin" of historical operations.

**Process Reconstruction:**
I will use **Case-Centric Process Mining** (using the Job ID as the case) to visualize the "Happy Path" versus the "Actual Path." By mapping the sequence of activities (Cutting $\rightarrow$ Milling $\rightarrow$ etc.), we can identify common routing variants. Simultaneously, I will apply **Resource-Centric Process Mining** to view the shop floor from the machine's perspective, treating the Machine ID as the case to see the sequence of jobs processed on a specific resource.

**Quantifying Metrics via Process Mining:**
*   **Flow and Lead Times:** I will calculate the **Cycle Time** (Task Start to Task End) and **Lead Time** (Job Released to Final Inspection). By plotting these as probability density functions (PDFs), we can identify if lead times are normally distributed or exhibit "long tails" (indicating unpredictable outliers).
*   **Task Waiting Times (Queue Analysis):** By calculating the delta between `Queue Entry` and `Setup Start`, we can quantify **Queue Time**. I will map these wait times across the process map to identify where jobs "pile up," distinguishing between waiting for a machine and waiting for an operator.
*   **Resource Utilization:** I will categorize every single minute of a machine's timeline into four states: *Productive* (Task Start to End), *Setup* (Setup Start to End), *Idle* (No activity), and *Down* (Breakdown Start to End).
*   **Sequence-Dependent Setup Times:** I will extract all consecutive pairs of jobs on each machine (e.g., $\text{Job}_A \rightarrow \text{Job}_B$). By aggregating the `Setup Duration` for these pairs, I will construct a **Setup Matrix**. This reveals, for example, that switching from "Aluminum" to "Steel" takes 60 minutes, while "Steel" to "Steel" takes 10 minutes.
*   **Schedule Adherence and Tardiness:** I will calculate the **Lateness** (Actual Completion - Due Date). I will use **Conformance Checking** to compare the planned sequence (if any existed) against the actual execution sequence to see where the plan deviated.
*   **Disruption Impact:** Using "Breakdown" and "Priority Change" events as anchors, I will perform a **Temporal Impact Analysis**. I will measure the "Recovery Time"—how long it takes for the queue length and tardiness levels to return to baseline after a disruption.

### 2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies

Using the metrics above, I will identify specific systemic failures:

*   **Bottleneck Identification:** I will look for resources with the highest **Average Queue Time** and **Utilization $\approx 100\%$**, while downstream resources show high **Idle Time** (starvation). If the "Heat Treatment" station has a 48-hour queue while "Grinding" is idle 40% of the time, Heat Treatment is the primary constraint.
*   **Prioritization Failure:** By creating a scatter plot of `Order Priority` vs. `Tardiness`, I can diagnose "Priority Inversion." If "High Priority" jobs have a similar tardiness distribution to "Medium Priority" jobs, it proves that the local EDD/FCFS rules are being overridden or are insufficient to "push" urgent jobs through the system.
*   **Setup Inefficiency:** I will analyze the **Setup-to-Processing Ratio**. If a machine spends 40% of its time in setup, and the log shows a "ping-pong" effect (switching between different job types rapidly), it indicates a lack of batching logic.
*   **WIP Bullwhip Effect:** By plotting **WIP levels** (number of active cases) over time across different work centers, I can identify "waves" of inventory. High variability in the "Cutting" stage often leads to amplified volatility in "Milling," creating an unstable flow.
*   **Evidence Generation:** I will use **Variant Analysis**. By comparing the process traces of "On-Time Jobs" vs. "Late Jobs," I can isolate the specific activity or resource where late jobs typically deviate from the successful path.

### 3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness

I will differentiate between **Capacity Issues** (not enough machines) and **Logic Issues** (poor scheduling).

*   **Logic vs. Capacity:** If a machine is under-utilized but the queue is long, the root cause is **poor sequencing** (e.g., a job is waiting for a specific operator or a specific setup). If the machine is at 100% utilization and the queue is still growing, the root cause is a **capacity deficit**.
*   **The "Local Optima" Trap:** Current dispatching (FCFS/EDD) is applied *locally*. Process mining will likely show that the Cutting station is optimizing for its own throughput, unknowingly flooding the Milling station (the bottleneck) with jobs it cannot handle, thereby increasing overall WIP without improving makespan.
*   **Estimation Gap:** I will compare `Task Duration (Planned)` vs. `Task Duration (Actual)`. If the variance is high and skewed, the scheduling is based on "ideal" times rather than "real" times, leading to a cascade of missed deadlines.
*   **Response Fragility:** Analysis of "Hot Jobs" will likely show that when a high-priority job is inserted, it disrupts the setup sequence for five other jobs, causing a net loss in productivity that outweighs the benefit of the urgent job's speed.

### 4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies

I propose the following three strategies, leveraging the mined data:

#### Strategy 1: Dynamic Multi-Criteria Dispatching (DMCD)
*   **Core Logic:** Replace simple EDD with a **Weighted Priority Score** calculated in real-time:
    $\text{Score} = (w_1 \times \text{Urgency}) + (w_2 \times \text{Setup\_Saving}) + (w_3 \times \text{Downstream\_Pressure})$
*   **Process Mining Integration:**
    *   $w_1$ (Urgency) is derived from the historical tardiness distribution.
    *   $w_2$ (Setup\_Saving) uses the **Setup Matrix** to reward jobs that match the current machine state.
    *   $w_3$ (Downstream\_Pressure) uses real-time queue lengths from the MES.
*   **Impact:** Reduces total setup time and prevents the "flooding" of bottlenecks, decreasing overall WIP and tardiness.

#### Strategy 2: Predictive Stochastic Scheduling
*   **Core Logic:** Instead of using a single "Planned Duration," the schedule will use **Probabilistic Time Windows** based on the 80th percentile of historical actuals for that specific Job-Resource-Operator combination.
*   **Process Mining Integration:** I will use the event logs to build a **Duration Prediction Model**. If the logs show that `Operator-105` is 20% slower at `CNC Milling` than `Operator-110`, the schedule adjusts dynamically based on who is assigned.
*   **Impact:** Increases the reliability of completion estimates provided to customers and reduces the "cascading delay" effect when a single task runs over.

#### Strategy 3: Intelligent Sequence-Based Batching
*   **Core Logic:** At bottleneck resources, the system will implement **"Cluster-First, Schedule-Second."** It groups jobs with similar setup requirements into "virtual batches," then sequences these batches based on the aggregate due date of the group.
*   **Process Mining Integration:** I will use **Clustering Algorithms** on the historical setup data to identify "Family Groups" of parts that share setup characteristics.
*   **Impact:** Drastically reduces the total time spent on setups at the bottleneck, effectively increasing the *available capacity* of the machine without buying new hardware.
