### 2. Root Cause Analysis

By examining the timestamps of the slow cases, three primary bottlenecks emerge:

#### A. The "Overnight" Dead Zone (Non-Working Hours Gap)
In every slow case, there is a massive jump in time between the final activity of Day 1 and the resolution on the following day.
*   **Case 102:** Investigate (03-01 14:00) $\rightarrow$ Resolve (03-02 09:00) = **19-hour gap**.
*   **Case 104:** Investigate (03-01 13:00) $\rightarrow$ Resolve (03-02 08:00) = **19-hour gap**.
*   **Case 105:** Investigate (03-02 14:00) $\rightarrow$ Resolve (03-03 09:00) = **19-hour gap**.
*   **Root Cause:** This suggests a lack of 24/7 coverage or a failure to hand over pending tickets to a night shift/next-day team, causing tickets to sit idle for nearly an entire day.

#### B. Escalation Latency
Escalations to Level-2 agents introduce severe delays.
*   **Case 102:** After escalating at 11:30, it took 2.5 hours to start investigation.
*   **Case 105:** After escalating at 10:00 on 03-01, the ticket was not investigated again until 14:00 on 03-02 (**a 28-hour delay**).
*   **Root Cause:** The Level-2 team appears to have a much lower capacity or a slower response time than the Level-1 team, creating a queue bottleneck.

#### C. "Investigate $\rightarrow$ Resolve" Friction
Even without escalation, Case 104 took over 24 hours. 
*   **Case 104:** The ticket was assigned to L1 and investigated on 03-01, but not resolved until 03-02.
*   **Root Cause:** This indicates that "Investigation" is not always the final step before resolution; there may be an undocumented waiting period (e.g., waiting for customer feedback or system updates) that is not being captured as a specific activity in the log.

---

### 3. Insights and Recommendations

#### Insights:
*   **Escalations are High-Risk:** Escalating a ticket currently increases the resolution time from hours to days.
*   **The "Wall" Effect:** The process suffers from a recurring 19-hour stoppage, suggesting that the business operates on a strict 8-hour window with no continuity between days.
*   **Redundancy in Investigation:** Case 105 underwent investigation twice (before and after escalation), suggesting that L1 agents may be spending too much time on issues they cannot solve before escalating.

#### Recommendations:
1.  **Implement a Handover Protocol:** To eliminate the 19-hour "dead zone," implement a shift-handover process or an automated notification system that alerts the morning team to "High Priority" tickets that remained unresolved overnight.
2.  **Optimize L2 Routing:** The delay in Case 105 (28 hours) is unacceptable. The organization should evaluate the workload of Level-2 agents or implement a "SLA (Service Level Agreement)" for escalations to ensure they are picked up within a few hours, not days.
3.  **Refine Triage Criteria:** To prevent the "double investigation" seen in Case 105, improve the Triage step. If a ticket meets certain complex criteria, it should be assigned directly to Level-2 to avoid wasting L1's time and delaying the ultimate resolution.
4.  **Capture "Waiting" States:** The log currently jumps from "Investigate" to "Resolve." Adding an activity like "Waiting for Customer" or "Pending System Sync" would provide better visibility into why Case 104 took 24 hours despite having no escalation.
