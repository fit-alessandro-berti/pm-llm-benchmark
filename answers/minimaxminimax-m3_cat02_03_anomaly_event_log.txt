# Anomaly Analysis of Procure-to-Pay Event Log

## Standard P2P Process Flow (Baseline)

The expected sequence in a properly controlled P2P process is:

1. **Create Purchase Requisition**  
2. **Approve Purchase Requisition**  
3. **Issue Purchase Order (PO)**  
4. **Receive Goods**  
5. **Vendor Invoice Received**  
6. **Match Invoice to PO** (3-way match)  
7. **Release Payment**  
8. **Close Case**

**Case 1001** follows this sequence correctly. The other three cases contain notable anomalies.

---

##  Case 1002 — Multiple Critical Anomalies

| Issue | Activity Order | Problem |
|---|---|---|
| PO issued before approval | Issue PO (08:40)  Approve Requisition (08:00 *next day*) | Requisition should be **approved before** a PO is issued. Otherwise, unauthorized commitments are made to vendors. |
| Payment released before goods receipt | Release Payment (Feb 3, 07:15)  Receive Goods (Feb 4) | Paying for goods that haven't been received creates a high risk of loss, fraud, or non-delivery. |
| Payment released before 3-way match | Release Payment (Feb 3)  Match Invoice to PO (Feb 5) | The 3-way match (PO + Goods Receipt + Invoice) is a **key internal control**. Releasing payment before matching means paying on trust, exposing the company to duplicate payments, overpayments, or fictitious invoices. |
| Approval happens *after* PO and payment | Approve Requisition (Feb 3, 08:00) | The approval — a critical control gate — comes *after* money has already left the company. This is a **segregation-of-duties failure** and a strong fraud indicator. |
| Goods received after payment | Receive Goods (Feb 4) | Company paid for something it didn't have physical custody of. |

**Why it’s problematic:** Case 1002 shows the process running almost in reverse. It looks like an attempt to circumvent controls — the requisition was *back-approved* after the payment was already released, which is a classic red flag for **fraud or process circumvention**.

##  Case 1003 — Payment Made Before Goods and Invoice

| Issue | Activity Order | Problem |
|---|---|---|
| Payment before invoice | Release Payment (Feb 8)  Vendor Invoice Received (Feb 9) | You can't validate a payment amount without an invoice. Paying before the invoice arrives is impossible to reconcile and may indicate a fictitious or pre-emptive payment. |
| Payment before goods | Release Payment (Feb 8)  Receive Goods (Feb 10) | Money is paid two days *before* the goods physically arrive. |
| 3-way match after payment | Match Invoice to PO (Feb 11) — *after* payment on Feb 8 | Defeats the purpose of the match, which is to **prevent** payment in case of discrepancies. |

**Why it’s problematic:** Case 1003 is a classic example of a **broken 3-way match control**. Paying first and matching later is functionally the same as having no control at all. The case may suggest **fraud (ghost vendor, fictitious PO) or a serious system bypass**.

---

##  Case 1004 — Goods Received Before Purchase Order

| Issue | Activity Order | Problem |
|---|---|---|
| Goods received before PO | Receive Goods (Feb 6, 09:00)  Issue PO (Feb 6, 09:15) | Goods were received **15 minutes before** the PO was even issued. |
| Invoice matched and paid against a PO that didn't exist at receipt time | Match Invoice to PO (Feb 7) | The PO used for matching is a retroactive document, not a real pre-commitment. |

**Why it’s problematic:** Receiving goods without an existing PO is known as **"maverick buying"** or unauthorized purchasing. It means:
- No pre-approved pricing or vendor.
- No contract terms in place at the time of receipt.
- The 3-way match is meaningless because the PO was created *after the fact* to justify an already-completed transaction.
- High risk of **overcharging, wrong items, or fraudulent vendor setup**.

## Recommended Follow-up Actions
1. **Investigate Case 1002 and 1003 immediately** — potential fraud; review who released the payment and whether segregation of duties was violated.
2. **Audit Case 1004** — identify how goods arrived without a PO; strengthen "PO required for receipt" enforcement in the ERP system.
