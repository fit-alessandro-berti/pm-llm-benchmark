Below is a structured analysis of the DECLARE model, treating it as a **constraintbased description** of a loanapproval process. In DECLARE, a model does not prescribe one fixed sequence; instead, every trace (every case) must satisfy all of the listed constraints. Because every rule has `support = 1.0` and `confidence = 1.0`, the bank treats them as **hard, nonnegotiable policies**.

I will use the short activity labels (A–H, Z) to keep the discussion readable.

| Activity | Reallife meaning |
|---|---|
| **A – Receive_Application** | The loan application is received from the customer. |
| **B – Preliminary_Credit_Check** | A first creditworthiness screen is performed. |
| **C – Gather_Additional_Documents** | Income statements, tax returns, KYC evidence, etc. are collected. |
| **D – Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package** | The concrete loan terms, interest rate, and contract are prepared. |
| **E – Quality_Assurance_Review** | A compliance/quality review of the file and offer is performed. |
| **F – Authorize_Contract_Terms** | The contract terms are formally approved. |
| **G – Transfer_Funds** | The loan amount is disbursed. |
| **H – Notify_Customer** | The customer is informed of the decision, terms, or next steps. |
| **Z – Proceed_Without_Compliance** | A prohibited “skip compliance” action. |

## 1. Unary constraints: what must, must not, and may happen

| Template | Activity | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **init** | A | Every case starts with receiving the application. There is no loan process without an application. |
| **existence** | A, C | Both A and C must occur at least once in every case. The bank never skips application intake, and it always collects additional documents. |
| **exactly_one** | B | The preliminary credit check happens **once and only once**. This avoids repeated credit pulls (cost, customer annoyance, privacy/FCRA concerns) and gives a single, consistent credit snapshot for the case. |
| **absence** | Z | **Proceed_Without_Compliance is forbidden.** This is the “red line” compliance rule. |

These four rules already frame a very controlled process: a single entry point, a single credit check, mandatory document collection, and an explicit ban on bypassing compliance.

---

## 2. Binary positive constraints: dependencies, ordering, and pairings

### Existencestyle dependencies

| Template | Source  Target | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **responded_existence** | D  E | If a loan offer package is assembled, a qualityassurance review must also occur somewhere in the case. You cannot build an offer without QA being part of the case. |
| **coexistence** | C  F | Gathering additional documents and authorizing contract terms **go together**. If one happens, the other must happen, and if one never happens, neither can happen. This prevents “request docs but never authorize” and “authorize without ever collecting supporting documents.” |

### Ordering constraints

| Template | Source  Target | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **response** | A  B | Every received application must eventually be followed by a preliminary credit check. |
| **precedence** | E  F | A qualityassurance review can occur only if contract terms were authorized **before** it. So the authorization step (F) precedes the QA review (E). |
| **succession** | B  C | The preliminary credit check must be followed by document gathering, and document gathering can only occur after the credit check. The credit decision triggers the document collection phase. |

### Alternate constraints (1to1 discipline)

| Template | Source  Target | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **altresponse** | G  H | Every fund transfer must eventually be followed by a customer notification, and there cannot be a second transfer before that notification occurs. |
| **altprecedence** | H  G | Every customer notification must be preceded by a fund transfer, and notifications cannot “stack up” without a matching transfer. |

Taken together, **altresponse(G,H)** and **altprecedence(H,G)** enforce a **onetransfer–onenotification** discipline. In practical terms, the bank cannot disburse money multiple times without telling the customer, and it cannot notify the customer of a disbursement that never happened.

| Template | Source  Target | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **altsuccession** | C  E | Document gathering and QA review must alternate, with document gathering first. After a round of documents is gathered, a QA review must occur before another documentgathering round. |

### Chain constraints (immediate handoffs)

| Template | Source  Target | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **chainprecedence** | F  B | Authorizing contract terms must be **immediately preceded** by the preliminary credit check. No other activity can sit between the credit check and the authorization. This suggests a tight, possibly automated decision: the credit score directly drives the authorization. |
| **chainsuccession** | E  D | Quality assurance and offer assembly are adjacent in both directions. As soon as QA is finished, the offer package is assembled; conversely, an offer package can only be assembled immediately after QA. |
| **chainresponse** | D  G | Assembling the loan offer package is **immediately followed** by transferring funds. Once the offer is finalized, disbursement happens right away. |

These chain rules make the process feel like a **straightthrough processing pipeline** between key control points: credit check  authorization  (later) documents  QA  offer assembly  disbursement.

## 3. Negative constraints: fences and prohibitions

| Template | Source  Target | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **nonsuccession** | H  B | After the customer has been notified, the bank cannot run a preliminary credit check later. The notification marks a point of finality; rechecking credit after notification would be confusing and possibly unfair. |
| **nonchainsuccession** | F  H | Authorizing contract terms cannot be **immediately followed** by notifying the customer. This prevents the bank from telling the customer “you are approved” in the very next moment after authorization; other steps (QA, offer assembly, disbursement) must intervene. |
| **noncoexistence** | G vs A | Transferring funds and receiving the application **cannot coexist** in the same case. |

### Important sanity check on `noncoexistence(G, A)`
Under standard DECLARE semantics, **noncoexistence(G, A)** says a case can contain *either* a fund transfer *or* the application event, but not both. Because **init(A)** and **existence(A)** force A to appear in every case, this rule makes **G impossible in any valid trace**. In other words, as written, the model forbids disbursement entirely.

Most likely this is a **modeling error**: the intent was probably to prevent disbursement before the application is received, not to forbid them from appearing in the same case. The intended rule would normally be a **precedence** or **response** constraint such as **precedence(G, A)** or **response(A, G)**. I will note this again in the conclusion, because it is the one place where the model as given is internally inconsistent.

## 4. How the rules shape a concrete loan scenario

Ignoring the contradictory `noncoexistence(G, A)`, a valid trace could look like this:

```
A  B  F  C  E  D  G  H
```

Reading it in business terms:

1. **Receive_Application (A)** – the process starts with customer intake.  
2. **Preliminary_Credit_Check (B)** – a single credit screen is run.  
3. **Authorize_Contract_Terms (F)** – because of `chainprecedence(F,B)`, the authorization happens immediately after the credit check.  
4. **Gather_Additional_Documents (C)** – additional KYC/income documents are collected.  
5. **Quality_Assurance_Review (E)** – compliance/quality reviews the file.  
6. **Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package (D)** – immediately after QA, the offer is built.  
7. **Transfer_Funds (G)** – immediately after the offer is assembled, the money is disbursed.  
8. **Notify_Customer (H)** – finally, the customer is notified of the disbursement.

Notice how the model enforces **control points** at every transition:

- A single credit snapshot drives the decision (`exactly_one B`, `chainprecedence FB`).
- Documents are tied to authorization (`coexistence CF`), and document collection follows the credit check (`succession BC`).
- QA precedes offer creation and is chained to it (`chainsuccession ED`).
- Offer creation is chained to disbursement (`chainresponse DG`).
- Disbursement and notification are onetoone (`altresponse GH`, `altprecedence HG`).

The negative rules add **finality and transparency**: once the customer is notified, the bank cannot go back and rerun the credit check (`nonsuccession HB`), and it cannot notify the customer instantly after authorization without the intervening QA and disbursement steps (`nonchainsuccession FH`).

## 5. Compliance, quality, and transparency goals

### Compliance
- **`absence(Z)`** is the explicit compliance gate. It rules out any variant where staff try to skip compliance checks.
- **`exactly_one(B)`** supports fairlending and dataprivacy rules by preventing repeated credit pulls that could alter the decision or annoy the applicant.
- **`coexistence(C,F)`** ensures documentation and authorization are never decoupled, which is important for KYC/AML and internal audit.
- **`nonsuccession(HB)`** and **`nonchainsuccession(FH)`** prevent misleading customer communications and “approval before funding” scenarios.

### Quality assurance
- **`responded_existence(DE)`** and **`chainsuccession(ED)`** make QA a mandatory, immediate precursor to offer assembly. No offer leaves the bank without a quality review.
- **`altsuccession(CE)`** ensures that after more documents are requested, a QA review follows before another document round, preventing endless backandforth without oversight.

### Process structure and transparency
- **`init(A)`**, **`response(AB)`**, and **`succession(BC)`** create a clear, linear backbone: intake  credit check  documents.
- The chain constraints compress the later stages into a tight, auditable sequence: authorization follows directly from the credit check; offer assembly follows directly from QA; disbursement follows directly from offer assembly.
- The alternate constraints between disbursement and notification create an **auditable onetoone pairing** for every loan paid out.

## 7. Closing observation: one rule that needs attention

The only rule that undermines the whole model is:

```python
'noncoexistence': {
    'Transfer_Funds': {'target': 'Receive_Application', ...}
}
```

Because `Receive_Application` is both `init` and `existence`, it appears in **every** case. Standard `noncoexistence(G, A)` therefore forbids `Transfer_Funds` in every case, which conflicts with the rest of the model (which clearly prepares for disbursement). In practice, this should be replaced by an ordering rule such as:

```python
'precedence': {
    'Transfer_Funds': {'target': 'Receive_Application'}
}
```

That would simply say: *if funds are transferred, the application must have been received earlier*—a much more natural reading of “preventing disbursement before the application is even received.”

Apart from that inconsistency, the DECLARE model paints a picture of a **rigid, compliancecentric, fully traceable loan process** where every major control point is gated, paired, and audited.
