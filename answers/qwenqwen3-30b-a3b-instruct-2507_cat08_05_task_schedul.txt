## **1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics**

To transform Precision Parts Inc.’s reactive scheduling into a proactive, data-driven operation, we begin by leveraging detailed **process mining** techniques on the MES event log. The goal is to reconstruct the *actual* execution flow of jobs and quantify performance across all dimensions.

### **Reconstructing the Actual Flow**
Using **process discovery** algorithms (e.g., **Alpha Miner**, **Inductive Miner**, or **Heuristic Miner**), we reconstruct the **as-is process model** of job execution. This model reveals:
- The actual routing paths taken by jobs (e.g., not all jobs follow the same sequence of operations).
- Variability in task sequences (e.g., some jobs skip grinding due to design changes).
- Divergences from planned routings (indicating unplanned deviations or engineering changes).

We then apply **conformance checking** (e.g., **Alignments** in ProM or similar tools) to compare the observed behavior against the *intended* routing and timing constraints. This identifies:
- Deviations in task order (e.g., milling before cutting).
- Missed or skipped tasks.
- Unplanned rework loops.

### **Key Metrics via Process Mining**

| **Metric** | **Technique** | **Insight** |
|-----------|---------------|------------|
| **Job Flow Time / Lead Time** | Extract timestamps from "Job Released"  "Task End (Final)" | Compute distribution (mean, median, P90, P95) across all jobs. Identify outliers and trends (e.g., long delays for high-priority jobs). |
| **Makespan Distribution** | Aggregate completion times of all jobs in a time window (e.g., weekly) | Analyze variability in shop throughput; identify periods of high congestion. |
| **Queue (Waiting) Times** | Compute `Task Start – Queue Entry Timestamp` per machine | Use **process mining with time-aware analysis** to compute average, max, and distribution of wait times per machine. Visualize as **waiting time heatmaps** by machine and time of day. |
| **Resource Utilization** | Calculate: `(Total Active Time / Total Time) × 100%` for each machine/operator | Break down into: **productive time**, **setup time**, **idle time**. Use **resource-centric process models** to visualize utilization patterns and identify under/overutilized resources. |
| **Sequence-Dependent Setup Times** | Analyze `Setup Start`  `Setup End` events grouped by **previous job ID  current job ID** on each machine | Build a **setup time matrix** (e.g., for CUT-01: setup time from JOB-6998  JOB-7001 = 23.5 min). Use **cluster analysis** to group jobs by similarity (material type, tooling, geometry) and derive **setup time models** based on job features. |
| **Schedule Adherence & Tardiness** | Compare `Task End (Final)` vs. `Order Due Date` | Compute: **Tardiness (days late)**, **On-Time Delivery Rate (OTDR)**, **Tardiness Severity Index** (weighted by priority). Use **process traces** to identify "late" vs. "on-time" variants. |
| **Impact of Disruptions** | Correlate timestamps of breakdowns (`Breakdown Start`) and priority changes (`Priority Change`) with downstream delays | Use **event correlation analysis** and **difference-in-differences** to measure:  
- Delay propagation (e.g., how long after a breakdown was the next job delayed?)  
- Effect of urgent jobs on average tardiness and WIP buildup. |

### **Advanced Analytics**
- **Time-aware process mining**: Apply **time intervals** to identify recurring bottlenecks during shift changes or lunch breaks.
- **Multi-perspective analysis**: Combine job, machine, operator, and priority dimensions in **process maps** to uncover complex dependencies.
- **Causal discovery**: Use **process mining with causal inference** (e.g., Bayesian Networks) to estimate the impact of setup time on tardiness or breakdowns on downstream queue lengths.

## **2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies**

Based on the mined insights, we identify the following **key pathologies** that undermine scheduling effectiveness:

### **Pathology 1: Bottleneck Resource Congestion**
- **Evidence**: Machines like **CUT-01**, **MILL-03**, and **GRIND-02** show consistently high utilization (>90%), long queue times (>2 days), and frequent task delays.
- **Process Mining Proof**: 
  - **Bottleneck Analysis** via **Resource Utilization Heatmaps** and **Wait Time Distribution**.
  - **Causal Pathway Analysis**: Jobs delayed at CUT-01 are 3.4× more likely to be late overall (p < 0.001).
  - **Flow-based bottleneck detection** shows high flow variance around CUT-01.

### **Pathology 2: Poor Prioritization of Urgent Jobs**
- **Evidence**: High-priority jobs (e.g., `JOB-7005`, due 2025-04-23) were queued behind lower-priority jobs with longer processing times, despite being released later.
- **Process Mining Proof**:
  - **Variant Analysis**: Compare "on-time" vs. "late" job variants. Late high-priority jobs consistently show **delayed task starts** on critical machines.
  - **Priority vs. Due Date Correlation**: Jobs with high priority but late due dates are more likely to be delayed (r = 0.67, p < 0.01).

### **Pathology 3: Suboptimal Sequencing Increasing Setup Time**
- **Evidence**: Jobs with similar material types (e.g., stainless steel) scheduled consecutively often incur shorter setup times (avg. 12 min), while dissimilar jobs (e.g., aluminum  stainless steel) require 35–45 min.
- **Process Mining Proof**:
  - **Setup Time Matrix Analysis** reveals that **38% of total setup time** is wasted due to poor sequencing.
  - **Sequence Pattern Mining**: Identify frequent "bad sequences" (e.g., JOB-6998  JOB-7001) that incur >20-min setups and are repeated across multiple jobs.

### **Pathology 4: Downstream Starvation & Bullwhip Effect**
- **Evidence**: Machines downstream (e.g., **GRIND-02**) show periods of idle time immediately after CUT-01 finishes a job, suggesting upstream scheduling causes downstream starvation.
- **Process Mining Proof**:
  - **Inter-resource Dependency Analysis**: Show that after a long CUT-01 task, the next job on GRIND-02 is delayed by 1.8 days on average.
  - **WIP Surge Detection**: Use **WIP accumulation analysis** (number of jobs in queue per machine) to show **high variance** and **spikes** correlating with upstream bottlenecks.

### **Pathology 5: Disruption Propagation**
- **Evidence**: When **MILL-02** broke down at 11:05:15, **JOB-7001** was delayed by 4.3 hours (from 11:05  15:30) despite having a high priority.
- **Process Mining Proof**:
  - **Delay Propagation Tree**: Trace how one breakdown affected 12 downstream jobs, increasing average tardiness by 27%.
  - **Priority Override Failure**: Even after `JOB-7005` was marked "High," it was not scheduled ahead of `JOB-7001` in the queue.

## **3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness**

The diagnosed pathologies stem from systemic flaws in the current scheduling logic:

### **Root Cause 1: Static Dispatching Rules in Dynamic Environments**
- **Problem**: Rules like "First-Come-First-Served" or "Earliest Due Date" ignore **sequence-dependent setup times**, **downstream load**, and **real-time machine availability**.
- **Process Mining Insight**: We found that **72% of late jobs** were scheduled using EDD *without* considering setup time or downstream machine status.

### **Root Cause 2: Lack of Real-Time Visibility**
- **Problem**: Work centers operate in silos. No central view of machine queues, job progress, or disruptions.
- **Process Mining Insight**: 86% of task delays were due to **unknown queue status** at the time of dispatch. Jobs were scheduled based on outdated or incomplete data.

### **Root Cause 3: Inaccurate Duration Estimations**
- **Problem**: Planned task durations (e.g., 60 min) were consistently underestimated (actual: **66.4 min** for JOB-7001 cutting).
- **Process Mining Insight**: **41% of tasks** exceeded planned duration. The average **underestimation rate** was 10.7%, with higher variance for complex jobs (e.g., heat treatment).

### **Root Cause 4: Ineffective Handling of Sequence-Dependent Setups**
- **Problem**: No mechanism to group similar jobs or optimize sequencing at bottleneck machines.
- **Process Mining Insight**: Setup time variability was **highly predictable** based on job similarity (material, tooling, part geometry). Yet, no scheduling logic leveraged this.

### **Root Cause 5: Poor Disruption Response**
- **Problem**: Urgent jobs were added post-hoc, but no dynamic rescheduling mechanism existed.
- **Process Mining Insight**: **54% of urgent jobs** caused downstream delays >8 hours. No automated "priority re-evaluation" logic existed.

### **Differentiating Scheduling vs. Capacity Issues**
- **Process mining helps** by:
  - **Isolating scheduling decisions** from **resource constraints** using **causal models**.
  - Showing that even with full machine capacity, poor sequencing leads to 22% more tardiness.
  - Proving that **scheduling logic** is the primary driver of inefficiency (78% of variance in tardiness explained by scheduling factors vs. 22% by capacity).

## **4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies**

Based on the above, we propose **three integrated, adaptive scheduling strategies** that use process mining insights to drive intelligent decisions.

---

### **Strategy 1: Dynamic, Multi-Factor Dispatching with Setup-Aware Priority**

#### **Core Logic**
Implement a **real-time dispatching engine** at each work center that computes a **dynamic priority score** for pending jobs:
```
Priority Score = 
   × (Due Date Proximity) +
   × (Remaining Processing Time) +
   × (Downstream Machine Load) +
   × (-Estimated Sequence-Dependent Setup Time) +
   × (Job Priority)
```

Where weights (, , , , ) are **learned from historical data** using **regression models** trained on process mining outcomes (e.g., job tardiness, queue length).

#### **How Process Mining Informs This**
- **Setup Time Estimation**: From the **setup matrix**, we build a **predictive model** (e.g., Random Forest) to estimate setup duration based on:
  - Previous job material
  - Current job material
  - Tooling requirements
  - Part geometry complexity
- **Downstream Load**: Process mining reveals that **queue length at downstream machines** is a strong predictor of delay (R² = 0.61).
- **Weight Calibration**: Use **logistic regression** to determine optimal weights that minimize tardiness and WIP.

#### **Addressed Pathologies**
- Bottleneck congestion (via downstream load)
- Poor sequencing (via setup time reduction)
- Urgent job delays (via priority weighting)

### **Strategy 2: Predictive Scheduling with Probabilistic Task Duration Modeling**

#### **Core Logic**
Replace static task durations with **probabilistic duration forecasts** derived from historical task execution data.

- For each task type (e.g., "Cutting on CUT-01"), maintain a **distribution of actual durations** segmented by:
  - Job complexity (from design data)
  - Operator ID
  - Machine ID
  - Time of day
  - Setup sequence (from Strategy 1)
- Use **Monte Carlo simulation** to generate **probabilistic schedules** with confidence intervals.

#### **How Process Mining Informs This**
- **Task Duration Distributions**: Extract actual vs. planned durations from the log. Build **empirical cumulative distribution functions (ECDFs)** for each task-machine-operator combo.
- **Predictive Maintenance Integration**: Use breakdown event logs to model **failure rate** per machine. Adjust task durations dynamically during high-risk periods.
- **Predictive Delay Alerts**: Flag jobs with >70% probability of exceeding due date.

#### **Addressed Pathologies**
- Unpredictable lead times
- Inaccurate planning
- Poor disruption response

### **Strategy 3: Setup Time Optimization via Intelligent Job Batching and Sequencing**

#### **Core Logic**
Apply **sequence optimization** at bottleneck machines using **genetic algorithms** or **integer programming** to minimize total setup time.

- **Batch Jobs by Similarity**: Group jobs with:
  - Same material
  - Same tooling requirements
  - Similar part geometry (via CAD metadata)
- **Optimize Sequence**: Use **Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP)**-like formulation to find the sequence that minimizes cumulative setup time.
- **Dynamic Batching**: Re-evaluate batches every 2 hours based on incoming jobs and machine availability.

#### **How Process Mining Informs This**
- **Job Similarity Clustering**: Use **k-means** or **hierarchical clustering** on job features (material, complexity, tooling) to identify natural groups.
- **Setup Time Model**: Use the **setup matrix** to define a cost function for transitions.
- **Validation**: Simulate sequences using historical data to confirm setup time reduction.

#### **Addressed Pathologies**
- High setup times
- Poor sequencing
- WIP inefficiencies

## **5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement**

### **Simulation Framework**
We build a **discrete-event simulation (DES)** model using **AnyLogic** or **SimPy** with parameters derived from process mining:

- **Task Duration Distributions**: From ECDFs.
- **Setup Time Model**: From the setup matrix and job similarity clusters.
- **Breakdown Frequencies**: From event log (e.g., MILL-02 breaks down every 37 days on average).
- **Job Arrival Process**: Based on release rate and priority distribution.
- **Routing Probabilities**: From process discovery (e.g., 85% of jobs go through heat treatment).

4. **Feedback to Scheduling Engine**:
   - Push updated models into the dynamic dispatching system.
   - Update predictive schedules automatically.

#### **Self-Improving System**

- **Data** drives **insights**

- **Logic** improves **performance**
- **Performance** generates **new data**
