The visualization is a BPMN-style process diagram showing an order fulfillment workflow across four swimlanes: **Sales**, **Production**, **Procurement**, and **Warehouse**.

The process begins in **Sales** with a timer event labeled **“Monday morning.”** Sales checks the customer’s order for completeness, then checks whether the customer already exists in the database. An exclusive gateway decides between two paths: if the customer is not in the database, Sales adds the customer to the database; if the customer already exists, the process continues directly. After this customer/order check, Sales sends the order details onward.

The **order details** are then communicated via dashed message flows to both **Production** and **Procurement**.

In **Production**, the arrival of order details triggers planning. A parallel gateway indicates that production planning and material confirmation are both required. Production plans the production work, then waits for confirmation that the material is not faulty. Once both conditions are satisfied, Production manufactures the product and sends a **“Product complete”** message to the Warehouse.

In **Procurement**, the order details trigger a material-handling process. There is a **Material Compensation Subprocess**, where materials are ordered. A compensation activity labeled **“Return material”** is shown, indicating that ordered materials may need to be returned if compensation is triggered. After ordering, Procurement checks the materials. If the materials are acceptable, Procurement sends a **“Material not faulty”** message to Production. If there is a problem, the flow can lead to **order cancellation** and termination.

In the **Warehouse** lane, the process starts when a **“Product complete”** message is received from Production. The Warehouse then sends the product and emits a **“Product sent”** message.

Back in **Sales**, once the **“Product sent”** message is received, Sales drafts the invoice and the process ends.

Overall, the diagram models a cross-department order-to-delivery process, including customer validation, order distribution, material procurement, production, shipping, and invoicing. Solid arrows show internal sequence flow, while dashed arrows represent messages exchanged between departments.
