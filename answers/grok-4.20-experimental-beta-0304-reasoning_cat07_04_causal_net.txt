**Visualization Description:**

This image shows a **Workflow Net** (a special type of Petri net used to model business processes), accompanied by a legend of workflow patterns.

### Main Process (Top/Left)

The diagram represents a **request/complaint handling process** with the following flow:

- **Place a**: Entry point where a request is registered.
- After registration, the process uses a **parallel split** (likely an AND-split), leading to three activities that can occur:
  - **b**: "examine thoroughly"
  - **c**: "examine casually"
  - **d**: "check ticket"

- These three paths converge at **place e** ("decide"). This is a **synchronization point** (AND-join), meaning the decision can only be made after all three activities are completed.

- From **e** ("decide"), an **XOR-split** occurs, leading to three mutually exclusive outcomes:
  - **g**: "pay compensation"  leads to **z** (end)
  - **h**: "reject request"  leads to **z** (end)
  - **f**: "reinitiate request"  loops back to the beginning (place a)

**Place z** represents the final end state of the process.

### Legend (Bottom Right)

The gray box explains the standard **workflow routing patterns** used in the diagram:

**Splits (diverging paths):**
- **XOR-split**: Exclusive choice (only one path is taken)
- **AND-split**: Parallel split (all paths are taken)
- **OR-split**: Inclusive choice (one or more paths are taken)

**Joins (converging paths):**
- **XOR-join**: Simple merge (waits for one incoming path)
- **AND-join**: Synchronization (waits for all incoming paths)
- **OR-join**: Synchronizing merge (complex join that waits for all active paths)

This is a classic example from the field of **Process Mining** and **Business Process Management**, frequently used in textbooks and papers (particularly by Wil van der Aalst) to illustrate how Petri nets can model real-world workflows with loops, parallelism, and decision points.
