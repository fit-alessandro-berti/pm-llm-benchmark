### Identified Temporal Anomalies

The temporal profile model shows several time intervals that are statistically unusual, suggesting that the process flow is not uniform or stable.

1. **R $\rightarrow$ P (Receive to Approve):** The average time (approx. 1.04 days) combined with a very low standard deviation (1 hour) is highly suspicious. This suggests that the approval process happens on a rigidly predictable, systematic schedule, which is rare in real-world, human-managed claim processing.
2. **P $\rightarrow$ N (Approve to Notify):** This interval exhibits an extremely long average time (7 days) paired with a very large standard deviation (2 days). This indicates massive inconsistency, suggesting that the notification step is often blocked or delayed significantly, but sometimes happens rapidly.
3. **A $\rightarrow$ C (Assign to Close):** The average time (2 hours) is remarkably short, especially when considering the potential complexity of claims. This suggests a significant proportion of claims may be closed quickly immediately after assignment, potentially bypassing crucial evaluation or approval stages.
4. **E $\rightarrow$ N (Evaluate to Notify):** The average time (5 minutes) is unrealistically short. This quick transition may suggest that the "Notify" step is often triggered automatically immediately upon evaluation, potentially skipping manual review time or failing to account for necessary communication preparation.

***

### Hypotheses on Anomaly Causes

Based on the observed timing inconsistencies, the anomalies may arise from a combination of system limitations, operational practices, and workflow shortcuts:

* **Rigid/Automated Steps (R $\rightarrow$ P):** The low variance in approval timing suggests that the approval step might be governed by an automated, timed workflow (e.g., "Approvals happen every 24 hours on Tuesdays") rather than discretionary human judgment. This is a mechanical bottleneck, not a business one.
* **Backlogs and Resource Dependency (P $\rightarrow$ N):** The massive variance and long average for P $\rightarrow$ N points to a severe process bottleneck, likely related to resource capacity (e.g., the notification team is severely understaffed, leading to long delays, but when they become free, they process large batches quickly).
* **Premature Closure/Simplification (A $\rightarrow$ C):** The quick closure suggests that certain low-value, simple claims (e.g., minor auto damage) may be subject to a rapid, simplified "fast track" process, bypassing full evaluation to meet service level agreements (SLAs).
* **Systemic Triggering (E $\rightarrow$ N):** The ultra-short time between evaluation and notification implies that the system is configured to auto-send a notice (or a preliminary notice) as soon as the evaluation status is logged, rather than waiting for a human resource to review the evaluation results and manually initiate the communication.

### SQL Verification Queries (PostgreSQL)

The following queries focus on identifying specific instances where the time lapse between required sequential activities deviates significantly from the established norms.

#### 1. Identifying Claims with Excessive Delay (Targeting P $\rightarrow$ N Anomaly)

This query identifies claims where the time between 'P' (Approve) and 'N' (Notify) exceeds 5 standard deviations (a strong indicator of an outlier or systemic failure).

```sql
WITH RankedEvents AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        activity,
        timestamp,
        resource,
        LAG(activity) OVER (PARTITION BY claim_id ORDER BY timestamp) AS previous_activity,
        LAG(timestamp) OVER (PARTITION BY claim_id ORDER BY timestamp) AS previous_timestamp
    FROM claim_events
)
SELECT
    claim_id,
    previous_activity,
    activity,
    timestamp,
    (EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (timestamp - previous_timestamp)) / 3600)::NUMERIC AS hours_elapsed
FROM RankedEvents
WHERE previous_activity = 'P'
  AND activity = 'N'
  AND (timestamp - previous_timestamp) > INTERVAL '2 day' * 3 -- Selecting instances > 2 days
ORDER BY 
    hours_elapsed DESC
LIMIT 10;
```

#### 2. Identifying Claims Closed Immediately After Assignment (Targeting A $\rightarrow$ C Anomaly)

This query isolates the fastest claims closed after assignment, helping confirm if a "fast track" process is operating outside standard controls.

```sql
WITH AssignedClaims AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        timestamp AS assignment_time,
        resource AS adjuster_resource
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity = 'A'
),
ClosedClaims AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        timestamp AS close_time
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity = 'C'
)
SELECT
    T1.claim_id,
    T1.adjuster_resource,
    T1.assignment_time,
    T2.close_time,
    (T2.close_time - T1.assignment_time) AS time_elapsed
FROM AssignedClaims T1
JOIN ClosedClaims T2 ON T1.claim_id = T2.claim_id
WHERE T2.close_time > T1.assignment_time
  -- Filter for claims closed within 4 hours
  AND T2.close_time - T1.assignment_time < INTERVAL '4 hours'
ORDER BY
    time_elapsed ASC;
```

#### 3. Correlating Short Intervals with Specific Resources or Claim Types (Targeting E $\rightarrow$ N Anomaly)

This query identifies instances where the time between 'E' (Evaluate) and 'N' (Notify) is extremely short (e.g., less than 15 minutes) and attempts to correlate these rapid transitions with specific adjusters or high-volume claim types, suggesting automated flow bypassing manual checks.

```sql
WITH EvaluationEvents AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        timestamp AS evaluate_time,
        resource AS evaluator
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity = 'E'
),
NotificationEvents AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        timestamp AS notify_time
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity = 'N'
)
SELECT
    CE.claim_id,
    CE.evaluator,
    CE.evaluate_time,
    NE.notify_time,
    (NE.notify_time - CE.evaluate_time) AS time_difference,
    C.claim_type
FROM EvaluationEvents CE
JOIN NotificationEvents NE ON CE.claim_id = NE.claim_id
JOIN claims C ON CE.claim_id = C.claim_id
WHERE 
    -- Filter for intervals less than 15 minutes
    NE.notify_time - CE.evaluate_time < INTERVAL '15 minutes'
ORDER BY
    time_difference ASC, C.claim_type;
```

#### 4. Detecting Records Violating Predicted Schedules (Targeting R $\rightarrow$ P Anomaly)

This query checks claims where the initial steps (R $\rightarrow$ E $\rightarrow$ P) are suspiciously close to the expected schedule average, but we check the *variance* by segmenting the data to see if deviation is correlated with the claim type.

```sql
-- Calculate the total time span between Receive and Approve (R to P)
WITH TimeSpan AS (
    SELECT
        claim_id,
        MIN(CASE WHEN activity = 'R' THEN timestamp END) AS receive_time,
        MAX(CASE WHEN activity = 'P' THEN timestamp END) AS approve_time
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity IN ('R', 'P')
    GROUP BY claim_id
)
SELECT
    T.claim_id,
    C.claim_type,
    T.receive_time,
    T.approve_time,
    (T.approve_time - T.receive_time) AS total_duration,
    -- Calculate days elapsed
    (EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (T.approve_time - T.receive_time)) / (3600 * 24.0))::NUMERIC AS days_elapsed
FROM TimeSpan T
JOIN claims C ON T.claim_id = C.claim_id
-- Look for claims where the duration is suspiciously consistent (e.g., within 10% of the expected 1 day mark)
WHERE 
    EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM (T.approve_time - T.receive_time)) BETWEEN 
    (1.04 * 24 * 3600 * 0.9) AND (1.04 * 24 * 3600 * 1.1)
ORDER BY
    C.claim_type, total_duration;
```
