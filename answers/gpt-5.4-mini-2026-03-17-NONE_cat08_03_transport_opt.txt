# 1. Process Discovery and Conformance Checking

## 1.1 Preprocess and integrate the data into a cohesive event log

### A. Define the process scope and case notion
For Speedy Parcels, there are multiple useful “case” definitions depending on the question:

- **Vehicle-day case**: one delivery van on one operational day  
  Good for analyzing route execution, stops, delays, and maintenance interruptions.
- **Package case**: one parcel from depot scan to final delivery or failure  
  Good for on-time delivery, failed attempts, and customer-level flow.
- **Route/shift case**: one driver shift or planned route instance  
  Good for comparing planned vs. actual route execution.

In practice, I would build a **multi-level event log**:
- a **vehicle-day log** for fleet/process performance,
- a **package log** for delivery outcomes,
- and a **route log** for conformance to dispatch plans.

### B. Standardize event data
All sources must be converted into a common schema with at least:

- **Case ID**
- **Activity**
- **Timestamp**
- **Resource** (driver, vehicle)
- **Location**
- **Package ID**
- **Event source**
- **Status/attributes** such as speed, stop reason, delay reason, maintenance type, planned time window

### C. Harmonize events from different systems
Examples:
- GPS tracker data:
  - infer activities such as `Depart Depot`, `Travel`, `Idle`, `Low Speed`, `Arrive Customer`, `Unscheduled Stop`
- Scanner data:
  - direct business events such as `Scan at Depot`, `Arrive Customer`, `Delivery Success`, `Delivery Failed`, `Depart Customer`
- Dispatch system:
  - `Route Assigned`, planned stop sequence, expected service window, vehicle capacity, assigned driver
- Maintenance logs:
  - `Maintenance Start`, `Maintenance End`, `Repair Stop`, `Scheduled Service`

### D. Event cleaning and enrichment
Important steps:
- **Timestamp alignment** across systems and time zones
- **Duplicate removal**
- **Event ordering resolution** when multiple systems record the same moment
- **Missing event handling**, e.g. infer depot departure from GPS movement after depot scan
- **Activity abstraction**, because raw GPS points are too granular

Useful enrichments:
- derive **travel time** between stops
- derive **service time** at customer location
- derive **waiting time / idle time**
- classify stops as:
  - planned customer stop
  - depot stop
  - traffic delay
  - maintenance stop
  - exception stop

### E. Main challenges
1. **Event granularity mismatch**  
   GPS is high-frequency, scanner events are milestone-based, dispatch is planned, maintenance is episodic.

2. **Case correlation**
   - a GPS point belongs to a vehicle-day, but a delivery milestone belongs to a package
   - need rules to link package events to vehicle trips and driver shifts

3. **Noise and ambiguity**
   - low speed may mean traffic, parking, or congestion
   - idle may mean waiting for customer or break time

4. **Incomplete logs**
   - missed scans, delayed scanner uploads, GPS gaps

5. **Planned vs. actual alignment**
   - dispatch data may not reflect real-world execution

## 1.2 Discover the actual end-to-end delivery process

Once the event log is prepared, I would apply process discovery to visualize the real process as executed.

### A. Recommended discovery techniques
- **Heuristic Miner**  
  Good for noisy operational data and frequent routing patterns.
- **Inductive Miner**  
  Useful for producing structured, sound models.
- **Fuzzy / directly-follows graphs**  
  Very useful for quickly seeing common transitions, loops, and exceptions.

### B. Likely discovered process structure
For a vehicle-day case, the discovered model might look like:

1. Start shift
2. Scan at depot / load van
3. Route assigned
4. Depart depot
5. Travel between stops
6. Arrive customer
7. Delivery success or failure
8. Depart customer
9. Optional exception loops:
   - traffic delay
   - unplanned stop
   - maintenance/repair
   - return to depot
10. Arrive depot
11. End shift

### C. What the model should reveal
The actual model may show:
- repeated loops between travel and idle
- frequent failed delivery attempts followed by reattempts
- unexpected detours or unscheduled stops
- prolonged dwell times at certain customers
- route interruptions due to maintenance
- overtime patterns

This gives a realistic view of the “as-is” logistics process rather than the ideal planned one.

## 1.3 Conformance checking against planned routes

Conformance checking compares the discovered behavior against the dispatch plan.

### A. Planned model
The dispatch system provides:
- stop sequence
- assigned packages
- vehicle capacity
- customer time windows
- planned route and driver assignment

This becomes the **reference model**.

### B. Actual vs. planned comparisons
I would measure:

1. **Sequence deviations**
   - stops executed in a different order than planned
   - skipped stops
   - extra stops
   - repeated visits to same location

2. **Timing deviations**
   - late departure from depot
   - late arrival to customer
   - early/late service within time windows
   - excessive dwell time
   - overtime at shift end

3. **Behavioral deviations**
   - unplanned maintenance stops
   - detours off route
   - failed delivery attempts
   - excessive idling
   - route abandonment or re-optimization in the field

4. **Resource deviations**
   - wrong driver assigned
   - vehicle substitution
   - capacity violations

### C. Conformance metrics
Useful metrics:
- **fitness**: how much actual behavior fits the planned model
- **precision**: whether the model avoids too much unwanted behavior
- **alignment cost**: the cost of deviations
- **time deviation per activity**
- **case-level conformance score**

### D. Typical findings
For Speedy Parcels, I would expect deviations such as:
- drivers reordering stops due to traffic or convenience
- late depot departures causing cascading lateness
- unplanned stops caused by breakdowns
- failed deliveries due to customer unavailability
- excessive detours around congestion hotspots

# 2. Performance Analysis and Bottleneck Identification

## 2.1 Key KPIs and how to calculate them

### 1. On-Time Delivery Rate (OTD)
**Definition:** percentage of deliveries completed within the customer’s promised time window.

**Calculation:**
- For each package:
  - compare `Delivery Success` timestamp to promised delivery window
- OTD = on-time deliveries / total completed deliveries

### 2. Average Time per Delivery Stop
**Definition:** average time spent at each customer stop.

**Calculation:**
- `Depart Customer` timestamp - `Arrive Customer` timestamp
- average across all stops

### 3. Travel Time vs. Service Time Ratio
**Definition:** how much of the shift is spent driving versus serving customers.

**Calculation:**
- Travel time = time between departure from one stop and arrival at next stop
- Service time = arrival to departure at customer
- Ratio = travel time / service time

This helps show whether inefficiency is mostly driving-related or stop-related.

### 4. Fuel Consumption per km / per package
**Definition:** operational efficiency of the fleet.

**Calculation:**
- integrate fuel data if available; if not, estimate from telematics/vehicle type and idling
- fuel per km = total fuel used / distance traveled
- fuel per package = total fuel used / delivered packages

### 5. Vehicle Utilization Rate
**Definition:** proportion of vehicle operating time spent in productive delivery work.

**Calculation:**
- productive time = travel + delivery service
- total shift time = end shift - start shift
- utilization = productive time / total shift time

### 6. Frequency and Duration of Traffic Delays
**Definition:** how often and how long vehicles are slowed by congestion.

**Calculation:**
- identify low-speed or prolonged travel segments
- count delay events and total delay duration

### 7. Rate of Failed Deliveries
**Definition:** proportion of delivery attempts that fail.

**Calculation:**
- failed attempts / total attempts
- can also split by reason if available:
  - customer not home
  - access issue
  - wrong address
  - unavailable parcel locker

### 8. Overtime Rate
**Definition:** percentage of shifts exceeding planned working hours.

**Calculation:**
- actual shift end - planned shift end
- overtime cases / total shifts

## 2.2 Bottleneck identification techniques

### A. Process performance analysis
Use process mining overlays on the discovered model:
- average waiting time per activity
- throughput time by route
- bottleneck coloring on maps and process graphs
- service time distribution by stop type

### B. Variant analysis
Compare:
- high-performing vs. low-performing routes
- on-time vs. late deliveries
- experienced vs. new drivers
- weekdays vs. weekends
- urban vs. suburban areas

This often reveals that a small number of variants account for a large share of delays.

### C. Temporal analysis
- heatmaps by hour of day and day of week
- congestion peaks
- peak failure times for customer availability
- maintenance interruptions by shift length

### D. Spatial analysis
Map delays and stop durations by location:
- recurring traffic hotspots
- hard-to-park zones
- customers with long service times
- depot loading bottlenecks

### E. Resource analysis
Compare performance by:
- driver
- vehicle type
- route
- depot
- shift

### F. Quantifying impact
For each bottleneck, estimate:
- average delay added per case
- percentage of late deliveries attributable to it
- fuel waste due to idling or detours
- overtime minutes caused
- additional failed attempt risk

Example:
- “Stops in zone A add 6.4 minutes average dwell time and increase late delivery risk by 18%.”
- “Low-speed traffic segments between 08:00–09:30 contribute 22% of daily lateness.”

# 3. Root Cause Analysis for Inefficiencies

## 3.1 Likely root causes

### A. Suboptimal route planning
Static routes may ignore:
- live traffic
- road closures
- delivery time windows
- real stop durations

### B. Inaccurate travel time estimates
If planned travel times are too optimistic:
- late arrivals cascade through the route
- drivers skip or reorder stops
- overtime increases

### C. Traffic congestion patterns
Certain corridors may systematically slow the fleet during peak hours.

### D. High variability in service time
Some customers require:
- gated access
- parking search
- signature collection
- long handoff times
This variability can make planned schedules unrealistic.

### E. Vehicle breakdowns / maintenance issues
Unscheduled repairs increase:
- route interruptions
- missed time windows
- replacement vehicle costs
- service instability

### F. Driver behavior or skill differences
Drivers may differ in:
- parking efficiency
- route adherence
- handling failed attempts
- driving smoothness and fuel efficiency

### G. Failed delivery attempts
Repeated failures create:
- re-delivery costs
- customer dissatisfaction
- route inefficiency
- extra mileage

---

## 3.2 How process mining validates these root causes

### Variant analysis
Compare process variants:
- efficient routes may have fewer stops, fewer idle periods, and higher on-time rates
- inefficient routes may show loops, reattempts, or extra waiting

### Correlation analysis
Link event-log attributes with outcomes:
- traffic level vs. delivery lateness
- driver ID vs. average stop time
- vehicle ID vs. breakdown frequency
- time of day vs. fuel use and delay

### Dwell-time analysis
Examine time spent:
- at customer sites
- at depot loading
- in unscheduled stops
- during maintenance

Long dwell times can indicate:
- access problems
- manual paperwork
- poor customer readiness
- vehicle issues

### Performance clustering
Group routes or drivers with similar behavior:
- high-late/high-fuel
- low-late/low-fuel
- high-failed-attempt routes
This helps isolate operational patterns.

### Predictive analysis
Use historical log data to predict:
- probability of late delivery
- probability of failed attempt
- probability of unplanned stop
- expected route duration

This is especially useful for validating whether congestion, time windows, or service variability drive the problem.

# 4. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

## Strategy 1: Dynamic routing based on real-time traffic and historical process insights

### Targeted inefficiency
- traffic delays
- late deliveries
- unnecessary mileage
- excessive idle time

### Root cause addressed
- static route plans that do not reflect actual congestion or service durations

### How process mining supports it
- identifies recurring delay hotspots and timing patterns
- reveals which route variants perform best under which conditions
- shows where route deviations actually improve or worsen outcomes

### What to implement
- real-time re-routing using traffic feeds
- route recommendations based on historical stop-duration models
- dynamic stop sequencing for same-day changes

### Expected KPI impact
- higher On-Time Delivery Rate
- lower average travel time
- lower fuel consumption
- reduced overtime

---

## Strategy 2: Territory and route redesign using historical stop and delay patterns

### Targeted inefficiency
- unbalanced route workloads
- poor geographic clustering
- high route variability

### Root cause addressed
- delivery territories and sequences not aligned with real service times and traffic conditions

### How process mining supports it
- identifies which clusters of stops consistently run late
- shows which delivery zones produce high dwell times or repeated failures
- quantifies route duration by area and time window

### What to implement
- redesign delivery territories using historical service durations and travel times
- group customers by actual service behavior, not just geography
- rebalance stops per route based on complexity, not only count

### Expected KPI impact
- improved vehicle utilization
- reduced route variability
- better on-time performance
- lower overtime and mileage

---

## Strategy 3: Failed-delivery reduction through better customer communication and time-window management

### Targeted inefficiency
- failed attempts
- re-delivery costs
- unnecessary revisits
- wasted driver time

### Root cause addressed
- customers unavailable during attempted delivery windows
- unrealistic or poorly matched time windows
- insufficient pre-arrival communication

### How process mining supports it
- identifies when and where failures occur most often
- reveals patterns by customer segment, time of day, and geography
- measures cost of failed attempts in time and mileage

### What to implement
- tighter ETA notifications
- customer confirmation before arrival
- smarter time-window allocation using historical success patterns
- alternative drop-off options, lockers, or pickup points for high-failure zones

### Expected KPI impact
- lower failed delivery rate
- higher first-attempt success rate
- reduced re-delivery cost
- better customer satisfaction

---

## Strategy 4: Predictive maintenance scheduling based on usage and exception patterns

### Targeted inefficiency
- unscheduled breakdowns
- route interruptions
- maintenance-related delays

### Root cause addressed
- reactive maintenance rather than condition- or usage-based maintenance

### How process mining supports it
- detects recurring unscheduled stops and repairs
- identifies vehicle-specific patterns of route disruption
- links mileage, idling, and operating hours to failure risk

### What to implement
- predictive maintenance triggers based on telematics and event history
- schedule preventive service during low-impact windows
- replace high-failure vehicles earlier

### Expected KPI impact
- fewer unplanned stops
- lower maintenance cost volatility
- improved route completion rate
- less overtime and missed windows

## Strategy 5: Driver coaching based on process behavior profiles

### Targeted inefficiency
- inconsistent service time
- excessive idling
- route non-adherence
- inefficient stop handling

### Root cause addressed
- variation in driver behavior, skills, or adherence to best practices

### How process mining supports it

# 5. Considering Operational Constraints and Monitoring

## 5.1 Accounting for operational constraints

Any optimization must respect real operational constraints:

### Driver working hours
- enforce legal driving and rest periods
- avoid route plans that systematically create overtime
- incorporate shift-end buffers

### Vehicle capacity
- do not assign more packages than load capacity or realistic handling time
- consider weight/volume and stop density

### Customer time windows
- keep deliveries within promised windows
- prioritize high-penalty windows
- use flexible sequencing where possible

### Maintenance windows
- avoid dispatching vehicles close to known service needs
- integrate preventive maintenance into planning

### Depot loading constraints
- control departure waves to reduce congestion at the depot
- ensure loading times are realistic

These constraints should be embedded into route optimization, not treated as post-processing rules.
