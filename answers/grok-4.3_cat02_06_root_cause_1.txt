**Analysis of Customer Support Ticket Resolution Process**

### 1. Cases with Significantly Longer Resolution Times

I calculated the total cycle time (from **Receive Ticket** to **Close Ticket**) for each case:

| Case ID | Resolution Time | Duration | Notes |
|---------|------------------|----------|-------|
| 101     | 2h 15m          | Short   | Normal flow |
| 103     | 1h 20m          | Short   | Fastest case |
| 102     | **25h 10m**     | Long    | Escalated |
| 104     | **24h 10m**     | Long    | No escalation |
| 105     | **49h 5m**      | **Very Long** | Escalated + multiple delays |

**Significantly delayed cases**: **102**, **104**, and especially **105**. Cases 101 and 103 represent the expected performance baseline (under 2.5 hours).

### 2. Root Causes of Performance Issues

The main factors driving the delays are:

**A. Escalation-Related Delays (Primary Driver)**
- Cases **102** and **105** were escalated to Level-2.
- Escalation introduces additional handoff points, which significantly extend cycle time.
- In **Case 105**, after escalation at 10:00 on Day 1, the next activity ("Investigate Issue") only occurred at 14:00 on Day 2 — a **28-hour gap**. This suggests poor escalation routing or Level-2 agent availability.

**B. Long Waiting Times Between Activities**
Several cases show excessive idle time:

| Case | Critical Delay | Duration | Likely Cause |
|------|----------------|----------|--------------|
| 102  | Escalate  Investigate | 2.5 hours | Handoff delay |
| 102  | Investigate  Resolve | 19 hours | Overnight wait |
| 104  | Assign  Investigate | 3.5 hours | Agent availability |
| 104  | Investigate  Resolve | 19 hours | Overnight wait |
| 105  | Escalate  Investigate (2nd) | **28 hours** | Severe escalation bottleneck |

**C. Overnight / End-of-Day Resolution Pattern**
- Multiple cases (102, 104, 105) show resolutions occurring the following morning.
- This suggests the process is heavily impacted by **standard business hours** rather than continuous handling. Tickets that aren't resolved by late afternoon tend to carry over to the next day, adding ~19–24 hours automatically.

**D. Inefficient Triage-to-Investigation Transition**
- Even in non-escalated Case 104, there was a 3.5-hour gap between assignment and investigation.
- This indicates potential issues with agent workload, ticket prioritization, or lack of immediate assignment to available agents.

### 3. How These Factors Increase Cycle Time

- **Handoff friction**: Every escalation adds waiting time for the next team to pick up the ticket. Case 105 demonstrates how one escalation can nearly double the cycle time.
- **Compounding delays**: Small delays early in the process (triage/assignment) combine with large gaps later (investigation and resolution), creating multiplicative effects.
- **Batch processing behavior**: The consistent overnight gaps suggest agents may only resolve tickets during specific time windows rather than continuously.
- **Resource contention**: Level-2 agents appear to be a bottleneck, as evidenced by the 28-hour gap in Case 105.

### Recommendations

1. **Reduce Unnecessary Escalations**
   - Improve initial triage quality (possibly with better decision trees or AI-assisted classification) to lower escalation rates.
   - Set clearer criteria for when escalation is truly needed.

2. **Address Escalation Bottlenecks**
   - Implement **escalation SLAs** (e.g., Level-2 must acknowledge within 2 hours).
   - Consider dedicated escalation queues or rotating Level-2 coverage to reduce the 28-hour gaps seen in Case 105.

3. **Minimize Idle Time**
   - Introduce **auto-assignment** rules that assign tickets to available agents immediately after triage.
   - Monitor and alert on tickets that remain in "Assigned" status beyond a threshold (e.g., 1 hour).

4. **Reduce Overnight Carryover**
   - Explore **shift coverage** or on-call rotation for late-day tickets.
   - Set a target to resolve at least 80% of tickets within the same business day when possible.

5. **Process Improvement Opportunities**
   - Track **escalation rate** as a key performance indicator.
   - Implement a "Fast Track" path for simple issues to avoid unnecessary steps.
   - Consider parallelizing investigation where possible (e.g., Level-1 can start basic checks even before full assignment).

**Summary**: The primary performance issues stem from **escalation handoffs** and **long idle periods** between activities, particularly after escalation and before resolution. Case 105 is the clearest example of how these factors compound. Addressing escalation efficiency and reducing waiting times between steps would likely yield the largest improvement in overall cycle time.
