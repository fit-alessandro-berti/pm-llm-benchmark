## Explanation of the Constraints

### 1. **Equivalence**
These pairs guarantee that certain activities always appear in the same number. For instance, every received order (`RO`) results in exactly one dispatch (`D`), and every packed order corresponds to one generated shipping label and one dispatch.

### 2. **Always Before / Always After**
These two constraints together capture the strict temporal ordering of the workflow. `Always Before` enforces the predecessor relationship (the trigger activity requires the second to have already happened), and `Always After` enforces the successor relationship (the trigger activity requires the second to occur later). The chain reflects the natural pipeline:
> **RO  CA  RI  PI  QC  PO  GSL  D**, with **RO  IC  RP** as a parallel sub-process.

### 3. **Never Together**
Since all activities in the process can co-exist within the same order (case), this set is empty.

### 4. **Activity Occurrences**
All core operational activities happen **exactly once** per order. `Record Payment (RP)` is the only flexible one — `{0, 1}` — because the payment may not have been recorded by the time the case is observed in the system.

### 5. **Directly-Follows Constraints**
These represent tight, immediate transitions that typically occur without any intervening activity or significant delay in the warehouse workflow — for example, generating a shipping label and dispatching the package happen back-to-back. Activities like `RI  PI` or `QC  PO` are not modeled as *directly following* because in real warehouse operations there is often a small time gap (e.g., a worker walking to the shelf, or moving items to a packing station).
