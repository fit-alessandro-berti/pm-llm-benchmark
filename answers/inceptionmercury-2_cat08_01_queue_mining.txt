## 1. Queue Identification & Characterization  

### 1.1  From timestamps to “waiting time”  

| Event log element | How it is used |
|-------------------|----------------|
| **START** of activity *A* (e.g., *Nurse Assessment*) | Marks the moment a patient **enters** the queue for *A*. |
| **COMPLETE** of the preceding activity *B* (e.g., *Registration*) | Marks the moment the patient **leaves** the queue for *B* and becomes eligible for *A*. |
| **Waiting time (queue time) for activity *A*** | `START_A  –  COMPLETE_B`  (difference in minutes/seconds).  If the patient starts *A* before *B* finishes (overlap), waiting time = 0 (parallel execution). |
| **Service time (processing time) for activity *A*** | `COMPLETE_A – START_A`.  Used later to separate “waiting” from “work”. |

**Definition (clinic context).**  
Waiting time = the elapsed time a patient spends **idle** between the moment the previous activity finishes and the moment the next activity begins. It captures the realworld “queue” a patient experiences (e.g., time spent in the hallway after registration while waiting for a nurse).

### 1.2  Core queuecharacterization metrics  

| Metric | Formula (per activity transition) | What it tells you |
|--------|-----------------------------------|-------------------|
| **Average waiting time (AWT)** | ` waiting_i / N_cases` | Typical experience; easy to compare across transitions. |
| **Median waiting time (MWT)** | 50th percentile of waiting_i | Robust to outliers; reflects “typical” patient. |
| **90th percentile waiting time (P90)** | 90th percentile of waiting_i | Highlights the worstcase experience for the majority. |
| **Maximum waiting time (Max)** | `max(waiting_i)` | Extreme outliers that may indicate process failures. |
| **Queue frequency (QF)** | `#cases with waiting_i > 0` | How often a queue actually forms (useful for lowvolume but highimpact steps). |
| **Excessivewait count (EWC)** | `#cases with waiting_i > threshold` (e.g., > 10min) | Direct link to patientsatisfaction complaints. |
| **Weighted wait (WW)** | ` (waiting_i × severity_i) /  severity_i` (severity could be urgency, patient type) | Gives more importance to urgent or highrisk patients. |

All metrics are computed **per transition** (e.g., *Registration  Nurse Assessment*, *Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation*, etc.) and can be aggregated by **patient type**, **urgency**, **dayofweek**, **timeofday**, or **resource**.

### 1.3  Identifying the *most critical* queues  

1. **Multicriteria ranking** – create a composite score for each transition:  

   ```
   CriticalScore = w1·(AWT) + w2·(P90) + w3·(QF) + w4·(EWC)
   ```
   where `w1…w4` reflect management priorities (e.g., w1 = 0.4, w2 = 0.3, w3 = 0.2, w4 = 0.1).  

2. **Impact on highvalue patients** – compute the same scores **segmented** by *Urgency* (Urgent vs. Normal) and *Patient Type* (New vs. Followup). A queue that is moderate overall but extremely long for urgent cases gets a higher *urgencyadjusted* score.  

3. **Bottleneck detection** – use the ** Throughleneckratio** (total waiting time for a transition ÷ total process time across all cases). The transition with the highest ratio consumes the largest share of overall cycle time.  

4. **Pareto analysis** – plot cumulative waiting time contribution of each transition. Typically, 20% of the transitions generate 80% of total waiting. Those are the firstorder targets.  

**Outcome:** The transition(s) that score highest on the composite metric, dominate the bottleneckratio, and appear in the Pareto “head” are flagged as *critical queues* for immediate remediation.

## 2. RootCause Analysis  

### 2.1  Potential sources of long queues  

| Source | How it manifests in the log | Typical diagnostic view |
|--------|----------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Resource bottleneck** (staff/room/equipment) | High **resource utilization** (> 85%) and long **idletime** before a resource can start the next case. | Resourceutilization heatmaps, resourcewaitingtime per activity. |
| **Activity dependency / handover delay** | Large gaps between *COMPLETE* of *B* and *START* of *A* even though the same resource could have performed *A*. | Variant analysis showing “idle handover” patterns, caselevel “handover time” distribution. |
| **High variability in service time** | Wide spread of **servicetime** for a given activity (e.g., Doctor Consultation 530min). | Control charts (Xbar, R) per activity; coefficient of variation (CV). |
| **Scheduling policy mismatch** | Peaks of arrivals (e.g., many 9am slots) causing simultaneous start of *Registration* but later overload of *Nurse Assessment*. | Arrivalrate curve vs. resource capacity curve; timeslice heatmaps. |
| **Patienttype differences** | New patients have longer *Registration* and *Nurse Assessment* times than Followup, leading to separate queues. | Segmented metrics (AWT, service time) by *Patient Type*. |
| **Urgencydriven preemptive routing** | Urgent cases may “jump” ahead of normal cases, causing normalcase queues to swell. | Queuejump detection (cases where `START_A` occurs before earlier cases’ `COMPLETE_B`). |
| **Equipment downtime / maintenance** | Gaps in *ECG Test* start times coinciding with known maintenance windows. | Correlation of equipmentdowntime logs with waiting spikes. |

### 2.2  Processmining techniques to surface root causes  

| Technique | What it extracts | How it helps pinpoint the cause |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------------------|
| **Resource performance analysis** (e.g., “resource workload” and “resource waiting time” in ProM/PM4Py) | For each resource: total busy time, idle time, number of cases handled, average waiting before the resource can start. | High idletime before a resource starts a case  *resource availability* issue; high busytime  *overallocation*. |
| **Bottleneck analysis (throughput time decomposition)** | Decomposes total case duration into activitylevel waiting + service times. | The activity with the largest waiting component is the bottleneck. |
| **Variant analysis** (casetype clustering) | Groups cases by identical activity sequences and timestamps. | Reveals whether certain *patient types* (e.g., NewUrgent) follow a longer path or experience extra waits. |
| **Conformance checking** (e.g., “deviation” from the ideal model) | Highlights cases that deviate from the expected order or timing constraints. | Deviations often correspond to *adhoc* workarounds or emergency overrides that create queues. |
| **Process discovery with “parallel gateways”** | Discovers where activities could be executed in parallel but are currently sequential. | If the discovered model shows a parallel branch that is not realized in reality, it suggests an *opportunity for parallelization*. |
| **Performance heatmaps** (timeslice analysis) | Visualizes waiting time per hourofday or dayofweek. | Identifies *peak periods* where staffing may be insufficient. |
| **Correlation analysis** (e.g., waiting time vs. resource skill level) | Computes statistical relationships between waiting and categorical variables. | Shows whether a particular nurse or room is associated with longer waits (training/experience issue). |

By combining these analyses, you can move from “*where* is the queue?” to “*why* does it exist?” – the essential step before designing interventions.

## 3. DataDriven Optimization Strategies  

Below are three concrete, mutually reinforcing strategies. Each is linked to a specific queue, a root cause, and quantitative expectations derived from the current log.

### Strategy1 – **Dynamic StaffShift Rebalancing (Resource Allocation)**  

| Element | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Target queue(s)** | *Registration  Nurse Assessment* and *Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation* (the two longest waiting transitions identified). |
| **Root cause** | **Resource bottleneck** – nurses are at 92% utilization during 9am11am, causing average wait of 12min (P90=22min). |
| **Data support** | Resourceutilization chart shows a steep rise from 70% (8am) to 92% (9am). Variant analysis shows that cases arriving between 9am10am have a 2× higher waiting time than those arriving at 11am. |
| **Proposed action** | Introduce a **flexshift pool** of 1–2 crosstrained nurses who can be called in when nurse utilization exceeds 85% (detected by a realtime dashboard). The pool works on a “floating” basis, covering both *Nurse Assessment* and *BloodTest* prep. |
| **Expected impact** | Simulation (using the log as input) predicts a **30% reduction** in average waiting time for the targeted transition (from 12min  8min) and a **15% drop** in overall visit duration. |
| **Cost** | Additional 0.5FTE per day ($2,500/month) – modest compared with the potential revenue from higher patient throughput. |

### Strategy2 – **Staggered Appointment Slotting (Scheduling Policy)**  

| Element | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Target queue(s)** | *Registration  Nurse Assessment* (peakhour surge) and *Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Test* (postconsultation backlog). |
| **Root cause** | **Arrivalrate mismatch** – 30% of daily appointments are booked at the top of the hour, creating a “burst” that overwhelms downstream resources. |
| **Data support** | Arrivalrate histogram shows spikes at 9:00, 10:00, 11:00. The 90th percentile waiting time for the 9am slot is 28min versus 9min for the 9:30 slot. |
| **Proposed action** | Implement a **“microbatch” schedule**: instead of 15minute blocks, allocate 7minute “firsthalf” slots followed by a 3minute buffer, allowing a smoother flow. Use the existing scheduling software to automatically spread appointments across the hour (e.g., 9:00, 9:07, 9:14, …). |
| **Expected impact** | Reduces the **arrivalrate variance** by ~40%, which in queueing theory translates to a **~20% reduction** in average waiting time for the registrationtonurse transition. Overall visit duration expected to drop by ~5min per case. |
| **Cost** | No additional staff; only a change in the scheduling algorithm (implementation effort 1personweek). |

### Strategy3 – **Parallel PreScreening Kiosk (Process Redesign & Technology)**  

| Element | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Target queue(s)** | *Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation* (longest wait after nurse) and *Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Test* (delay caused by ordering and preparing equipment). |
| **Root cause** | **Sequential handover** – the nurse completes assessment, then the doctor must manually retrieve the assessment notes before starting the consultation. |
| **Data support** | Handover analysis shows an average “handover gap” of 6min (max=18min). Variant analysis shows that cases where the nurse **preenters** data into the EHR have a 40% lower handover gap. |
| **Proposed action** | Deploy a **selfservice kiosk** (tablet + barcode scanner) in the waiting area where patients complete a short questionnaire and vitalsign capture (BP, HR) while waiting for the nurse. The kiosk automatically pushes data to the EHR, allowing the doctor to start the consultation immediately after the nurse’s brief check. |
| **Expected impact** | Reduces handover gap by ~4min, translating to a **~10% reduction** in total visit duration for NewUrgent patients (who typically have the longest handovers). |
| **Cost** | One kiosk ($3,500) plus integration time (2weeks). Expected ROI within 12months due to increased patient throughput and higher satisfaction scores. |

## 4. Tradeoffs & Constraints  

| Strategy | Potential downside | Mitigation / Balancing |
|----------|--------------------|------------------------|
| **Dynamic staffshift rebalancing** | May increase staff fatigue; risk of underutilizing nurses during lowdemand periods. | Use a **predictive staffing model** (e.g., ARIMA on historic arrivals) to trigger the flex pool only when utilization >85%; schedule optional “recovery” breaks. |
| **Staggered appointment slotting** | Could reduce the number of slots per hour, potentially lowering daily booking capacity. | Compensate by extending clinic hours by 30min (e.g., open at 8:30am instead of 9:00am) or by improving downstream capacity (Strategy1). |
| **Parallel prescreening kiosk** | Requires patient techliteracy; may create a new bottleneck at the kiosk if many patients wait to use it. | Deploy **two kiosks** and monitor usage; provide staff assistance for lowtech patients; include a fallback “paper” process. |
| **General** | Any change may temporarily increase *process variability* as staff adapt. | Run a **pilot** (2week “controlled” period) and compare KPIs before full rollout; provide targeted training. |

**Balancing objectives** –  
- **Cost vs. waittime**: Prioritize lowcost, highimpact changes first (e.g., scheduling redesign).  
- **Throughput vs. care quality**: Ensure that any reduction in waiting time does not truncate clinical assessment. Use **clinical quality metrics** (e.g., revisit rate, diagnostic accuracy) as guardrails.  
- **Staff satisfaction**: Incorporate staff feedback loops (surveys, daily huddles) to avoid burnout; a happy workforce is a prerequisite for sustained performance.

## 5. Measuring Success  

### 5.1  Core KPIs (postimplementation)  

| KPI | Definition | Target (example) |
|-----|------------|------------------|
| **Average total visit duration (ATVD)** | Mean of `(final checkout COMPLETE – first registration START)` per case. | 45min (down from 58min). |
| **Average waiting time per critical transition (AWTCT)** | Mean waiting for the two targeted queues (RegistrationNurse, NurseDoctor). | 6min (down from 12min). |
| **90th percentile waiting time (P90WT)** | 90% of cases experience waiting P90WT. | 15min (down from 22min). |
| **Patient satisfaction score (PS) – “wait time” item** | Survey Likert (15) on perceived waiting. | 4.2 (up from 3.5). |
| **Resource utilization (RU)** | % of time each resource is busy (excluding idle waiting). | 7585% (avoid >90%). |
| **Number of “excessivewait” cases (EWC)** | Cases with any transition waiting >10min. | 5% of total cases. |
| **Staff overtime hours** | Total overtime per week. | 2h (maintain current level). |

### 5.2  Ongoing process monitoring  

1. **Automated ETL pipeline** – nightly ingestion of the day’s event log into a processmining platform (e.g., PM4Py, Celonis).  
2. **Dashboard** – realtime visualizations of the KPIs above, plus heatmaps of waiting times per hourofday.  
3. **Alerting** – set thresholds (e.g., P90WT >20min) that trigger an email to the operations manager.  
4. **Periodic rediscovery** – every month, rerun process discovery to detect emergent variants (e.g., new pathways introduced by the kiosk).  
5. **A/B testing** – if multiple scheduling policies are experimented with, use **statistical process control (SPC)** charts to compare their impact on waiting times.  

By maintaining this closedloop monitoring, the clinic can quickly spot regressions (e.g., a new bottleneck emerging after a staff change) and iterate on the optimization measures.

---

### **Takeaway**  

1. **Quantify** every queue using start/completion timestamps  waitingtime metrics.  
2. **Rank** queues with a composite criticality score, focusing on those that dominate overall cycle time and affect highurgency patients.  
3. **Diagnose** root causes through resource, variant, and handover analyses.  
4. **Act** with three databacked interventions: flexible staffing, smarter appointment slotting, and a parallel prescreening kiosk.  
5. **Monitor** continuously with a set of KPIs and an automated dashboard to ensure that improvements are sustained and that new bottlenecks are caught early.  

Implementing this structured, evidencedriven workflow will enable the clinic to cut waiting times, improve patient satisfaction, and do so without a disproportionate rise in operational cost.
