## 1Identify the Anomalies in the POWL Model  

| **Anomaly** | **What it Looks Like in the POWL Diagram** | **Why It Is Problematic (Business Perspective)** |
|-------------|--------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| **Loop between Evaluation & Approval** (`E  P` inside a `LOOP`) | A cyclic edge that can be traversed repeatedly. After an evaluation step, the claim may go back to *Evaluate* again – possibly because the system does not enforce “approval once only”. | If an adjuster never reaches the final approval event, the claim could remain in an endless loop of reevaluation (e.g., a bug or an incomplete rule). |
| **XOR that May Omit Customer Notification** (`N  skip`) | An exclusiveor edge after the loop. Execution may choose *Notify*  *Close*, or it may stay on `skip` and jump directly to *Close*. | Business expects every claim to be notified; skipping can lead to compliance issues, loss of audit trail, and unhappy customers who receive no communication about status changes. |
| **Premature Partial Ordering (A  C)** | A direct edge from *Assign Adjuster* (`A`) to *Close Claim* (`C`). Even before evaluation/ approval the claim may be closed by a senior manager or an emergency override. | Could result in claims that are “closed” without any substantive work being done, violating SLAs and creating hidden liability for the insurer. |
| **Missing Order Between Loop & Close** (no explicit edge from `xor`  `C`) | The loop may end with *Notify* but there is no rule forcing a final close event after notification if approval was never reached. | Leads to orphaned claim events that are not properly persisted in the system, making it hard to reconcile financials or legal obligations. |

## 2Hypotheses on Why These Anomalies Might Exist  

| **Possible Cause** | **Rationale / Evidence That Could Support It** |
|--------------------|-----------------------------------------------|
| **Incomplete Business Rule Implementation** | The loop may be a placeholder for “reevaluate” due to a rule change that was only partially coded (e.g., an oversight when adding a new policy type). This is often seen in legacy systems where developers added extra validation steps without fully updating the process model. |
| **Miscommunication Between Departments** | If adjusters and compliance teams disagreed on how “approval” should be recorded, some may have been instructed to close claims after a single approval attempt (i.e., they bypassed later loops). Communication gaps can lead to adhoc shortcuts being coded into the workflow engine. |
| **Technical Error in Workflow Engine** | The model might be generated from an outdated BPMN/DMN export that inadvertently retained legacy conditional logic (`E  P`) and a missing edge from `xor` to `C`. A bug could prevent downstream events from firing, causing the loop to persist indefinitely until manually fixed. |
| **Insufficient Constraints / Controls** | There may be no audit trails or alerts when an event is skipped (e.g., notification omitted). Without proper enforcement of required sequential steps, users can “shortcircuit” the process without detection. |
| **Regulatory Pressure for Speed** | In some markets, insurers feel pressure to close claims quickly; thus, a shortcut (`A  C`) may have been introduced to meet SLAs despite not satisfying full compliance checks (e.g., missing review). |

## 3How to Verify These Hypotheses Using the Underlying Database  

Below are concrete PostgreSQL queries that can be run against the three tables you provided (`claims`, `adjusters`, and `claim_events`) to surface evidence of each anomaly.

### A. Claims Closed Without Proper Evaluation/Approval  
**Goal:** Find claims where a *Close Claim* event occurs **before** any *Evaluate* (E) or *Approve* (P) step has been recorded in the workflow log.

```sql
-- Identify claim events that are missing an evaluation/approval activity
WITH all_events AS (
    SELECT ce.claim_id,
           ce.timestamp,
           ce.activity
    FROM claim_events ce
),
max_event_ts AS (
    SELECT claim_id, MAX(timestamp) AS latest_ts
    FROM claim_events
    GROUP BY claim_id
)
SELECT a.claim_id,
       c.claim_amount,
       a.timestamp               -- timestamp of the earliest event (often Close Claim)
FROM all_events a
JOIN max_event_ts m
  ON a.claim_id = m.claim_id
 AND a.timestamp = m.latest_ts   -- assumes latest_ts corresponds to Close Claim
WHERE NOT EXISTS (
    SELECT 1
    FROM all_events b
    WHERE b.claim_id = a.claim_id
      AND LOWER(b.activity) IN ('evaluate', 'approve')
);
```

**Interpretation:** Any claim returned here likely violates the “must have evaluation/approval” rule, suggesting either premature closure or missing activity logs.

### B. Claims Where Customer Notification Was Skipped (XOR Path Not Taken)  
**Goal:** Detect cases where after a loop/evaluation, the system chose *skip* rather than sending a notification (`N`).

```sql
SELECT claim_id,
       submission_date,
       MAX(timestamp) AS last_activity_ts
FROM claim_events
WHERE activity IN ('evaluate', 'approve')   -- only events that should precede Notify
GROUP BY claim_id
HAVING MAX(timestamp) < (
    SELECT MIN(timestamp)
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE activity = 'notify'
);
```

**Interpretation:** If a *Notify* event timestamp is later than the latest evaluation/approval, it indicates the notification step was omitted (perhaps because of an XOR shortcut).

---

### C. Premature Claim Closure Without Proper Evaluation/Approval  
**Goal:** Identify claims that were closed directly after assignment (`A`) without any prior evaluation or approval.

```sql
SELECT claim_id,
       submission_date,
       MAX(timestamp) AS close_ts
FROM claim_events
WHERE activity = 'close_claim'   -- assuming the event label maps to C in POWL diagram
GROUP BY claim_id
HAVING MAX(timestamp) <= (
    SELECT MIN(timestamp)
    FROM claim_events
    WHERE LOWER(activity) IN ('evaluate', 'approve')
);
```

**Interpretation:** A claim closed before any evaluation/approval suggests either a policy exception, an emergency override, or simply a process bug (missing constraint enforcement).

### D. Claims Entering the Loop More Than Once Without Subsequent Approval  
**Goal:** Spot claims that repeatedly enter the *Evaluate  Approve* loop but never reach a final approval event.

```sql
WITH claim_activity AS (
    SELECT ce.claim_id,
           COUNT(*) OVER (PARTITION BY ce.claim_id) AS cnt_loop_enter,
           MAX(timestamp) OVER (PARTITION BY ce.claim_id) AS last_ts
    FROM claim_events ce
    WHERE LOWER(activity) IN ('evaluate', 'approve')
),
loop_claim AS (
    SELECT claim_id
    FROM claim_activity
    GROUP BY claim_id, cnt_loop_enter
    HAVING cnt_loop_enter > 1   -- entered loop multiple times
       AND MAX(timestamp) < (SELECT MIN(timestamp)
                              FROM claim_events
                              WHERE activity = 'approve');
    )
SELECT lc.claim_id,
       c.claim_amount,
       lc.cnt_loop_enter AS loops_entered,
       a.timestamp            -- timestamp of the latest approve event (if any)
FROM loop_claim lc
JOIN claims c ON lc.claim_id = c.claim_id
LEFT JOIN claim_events a
  ON lc.claim_id = a.claim_id AND LOWER(a.activity) = 'approve';
```

**Interpretation:** If `a.timestamp` is `NULL` (or far in the past), it signals that no final approval occurred, likely due to an endless loop or missing termination condition.

## Putting It All Together  

1. **Run the anomaly detection queries** on a recent slice of data (e.g., last 612 months) to generate candidate cases.  
2. **Correlate findings with adjuster notes / customer feedback** in your CRM system to see whether any of these cases were intentional overrides or merely errors.  
3. **Investigate the root cause**: If you find many premature closures, it may be a regulatory pressure issue; if loops are repeatedly entered without termination, that points toward incomplete rule handling (likely a bug).  

By combining statistical anomaly detection with concrete evidence from the database, you can pinpoint whether these deviations stem from business policy changes, miscommunication, technical failures, or enforcement gaps—and then take corrective actions (e.g., update workflow rules, add audit alerts, retrain staff on proper claim handling).

Feel free to adapt the queries for your exact event labels (`activity`) and column names; the logic remains the same: **measure what should exist** versus **what actually exists**, and use that discrepancy as a diagnostic tool.
