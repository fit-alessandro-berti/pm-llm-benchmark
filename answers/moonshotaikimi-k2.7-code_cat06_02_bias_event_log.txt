## Where bias appears

Bias is introduced primarily in the **PreliminaryScoring** activity and is then carried through, unchanged, to the **FinalDecision**.

| Activity | What happens | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **PreliminaryScoring** (Scoring Engine) | Adds `+10 (Community)` whenever the applicant belongs to **“Highland Civic Darts Club.”** | This adjustment is not tied to credit risk in the log. It gives a points bonus to one specific social/community group. |
| **ManualReview** (Underwriter) | Reviewer sees the adjusted score and the community label, but never removes or revises the bonus. | The manual step acts as a rubber stamp, legitimizing the community-based advantage rather than checking it. |
| **FinalDecision** (Rules Engine) | Approves cases using the community-inflated score (and/or a separate local/community rule). | The final decision rewards affiliation rather than pure creditworthiness. |

## How this influences fairness and equity

### 1. **It creates unequal thresholds**
Applicants who belong to the favored club face a lower effective approval bar than equally—or more—creditworthy applicants who do not. The system is not comparing “apples to apples.”

### 2. **It confuses “creditworthiness” with “social connections”**
The `PreliminaryScore` is meant to reflect underlying creditworthiness. Adding points for club membership mixes in a non-credit factor. Unless club membership is independently shown to predict repayment risk, the adjustment is arbitrary from a risk perspective.

### 3. **It can act as a proxy for protected characteristics**
Even if `CommunityGroup` and `LocalResident` are not themselves legally protected traits, they can correlate with race, ethnicity, national origin, age, religion, disability, or socioeconomic status. A “Highland Civic Darts Club” may, for example, be overwhelmingly older, male, of a particular ethnicity, or from a specific neighborhood. If so, the bonus produces **disparate impact**: a neutral-sounding rule systematically disadvantages people outside that network.

### 4. **It undermines transparency and trust**
The log shows the adjustment explicitly, which is good for auditability, but the **reason** for the adjustment is not justified by risk data. When applicants are rejected despite higher scores, it becomes hard to explain why the process is fair.

## Implications for individuals without the “right” affiliation

- **Must score higher to compensate:** A non-member, or someone who is not a local resident, must have a substantially higher preliminary score to receive the same decision as a club member.  
- **Exclusion by circumstance:** Many people cannot join a specific civic club because of cost, location, time, language, cultural barriers, or simply not being invited. They are penalized for factors unrelated to their ability to repay.  
- **Geographic unfairness:** Because the bonus appears available only to local residents affiliated with a local club, non-local applicants are permanently excluded from the advantage, even if their finances are stronger.  
- **Feedback loop of privilege:** If club members are approved more easily, they may accumulate more assets and access, widening inequality over time.

---

## Bottom line

The process contains **procedural bias**: a score adjustment and/or decision rule rewards membership in **Highland Civic Darts Club** and, by extension, **local residency**. This produces **outcome unfairness**—lower-scoring affiliated applicants are approved while higher-scoring unaffiliated applicants are rejected.

To improve fairness and equity, the organization should:

1. **Remove or justify** community-based score adjustments with evidence that they predict repayment risk.  
2. **Audit** whether `LocalResident` and `CommunityGroup` correlate with protected characteristics.  
3. **Blind manual reviewers** to non-credit attributes like community group or local status.  
4. **Monitor approval rates** across demographic and geographic groups for disparate impact.  
5. **Document the decision rule** so that similarly creditworthy applicants are treated similarly, regardless of social affiliation.
