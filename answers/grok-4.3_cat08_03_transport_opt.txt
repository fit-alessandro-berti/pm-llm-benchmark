**1. Process Discovery and Conformance Checking**

To create a cohesive event log, integrate the four data sources by using Vehicle ID and timestamps as primary keys, with Case ID defined as Vehicle-Day (as in the provided snippet). GPS data provides continuous location/speed traces that are aggregated into discrete events (e.g., "Travel Segment" between stops or "Low Speed Detected" when speed drops below a threshold like 10 km/h for >2 minutes). Scanner events are directly mapped as activities (Arrive Customer, Delivery Success/Failed, Depart Customer). Dispatch data supplies planned attributes (route sequence, time windows, package count) attached as case attributes or separate "Planned Activity" events. Maintenance logs add events like "Unscheduled Stop" or "Maintenance Start" when timestamps align with GPS idle periods. Preprocessing involves: timestamp normalization (handling potential clock drifts across systems), deduplication of overlapping events, imputation of missing locations via interpolation of GPS points, and filtering of noise (e.g., GPS outliers >50m from expected paths). The resulting event log would contain attributes such as Case ID, Activity, Timestamp, Vehicle ID, Driver ID, Package ID, Location, Speed, Planned vs Actual flag, and Notes.

Challenges include high-volume GPS streams (potentially millions of records), incomplete synchronization (scanner timestamps may lag GPS), multi-granularity data (GPS is continuous while scanners are event-based), and privacy/accuracy issues with location data. These require robust ETL pipelines with spatial joins and event abstraction techniques.

Process discovery would apply algorithms such as the Inductive Miner or Heuristic Miner to the integrated log to generate an as-is process model. This model would visualize the end-to-end flow: Start Shift  Route Assigned  Depart Depot  Travel Segments (with embedded Low Speed Detected or Unscheduled Stop branches)  Arrive Customer  Delivery Success/Failed  Depart Customer (loops for multiple stops)  possible Maintenance events  Arrive Depot  End Shift. The model would highlight parallel paths for failed deliveries (re-attempts) and deviations like unscheduled stops interrupting travel.

Conformance checking would import the dispatch system's planned route sequences as a normative model (e.g., via a BPMN or Petri net representation of the 35 stops). Using alignment-based conformance (e.g., in ProM or Celonis), compute fitness (how well the log fits the model) and precision (avoiding overgeneralization). Key deviations to examine include: sequence deviations (e.g., visiting stops out of planned order), insertion of unplanned activities (Unscheduled Stop for engine issues), time deviations (actual travel times exceeding planned estimates by >20%), and skips (missing time windows leading to failed deliveries). In the snippet, an example deviation is the Unscheduled Stop at 11:05 interrupting the route, which would lower conformance fitness.

**2. Performance Analysis and Bottleneck Identification**

Key KPIs derived directly from the event log include:
- On-Time Delivery Rate: Percentage of Delivery Success events where timestamp falls within dispatch time windows (calculated via attribute comparison).
- Average Time per Delivery Stop: Mean duration from Arrive Customer to Depart Customer per stop (using timestamp differences, filtered by Package ID).
- Travel Time vs. Service Time Ratio: Total moving time (GPS speed >5 km/h segments) divided by total service time (customer dwell periods).
- Fuel Consumption Proxy per km/package: Estimated from cumulative distance (GPS coordinates) and idle time (speed=0 periods), correlated with vehicle utilization; actual fuel data can be joined if available from maintenance logs.
- Vehicle Utilization Rate: Percentage of shift time spent moving (from Depart Depot to Arrive Depot, excluding idle > threshold).
- Frequency/Duration of Traffic Delays: Count and total time of "Low Speed Detected" events, grouped by location clusters.
- Rate of Failed Deliveries: Proportion of Delivery Failed events to total customer attempts.

These are computed using standard process mining performance views: throughput time analysis on case-level, activity duration histograms, and resource (Driver/Vehicle) utilization metrics.

Bottleneck identification uses waiting time analysis between activities, activity frequency heatmaps, and throughput time breakdowns. Techniques include filtering by route (via Location clusters), time of day (timestamp buckets), driver (Driver ID variants), vehicle type, or traffic hotspots (spatial aggregation of low-speed events). For instance, long durations between Depart Customer and next Arrive Customer indicate travel bottlenecks, while extended Arrive-to-Depart times point to customer interaction or parking issues. Impact is quantified by cost of delay (extra overtime from End Shift timestamps) or lost packages (failed delivery counts). In the snippet, the 11:05 Unscheduled Stop and 16:50 depot arrival with overtime suggest maintenance and late-shift bottlenecks.

**3. Root Cause Analysis for Inefficiencies**

Beyond location of delays, root causes are investigated via targeted analyses. Suboptimal route planning appears in variant analysis: compare high-performing cases (short throughput, few failures) vs. low-performing (high overtime, traffic events) using process variants or decision mining. Inaccurate travel estimates are validated by correlating planned dispatch times with actual GPS-derived travel durations, revealing systematic underestimation during peak hours. Traffic congestion patterns are analyzed via spatial clustering of "Low Speed Detected" events, correlated with timestamp and location to identify recurring hotspots. High service time variability is measured through dwell time distributions per customer/location, potentially linked to customer density or parking difficulties. Vehicle breakdowns are examined by aligning maintenance log events with preceding GPS idle periods and speed anomalies (e.g., the engine warning in the snippet). Driver behavior differences are uncovered via social network mining or performance leaderboards across Driver IDs. Failed deliveries are root-caused by correlating Delivery Failed events with time windows, driver, and prior traffic delays, often indicating re-delivery loops increasing fuel costs.

Process mining validates these through: variant analysis (high vs. low performers), correlation rules (e.g., traffic events preceding long service times), and dwell time analysis (Arrive-to-Depart distributions). This distinguishes symptoms (e.g., late arrivals) from causes (e.g., static routing ignoring real-time speed data).

**4. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies**

**Strategy 1: Dynamic Routing with Real-Time Traffic Integration**  
Targets: Suboptimal static routes and traffic-induced delays. Root cause: Inaccurate pre-shift travel estimates ignoring live GPS speed patterns. Supported by: Discovery of frequent "Low Speed Detected" clusters and conformance deviations in travel segments. Expected impacts: 15-25% reduction in Travel Time vs. Service Time ratio, improved On-Time Delivery Rate, and lower fuel consumption per km through rerouting around hotspots.

**Strategy 2: Historical Performance-Based Territory and Sequence Optimization**  
Targets: Inefficient stop ordering and high failed delivery rates. Root cause: Planned routes not reflecting actual variability in service times and customer accessibility. Supported by: Variant analysis showing better performance on certain driver/route combinations and spatial clustering of failed deliveries. Expected impacts: Higher Vehicle Utilization Rate, reduced Rate of Failed Deliveries (by 20-30%), and shorter Average Time per Delivery Stop via re-sequenced routes.

**Strategy 3: Predictive Maintenance Scheduling**  
Targets: Unscheduled stops and overtime from breakdowns. Root cause: Reactive maintenance triggered mid-shift (as in the 11:05 engine warning event). Supported by: Correlation of GPS idle periods and speed anomalies with maintenance log timestamps, plus utilization metrics preceding failures. Expected impacts: Lower Frequency/Duration of Unscheduled Stops, improved On-Time Delivery Rate, and reduced maintenance-related fuel waste.

**5. Considering Operational Constraints and Monitoring**

Strategies incorporate constraints by embedding them as hard filters in optimization models: driver working hours (enforced via Start/End Shift timestamps to avoid overtime), vehicle capacities (from dispatch Package ID counts), and customer time windows (prioritized in routing to minimize Delivery Failed events). Dynamic routing would use real-time GPS but cap adjustments within remaining shift time. Territory optimization respects depot return deadlines.

For continuous monitoring, implement process mining dashboards (e.g., in Celonis or Power BI with PM plugins) refreshed daily or in near-real-time from streaming logs. Track: live process maps with deviation overlays, KPI trend lines (On-Time Delivery Rate, utilization), variant heatmaps (high- vs. low-performer routes), and automated alerts for conformance fitness drops below 80% or new bottleneck emergence (e.g., sudden increase in Low Speed events). Weekly reviews would compare pre/post-implementation conformance and performance, triggering re-discovery if new patterns (e.g., seasonal traffic) appear. This ensures optimizations remain effective and adaptive to evolving operations.
