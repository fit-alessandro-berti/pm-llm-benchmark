## 1) Queue Identification and Characterization

### 1.1 How to compute waiting (queue) times from the event log
Given the log contains **start** and **completion** timestamps for activities per visit (case), we can compute *waiting time between activities* by ordering the activities along each case’s timeline.

For two consecutive activities in the care pathway for the same visit—say activity **A** completes at time `t_complete(A)` and the next activity **B** starts at time `t_start(B)`—define the **waiting/queue time** as:

\[
\text{Wait}(A\rightarrow B) = t_{start}(B) - t_{complete}(A)
\]

**Important details to handle in implementation:**
- **Activity ordering**: Use timestamps (and case-consistency rules) to determine the next activity. If multiple activities can occur “in parallel” (e.g., simultaneous diagnostics), you may need a “closest-in-time next step” rule or model concurrency explicitly (queue mining often assumes a local successor relationship).
- **Same-activity repeats**: If an activity repeats (e.g., blood test re-run), treat each occurrence as separate events and recompute waits accordingly.
- **Resource-based waiting vs. patient-based waiting**:
  - *Patient waiting* as above measures time a patient is not in service.
  - If resources are recorded, you can also compute **queue length proxy** and **resource waiting** (e.g., how long a patient waited for “the same room/equipment/staff”).
- **Cleaning/imputation**:
  - Filter out negative waits (clock issues).
  - Decide how to treat long gaps that correspond to intentional off-hours waiting or scheduled delays (e.g., patient returns later). If timestamps span breaks across days, tag these as “external waiting” vs “process waiting”.

### 1.2 Queue mining “where to look”
Once you compute `Wait(AB)` for each case, you can treat each **arc** `(AB)` (or more generally each *handover boundary*) as a queue segment:
- Examples: `RegistrationNurse Assessment`, `Doctor ConsultationECG Test`, `Lab TestSpecialist Review`, `Last ClinicalCheck-out`.

If resource IDs exist, you can further refine queues as:
- **Resource-specific queues**: `Doctor ConsultationECG Test` waiting **for Room/Tech X** (or for a category like “Cardiology equipment”).
- **Staff-specific queues**: waiting for a specific clinician/nurse.

### 1.3 Key metrics to characterize queues
For each queue segment `(AB)` (and optionally per patient type / urgency / resource), calculate:

**Central tendency & tail risk**
- **Average waiting time** (mean)
- **Median waiting time** (robust to outliers)
- **Max waiting time** (useful but often dominated by rare events)
- **90th / 95th percentile waiting times** (clinically meaningful “worst typical experience”)

**Volume & prevalence**
- **Queue frequency**: number of occurrences of `(AB)` across all visits
- **Case impact rate**: % of cases where `(AB)` wait exceeds a threshold (e.g., >15 min, >30 min)
- **Urgent vs. non-urgent exposure**: same metrics split by urgency and patient type

**Concurrency-aware proxies (optional but valuable)**
- If resources are available from the log, you can approximate:
  - **Utilization pressure** (service times + overlap)
  - **Queueing pressure** at decision points by aggregating overlaps for the same resource.

### 1.4 Identifying the most critical queues
Use a **priority scoring** approach rather than ranking by a single metric. A practical scoring rubric:

**(1) Severity of tail waiting**
- Highest 90th percentile (or 95th percentile) waits indicate consistent dissatisfaction risk.

**(2) Magnitude of average delay**
- Long mean waits suggest systematic bottlenecks.

**(3) Reach / frequency**
- A queue that affects 80% of visits may be more urgent than one affecting 1%, even if the maximum is larger.

**(4) Patient segment impact**
- Separate by **patient type** (New vs Follow-up) and **urgency**.
- Prioritize queues where **urgent patients** have disproportionate waits (fairness/safety perception issues).

**(5) Downstream amplification**
- Identify queues that sit “upstream” of many downstream steps: delays early in the pathway inflate later waits. You can estimate this by how often the arc appears in long overall journeys.

A typical outcome is a **Top-K queue list** (e.g., 5–10 queue segments) with stratified statistics per patient urgency/type.

## 2) Root Cause Analysis (RCA) Using Process Mining

Identifying “where waiting occurs” is not enough; the next step is “why it occurs.” In healthcare queue problems, root causes usually come from one or more of: **capacity constraints**, **variability**, **routing rules**, or **system coordination**.

### 2.1 Resource bottlenecks (capacity & utilization)
**Hypothesis examples**
- Nurse assessments: limited nurses  patients pile up before nurse availability.
- Doctor consultations: physician time is the bottleneck, especially for certain specialties.
- Diagnostics: single ECG room/equipment or tech  queue before test start.

**How to confirm using log data**
- **Resource utilization & saturation**:
  - Compute per-resource:
    - total busy time (sum of service durations),
    - idle time,
    - overlap burden (if you can infer simultaneous service).
- **Wait vs utilization correlation**:
  - For queue segment `(AB)` that targets resource R, test whether higher utilization of R corresponds to higher patient waits at that segment.
- **Service time distributions**:
  - Compare waiting time and service time. Sometimes waits are “not capacity but variability.”
- **Bottleneck analysis**:
  - Identify resources with high “work content” and low effective throughput.

### 2.2 Activity dependencies and handovers (process logic & rework)
**Hypothesis examples**
- Missing documentation after Registration  repeated triage steps.
- Doctor consult must be completed before diagnostic scheduling; scheduling delay causes systematic waiting.
- “Check-out” requires multiple validations; staff may wait on upstream orders.

**Log-based techniques**
- **Handover analysis**: identify queue arcs with long waits and check if they correlate with:
  - reruns/rework variants,
  - missing/late prerequisites (e.g., lab results not ready by time doctor expects them).
- **Variant/Path analysis**:
  - Use process mining conformance/variant clustering to find patient pathways associated with longer waits.
  - Example: variants where “Specialist Review” happens after “Test results delay” vs after “Test completed promptly.”

### 2.3 Variability in service times (statistical variability creates queues)
**Hypothesis examples**
- Doctor consultation duration varies widely by specialty, urgency, or patient complexity.
- Lab turnaround times are variable (sample problems, batching).

**Log-based confirmation**
- For each activity, analyze:
  - service time mean/median/percentiles,
  - coefficient of variation (CV),
  - outlier rate.
- Check whether queues appear when upstream service time variability is high.
- If variability differs by patient type, you can explain why certain segments disproportionately affect “New” or “Urgent.”

### 2.4 Appointment scheduling policies & arrival patterns
**Hypothesis examples**
- Front-loaded schedule leads to bursts arriving at Registration.
- Clinics schedule diagnostics at fixed times  batching effect.

**How to analyze**
- **Arrival-time distribution**: compute case arrival patterns (based on Registration START).
- Identify peaks: time-of-day / day-of-week.
- **Burstiness**: compute inter-arrival times; correlate bursts with queue growth.
- Compare queues across appointment types (if present via patient urgency/type).

### 2.5 Patient routing differences by type/urgency
**Hypothesis examples**
- Urgent patients should bypass some steps but still get queued at specific capacity (e.g., imaging equipment).
- Follow-up patients may be routed faster, but they still wait for a shared doctor resource.

**How to validate**
- Stratify queue metrics by:
  - patient type (New vs Follow-up),
  - urgency,
  - specialty,
  - insurance or other attributes if available.
- Determine whether:
  - routing logic increases steps for some patients,
  - or waits are “equally bad” (capacity problem) vs “selectively bad” (routing/scheduling issue).

### 2.6 Concrete process-mining techniques to pinpoint causes
In addition to pure queue time computation, you can apply:
- **Performance bottleneck discovery**: identify activities/resources whose throughput constraints align with queue segments.
- **Resource-task replay / alignment**: check whether assigned resources match expected process behavior; flag deviations (e.g., wrong staff leads to rework).
- **Variant clustering**: find top process variants and compare their queue arcs.
- **Time-of-day heatmaps**: queue metrics by hour/day to uncover scheduling mismatch.
- **Queue mining with “work in progress” proxies**: approximate backlog by counting concurrent cases waiting for a resource (if timestamps allow inference).

## 3) Data-Driven Optimization Strategies (Concrete, Targeted, Measurable)

Below are **three+ distinct strategies**, each targeting specific critical queues and hypothesized root causes. (You can implement them sequentially with A/B or phased rollouts.)

### Strategy A — Rebalance capacity and staffing using queue-to-resource evidence (micro-scheduling)
**Targets**
- Queue segments where waits are strongly tied to specific resources (e.g., `RegistrationNurse Assessment` waiting for Nurse pool, or `Doctor ConsultationDiagnostics` waiting for a particular room/tech).

**Root cause addressed**
- Resource bottlenecks (insufficient staffing/room/equipment capacity during demand peaks) and mismatched schedules.

**How data supports it**
- From queue mining:
  - show which resource R causes the largest waits for `(AB)`.
- From utilization analysis:
  - find times when R is most saturated (high busy fraction) and coincides with high queue waits.

**Optimization actions**
- Implement **time-bucket staffing adjustments** (e.g., nurse coverage +20% between 9:30–11:00).
- Use **flex staffing**: pool “float” staff trained for both registration/Nurse triage where feasible.
- If equipment is the bottleneck: add **parallel sessions** (extra room/extra tech shift for peak hours).

**Expected impact (typical)**
- Queue mean/median waits for the targeted segment often drop first (e.g., **10–30% reduction**), with larger improvements in the **90th percentile** if saturation is the cause.

---

### Strategy B — Decouple pathway steps via parallelization or “prework” (reduce handover dead time)
**Targets**
- Queues with long gaps between clinically preparatory tasks and the next activity, such as:
  - `Doctor ConsultationDiagnostic Test` waiting because test scheduling/order processing happens after doctor finishes.
  - `Nurse AssessmentDoctor Consultation` if physician is waiting on missing order details.

**Root cause addressed**
- Handovers and process dependencies create unnecessary waiting windows; sequential design amplifies delays.

**How data supports it**
- Variant analysis shows long-wait variants share the same dependency arc.
- Queue arcs with high waits likely indicate that the “next activity” is blocked on administrative/ordering rather than physical readiness.

**Optimization actions**
- **Pre-authorize diagnostics** during nurse assessment when clinically appropriate (create orders that can be queued immediately for testing).
- **Parallelize** where safe:
  - e.g., while doctor consult begins, run basic vitals/ECG prep if rules allow.
- Introduce standardized “ready-for-test” checklists so tests can start immediately once ordered.

**Expected impact**
- Reduces “dead time” at handovers. Often yields a **meaningful reduction in overall visit duration**, not just the specific queue, because it prevents downstream cascades (commonly **5–20% improvement** in end-to-end time, depending on how much is decoupled).

---

### Strategy C — Smart scheduling / throttling based on learned demand patterns (reduce burst-induced queues)
**Targets**
- Queues strongly correlated with **arrival bursts** and time-of-day effects:
  - `RegistrationNurse Assessment`,
  - `Doctor Consultation` start queues.

**Root cause addressed**
- Scheduling policies that create arrival spikes exceeding downstream capacity, even if average daily demand is manageable.

**How data supports it**
- Time-of-day heatmaps reveal peaks where queue waits surge.
- Inter-arrival times show burstiness that matches increased queue frequency and tail waits.

**Optimization actions**
- **Reschedule to smooth demand**:
  - reduce batch scheduling; increase spread of appointment start times.
- **Dynamic throttling**:
  - limit the number of patients booked into a pathway specialty slot when diagnostic rooms/physicians saturate.
- Use urgency rules:
  - allocate urgent patients to a separate fast lane *only if* the bottleneck resource is actually available; otherwise urgency still queues (log evidence guides where fast-lane helps).

**Expected impact**
- Particularly improves **90th/95th percentile** waits and reduces dissatisfaction spikes. Mean may improve less, but tail reductions can be dramatic (e.g., **20–40% reduction** in 90th percentile for the affected arcs in many queueing systems).

---

### Strategy D (optional but often powerful) — Improve throughput of slow/variable services with targeted operational fixes (reduce service time variability)
**Targets**
- Queues downstream of high-variance service activities:
  - `Diagnostic TestSpecialist Review`,
  - `Lab TestDoctor Consultation`.

**Root cause addressed**
- Service-time variability (not just capacity), causing unpredictable completion times and resulting queues.

**How data supports it**
- Activity-level service-time distributions show high variance for particular diagnostic types or specialties.
- Long waits correlate with upstream service outliers.

**Optimization actions**
- Investigate outlier causes:
  - repeat sampling,
  - equipment downtime,
  - missing prep instructions.
- Standardize prep and reduce rework:
  - patient instructions,
  - specimen labeling checks,
  - equipment calibration schedules.
- Introduce batching only if it reduces variance without causing excessive waiting.

**Expected impact**
- More stable service reduces queue build-up after spikes; improves median and tail waits (often **10–25% reductions**).

## 4) Trade-offs and Constraints

### Common trade-offs
- **Shifting the bottleneck**: Increasing capacity at one step can move waiting to the next constrained resource. You must re-run queue mining after each change.
- **Staff workload vs. throughput**: Aggressive staffing may increase overtime or fatigue, potentially increasing service time variance or reducing quality.
- **Quality/safety constraints**: Decoupling steps (“prework” or parallelization) must preserve clinical correctness and documentation completeness.
- **Cost vs. benefit**: Some strategies reduce waits without major cost (process changes, sequencing). Others (extra staffing, rooms, equipment) may require budget or require reallocations.

### Balancing objectives
Use a structured optimization approach:
- Define constraints:
  - maximum overtime,
  - minimum service quality thresholds,
  - maximum concurrency rules for clinical safety.
- Evaluate strategies with queue mining metrics + operational metrics:
  - throughput (cases/day),
  - total clinician hours,
  - rework rate / cancellations (if logged),
  - patient satisfaction proxy (e.g., end-to-end waiting/tail delays).
- Consider staged rollouts:
  - pilot on one specialty/day-part,
  - compare pre/post with the same KPIs.

## 5) Measuring Success (KPIs + Continuous Monitoring)

### 5.1 KPIs to measure effectiveness after deployment
Use KPIs that directly reflect patient experience and operational performance:

**Queue/Waiting KPIs**
- **Avg and median waiting time** for each targeted queue segment `(AB)`
- **90th percentile waiting time** (critical for satisfaction)
- **% of cases exceeding thresholds**:
  - e.g., proportion of patients waiting >15/30 minutes between key arcs
- **Queue frequency** (number of occurrences) to ensure reduced “incidents,” not just average improvement.

**End-to-end patient flow KPIs**
- **Overall visit duration** (from first START to final COMPLETE or Check-out COMPLETE)
- **Time in system by patient type/urgency**
- **Bottleneck propagation**: measure how changes impact downstream waits.

**Operational KPIs**
- **Throughput**: visits completed per day (or per clinic session)
- **Resource utilization**: ensure capacity is used effectively without extreme saturation
- **Rework / deviations** (if logs allow): repeats, missing prerequisites, cancellations.

### 5.2 Ongoing process monitoring (sustained improvement)
Implement **continuous queue mining dashboards**:
- Run the same waiting-time computation on new logs weekly/daily.
- Monitor KPIs by:
  - time-of-day,
  - specialty,
  - resource,
  - patient urgency/type.
- Use alerting:
  - if 90th percentile wait for a critical arc exceeds a control limit (e.g., +2 SD or clinically defined SLA breaches), trigger investigation.
- Maintain “model drift” checks:
  - if appointment mix changes or new workflows are introduced, the expected queue patterns will shift—recompute queue priorities.

**Practical deployment approach**
1. Establish baseline queue metrics and Top-K arcs.
2. Deploy one change at a time (or one coordinated bundle).
3. Recompute KPIs and verify whether:
   - targeted queues improved,
   - bottlenecks did not simply move (or moved acceptably),
   - end-to-end patient duration improved.

### Summary of the data-driven approach
- Compute **waiting times** as `next_start - prev_complete` per case and characterize queue arcs with **tail and frequency metrics**.
- Identify critical queues via **tail risk + prevalence + patient segmentation impact**.
- Perform RCA using **resource utilization, service time variability, variant clustering, dependency/handovers, arrival patterns**.
- Implement targeted strategies such as **capacity rebalancing**, **decoupling/parallelization of handovers**, and **demand smoothing/smart scheduling** (plus optional service stabilization).
- Measure success using **queue KPIs + end-to-end duration + operational constraints**, and maintain continuous monitoring.
