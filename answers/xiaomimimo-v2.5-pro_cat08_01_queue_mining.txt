#### **1. Queue Identification and Characterization**

**Calculating Waiting Times:**
Using the event log, "waiting time" (or queue time) for a specific patient between two consecutive activities is calculated as the difference between the *start timestamp* of the subsequent activity and the *complete timestamp* of the previous activity.
*   **Formula:** `Waiting Time (Activity A  Activity B) = Start_Time(B) - Complete_Time(A)`
*   **Example from snippet:** For Case V1001, waiting time from Registration (complete at 09:08:45) to Nurse Assessment (start at 09:15:20) is **6 minutes and 35 seconds**.
*   **Scope:** Waiting time is measured for every sequential pair of activities in each patient's trace (visit). This includes the initial wait upon arrival (e.g., before Registration starts) if an initial "Arrival" event is logged.

**Key Characterization Metrics:**
For each identified queue (e.g., "Registration  Nurse Assessment"), I would calculate:
*   **Central Tendency:** Average and Median waiting time. Median is more robust to outliers (e.g., a few extremely long waits).
*   **Dispersion:** Standard deviation, Maximum waiting time, 90th/95th percentile waiting time. The high percentiles are critical for understanding the "tail risk" and worst-case patient experience.
*   **Volume & Frequency:** Total number of cases experiencing this queue, and the frequency of the queue occurring within the overall process flow.
*   **Impact Metrics:** Total patient-hours lost in waiting for that queue. Average wait time segmented by patient attributes (e.g., New vs. Follow-up, Urgent vs. Normal).

**Identifying Critical Queues for Immediate Attention:**
I would prioritize queues using a multi-criteria scoring system:
1.  **Severity (Avg/90th Pctl Wait):** Queues with the highest absolute waiting times.
2.  **Prevalence (Frequency):** Queues experienced by a large percentage of patients.
3.  **Impact on High-Value Cases:** Queues that disproportionately affect "Urgent" or "New" patients (who may require more complex care).
4.  **Downstream Impact:** Queues that, if reduced, would unlock the biggest reduction in *overall visit duration* (e.g., a bottleneck queue that everything else backs up behind).

#### **2. Root Cause Analysis**

Beyond identifying *where* waits occur, process mining helps uncover *why*.

*   **Resource Bottlenecks & Utilization:** By linking activity timestamps to the `Resource` field, I can calculate:

    *   **Resource-Specific Queues:** Analyzing wait times filtered by specific staff can reveal uneven workloads or skills. For instance, "Nurse Assessment" wait times may be long only before "Nurse 1," not "Nurse 2."
*   **Activity Duration Variability:** By calculating `Service Time = Complete_Time(A) - Start_Time(A)`, I can assess the consistency of activity execution. *   **Handover & Coordination Issues:** Long waits between activities performed by different resources (e.g., Nurse  Doctor) may indicate poor communication or lack of real-time notification that a patient is ready.
*   **Variant Analysis:** Process mining can discover the most common patient paths (variants). A variant common for "Follow-up" patients that bypasses certain tests might have much shorter overall waits, highlighting inefficiencies in the standard "New Patient" pathway.

**Example Root Cause Hypothesis:** A long, variable wait before "Doctor Consultation" could be caused by: (1) Doctors running over schedule due to variable consultation times (high service time variability), (2) Inefficient handoff from nurses (the doctor isn't promptly notified), or (3) Poor scheduling that doesn't account for consultation length variability or buffer times.
