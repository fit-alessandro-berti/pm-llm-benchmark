## 2. Root causes and delay patterns

### Case 102: escalation plus long waiting after investigation

| Transition                        | Waiting Time |
| --------------------------------- | -----------: |
| Receive → Triage                  |          25m |
| Triage → Assign Level-1           |          30m |
| Assign Level-1 → Escalate Level-2 |   **2h 30m** |
| Escalate Level-2 → Investigate    |   **2h 30m** |
| Investigate → Resolve             |      **19h** |
| Resolve → Close                   |          15m |

Case 102 has two clear bottlenecks:

First, the ticket spent **2.5 hours with Level-1 before escalation**. That suggests Level-1 may have taken too long to recognize that the issue needed specialist support.

Second, after escalation, there was another **2.5-hour wait before investigation**, followed by a very large **19-hour gap between investigation and resolution**. This likely indicates queueing, lack of Level-2 availability, overnight delay, or unresolved dependency after investigation.

## 3. Main performance bottlenecks

### A. Escalations are associated with longer cycle times

The two escalated cases are:

| Case ID | Cycle Time |
| ------: | ---------: |
|     102 |    25h 10m |
|     105 |    49h 05m |

Escalation appears to increase cycle time because it introduces handoff delays. In Case 102, the Level-2 handoff added at least **2h 30m** before investigation. In Case 105, it added **28 hours** before the next investigation step.

This suggests that escalated tickets may be entering a separate queue without sufficient urgency or capacity.

---

### B. Long waits between investigation and resolution

Cases 102, 104, and 105 all have a **19-hour delay** between **Investigate Issue** and **Resolve Ticket**.

That repeated pattern is important. It suggests a systemic issue, not a one-off anomaly.

Possible explanations include:

* Investigations are started late in the day and resolution is deferred until the next day.
* Agents lack authority or information to resolve immediately.
* Tickets require external input after investigation.
* There is no SLA controlling the time from investigation to resolution.
* Complex tickets are investigated but then left idle in a pending queue.

This is one of the strongest root-cause signals in the log.

---

### C. Delays before investigation begins

Case 104 waited **3h 30m** from assignment to investigation. Case 102 waited **2h 30m** before escalation, then another **2h 30m** before investigation. These delays happen before meaningful problem-solving begins.

That means some cycle time is being consumed by queueing and handoff delays rather than actual work.

---

### D. Closure is not the main bottleneck

The time from **Resolve Ticket** to **Close Ticket** is short in all cases:

| Case ID | Resolve → Close |
| ------: | --------------: |
|     101 |             15m |
|     102 |             15m |
|     103 |             15m |
|     104 |             30m |
|     105 |             30m |

Closure delays are minor compared with the multi-hour and overnight waits earlier in the process. The main issue is not administrative closure; it is investigation, escalation, and resolution flow.

---

## Recommendations

The biggest improvement opportunities are:

**1. Improve escalation triage.**
Cases that need Level-2 support should be identified earlier during triage or early Level-1 investigation. This would reduce time lost before escalation.

**2. Set an SLA for Level-2 pickup time.**
Case 105 waited **28 hours** after escalation before Level-2 investigation. A target such as “Level-2 must start investigation within 1–2 hours for escalated tickets” would directly address this bottleneck.

**3. Monitor the investigation-to-resolution gap.**
The repeated **19-hour delay** in cases 102, 104, and 105 suggests a systemic bottleneck. This transition should be tracked as a key performance metric.

**4. Review Level-2 capacity and queue management.**
The escalated tickets are much slower, especially Case 105. This may indicate insufficient specialist staffing, unclear ownership after escalation, or poor prioritization of escalated work.

**5. Introduce aging alerts.**
Tickets that sit too long after assignment, escalation, or investigation should trigger alerts before they become overnight delays.

**6. Separate simple and complex ticket flows.**
Cases 101 and 103 follow a fast path and close within about 1–2 hours. Complex or escalated cases should have a dedicated path with stricter handoff controls, so they do not sit idle between teams.

---

## Overall conclusion

The primary delayed cases are **102, 104, and 105**, with **105** being the most severe outlier.

The strongest delay patterns are:

1. **Escalation-related waiting**, especially the **28-hour Level-2 pickup delay** in Case 105.
2. **Long investigation-to-resolution delays**, consistently **19 hours** in cases 102, 104, and 105.
3. **Queueing before investigation**, especially in Case 104 and Case 102.

The process appears efficient for straightforward tickets, but complex tickets suffer from handoff delays, Level-2 queue delays, and unresolved overnight waiting after investigation.
