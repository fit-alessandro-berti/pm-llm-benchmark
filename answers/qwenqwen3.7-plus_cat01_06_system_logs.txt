### Explanation of Transformation Logic

**1. Case Identification (Artifact-Centric Grouping)**
Following the guidance that a case should represent a "logical unit of user work," I grouped events by the primary **Artifact** (document, email thread, or spreadsheet) being manipulated. This results in 5 distinct cases:
*   `CASE-QR`: Work on the Quarterly Report.
*   `CASE-DOC1`: Drafting and finalizing a new document.
*   `CASE-EMAIL`: Handling a specific email thread (Annual Meeting).
*   `CASE-PDF`: Reviewing the draft report.
*   `CASE-XLS`: Updating the 2024 budget.

This approach naturally captures the reality of knowledge work, where a primary task (like drafting `Document1`) is temporarily interrupted by supporting tasks (checking email, reviewing a PDF, updating a budget), before the user returns to finalize the primary task. 

**2. Activity Naming (Standardization)**
Raw system actions (e.g., `TYPING`, `SCROLL`, `CLICK`, `SWITCH`) are too granular and noisy for high-level process discovery. I abstracted them into standardized, semantically meaningful activity names:
*   `FOCUS` / `SWITCH` (to a new window)  **Open Artifact** or **Resume Artifact** (distinguishing between a fresh start and returning to an interrupted task).
*   `TYPING`  **Edit Content** or **Update Data** (depending on whether it's a document or a structured spreadsheet).
*   `SCROLL` / `CLICK` (to open)  **Review Content**.
*   `HIGHLIGHT`  **Analyze Content**.
*   `CLICK` (Reply/Send)  **Initiate Reply** / **Send Message**.
*   `SAVE` / `CLOSE`  **Save Artifact** / **Close Artifact**.

**3. Coherent Narrative**
The resulting event log tells a clear story of a morning work session: The user briefly opens the Quarterly Report, shifts to draft a new document, gets interrupted by an urgent email, gathers necessary data by reviewing a PDF and updating a budget spreadsheet, returns to the new document to insert the budget reference and finalize it, and finally returns to the Quarterly Report to draft the executive summary and close out their work. 

This structure is immediately ready to be imported into tools like Celonis, ProM, or Disco to analyze metrics like average handling time per artifact, frequency of task-switching, and common activity sequences (e.g., `Open`  `Edit`  `Save`  `Close`).
