## **1. Identifying Instance-Spanning Constraints and Their Impact**

### **1.1. Methodology for Identifying Constraints Using Process Mining**
To formally identify and quantify the impact of instance-spanning constraints, we will leverage **process mining techniques**—specifically **conformance checking**, **performance analysis**, and **resource-activity dependency mining**—on the event log. The goal is to isolate delays caused by *between-instance dependencies* (e.g., resource contention, batching, priority interrupts) from *within-instance delays* (e.g., long activity durations, staff fatigue).

#### **A. Shared Cold-Packing Stations**
- **Identification:**
  - Filter the event log for orders where `Requires Cold Packing = TRUE`.
  - Use **resource-activity dependency mining** to identify bottlenecks at cold-packing stations (e.g., `Station C1` to `Station C5`).
  - Apply **queue mining** to detect orders waiting for cold-packing resources after `Item Picking` completes.
- **Quantification:**
  - **Waiting Time for Cold-Packing (`WT_CP`):**
    \[
    WT_{CP} = \text{Timestamp(Packing START)} - \text{Timestamp(Item Picking COMPLETE)}
    \]
    for orders requiring cold-packing.
  - **Resource Utilization (`U_CP`):**
    \[
    U_{CP} = \frac{\text{Total time cold stations are busy}}{\text{Total available time} \times \text{Number of stations}}
    \]
  - **Impact Metric:** *Average waiting time per cold-packing order* and *percentage of orders delayed >30 minutes due to cold-packing contention*.
  - **Differentiation from Within-Instance Delays:**
    - Compare `WT_CP` against the **median duration of the Packing activity** for cold-packing orders.
    - If `WT_CP` >> median Packing duration, the delay is likely due to resource contention (between-instance).
    - Use **process discovery** (e.g., Disco, ProM) to visualize queues at cold-packing stations.

#### **B. Shipping Batches**
- **Identification:**
  - Filter for orders with the same `Destination Region` and `Shipping Label Generation` activity.
  - Use **batch mining** to detect:
    - Time gaps between the first order completing Packing and the batch forming.
    - Orders waiting in a "batch queue" (e.g., orders stuck at Packing until batch size  threshold).
- **Quantification:**
  - **Batching Delay (`BD`):**
    \[
    BD = \text{Timestamp(Shipping Label Gen. START)} - \text{Timestamp(Packing COMPLETE)}
    \]
    for orders in the same batch.
  - **Batch Formation Time (`BFT`):**
    \[
    BFT = \text{Timestamp(last order in batch completes Packing)} - \text{Timestamp(first order in batch completes Packing)}
    \]
  - **Impact Metric:** *Average batching delay* and *percentage of orders waiting >20 minutes for batch formation*.
  - **Differentiation:**
    - Compare `BD` against the **median time to generate a shipping label** (within-instance).
    - If `BD` is high while label generation is fast, the delay is due to batching (between-instance).

#### **C. Priority Handling for Express Orders**
- **Identification:**
  - Filter for `Order Type = Express`.
  - Use **interrupt mining** to detect:
    - Instances where an express order preempts a standard order at a shared resource (e.g., Packing, Quality Check).
    - Delays imposed on standard orders due to express order priority.
- **Quantification:**
  - **Express Order Interrupt Delay (`EID`):**
    \[
    EID = \text{Time standard order is paused} = \text{Timestamp(express order starts)} - \text{Timestamp(standard order was paused)}
    \]
  - **Standard Order Delay Due to Express (`SDE`):**
    \[
    SDE = \text{Additional time standard order takes} - \text{Baseline processing time}
    \]
  - **Impact Metric:** *Average delay imposed on standard orders per express order* and *percentage of standard orders delayed by express orders*.
  - **Differentiation:**
    - Compare `SDE` against the **median processing time for the interrupted activity**.
    - Use **sequence mining** to identify patterns like "Express order interrupts standard order at Packing  standard order resumes after express completes."

#### **D. Hazardous Material Regulatory Limits**
- **Identification:**
  - Filter for `Hazardous Material = TRUE`.
  - Use **concurrent activity mining** to track the number of hazardous orders in `Packing` or `Quality Check` at any timestamp.
  - Apply **sliding window analysis** (e.g., 1-hour windows) to detect violations of the "10 orders" limit.
- **Quantification:**
  - **Concurrent Hazardous Orders (`CHO`):**
    \[
    CHO(t) = \text{Number of hazardous orders in Packing or QC at time } t
    \]
  - **Violation Frequency (`VF`):**
    \[
    VF = \frac{\text{Number of time windows where } CHO(t) > 10}{\text{Total time windows}}
    \]
  - **Impact Metric:** *Frequency of regulatory violations* and *average delay caused by halting hazardous orders when limit is reached*.
  - **Differentiation:**
    - Compare `CHO(t)` against the **median number of hazardous orders processed per hour** (within-instance capacity).
    - If `CHO(t)` frequently exceeds 10, the constraint is binding (between-instance).

### **1.2. Differentiating Between-Instance vs. Within-Instance Waiting Time**
To isolate the root cause of delays, we use the following **diagnostic framework**:

| **Delay Type**               | **Data Source**                          | **Analysis Technique**                     | **Example Metric**                     |
|------------------------------|------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Within-Instance**          | Median activity durations in the log     | Compare actual vs. expected duration       | `Packing duration > median Packing time` |
| **Resource Contention**      | Resource availability and queue lengths  | Resource utilization + queue mining        | `WT_CP >> median Packing time`          |
| **Batching Delays**          | Batch formation timestamps               | Time gaps between batch members            | `BD > 20 minutes`                      |
| **Priority Interrupts**      | Express order preemption events          | Sequence mining for interrupts             | `SDE > 15 minutes`                     |
| **Regulatory Limits**        | Concurrent hazardous order counts        | Sliding window analysis                    | `CHO(t) > 10`                          |

**Practical Example:**
- Order ORD-5002 (Express, Cold-Packing) arrives at Packing at 10:22:50.
- The next available cold station (C2) is busy until 10:30:00 (due to ORD-5001 waiting).
- `WT_CP = 7 minutes 10 seconds` (10:22:50 to 10:30:00).
- Median Packing time for cold orders = 5 minutes.
- **Conclusion:** The 2+ minute excess is due to resource contention (between-instance).

## **2. Analyzing Constraint Interactions**

The four constraints do not operate in isolation; their interactions can **amplify delays** during peak periods. Below are critical interdependencies and their implications:

### **2.1. Cold-Packing + Express Priority**
- **Interaction:**
  Express orders requiring cold-packing (e.g., perishable goods) may **preempt standard orders** at cold stations, but if all cold stations are occupied by standard orders, the express order must wait.
  - *Cascade Effect:* Standard orders with cold-packing requirements are delayed further, increasing their batching time for shipping.
- **Example:**
  - 5 cold stations are busy with standard orders.
  - An express order arrives needing cold-packing  must wait until a station frees up.
  - While waiting, the express order’s downstream activities (Quality Check, Shipping) are delayed, risking missed delivery deadlines.

### **2.2. Batching + Hazardous Material Limits**
- **Interaction:**
  If multiple hazardous orders are destined for the same region, they may be **batched together** for shipping efficiency.
  - *Regulatory Risk:* If the batch size is large (e.g., 15 orders), the hazardous material limit (10 in Packing/QC) may be violated during packing.
  - *Delay Mechanism:* Batching logic may force hazardous orders to wait until the batch is full, increasing their time in Packing/QC and risking violations.
- **Example:**
  - 8 hazardous orders for the "South" region arrive within 30 minutes.
  - Batching logic waits for 2 more orders to form a batch of 10.
  - During this wait, 3 hazardous orders enter Packing  `CHO(t) = 11` (violation).

### **2.3. Priority Handling + Regulatory Limits**
- **Interaction:**
  Express orders (even non-hazardous) may **disrupt hazardous order processing** if they share resources (e.g., Quality Check staff).
  - *Safety Risk:* If express orders frequently interrupt hazardous orders, the facility may struggle to stay under the 10-order limit.
  - *Delay Mechanism:* Hazardous orders take longer to process, increasing their exposure time and risk of violations.
- **Example:**
  - QC Staff A is processing a hazardous order when an express order preempts them.
  - The hazardous order resumes after 10 minutes  now 11 hazardous orders are in QC (violation).

### **2.4. Cold-Packing + Batching**
- **Interaction:**
  Orders requiring cold-packing may be **geographically dispersed**, making batching less efficient.
  - *Batching Delay:* Cold-packing orders may wait longer for batch formation if their destinations are spread across regions.
  - *Resource Waste:* Cold stations sit idle while orders wait for batches, reducing throughput.
- **Example:**
  - 3 cold-packing orders arrive for "North," "West," and "South" regions.
  - Batching logic waits for more orders to the same region  cold stations are underutilized.

### **2.5. Why Interaction Analysis Matters**
Ignoring these interactions can lead to **suboptimal fixes**:
- Increasing cold-packing stations may reduce express order delays but worsen hazardous material violations if more hazardous orders use cold stations.
- Relaxing batching thresholds may speed up shipping but increase regulatory risks.
- **Holistic optimization** requires modeling these dependencies, as optimizing one constraint may inadvertently worsen another.

### **Strategy 1: Dynamic Capacity Allocation with Predictive Resource Scheduling**
**Primary Constraints Addressed:**
- Shared Cold-Packing stations
- Priority Handling for Express Orders
- Hazardous Material Limits (indirectly)

**Proposed Changes:**
1. **Predictive Resource Allocation:**
   - Use **machine learning (ML)** to forecast demand for cold-packing and hazardous material processing.
     - Features: Historical order volumes, seasonality, order attributes (`Requires Cold Packing`, `Hazardous Material`), and regional demand.
     - Model: Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) or Random Forest to predict:
       - `Cold-Packing Demand(t)` = Number of orders requiring cold-packing in the next 2 hours.
       - `Hazardous Order Load(t)` = Number of hazardous orders in Packing/QC at time `t`.
   - **Dynamic Station Allocation:**
     - Reserve **2 cold-packing stations exclusively for express orders** during peak hours (e.g., 10 AM–4 PM).
     - Allocate the remaining 3 stations for standard orders, with **priority given to hazardous orders** when the limit is near (8 hazardous orders in progress).
     - If `Hazardous Order Load(t) > 8`, **pause standard order processing** at cold stations until the count drops below 10.

2. **Priority Interrupt Rules:**
   - Express orders **preempt standard orders** at cold stations **only if**:
     - A cold station is free **OR** a standard order has been processing for <5 minutes (to avoid excessive delays).
   - Standard orders **resume** after the express order completes or after a **maximum preemption time** (e.g., 10 minutes).

**Leveraging Data/Analysis:**
- **Historical Demand Patterns:** Identify peak hours for cold-packing and hazardous orders (e.g., 11 AM–2 PM for express perishables).
- **Resource Utilization Heatmaps:** Use process mining to identify underutilized stations during off-peak times.
- **Simulation of Preemption Rules:** Test different preemption thresholds (e.g., 5 vs. 10 minutes) to balance express order speed and standard order fairness.

**Expected Outcomes:**

|-------------------------------------|--------------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Avg. wait time for cold-packing (express orders) | 12 min       | 3 min                    | **75% reduction** |
| Avg. wait time for cold-packing (standard orders) | 8 min        | 5 min                    | **37.5% reduction** |
| Regulatory violations (hazardous)   | 15% of days  | 2% of days               | **87% reduction** |
| Throughput (orders/hour)            | 120          | 145                      | **+21%**        |

**Why It Works:**
- Reduces express order delays by dedicating capacity.
- Prevents hazardous material violations by dynamically adjusting standard order processing.
- Minimizes preemption disruption for standard orders with short time limits.

### **Strategy 2: Adaptive Batching with Real-Time Regional Clustering**
**Primary Constraints Addressed:**
- Shipping Batches
- Hazardous Material Limits
- Cold-Packing + Batching Interaction

**Proposed Changes:**
1. **Dynamic Batch Formation:**
   - Replace **fixed batch thresholds** (e.g., "wait for 10 orders") with **real-time regional clustering**:
     - **Trigger Condition:** Form a batch when:
       - The **regional demand** reaches a **predicted threshold** (e.g., 80% of the median batch size for that region).
       - **OR** the **oldest order in the queue** has waited >15 minutes (to prevent excessive delays).
     - **Batch Size Limits:**
       - **Non-hazardous regions:** Max batch size = 12 orders.
       - **Hazardous regions:** Max batch size = 8 orders (to stay under the 10-order hazardous limit during packing/QC).

2. **Cold-Packing Order Prioritization in Batching:**
   - Orders requiring cold-packing are **clustered first** within their region to minimize cold station idle time.
   - If a cold-packing order is in the queue, the batch forms **as soon as 60% of the regional demand is met** (to avoid cold station underutilization).

3. **Batching Dashboard for Staff:**
   - A real-time dashboard shows:
     - Current batch formation status per region.
     - Estimated wait time for orders in the queue.
     - Hazardous material count in Packing/QC.

**Leveraging Data/Analysis:**
- **Regional Demand Forecasting:** Use time-series models (e.g., ARIMA) to predict order volumes per region.
- **Batching Delay Analysis:** Identify regions with historically high batching delays (e.g., "South" region waits 25 minutes on average).
- **Cold-Packing Order Distribution:** Map cold-packing orders to regions to optimize clustering.

**Expected Outcomes:**
| **Metric**                          | **Baseline** | **After Implementation** | **Improvement** |
|-------------------------------------|--------------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Avg. batching delay                 | 18 min       | 8 min                    | **56% reduction** |
| Max batching delay                  | 45 min       | 20 min                   | **56% reduction** |
| Hazardous material violations       | 15% of days  | 3% of days               | **80% reduction** |
| Cold station utilization            | 65%          | 85%                      | **+20 percentage points** |

**Why It Works:**
- Reduces batching delays by forming batches based on real-time demand, not fixed thresholds.
- Prevents hazardous material violations by capping batch sizes for hazardous regions.
- Improves cold station efficiency by clustering cold-packing orders.
