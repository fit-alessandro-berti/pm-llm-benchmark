## 1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics

### Reconstruction of Actual Job Flows and Task Sequences

Using the MES event log, I would first aggregate all events per **Case ID (Job ID)** to reconstruct each job’s end-to-end lifecycle. The log provides explicit event timestamps (release, queue entry, setup start/end, task start/end) and resource assignments, enabling a precise reconstruction of:

- **Job routings** – the sequence of activities and machines visited, including any rework loops or alternative resources.
- **Actual execution sequences on every machine** – by sorting task start/end events per resource, we obtain the exact processing order, including setup activities.
- **Waiting times** – gaps between “Queue Entry” and “Setup Start” (or between “Task End” at upstream and “Queue Entry” at downstream) quantify exactly how long a job waited for a machine.
- **Setup occurrences** – setup start and end events directly capture its duration and the context (previous job ID) needed to model sequence dependencies.
- **Disruptions** – breakdown start/end events and priority change events are stamped with resource or job, so they can be overlaid precisely on the timeline.

### Specific Process Mining Techniques and Metrics

**A. Lead Time, Flow Time, and Makespan Distributions**

- **Process maps** (Directly-Follows Graphs or Petri nets) are mined for all jobs, and enriched with performance metrics: each arc/node shows mean/median and distribution of the sojourn time (time between two consecutive activities) and the activity duration itself.
- **Dotted charts** plot every event for each case over time, revealing batching patterns, shift effects, and job staggering.
- **Overall metrics**:
  - *Job flow time* = `max(Task End) – Job Released`
  - *Lead time* compared to order entry date (if available) or release date to completion.
  - *Makespan* = last completion time of a set of jobs released in a period.
- I would use **histograms and box plots** per job type or overall to characterize the dispersion and tail behavior, directly highlighting the unpredictability the company faces.

**B. Task Waiting Times (Queue Times) at Each Work Center/Machine**

- Compute waiting time per task = `Setup Start – Queue Entry` (or if no queue entry event, the time elapsed between becoming available and setup start). This is aggregated by resource.
- **Performance spectrum** views align all cases by time, coloring queue segments; long waiting bands immediately spotlight overloaded resources.
- Metrics: mean, median, 90th percentile waiting time per machine, and the distribution of queue lengths (derived from counting jobs in the queue at any time using event intervals).

**C. Resource Utilization (Productive, Idle, Setup)**

- For each machine, I define a timeline partitioned into states:
  - **Productive time** = sum of (Task End – Task Start) for all jobs.
  - **Setup time** = sum of (Setup End – Setup Start). This can be further decomposed into sequence-dependent vs. family setups.
  - **Idle time** = periods when the machine was available but not processing or in setup. This is computed by subtracting productive, setup, and breakdown intervals from total available time.
  - **Breakdown time** = sum of (Breakdown End – Breakdown Start).
- **Machine utilization (productive)** = Productive / (Available – Breakdown). Overall OEE components can be derived.

**D. Quantifying Sequence-Dependent Setup Times**

- For each machine, I extract all pairs of consecutive jobs (`Previous Job ID  Next Job ID`). For each pair, the setup duration is the value from the log. I can then group these pairs by attributes of the jobs (e.g., material type, geometry family, tooling requirements) to build a **setup matrix**.
- Process mining’s **transition statistics** (frequency and mean duration between any two activities when performed on the same resource) directly quantify average sequence-dependent setup costs.
- I would also perform **regression analysis** with job attribute vectors to predict setup time, giving a continuous model that handles novel combinations.

**E. Schedule Adherence and Tardiness**

- For every job, **Tardiness** = max(0, `Actual Completion Time` – `Order Due Date`). **Earliness** = max(0, `Due Date – Completion`).
- Metrics: percentage of jobs on time, average tardiness, mean absolute lateness, and conditional tardiness (for late jobs only). Distribution plots of lateness highlight chronic vs. sporadic delays.
- **Conformance checking** can compare the actual completion date against the promised date (or the originally planned schedule if a plan was logged), measuring deviations and the propagation of delays across routing steps.

**F. Impact of Disruptions on KPIs**

- **Resource breakdowns**: By analyzing the timeline before and after a breakdown event, I can compute how much queue built up and the additional waiting time introduced. For instance, I identify all jobs queued at the broken machine and measure the delay compared to their expected start time under normal conditions.
- **Priority changes**: For jobs flagged as “Urgent,” I compare their actual waiting times and tardiness versus similar routine jobs. I also analyze whether the escalation “pushed out” other jobs, worsening overall tardiness. I can isolate the **cascading effect** by tracing how the insertion of a hot job shifted the queue at the bottleneck and then propagated to downstream work centers.
- **Statistical tests** (e.g., t-tests or Mann-Whitney) can determine whether disruption periods show a statistically significant degradation in flow time, on-time delivery, or WIP.

## 3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness

### Underlying Root Causes

1. **Limitations of Static Dispatching Rules in a Dynamic Environment**
   - FCFS ignores job urgency and setup synergies; EDD ignores shop congestion and setup. Neither adapts to real-time events like a breakdown that suddenly starves a downstream machine.
   - Process mining shows that the correlation between priority/due-date and actual start time is weak; a decision-tree model built on queue attributes could not explain why some jobs were expedited, proving the rule is ineffective.

2. **Lack of Real-Time Shop Floor Visibility**
   - The current MES logs events, but the dispatching decisions are made locally without a global view of queue lengths, downstream machine availability, or remaining work.
   - Process mining exposes that when a machine becomes free, the next job is chosen from its local queue without considering that a job may be needed urgently downstream to prevent starvation – a direct cause of the starvation pathology identified.

3. **Inaccurate Duration and Setup Estimations**
   - The log records both planned and actual durations. Process mining can statistically compare them: systematic underestimation of task times means that even a perfect schedule would be over-utilized. If setup durations vary widely by job combination but planners used a fixed allowance, then suboptimal sequencing has an outsized impact.
   - Root cause: the scheduling logic doesn’t use data-driven estimates; it may rely on nominal processing times, ignoring historical variability.

4. **Ineffective Handling of Sequence-Dependent Setups**
   - No mechanism groups similar jobs or sequences them to minimize setups. The process mining setup matrix shows high variance and significant cost, but no dispatching rule leverages this information.

5. **Poor Inter-Work Center Coordination**
   - Decisions at one machine are disconnected from those at other machines, causing a “push” behavior that inflates WIP. Process mining shows that WIP accumulates predominantly before the bottleneck, while downstream resources sit idle.

6. **Inadequate Disruption Response**
   - When breakdowns occur, overtaken jobs simply wait; there is no proactive rescheduling or reprioritization to mitigate the damage. Process mining reveals that a breakdown of even 30 minutes on a bottleneck can cause cumulative delays of hours downstream, because no alternative routing or expediting is triggered.

### Differentiating Capacity vs. Scheduling Logic vs. Variability Issues

Process mining helps isolate root causes by:
- **Capacity limitation**: If a bottleneck resource has utilization > 90% and still cannot meet demand, while all other machines have low utilization, then increasing capacity is needed. Process mining quantifies what fraction of delay is due to sheer overload (demand exceeding effective capacity) versus scheduling inefficiency.
- **Scheduling logic**: If, during periods of identical demand, certain jobs are delayed because they followed a poor sequence (high setup burden), while others with the same resource occupancy were on time, the scheduling logic is the culprit. We can compare actual performance with a simulation where we apply an optimal sequence (keeping capacity and variability constant).
- **Inherent process variability**: By analyzing distributions of task durations (actual), process mining can show that a large share of tardiness comes from unplanned variability (e.g., long tail of task times) rather than mean estimates. This indicates whether robust scheduling must incorporate buffer times or if real-time adjustment (dynamic dispatching) can cope.

Thus, process mining enables a nuanced decomposition: “How much of the tardiness is avoidable by better scheduling, and how much requires additional resources or improved prediction?”

## 4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies

### Strategy 1: Intelligent Composite Dispatching Rule (ICDR)

**Core Logic**: Each time a machine becomes free, all waiting jobs are scored based on a weighted sum of factors, and the job with the highest score is selected next.

The score factors are dynamically computed using process mining data:

- **Urgency factor**: `(Due Date – now – expected remaining processing time) / (critical ratio)` or slack per remaining operation. Expected remaining processing time is derived from historical flow time distributions (mined) for the job’s remaining route, conditional on current shop congestion.
- **Priority multiplier**: A hard weight for order priority (e.g., High=2, Medium=1), to ensure strategic customers are served first when urgency is equal.
- **Setup economy factor**: If the machine’s current setup (from previous job) is compatible with the candidate job (i.e., zero or minimal changeover), add a strong bonus. The setup matrix mined from the event log quantifies the expected setup time between any two job types. The rule favors jobs that minimize setup waste, but only to the extent that it doesn’t excessively delay urgent jobs.
- **Downstream load balancing factor**: For each candidate job, compute the **expected waiting time at its next operation** based on current queue length and machine utilization at that downstream resource (real-time data). If the downstream is starved or lightly loaded, prioritize this job to keep it fed; if overloaded, perhaps delay it to avoid exploding WIP.
- **Starvation avoidance**: If the machine itself is downstream of a bottleneck, the rule can prioritize jobs that have already been long in the system to relieve upstream pressure.

**Process Mining Input**: 
- Historical waiting time distributions and flow time models for each machine (used to predict remaining time).
- Sequence-dependent setup matrix (the “Setup Economy” weights).
- Real-time queue lengths and utilization derived from current event log snapshot (requires streaming extension of MES).

**Addressing Pathologies**: Directly minimizes setups, reduces unnecessary waiting due to downstream starvation, and enforces true priority/due-date differentiation, tackling poor prioritization and suboptimal sequencing.

**Expected KPI Impact**: 
- Average tardiness  25–40%
- Total setup time on bottlenecks  15–30%
- WIP reduction due to better synchronization.

### Strategy 2: Stochastic Predictive Scheduling with Continuous Rescheduling

**Core Logic**: Instead of a simple rule, the scheduler runs a **look-ahead simulation** using a rolling horizon. Whenever a decision point is reached (machine becomes free, new job arrives, breakdown ends), the system generates a set of candidate schedules for the next 4–8 hours by combining a heuristic (e.g., genetic algorithm) with Monte Carlo sampling of task durations, setup times, and potential disruptions. Each candidate schedule is evaluated for expected total tardiness cost or a composite KPI, and the job that appears in the best-performing schedules is dispatched.

The simulation model is parameterized directly by process mining:
- Task duration distributions (not just means) for each activity, possibly segmented by operator or job complexity.
- Setup time distributions for each job-pair.
- Breakdown probability distributions (time between failures and repair time) learned from historical resource breakdown events.
- Hot-job arrival patterns (time between priority change events, learned from log).

**Process Mining Input**: Full stochastic models, so that the predictive schedule can account for variability. The system learns that certain tasks have fat tails; it then builds buffers appropriately.

**Addressing Pathologies**: This approach inherently balances due-date adherence, setup optimization, and disruption robustness because it explicitly evaluates future consequences. It overcomes the static rule limitation by looking at the global impact of a local decision.

**Expected KPI Impact**:
- On-time delivery rate improvement over ICDR, especially under high variability.
- More predictable lead times, as the stochastic model can quote realistic completion dates.
- Better absorption of disruptions, because the rescheduling is event-driven and can reroute work.

### Strategy 3: Setup-Optimized Family Batching with Protected Due-Dates

**Core Logic**: This strategy specifically targets bottleneck machines with high sequence-dependent setups. Analysis of the setup matrix (from process mining) reveals clusters of jobs with negligible internal setup – these form “families.” At the bottleneck, a **periodic optimization** is performed: every time a certain number of jobs accumulate in the queue (or every fixed time window), the scheduler solves a Traveling Salesman Problem within each family to find the minimum-setup sequence, while still respecting due dates by inserting jobs from other families when their urgency becomes critical.

More concretely, a **dynamic batching algorithm**:
1. Maintain a pool of jobs waiting for the bottleneck.
2. When the machine becomes free, check if there are at least N jobs from the same family to justify a setup reduction batch.
3. If yes, sequence them optimally; if not, fall back to a composite dispatching rule that still favors minimal setup change but protects jobs that are close to their due date.
4. As a due date approaches, a job is “promoted” and will be processed even if it breaks the family batch.

**Process Mining Input**: 
- Setup matrix to define families (clustering based on job attributes that correlate with setup time).
- Historical trade-off: Data shows how much tardiness increases when batching causes a job to wait longer; this allows setting the batching threshold N and the promotion lead time.

**Addressing Pathologies**: Dramatically reduces non-value-added setup time, increasing effective capacity at the bottleneck. The protected due-date mechanism prevents the batching from causing late deliveries for isolated jobs. It directly cures suboptimal sequencing and improves bottleneck utilization.

**Expected KPI Impact**:
- Setup time at bottleneck  30–50%
- Bottleneck throughput  10–20%
- WIP before bottleneck may initially rise slightly due to batching, but overall system WIP  due to smoother flow.

Combined, these three strategies move from purely reactive dispatching to a layered approach: ICDR for non-bottleneck coordination, predictive scheduling for global optimization, and setup-optimized batching for bottleneck focus.
