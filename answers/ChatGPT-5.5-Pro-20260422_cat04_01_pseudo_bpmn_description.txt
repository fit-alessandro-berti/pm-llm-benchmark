The pseudo-BPMN describes a **customer request handling process** that branches early based on whether the customer request is **standard** or **custom**, then later converges into a shared approval, invoicing, and confirmation flow.

At a high level, the process is:

1. Receive a customer request.
2. Decide whether it is a standard or custom request.
3. Perform the appropriate validation or feasibility work.
4. Continue only if the request can proceed.
5. Determine whether approval is required.
6. If needed, obtain approval.
7. If approved, generate the invoice and confirm the order.
8. If not approved, rework the request and loop back.
9. End after confirmation, rejection, or successful completion.

---

## 1. Start Event

The **Start Event** represents the point where the process begins.

In this case, the process starts when a customer submits a request. The request may be for a normal product or service offering, or it may involve a customization that requires additional analysis.

---

## 2. Task A: “Receive Customer Request”

This is the first operational task.

Its purpose is to capture the customer’s request and gather enough information to determine how it should be handled. This may include details such as:

* customer identity,
* requested product or service,
* quantity,
* delivery expectations,
* customization requirements,
* pricing or contractual conditions,
* urgency or priority.

After this task, the business needs to decide what type of request it is.

---

## 3. XOR Gateway: “Check Request Type”

This is an **exclusive gateway**, also called an **XOR gateway**.

An XOR gateway means that **only one outgoing path is taken** based on a condition. Here, the condition is the request type.

There are two possible paths:

* **Standard request**
* **Custom request**

The request cannot follow both paths at the same time. It must be classified as either standard or custom.

---

# Standard Request Path

## 4. Task B1: “Perform Standard Validation”

This task is used when the request type is **standard**.

A standard request usually means the customer is asking for something the company already offers without special modifications. Because the request follows a known pattern, the validation process can be more routine.

The purpose of standard validation is to check whether the request meets normal business rules, such as:

* the customer is eligible,
* the requested item exists in the catalog,
* the order quantity is valid,
* pricing rules are clear,
* required information is complete.

Once the standard request passes this validation, the process moves into parallel checks.

---

## 5. AND Gateway: “Run Parallel Checks”

This is a **parallel gateway**, also called an **AND gateway**.

An AND gateway means that **all outgoing paths are activated at the same time**. Unlike an XOR gateway, it does not choose one path. It starts multiple activities in parallel.

In this process, two checks are launched:

* Task C1: Credit Check
* Task C2: Inventory Check

These can happen independently and at the same time.

---

## 6. Task C1: “Credit Check”

The credit check determines whether the customer is financially eligible to proceed.

This may involve checking:

* outstanding debts,
* payment history,
* credit limit,
* risk status,
* account standing.

The purpose is to reduce financial risk before the company commits to fulfilling the request.

---

## 7. Task C2: “Inventory Check”

The inventory check determines whether the requested product or materials are available.

This may involve checking:

* stock levels,
* warehouse availability,
* supplier availability,
* reserved inventory,
* production capacity.

The purpose is to confirm that the business can actually fulfill the standard request.

---

## 8. AND Join: “All Parallel Checks Completed”

After the credit check and inventory check are launched in parallel, the process reaches a corresponding **parallel join**.

The join waits until **both parallel tasks are complete**.

That means the process cannot continue to Task D until:

* the credit check is finished, and
* the inventory check is finished.

This prevents the process from calculating a delivery date before knowing both the customer’s eligibility and the availability of inventory.

---

## 9. Task D: “Calculate Delivery Date”

Once the standard checks are complete, the company calculates the expected delivery date.

This task may use information from:

* inventory availability,
* shipping schedules,
* production lead times,
* customer location,
* order priority,
* internal processing time.

This task belongs to the **standard request path** and prepares the request for the later approval/invoicing stage.

---

# Custom Request Path

## 10. Task B2: “Perform Custom Feasibility Analysis”

This task is used when the request type is **custom**.

A custom request requires additional review because the customer is asking for something outside the standard offering. It may involve special design, special pricing, non-standard materials, unusual delivery requirements, or extra approval.

The purpose of this task is to determine whether the business can realistically fulfill the customization.

This may include checking:

* technical feasibility,
* production capability,
* cost impact,
* delivery impact,
* resource availability,
* supplier constraints,
* legal or compliance restrictions.

---

## 11. XOR Gateway: “Is Customization Feasible?”

This is another **exclusive gateway**.

Only one path is taken based on the feasibility result.

There are two possible outcomes:

* **Yes, customization is feasible**
* **No, customization is not feasible**

---

## 12. If Feasible: Task E1: “Prepare Custom Quotation”

If the customization is feasible, the company prepares a custom quotation.

This quotation likely includes:

* custom price,
* delivery estimate,
* scope of customization,
* special terms,
* assumptions,
* validity period,
* possible risks or exclusions.

This task is necessary because custom requests often cannot use standard pricing or standard delivery commitments.

After this task, the custom path can move forward to the shared part of the process.

---

## 13. If Not Feasible: Task E2: “Send Rejection Notice” → End Event

If the customization is not feasible, the process does not continue to approval, invoicing, or confirmation.

Instead, the company sends a rejection notice to the customer.

The rejection notice may explain that the request cannot be fulfilled due to technical, operational, cost, compliance, or availability reasons.

After this notice is sent, the process ends.

This is an important exception path: not every customer request results in an invoice or confirmation.

---

# Convergence After Standard or Custom Processing

After the standard path finishes Task D, or the feasible custom path finishes Task E1, the process converges into a shared decision point:

**“Is Approval Needed?”**

This means that both successful standard requests and feasible custom requests eventually enter the same later-stage process.

However, the custom rejection path does **not** converge here, because it ends earlier.

A BPMN diagram would usually show this convergence with an **exclusive merge gateway** or a simple merge before the approval decision. The key idea is that only one path arrives there: either the completed standard path or the completed feasible custom path.

---

## 14. XOR Gateway: “Is Approval Needed?”

This gateway decides whether the request requires managerial approval.

It is an **exclusive gateway**, so only one of the following paths is taken:

* Approval is needed.
* Approval is not needed.

Approval may be required depending on business rules such as:

* high order value,
* customer risk level,
* large discount,
* custom quotation,
* unusual delivery date,
* low margin,
* inventory shortage,
* contract exception,
* special terms,
* compliance concerns.

If approval is not needed, the process can go directly to invoice generation.

If approval is needed, the process must go through manager approval first.

---

# Approval Path

## 15. Task F: “Obtain Manager Approval”

This task is performed when the request requires approval.

A manager reviews the request, quotation, delivery date, pricing, risks, or special conditions.

The manager may consider:

* profitability,
* customer importance,
* operational feasibility,
* risk exposure,
* policy compliance,
* available capacity,
* discount level,
* contractual obligations.

After the manager reviews the request, the process moves to another decision.

---

## 16. XOR Gateway: “Is Approval Granted?”

This is an exclusive decision point with two possible outcomes:

* **Approval granted**
* **Approval not granted**

Only one path is followed.

---

## 17. If Approval Is Granted: Task G: “Generate Final Invoice”

If the manager approves the request, the process continues to final invoicing.

Task G creates the official invoice for the customer.

This invoice likely reflects:

* agreed price,
* taxes,
* payment terms,
* delivery charges,
* custom charges if applicable,
* discounts or approvals,
* final customer and order details.

Both approved requests and requests that did not require approval eventually reach this task.

---

## 18. If Approval Is Not Granted: Task H: “Re-evaluate Conditions”

If approval is denied, the process does not immediately end. Instead, it enters a rework step.

The purpose of Task H is to revise the request so it may become acceptable.

This may involve:

* changing the price,
* adjusting the delivery date,
* reducing the discount,
* modifying custom requirements,
* checking alternative inventory,
* revising the quotation,
* changing terms and conditions,
* negotiating with the customer.

After re-evaluation, the process loops back to the appropriate earlier task depending on the original request type.

---

# Loop-Back Logic

The pseudo-BPMN states:

> Loop back to Task E1 for Custom Path or Task D for Standard Path

This means the process remembers whether the request originally followed the standard or custom route.

## For a standard request

If approval is denied, the process loops back to:

**Task D: “Calculate Delivery Date”**

This suggests that the delivery date, availability assumptions, or standard order conditions may need to be recalculated.

For example, a manager may reject the request because:

* the proposed delivery date is unrealistic,
* inventory assumptions changed,
* the order creates capacity issues,
* the standard terms need adjustment.

After recalculating or revising the standard conditions, the process returns again to the approval decision.

---

## For a custom request

If approval is denied, the process loops back to:

**Task E1: “Prepare Custom Quotation”**

This suggests that the custom quotation needs revision.

For example, the manager may reject the quotation because:

* the price is too low,
* the margin is insufficient,
* the customization risk is too high,
* the delivery commitment is too aggressive,
* special terms are unacceptable.

The quotation is then revised and resubmitted into the approval flow.

---

# No-Approval Path

## 19. If No Approval Is Needed: Task G: “Generate Final Invoice”

If the request does not require approval, the process bypasses manager approval entirely.

The request goes directly to invoice generation.

This is likely used for low-risk, routine, or policy-compliant requests.

For example:

* standard order within normal value limits,
* customer has good credit,
* no special discount,
* inventory is available,
* no customization,
* no exceptional terms.

---

# Final Steps

## 20. Task I: “Send Confirmation to Customer”

After the final invoice is generated, the company sends confirmation to the customer.

The confirmation may include:

* order confirmation,
* invoice details,
* delivery date,
* payment instructions,
* quotation reference,
* terms and conditions,
* next steps.

This task occurs after Task G, regardless of whether the request went through approval or skipped approval.

---

## 21. End Event

The process ends after the confirmation is sent.

There is also an earlier end event in the custom rejection path, where the process ends after the rejection notice is sent.

So the process has at least two possible endings:

1. **Successful completion**: invoice generated and confirmation sent.
2. **Rejected custom request**: rejection notice sent and process ended.

---

# Difference Between Standard and Custom Requests

The main difference is that standard requests follow a more structured and operationally predictable path, while custom requests require feasibility and quotation work.

| Aspect                    | Standard Request                        | Custom Request                        |
| ------------------------- | --------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Initial handling          | Standard validation                     | Custom feasibility analysis           |
| Main concern              | Can the normal order be processed?      | Can the customization be delivered?   |
| Checks                    | Credit and inventory checks in parallel | Feasibility decision                  |
| Output before shared flow | Delivery date                           | Custom quotation                      |
| Possible early rejection  | Not explicitly shown                    | Yes, if customization is not feasible |
| Rework loop               | Back to delivery date calculation       | Back to custom quotation preparation  |

The standard path is focused on validating and fulfilling a normal request. The custom path is focused on determining whether the business can offer a special solution and, if so, preparing a specific quotation.

---

# Meaning of the Gateways

## XOR: Check Request Type

Chooses exactly one path:

* Standard
* Custom

## AND: Run Parallel Checks

Runs multiple tasks at the same time:

* Credit Check
* Inventory Check

Both must complete before the process continues.

## XOR: Is Customization Feasible?

Chooses whether the custom request continues or ends:

* Feasible → prepare quotation
* Not feasible → send rejection notice and end

## XOR: Is Approval Needed?

Chooses whether manager approval is required:

* Yes → obtain approval
* No → generate invoice

## XOR: Is Approval Granted?

Chooses whether the request can proceed:

* Yes → generate invoice
* No → re-evaluate and loop back

---

# When Approvals Are Triggered

Approval is triggered after the standard or feasible custom path has completed its preparatory work.

Approval may be needed when the request exceeds normal business rules or risk thresholds.

Examples include:

* large order value,
* custom work,
* special pricing,
* unusual terms,
* low margin,
* risky customer,
* tight delivery date,
* insufficient inventory,
* policy exception.

If approval is not required, the process skips directly to invoicing.

---

# When Rework Is Triggered

Rework is triggered only when:

1. approval is required, and
2. the manager does not grant approval.

The process then performs Task H, “Re-evaluate Conditions,” and returns to the appropriate earlier task.

For a standard request, rework loops back to delivery date calculation.

For a custom request, rework loops back to custom quotation preparation.

This creates an iterative cycle:

**Prepare conditions → seek approval → if rejected, revise → seek approval again**

The loop continues until the request is approved, changed so that approval is no longer needed, or potentially terminated by a business rule not shown in the pseudo-BPMN.

---

# Important Modeling Notes

The pseudo-BPMN is logically understandable, but a formal BPMN diagram would benefit from a few clarifications.

First, after the standard path and the feasible custom path, there should be a clear merge before the approval-needed decision. Since only one of those paths is active, this would usually be an **exclusive merge**, not a parallel join.

Second, the loop-back after rejected approval depends on remembering whether the request was standard or custom. In a formal BPMN model, this could be represented by separate loop-back paths or by storing the request type as process data.

Third, the custom “not feasible” path ends immediately after the rejection notice. That means it does not proceed to approval, invoicing, or confirmation.

Fourth, the standard path does not currently show a rejection condition for failed credit or inventory checks. In a real process, those checks might produce additional gateways, such as “Credit approved?” or “Inventory available?” The current pseudo-BPMN assumes those checks complete and the process continues.

Overall, the process represents a structured customer request workflow with separate handling for standard and custom requests, parallel validation for standard orders, feasibility analysis for custom orders, optional approval, and rework loops when approval is denied.
