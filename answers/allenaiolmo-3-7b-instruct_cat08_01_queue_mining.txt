## 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

### a. Calculating Waiting Times from Event Logs

To quantify waiting times using process mining, I would first **extract the event timestamps** from the event log for each patient visit. The key activities (e.g., Registration, Nurse Assessment, Doctor Consultation, etc.) are sequenced for each patient case. For each activity *A* that is immediately followed by another activity *B*, the **waiting time for the patient between A and B** is calculated as:

\[
\text{Wait}_{A \to B} = (\text{Start time of B}) - (\text{Completion time of A} + \Delta)
\]
Where \(\Delta\) is a small buffer (e.g., 0–5 seconds) to account for minor log synchronization errors.

**Example:**  
If Registration (completed at 09:08:45) is followed by Nurse Assessment (starts at 09:15:20), the patient waits for 6 minutes and 35 seconds between these two events.

For each patient, the **total waiting time** across all activity transitions can be calculated by summing the waits between consecutive activities in their visit sequence.

#### Key Waiting Time Metrics to Calculate

- **Average Waiting Time per Activity:** The mean wait experienced between transitions for all patients.
- **Median Waiting Time:** To identify if the "worst case" is an outlier.
- **Maximum and 90th Percentile Waiting Times:** To identify high-tension points in the queue.
- **Queue Frequency:** How many patients experience waiting times above a threshold (e.g., 10 minutes).
- **Patient-specific Wait Distributions:** To see if certain patient types (New vs. Follow-up, Urgent vs. Routine) face longer waits.
- **Time spent in each queue:** Identifying stages (e.g., nurse assessment) that are consistently bottlenecks.

#### Identifying the Most Critical Queues

The most critical queues are identified using a **combined scoring approach**, such as:

1. **Weighted Average Wait Time:** Higher weight for stages that contribute most to the overall visit duration (e.g., waiting after registration before seeing a nurse).
2. **Impact on Overall Visit Duration:** Queues that account for the largest share of total patient time in the clinic.
3. **Patient Satisfaction Correlation:** If historical data link longer waits to negative feedback (even if not in the event log, this can be hypothesized or tested via A/B).
4. **Frequency of Exceedance of Thresholds:** How many patients are delayed beyond a clinically acceptable limit (e.g., 15 minutes).
5. **Resource Bottleneck Indicators:** If a single nurse or room is the cause of long waits across many patients, this queue is prioritized.

For example, if Nurse Assessment consistently shows a 10-minute average wait for 40% of new patients, it would be a high-priority queue for intervention.

## 4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints

- **Resource Reallocation (Strategy 1):** May require investment in scheduling software and potentially temporary staffing. However, it can reduce overtime and improve patient throughput, balancing cost and quality.
- **Process Redesign (Strategy 2):** Requires space or physical workflow adjustments (e.g., holding areas, separate triage lines). Must ensure that redesign doesn’t create new bottlenecks elsewhere (e.g., new queue at holding area).
- **Scheduling Optimization (Strategy 3):** Needs robust data science infrastructure but can be implemented incrementally. There is a risk of over-scheduling (cost) or under-scheduling (lower utilization).
- **Quality of Care:** Any change must preserve the ability to deliver thorough care. For example, reducing the time a patient waits for a test might risk rushing the test or missing nuances. Continuous monitoring (see next section) is essential to catch adverse effects.
- **Staff Workload:** Reallocation must consider workload balance to avoid burnout or excessive task complexity for staff.

A **balanced approach** involves piloting one strategy at a time, measuring outcomes using KPIs, and iterating based on feedback and data.
