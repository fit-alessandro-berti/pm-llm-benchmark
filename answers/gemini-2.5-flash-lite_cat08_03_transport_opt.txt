## Process Mining Engagement Plan for Speedy Parcels

**Prepared for:** Speedy Parcels Management

**Executive Summary:**
Speedy Parcels faces significant challenges in delivery punctuality and operational cost efficiency, driven by suspected inefficiencies in routing, scheduling, and operational execution. This engagement proposes a comprehensive process mining approach to analyze the collected event data from GPS trackers, driver handheld scanners, dispatch systems, and maintenance logs. By applying process discovery, conformance checking, performance analysis, and root cause analysis, we will identify critical bottlenecks and deviations. Based on these data-driven insights, we will recommend concrete, actionable optimization strategies to enhance delivery performance, reduce costs, and improve overall operational sustainability.

### 1. Process Discovery and Conformance Checking

The initial phase focuses on understanding the "as-is" reality of Speedy Parcels' delivery operations and comparing it against planned procedures.

*   **Data Preprocessing and Integration:**
    The primary challenge is integrating disparate data sources into a unified, structured event log. This requires creating a single table where each row represents an event.
    *   **Key Elements for Event Log:**
        *   **Timestamp:** The primary chronological anchor for all events.
        *   **Case ID:** This is crucial for grouping related events. For Speedy Parcels, a `Vehicle-Day` (e.g., `V12-20241205`) is an appropriate case identifier, representing a vehicle's operational cycle for a given day. This allows analysis of daily route completion, delays, and overall efficiency.
        *   **Activity/Event Name:** A standardized representation of what happened (e.g., `Depart Depot`, `Arrive Customer`, `Delivery Success`, `Unscheduled Stop`, `Start Shift`, `End Shift`, `Maintenance Start`). This will be derived by combining `Event Type` and `Activity/Status` from the source data.
        *   **Resource:** The primary resources involved (e.g., `Vehicle ID`, `Driver ID`).
        *   **Package ID:** Relevant for delivery-specific events, enabling analysis of individual package journeys or failures.
        *   **Location (Lat/Lon):** Essential for geographical analysis and visualizing routes.
        *   **Other Attributes:** `Speed`, `Notes` (e.g., "Customer Not Home," "Traffic Jam," "Engine Warning Light"), `Delivery Time Window` (from dispatch data, mapped to relevant customer events).
    *   **Integration Steps:**
        1.  **Schema Mapping:** Aligning column names and data types across sources.
        2.  **Timestamp Normalization:** Ensuring all timestamps are in a consistent format and timezone.
        3.  **Event Type Standardization:** Mapping diverse `Event Type` and `Activity/Status` values to a unified set of meaningful activities (e.g., `Scanner: Arrive Customer` becomes `Customer Arrival`).
        4.  **Case ID Generation:** Creating the `Case ID` by concatenating `Vehicle ID` and the date of the `Start Shift` event for that vehicle.
        5.  **Data Cleaning:** Handling missing values (e.g., imputing location for depot events), removing duplicate entries, and addressing data inconsistencies.
        6.  **Attribute Enrichment:** Linking dispatch data (like planned routes, customer time windows) and maintenance logs to the main event stream based on `Vehicle ID`, `Driver ID`, and `Timestamp`. For maintenance, a specific `Event Type` like `Maintenance` could be created, potentially overlapping with delivery shift times.
    *   **Potential Challenges:**
        *   **Data Granularity Mismatch:** GPS pings might be every few seconds, while scanner events are discrete milestones. Synchronization needs careful handling.
        *   **Event Synchronization:** Ensuring events that logically follow each other (e.g., leaving a customer and arriving at the next) are correctly sequenced and timed, especially if there are gaps between data sources.
        *   **Defining Start/End of Case:** Accurately determining the start (`Start Shift`) and end (`End Shift`) for each `Case ID` to define the operational window for a vehicle on a given day.
        *   **Data Sparsity:** Some event types might be infrequent or poorly recorded for certain vehicles or days.
        *   **Interpreting 'Notes':** Free-text notes require natural language processing or manual review to extract structured insights.

*   **Process Discovery:**
    Using process discovery algorithms (e.g., Inductive Miner, Heuristics Miner) on the integrated event log, we will generate a visual process map. This map will represent the **actual flow** of operations, showing:
    *   The most frequent sequences of activities (e.g., `Depart Depot` -> `Travel to Customer 1` -> `Arrive Customer 1` -> `Delivery Success` -> `Depart Customer 1` -> `Travel to Customer 2`...).
    *   All identified variants of the delivery process, highlighting deviations from the common path.
    *   **Key elements to visualize:**
        *   **Frequency of transitions:** How often a particular step follows another.
        *   **Throughput time:** The time taken for different process paths or individual activities.
        *   **Bottlenecks:** Activities or transitions that represent significant time sinks.
        *   **Deviations:** Unplanned stops (e.g., `Unscheduled Stop` for maintenance), failed deliveries, or alternative sequences.
    The discovered map will provide a holistic, empirical view of how deliveries are actually performed, offering immediate insights into common practices and exceptions.

*   **Conformance Checking:**
    This involves comparing the discovered "as-is" process model against the "to-be" or planned process.
    *   **The "Planned" Model:** The dispatch system data provides planned routes, which can be interpreted as a sequence of customer stops with associated time windows. For conformance checking, we will reconstruct planned routes for each `Case ID` based on the dispatch data and then compare the actual event sequence and timings against this plan.
    *   **Types of Deviations to Look For:**
        *   **Sequence Deviations:** Actual customer stops are visited in a different order than planned. This is a significant deviation impacting route efficiency.
        *   **Missing/Added Activities:**
            *   **Skipped Stops:** A planned delivery was not attempted or successfully completed.
            *   **Unplanned Stops:** Events like `Unscheduled Stop` (potentially for maintenance, breaks, or personal reasons) occurring during the delivery window.
            *   **Failed Deliveries:** `Delivery Failed` events that might not have been anticipated in the plan.
        *   **Timing Deviations:**
            *   **Significant Delays:** Actual arrival times at customers or departure from the depot/customers are substantially later than planned.
            *   **Early Arrivals:** While less problematic for punctuality, can indicate inefficient waiting times.
            *   **Exceeded Time Windows:** Actual delivery completion times falling outside the customer's requested `Delivery Time Window`.
        *   **Attribute Deviations:** Although less prominent in the sample, if dispatch assigns specific resources (e.g., "use refrigerated van V5"), deviations in actual vehicle/driver assignment would also be identified.
    By highlighting these deviations, we can pinpoint specific areas where operational execution fails to meet planning expectations, directly impacting punctuality and efficiency.

### 2. Performance Analysis and Bottleneck Identification

This phase quantizes the operational performance and pinpoints the most significant constraints.

*   **Key Performance Indicators (KPIs):**
    Based on Speedy Parcels' stated goals, the following KPIs will be defined and calculated:
    *   **Punctuality & Service Level:**
        *   **On-Time Delivery Rate (OTD%):** Percentage of successful deliveries completed within their respective customer time windows. Calculated as: `(Number of Delivery Success events within Time Window) / (Total Number of Delivery Success events)`.
        *   **Late Delivery Rate:** Complement of OTD%.
        *   **Delivery Time Window Adherence:** Average lateness or earliness for deliveries.
    *   **Efficiency & Cost:**
        *   **Average Total Route Duration:** Time from `Depart Depot` to `Arrive Depot` (End of Shift).
        *   **Average Travel Time per Stop:** Time spent between `Depart Customer` and `Arrive Customer`. Calculated by summing `Travel Time` (derived from GPS speed/distance data between two customer locations) for all segments and dividing by the number of stops.
        *   **Average Service Time per Stop:** Time spent at the customer location (`Arrive Customer` to `Depart Customer`). This includes parking, customer interaction, and package handling.
        *   **Idle Time:** Time spent with engine on but stationary (excluding customer service time). Differentiated into potentially productive idle (e.g., waiting for customer) and unproductive idle.
        *   **Vehicle Utilization Rate:** Percentage of time a vehicle is active (engine on and moving or performing service) during its scheduled shift.
        *   **Fuel Consumption:** Can be estimated based on speed, idle time, and vehicle type, and then normalized per km or per package.
        *   **Failed Delivery Rate:** Percentage of delivery attempts that fail. Calculated as: `(Number of Delivery Failed events) / (Total Number of Delivery Attempts)`.
        *   **Re-delivery Rate:** Number of packages requiring a second delivery attempt.
    *   **Operational Factors:**
        *   **Average Stops per Route:** Capacity utilization and route density.
        *   **Frequency/Duration of Unscheduled Stops:** Impact of maintenance or other disruptions.

*   **Bottleneck Identification Techniques:**
    Process mining tools provide sophisticated methods to identify bottlenecks:
    *   **Throughput Time Analysis:**
        *   **Activity Duration Analysis:** Identifying activities with the longest average or median durations. High service times at customers or long travel times between distant stops are common bottlenecks.
        *   **Transition Duration Analysis:** Analyzing the time spent between activities. Long durations between `Depart Customer` and `Arrive Customer` strongly suggest travel-related issues (traffic, distance, inefficient routing).
    *   **Queue and Wait Time Analysis:** While less common for vehicle-to-vehicle waiting in last-mile, it can apply to waiting for loading/unloading at the depot or if multiple vehicles are assigned to the same service area.
    *   **Performance Dashboards and Heatmaps:**
        *   **Geographical Heatmaps:** Visualizing high-congestion areas or regions with consistently long travel times.
        *   **Time-based Analysis:** Identifying peak hours for delays (e.g., rush hour traffic impacting travel times, or specific times of day for high service times).
        *   **Attribute-based Filtering:** Analyzing bottlenecks by:
            *   **Routes/Regions:** Are certain geographical territories inherently slower?
            *   **Drivers:** Are specific drivers consistently slower or experiencing more delays?
            *   **Vehicle Types:** Do certain vehicles have higher idle times or slower performance?
            *   **Time of Day/Day of Week:** Seasonal or cyclical delays.
    *   **Variant Analysis:** Comparing performance (e.g., total route duration, OTD%) of different process variants. Variants with longer durations often reveal inefficient sequences or prolonged activities.
    By quantifying these metrics and visualizing bottlenecks, we can move beyond anecdotal evidence to precise identification of where time and resources are being lost.

### 3. Root Cause Analysis for Inefficiencies

Identifying bottlenecks is the first step; understanding *why* they exist is critical for effective solutions.

*   **Potential Root Causes and How Process Mining Validates Them:**
    *   **Suboptimal Route Planning & Inaccurate Travel Time Estimations:**
        *   **Manifestation:** Long actual travel times between stops, frequent deviations from planned routes, missed delivery windows.
        *   **PM Analysis:**
            *   **Conformance Checking:** High rates of sequence deviations or significant timing differences between planned and actual routes.
            *   **Variant Analysis:** Comparing variants that closely follow the planned route vs. those that deviate. If deviations lead to better performance, the original plan was likely flawed.
            *   **Correlation Analysis:** Correlating `Travel Time` between stops with actual GPS speed data. If speed is consistently low for a segment, it points to inaccurate estimations or unforeseen obstacles.
    *   **Impact of Traffic Congestion Patterns:**
        *   **Manifestation:** Unpredictable delays, significantly longer travel times during specific periods or in certain areas.
        *   **PM Analysis:**
            *   **Time-of-Day Analysis:** Bottlenecks (especially travel time) concentrated during peak traffic hours.
            *   **Geographical Analysis:** High travel times correlating with known traffic-prone areas.
            *   **Speed Analysis:** Analyzing speed patterns within travel segments; periods of low speed (<20 km/h) are strong indicators of traffic.
    *   **High Variability in Service Time at Customer Locations:**
        *   **Manifestation:** Long and inconsistent `Arrive Customer` to `Depart Customer` durations.
        *   **PM Analysis:**
            *   **Dwell Time Analysis:** Focusing on `Service Time` per stop. High variance is key.
            *   **Attribute Analysis:** Examining `Notes` associated with long dwell times. Common reasons might include difficulty finding parking, long customer interaction, package issues, or finding secure drop-off locations.
            *   **Customer-Specific Analysis:** If data permits, analyzing if certain customers or types of customers consistently lead to longer service times.
    *   **Frequency and Impact of Vehicle Breakdowns or Maintenance Needs:**
        *   **Manifestation:** `Unscheduled Stop` events during delivery shifts, route disruptions, late returns, increased operational costs.
        *   **PM Analysis:**
            *   **Event Frequency Analysis:** Quantifying the occurrence of `Unscheduled Stop` events or `Maintenance Start`/`End` events during operational hours.
            *   **Correlation with Maintenance Logs:** Linking these events directly to logged maintenance activities.
            *   **Impact Analysis:** Analyzing the duration of `Unscheduled Stop` events and their effect on the remainder of the route (e.g., subsequent stops missed or delayed).
    *   **Driver Behavior or Skill Differences:**
        *   **Manifestation:** Inconsistent performance across drivers, higher fuel consumption, more frequent incidents (e.g., failed deliveries), longer service times.
        *   **PM Analysis:**
            *   **Performance Comparison:** Benchmarking KPIs (e.g., average travel time, service time, OTD%) across individual drivers.
            *   **Behavioral Analysis:** Analyzing speed profiles, idle times, and adherence to planned routes for different drivers.
            *   **Failed Delivery Analysis:** Identifying if specific drivers have higher rates of failed deliveries and investigating the associated `Notes`.
    *   **Issues Related to Failed Delivery Attempts:**
        *   **Manifestation:** High `Delivery Failed` rates, requiring costly re-deliveries.
        *   **PM Analysis:**
            *   **Root Cause Analysis of Failures:** Analyzing the sequence leading up to a `Delivery Failed` event. Is it always at a specific address type, time of day, or preceded by certain travel conditions?
            *   **Re-delivery Analysis:** Tracking packages that failed and were subsequently delivered. This highlights the cost of inefficiency.

By using process mining's ability to explore data from multiple perspectives (process flow, time, resources, attributes), we can validate these hypothesized root causes and build a strong, evidence-based foundation for optimization.

### 4. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

Based on the insights derived from process mining, we propose the following concrete optimization strategies.

1.  **Strategy: Implement Dynamic Routing and Real-time Adjustments**
    *   **Target Inefficiency/Bottleneck:** Long travel times, missed delivery windows due to unforeseen traffic or road closures, suboptimal route sequencing that doesn't adapt to real-time conditions.
    *   **Root Cause Addressed:** Suboptimal static route planning and inaccurate travel time estimations that fail to account for dynamic external factors like traffic.
    *   **Process Mining Support:**
        *   **Analysis:** Process maps will reveal frequent deviations from planned routes, and correlation analysis will show these deviations often coincide with periods of low GPS speed (traffic). Geographical heatmaps will highlight specific corridors prone to congestion. Variant analysis comparing routes that dynamically adjusted (if any informal attempts were made) vs. rigid routes will show performance differences.
        *   **Insight:** Identifying specific routes or segments where real-time adjustments would yield the greatest time savings and improve on-time delivery rates.
    *   **Proposal:** Integrate real-time traffic data (from external providers) and potentially predictive traffic models into the dispatch system. Implement algorithms that can dynamically re-sequence stops or suggest alternative routes for drivers *en route* when significant delays are detected or predicted. This could involve a dispatcher intervening or the system pushing updates to driver handhelds.
    *   **Expected KPI Impacts:**
        *   Increased On-Time Delivery Rate (OTD%).
        *   Reduced Average Travel Time per Stop.
        *   Reduced Total Route Duration.
        *   Potentially reduced fuel consumption due to more efficient driving paths.

2.  **Strategy: Optimize Delivery Territories and Route Sequencing**
    *   **Target Inefficiency/Bottleneck:** Inefficient route coverage, excessive travel time between delivery clusters within a territory, and low delivery density leading to longer overall routes.
    *   **Root Cause Addressed:** Suboptimal allocation of delivery zones and inefficient ordering of stops within those zones, leading to wasted travel.
    *   **Process Mining Support:**
        *   **Analysis:** Geographical analysis of delivery density, travel time maps, and average travel distance between consecutive stops. Variant analysis will highlight how routes with tightly clustered deliveries perform better. Analysis of `Service Time` vs. `Travel Time` ratios for different areas.
        *   **Insight:** Identifying under-utilized areas, inefficient "spaghetti" routes, and patterns of high travel time due to geographical spread of deliveries. This can inform re-drawing territory boundaries or optimizing the sequence of stops within existing territories.
    *   **Proposal:**
        *   **Territory Re-balancing:** Use geographical analysis from PM to re-align delivery territories to create more balanced workloads and reduce average travel distance per delivery.
        *   **Sequence Optimization:** Within optimized territories, use historical data (derived from PM analysis of best-performing routes) to generate optimal stop sequences. For example, prioritizing clusters of deliveries or sequencing based on predicted travel times throughout the day. This could involve leveraging advanced route optimization software informed by PM insights.
    *   **Expected KPI Impacts:**
        *   Reduced Total Travel Distance and Time per route.
        *   Increased Number of Deliveries per Route.
        *   Improved Vehicle Utilization Rate.
        *   Potentially reduced fuel consumption and driver overtime.

3.  **Strategy: Proactive Management of Failed Deliveries and Customer Time Window Adherence**
    *   **Target Inefficiency/Bottleneck:** High rate of failed deliveries and associated re-delivery costs; missed customer time windows leading to dissatisfaction and potential rescheduling.
    *   **Root Cause Addressed:** Inaccurate address data, poor customer communication regarding delivery expectations, insufficient information for drivers (e.g., access codes), customer unavailability, and inflexibility in time window management.
    *   **Process Mining Support:**
        *   **Analysis:** High frequency of `Delivery Failed` events for specific addresses, postcodes, or times of day. Analyzing the `Notes` associated with failed deliveries (e.g., "Customer Not Home," "Incorrect Address," "No Safe Place"). Analyzing dwell times before delivery success/failure events. Analyzing compliance with customer time windows.
        *   **Insight:** Identifying patterns in failed deliveries that can be mitigated through better customer engagement or data accuracy. Pinpointing specific times or customer segments where proactive communication is most needed.
    *   **Proposal:**
        *   **Enhanced Customer Communication:** Implement automated SMS/email notifications *before* delivery, confirming time windows and providing tracking links. For high-risk deliveries (identified by historical data), include options for customers to reschedule or provide specific delivery instructions (e.g., gate codes, safe place).
        *   **Address Validation:** Integrate address validation tools into the dispatch system.
        *   **Driver Information Enrichment:** Ensure driver handhelds provide clear access to customer notes and delivery preferences.
        *   **Dynamic Time Window Adjustments:** Where feasible, allow for minor, real-time adjustments to customer time windows if a driver is significantly delayed, with automated customer notification.
    *   **Expected KPI Impacts:**
        *   Reduced Failed Delivery Rate and Re-delivery Costs.
        *   Increased On-Time Delivery Rate (OTD%) and Time Window Adherence.
        *   Improved Customer Satisfaction.
        *   More predictable route durations by reducing unexpected delays from failed attempts.

### 5. Considering Operational Constraints and Monitoring

Implementing optimization strategies requires careful consideration of existing operational realities, and continuous monitoring is key to sustained success.

*   **Accounting for Operational Constraints:**
    *   **Driver Working Hours:** All proposed strategies, especially dynamic routing and territory optimization, must be developed within the legal framework of driver working hours. The `Start Shift`, `End Shift`, and `Overtime logged` events are critical inputs. Optimization algorithms should consider maximum driving and total working times, ensuring compliance and preventing driver fatigue.
    *   **Vehicle Capacity:** Routing and scheduling optimizations must respect vehicle capacity (volume, weight) as specified in the dispatch system. Dynamic routing should adjust the sequence or number of drops based on remaining capacity if partial loads are a factor.
    *   **Customer Time Windows:** While strategies aim to *meet* these, they remain a hard constraint. Dynamic routing and sequence optimization will prioritize deliveries within these windows, flagging any potential conflicts early.
    *   **Driver Skills and Preferences:** While process mining can highlight performance differences, driver training and load balancing should consider individual driver capabilities and comfort levels to ensure safe and effective operation.
    *   **Geographical Limitations:** Certain routes might be restricted by road type, weight limits, or delivery access restrictions, which need to be factored into route planning and dynamic adjustments.

*   **Continuous Monitoring Plan:**
    After implementing optimization strategies, a robust monitoring framework is essential to ensure effectiveness, sustainability, and to quickly identify new issues.
    *   **Process Mining Dashboards:**
        *   **Real-time KPI Tracking:** A live dashboard displaying key KPIs such as OTD%, Failed Delivery Rate, Average Travel Time, Average Service Time, and Vehicle Utilization.
        *   **Process Map Monitoring:** A continuously updated process map showing the most frequent paths and variants. Significant shifts in flow or the emergence of new, frequent variants would signal a change in operational behavior or effectiveness of interventions.
        *   **Conformance Checking Dashboard:** Periodically re-running conformance checks against the *optimized* planned models to ensure adherence.
    *   **Key Metrics and Views to Track:**
        *   **Performance Drift Alerts:** Set up automated alerts for significant drops in OTD%, spikes in failed deliveries, or noticeable increases in average route duration.
        *   **Bottleneck Evolution:** Regularly review duration analysis and transition times to see if newly identified bottlenecks are emerging or if previously identified ones have been resolved.
        *   **Attribute-based Analysis:** Regularly filter dashboards and reports by driver, vehicle, region, and time period. This helps in identifying any localized or systemic issues that might arise (e.g., a new traffic pattern affecting a specific route, or a driver consistently struggling with a new process).
        *   **Variant Analysis on Exceptions:** Focus on analyzing less frequent but problematic variants (e.g., routes involving unscheduled stops or multiple failed deliveries) to understand and address edge cases.
        *   **Feedback Loop Integration:** The monitoring system should facilitate a feedback loop. If a driver reports an issue, the data can be queried to see if it's an isolated incident or part of a pattern, informing further adjustments.

By embedding process mining into the operational monitoring process, Speedy Parcels can ensure that optimizations are sustained, adapt to changing conditions, and maintain a competitive edge in delivery punctuality and cost-efficiency.
