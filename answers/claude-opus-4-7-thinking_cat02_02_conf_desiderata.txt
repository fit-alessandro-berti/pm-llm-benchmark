# Analysis of Loan Application Process Event Log

## 1. Normative Rule Violations

### **Case 1:  Compliant**
Case 1 follows the prescribed sequence perfectly:
- Application Received  Credit Check  Risk Assessment  Loan Decision (Approved)  Inform Applicant  Finalize Documentation  Close Case
- All activities occur in the correct order, and since the loan was approved, documentation was finalized before closing. **No normative violations.**

### **Case 2:  Multiple Normative Violations**
Case 2 violates the strict rules in two significant ways:

1. **Order violation (Risk Assessment before Credit Check):**
   - The Risk Assessment occurred at 08:20, but the Credit Check wasn't performed until 09:00.
   - The rule explicitly states: *Credit Check must occur before Risk Assessment.*

2. **Contradictory action (Finalize Documentation after Rejection):**
   - The loan decision was **Rejected** at 09:30, yet Finalize Documentation occurred at 10:00.
   - The rule states: *If loan is rejected, do not finalize documentation.*

3. **Missing "Inform Applicant" activity:**
   - The applicant was never informed of the rejection decision before the case was closed. This violates the required sequence (*Inform Applicant must occur after Loan Decision*).

### **Case 3:  Normatively Compliant**
Despite some timing issues, the sequence of activities is correct:
- Application Received  Credit Check  Risk Assessment  Loan Decision (Approved)  Inform Applicant  Finalize Documentation  Close Case
- No hard rules are broken.

## 2. Desiderata Violations (Non-Severe)

### **Case 1:  Meets All Desiderata**
- Credit Check occurred within 30 minutes (well within 24 hours).
- Applicant was informed within 2 hours (well within 48 hours).
- However, **no Follow-up Call is recorded** — a minor desideratum deviation, but acceptable.

### **Case 2: Desiderata are secondary concerns here**
Given the severe normative violations, desiderata are less important, but we can still note:
- No Follow-up Call was made.
- Timing between activities appears acceptable, but the process itself is broken.

### **Case 3:  Multiple Desiderata Violations**
1. **Credit Check delay:** Performed ~49 hours after Application Received (recommended within 24 hours).
2. **Delayed notification:** Applicant informed ~75 hours after Loan Decision (recommended within 48 hours).
3. **No Follow-up Call** was performed before closing the case.

These are **suboptimal** but do **not constitute rule violations**.

---

## 3. Interpretations & Justifications

### **Case 1**
Represents the ideal scenario — fully compliant and executed promptly. The only minor note is the absence of a Follow-up Call, which might be justified if the applicant was sufficiently informed via other channels.

### **Case 2**
The violations suggest systemic or process-control issues:
- **Possible justification for order swap:** The Risk Assessment may have been logged out of order due to a data-entry error or a parallel workflow where a preliminary risk screening was mistakenly labeled.
- **Finalize Documentation after rejection:** This may reflect a misclassification — perhaps staff finalized rejection paperwork (e.g., a formal rejection letter) and logged it under "Finalize Documentation." However, per the normative rules, this is still a violation and should be corrected through staff training or system validation (e.g., disabling the "Finalize Documentation" option when decision = Rejected).
- **Missing Inform Applicant step** is a serious process gap — the applicant may not have received notice, which is both a compliance and ethical concern.

### **Case 3**
All normative rules are followed, but timing and optional steps show room for improvement:
- **Credit Check delay** (2 days) could be due to weekend processing, third-party credit bureau delays, or resource constraints.
- **Delayed notification** could be attributed to internal review cycles or applicant unavailability.
- **Missing Follow-up Call** may be acceptable if the applicant explicitly waived it or was unreachable.

These deviations are acceptable because desiderata are "best practices," not hard rules. However, recurring desiderata violations could indicate inefficiencies worth addressing (e.g., SLA monitoring, automated reminders).
