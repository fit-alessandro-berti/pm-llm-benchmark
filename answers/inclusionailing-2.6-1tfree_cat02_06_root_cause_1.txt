## 2. Root-cause patterns and contributing factors

### A. Escalation-related delays
- **Long handover and re-start cost:**  
  - Case 102: ~2.5 h from “Assign to Level-1” to “Escalate,” then ~14.5 h until Level-2 begins “Investigate Issue.”  
  - Case 105: ~1 h 50 m from “Investigate Issue” (L1) to “Escalate,” then ~19 h until Level-2 re-starts “Investigate Issue.”  
  - Escalation appears to introduce queue/wait time before the receiving team picks up the ticket.

### B. Idle time between activities (non-value-add waiting)
- **Before investigation:**  
  - Case 104: 3 h 30 m from assignment to “Investigate Issue.”  
  - Case 102: 2.5 h from assignment to escalation, then further delay before Level-2 picks up.  
- **After escalation (recovery lag):**  
  - Case 105: ~19 h gap between “Escalate” and the next “Investigate Issue” (Level-2).  
  - Case 102: ~14.5 h gap from escalation to Level-2 investigation.

### C. Resolution scheduling delays
- **Case 104:** Investigation finished one day, but resolution occurs the next morning (~19 h gap).  
- **Case 102 & 105:** Resolution occurs the calendar day after Level-2 investigation finishes, suggesting resolution may be batched or dependent on shift handovers or approval windows.

### D. Triage/assignment bottlenecks (minor)
- Case 104: 40 m from receive to triage and 30 m from triage to assignment—slightly slower than average but small compared to later idle periods.

### E. Summary of dominant factors
- **Primary:** Long idle periods (queue/wait) between steps, especially after escalation and before/after investigation and resolution.  
- **Secondary:** Escalation itself correlates with delay, but the mechanism is largely the waiting time introduced, not the act of escalating.  
- **Tertiary:** Calendar effects (overnight/weekend gaps) amplify delays when steps fall at shift boundaries.

---

## 3. Impact on cycle time and recommendations

### How these factors increase cycle time
- **Wait time inflates duration without progress:** Each multi-hour or overnight gap adds directly to total resolution time without advancing work.  
- **Escalations trigger rework and context-switching:** When Level-2 must re-investigate, the clock effectively resets, and handover friction creates additional delay.  
- **Batching or shift-based resolution windows:** If resolution is only performed at certain times, tickets are held in queue regardless of readiness, extending tail time.

### Insights and actionable recommendations

#### Short-term (quick wins)
- **Set and enforce WIP/time limits between steps:**  
  - Define maximum allowed idle time (e.g., 2 h) between assignment  investigation, and escalation  Level-2 pickup. Use SLA timers to trigger alerts or auto-routing.
- **Accelerate post-escalation handover:**  
  - Create a standardized handover note (summary, attempted fixes, logs) due at the moment of escalation to reduce Level-2 ramp-up and prevent “re-investigate from scratch.”
- **Enable immediate Level-2 investigation:**  
  - Route escalated tickets to an on-call or dedicated queue with priority, rather than letting them sit in a general queue until the next shift.

#### Medium-term (process improvements)
- **Parallelize or front-load work where possible:**  
  - Allow Level-1 to attach diagnostic artifacts so Level-2 can begin analysis as soon as they pick up, shortening the effective re-investigation time.
- **Reduce resolution batching:**  
  - Decouple “Resolve Ticket” from calendar batching. Enable resolution as soon as the fix is validated, even outside traditional windows (with appropriate controls/approvals).
- **Define clear escalation criteria and early triggers:**  
  - If a ticket cannot be diagnosed within a fixed time (e.g., 30–60 m), escalate sooner rather than later to minimize total elapsed time with L1 stall.

#### Long-term (structural fixes)
- **Balancing and specialization:**  
  - Ensure Level-2 capacity is aligned to escalation volume (queue-depth forecasting) to prevent backlog-induced delays.
- **Continuous measurement of idle vs. touch time:**  
  - Track “touch time” (actual work) vs. “wait time” in each phase. Use this to identify where queues form and right-size staffing or re-design flow.
- **Automation and self-service:**  
  - Move common, repetitive issues to self-service or automated resolution to reduce volume entering the escalation path.

By targeting idle-time reduction, smoother escalation handovers, and elimination of unnecessary batching, you can compress cycle times significantly—even for escalated cases—while preserving quality and agent workload balance.
