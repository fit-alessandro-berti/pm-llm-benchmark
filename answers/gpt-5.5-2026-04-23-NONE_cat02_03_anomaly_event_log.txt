A typical Procure-to-Pay process follows a control-oriented sequence such as:

1. **Create Purchase Requisition**
2. **Approve Purchase Requisition**
3. **Issue Purchase Order**
4. **Receive Goods**
5. **Vendor Invoice Received**
6. **Match Invoice to PO / goods receipt**
7. **Release Payment**
8. **Close Case**

Based on that expected flow, the event log contains several anomalous cases.

---

## Summary of Findings

| Case ID | Assessment | Main Issues |
|---|---|---|
| **1001** | Normal | Follows expected P2P sequence |
| **1002** | Anomalous | PO issued before requisition approval; payment released before approval, goods receipt, and invoice matching |
| **1003** | Anomalous | Payment released before invoice receipt, goods receipt, and invoice matching |
| **1004** | Anomalous | Goods received before purchase order was issued |

## Case 1001 — Normal Process Flow

### Sequence

1. Create Purchase Requisition  
2. Approve Purchase Requisition  
3. Issue Purchase Order  
4. Receive Goods  
5. Vendor Invoice Received  
6. Match Invoice to PO  
7. Release Payment  
8. Close Case  

### Assessment

This case follows the standard P2P process. The requisition is approved before the purchase order is issued, goods are received before payment, the invoice is matched to the PO, and payment is released only after matching.

### Conclusion

No obvious anomaly.

## Case 1002 — Multiple Anomalies

### Sequence

1. Create Purchase Requisition  
2. Issue Purchase Order  
3. Vendor Invoice Received  
4. Release Payment  
5. Approve Purchase Requisition  
6. Receive Goods  
7. Match Invoice to PO  
8. Close Case  

### Anomaly 1: Purchase Order Issued Before Requisition Approval

The purchase order is issued at **08:40**, but the purchase requisition is not approved until **08:00 on 2024-02-03**.

This is unusual because a purchase requisition should normally be approved before a purchase order is created or issued.

### Why this is problematic

Issuing a PO before approval can indicate:

- Bypassing internal authorization controls
- Unauthorized purchasing
- Budget control failure
- Increased risk of maverick buying or fraud

---

### Anomaly 2: Payment Released Before Requisition Approval

Payment is released on **2024-02-03 at 07:15**, but the purchase requisition is approved later that same day at **08:00**.

This means the company paid the vendor before the purchase request was formally approved.

### Why this is problematic

This is a significant control issue because payment should not occur before authorization. It may indicate:

- Weak approval controls
- Emergency or manual override
- Unauthorized expenditure
- Fraud risk

---

### Anomaly 3: Payment Released Before Goods Receipt

Payment is released on **2024-02-03 at 07:15**, while goods are not received until **2024-02-04 at 09:00**.

This means payment occurred before the company confirmed that the ordered goods were actually delivered.

### Why this is problematic

Paying before goods receipt can result in:

- Paying for goods that were never delivered
- Overpayment or duplicate payment risk
- Loss of leverage with the supplier
- Difficulty disputing non-delivery or poor-quality goods

In some business scenarios, prepayment may be valid, but it should normally be explicitly authorized and documented.

---

### Anomaly 4: Payment Released Before Invoice Matching

Payment is released on **2024-02-03 at 07:15**, while invoice matching happens on **2024-02-05 at 08:00**.

This violates the usual three-way match control: purchase order, goods receipt, and invoice should be checked before payment.

### Why this is problematic

This is one of the most serious issues in P2P because invoice matching is a key financial control. Paying before matching can lead to:

- Payment of incorrect invoice amounts
- Payment for unordered items
- Payment for undelivered goods
- Duplicate or fraudulent payments

---

## Case 1003 — Payment Occurs Too Early

### Sequence

1. Create Purchase Requisition  
2. Approve Purchase Requisition  
3. Issue Purchase Order  
4. Release Payment  
5. Vendor Invoice Received  
6. Receive Goods  
7. Match Invoice to PO  
8. Close Case  

### Anomaly 1: Payment Released Before Invoice Is Received

Payment is released on **2024-02-08 at 11:00**, but the vendor invoice is received later, on **2024-02-09 at 12:00**.

This is highly unusual because payment is normally based on an invoice or some formal payment request.

### Why this is problematic

Paying before receiving an invoice can indicate:

- Unauthorized advance payment
- Missing payment documentation
- Poor audit trail
- Increased risk of fraud or error

There may be valid exceptions, such as deposits or advance payments, but those should usually follow a separate approved advance-payment process.

---

### Anomaly 2: Payment Released Before Goods Receipt

Payment is released on **2024-02-08**, while goods are received on **2024-02-10**.

This means payment was made before confirming delivery.

### Why this is problematic

This can result in:

- Paying for goods never received
- Reduced ability to resolve delivery issues
- Increased supplier-performance risk
- Potential financial loss

---

### Anomaly 3: Payment Released Before Invoice Matching

Payment is released on **2024-02-08**, while invoice matching occurs on **2024-02-11**.

This violates the normal payment control sequence.

### Why this is problematic

Invoice matching is designed to ensure that:

- The invoice corresponds to an approved PO
- The goods or services were received
- The amount billed is correct
- Payment is justified

Releasing payment before matching undermines these controls.

---

## Case 1004 — Goods Received Before Purchase Order Issuance

### Sequence

1. Create Purchase Requisition  
2. Approve Purchase Requisition  
3. Receive Goods  
4. Issue Purchase Order  
5. Vendor Invoice Received  
6. Match Invoice to PO  
7. Release Payment  
8. Close Case  

### Anomaly: Goods Received Before Purchase Order

Goods are received on **2024-02-06 at 09:00**, but the purchase order is issued later at **09:15**.

This means the goods were received before the formal purchase order existed.

### Why this is problematic

This suggests that the vendor may have supplied goods before receiving a formal PO, or that the PO was created after the fact.

Possible issues include:

- Confirming orders retroactively
- Maverick or off-contract purchasing
- Weak procurement discipline
- Reduced visibility into commitments
- Difficulty enforcing agreed prices, quantities, and terms

This is less severe than paying before approval or matching, but it is still a control concern.

## Overall Anomalies Identified

The major anomalies are:

### 1. Purchase Order before Approval

Observed in:

- **Case 1002**

A PO should normally be issued only after the requisition is approved.

---

### 2. Payment before Goods Receipt

Observed in:

- **Case 1002**
- **Case 1003**

Payment before goods receipt increases the risk of paying for undelivered or incorrect goods.

---

### 3. Payment before Invoice Receipt

Observed in:

- **Case 1003**

Payment before receiving an invoice creates documentation and audit problems.

---

### 4. Payment before Invoice Matching

Observed in:

- **Case 1002**
- **Case 1003**

This is a significant control violation because matching is a core P2P control before payment.

---

### 5. Goods Receipt before Purchase Order

Observed in:

- **Case 1004**

This may indicate retroactive purchasing or unauthorized procurement activity.

---

## Final Assessment

- **Case 1001** appears compliant with standard P2P practices.
- **Case 1002** is highly anomalous due to payment occurring before approval, goods receipt, and matching.
- **Case 1003** is highly anomalous because payment occurs before invoice receipt, goods receipt, and matching.
- **Case 1004** is moderately anomalous because goods are received before the purchase order is issued.

The most serious concerns are in **Cases 1002 and 1003**, where payment is released before key controls are completed. These situations may expose the organization to financial loss, fraud, compliance violations, and audit findings.
