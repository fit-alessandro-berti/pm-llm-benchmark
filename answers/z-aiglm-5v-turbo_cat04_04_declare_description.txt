### **Real-World Motivations & Consequences**

**Why design a process this way?**

*   **Risk Management:** By enforcing `Exactly_One` on the Credit Check and `Succession` regarding Document Gathering, the bank mitigates the risk of issuing loans without income verification or credit assessment.
*   **Operational Efficiency:** Using **Chain** constraints (Chain Succession, Chain Response) between QA, Assembly, and Transfer removes "bloat time." In back-office operations, delays between QA approval and Fund Transfer cost money; chaining them forces an automated or rapid-handover workflow.
*   **Customer Experience (CX):** The flow ensures the customer isn't notified prematurely. By constraining `Notify_Customer` to happen only after `Transfer_Funds` (via Alt Response/Precedence), the bank avoids the embarrassing situation of telling a customer "Your money is coming!" before the wire has actually been sent.

**What happens if these constraints are violated?**

*   **Scenario A (Skipping the Credit Check):** If the `Response` constraint were ignored, the bank could issue a loan to an unqualified applicant, leading to immediate default risk.
*   **Scenario B (The Compliance Bypass):** If `Proceed_Without_Compliance` were allowed (violating the `Absence` constraint), the institution could face massive fines from regulators (like the CFPB or FTC) for unfair lending practices.
*   **Scenario C (Out of Order Notification):** If `Notify_Customer` happened *before* `Transfer_Funds` (violating `AltPrecedence`), it could constitute a breach of contract or false advertising if the transfer subsequently failed due to a technical error. The constraint ensures the action precedes the promise.
