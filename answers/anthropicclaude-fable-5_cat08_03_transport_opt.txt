# Process Mining Approach for Speedy Parcels: Last-Mile Delivery Optimization

---

## 1. Process Discovery and Conformance Checking

### 1.1 Data Preprocessing and Integration

**Step 1: Define the Case Notion(s)**

A critical design decision is the case identifier. For last-mile logistics, I recommend a **multi-perspective event log** with at least two complementary case notions:

- **Vehicle-Day (V12-20241205):** The primary case for analyzing the end-to-end operational day — depot departure, stop sequence, breaks, delays, depot return. This captures route-level efficiency.
- **Package ID (P9876):** A secondary case for the package lifecycle — depot scan  loaded  arrive customer  success/failure  (re-delivery attempts). This captures customer-facing punctuality and failed-delivery loops.

This dual view is essential because some questions ("Why was the route slow?") are vehicle-centric, while others ("Why was this parcel late?") are package-centric. An **object-centric event log (OCEL)** is the ideal target format, since one GPS event relates to a vehicle, a driver, and potentially multiple packages on board simultaneously.

**Step 2: Source-Specific Transformations**

| Source | Raw Form | Transformation Needed |
|---|---|---|
| GPS trackers | High-frequency telemetry (every few seconds) | **Event abstraction**: aggregate raw pings into meaningful activities — "Driving Segment," "Idle > 5 min," "Traffic Slowdown" (speed < threshold on a road segment), "Unscheduled Stop." Use geofencing around depot and customer addresses to derive "Arrive/Depart" events. |
| Scanner | Discrete milestone events | Mostly ready; validate against GPS (a "Delivery Success" scan should occur near the customer's geocoded address). |
| Dispatch | Planned routes (reference model, not events) | Convert each planned route into a **normative process model / planned event sequence** (planned stop order, planned arrival times, time windows) for conformance checking. |
| Maintenance logs | Interval data (start/end) | Convert to paired events ("Maintenance Start"/"Maintenance End") and link to Vehicle ID; flag whether maintenance interrupted an active shift. |

**Step 3: Correlation and Enrichment**
- Join all sources on Vehicle ID + Driver ID + timestamp proximity; geocode customer addresses; map-match GPS points to the road network.
- Enrich with **contextual attributes**: day of week, weather, planned vs. actual stop sequence number, parcel weight/volume, customer time window.

**Expected Challenges**

1. **Timestamp synchronization:** Different systems have different clocks and granularities (GPS = seconds; dispatch = planned times). Clock drift on handheld scanners is common — needs offset correction.
2. **Granularity mismatch:** GPS produces thousands of low-level events per day vs. ~35 scanner milestones. Without abstraction, discovered models become "spaghetti." Solution: hierarchical event abstraction (driving segments, stop episodes).
3. **Missing/erroneous data:** GPS signal loss in urban canyons, drivers scanning late or batch-scanning multiple deliveries at once (visible as several "Delivery Success" events with identical timestamps), forgotten "Depart Customer" scans.
4. **Convergence/divergence:** One vehicle-day involves many packages; flattening into a single-case log causes event duplication or loss — hence the OCEL recommendation.
5. **Linking unplanned events:** Attributing an "Unscheduled Stop" to a cause (breakdown vs. driver break vs. parking search) may require fuzzy matching against maintenance logs and notes fields.

### 1.2 Process Discovery

- **Start with Directly-Follows Graphs (DFG) / fuzzy mining** at the abstracted activity level (Start Shift  Route Assigned  Depart Depot  {Travel, Arrive Customer, Delivery Success/Failed, Depart Customer}*  Arrive Depot  End Shift). Frequency- and performance-annotated DFGs immediately reveal dominant flows and slow arcs.
- **Inductive Miner** (infrequent variant) for a sound, structured model of the canonical delivery loop, useful as a baseline for conformance.
- **Variant analysis:** Cluster vehicle-days by their activity sequences. Expect variants such as: clean route (all successes), route with failed deliveries, route with mid-shift maintenance stop, route with extended traffic delays.
- **Geospatial process maps:** Overlay the discovered process on a map — last-mile mining is uniquely enriched by location. Plot stop sequences, idle hotspots, and slowdown segments to see *where* the process bends.
- **Package-level discovery:** Mine the parcel lifecycle to expose **rework loops**: Delivery Failed  Return to Depot  Re-dispatch  Second Attempt. Loop frequency directly quantifies the cost of failed deliveries.

### 1.3 Conformance Checking Against Planned Routes

The dispatch system provides a per-case **normative model**: the planned stop sequence with planned timestamps and customer time windows. I would apply:

- **Alignment-based conformance checking:** Align actual stop sequences with planned ones, yielding fitness scores per vehicle-day and identifying *move-on-log* (unplanned activities, e.g., unscheduled stops) and *move-on-model* (planned stops skipped or never reached).
- **Deviation types to look for:**
  - **Sequence deviations:** Drivers visiting stops out of planned order — is the deviation harmful, or are experienced drivers *improving* on the plan (a key insight: "positive deviance")?
  - **Unplanned stops:** Breakdowns, breaks, parking-search loops, fuel stops — frequency, duration, location.
  - **Temporal conformance:** Actual arrival vs. planned arrival per stop; arrival vs. customer time window (the true on-time metric).
  - **Skipped/failed stops:** Stops attempted but failed vs. stops never attempted (route abandoned due to overtime limits).
  - **Route deviations (spatial):** Map-matched GPS trajectory vs. planned path — detours, wrong turns, avoidance of congested corridors.
- **Aggregate conformance dashboards:** Fitness per driver, per route zone, per weekday — to find systematic vs. incidental non-conformance.

## 2. Performance Analysis and Bottleneck Identification

### 2.1 KPIs and Their Calculation from the Event Log

| KPI | Calculation from log | Goal relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **On-Time Delivery Rate (OTD)** | % of "Delivery Success" events with timestamp inside the customer time window (dispatch data) | Punctuality |
| **First-Attempt Success Rate** | Successes ÷ (Successes + Failed) per package first attempt | Cost & punctuality |
| **Avg. Service Time per Stop** | ("Arrive Customer"  "Depart Customer"), split into pre-scan (parking/finding address) and scan-to-depart | Throughput |
| **Travel Time vs. Service Time Ratio** | Sum of inter-stop driving time ÷ sum of stop dwell time per vehicle-day | Route density / planning quality |
| **Stops per Hour / per Shift** | Count of stop episodes ÷ shift duration | Productivity |
| **Traffic Delay Frequency & Duration** | Aggregated "Low Speed Detected" episodes (speed < threshold for > X min on road segments), by location and time of day | Routing quality |
| **Fuel per km / per package** | Fuel data (telematics or refuel logs) ÷ GPS-derived distance ÷ delivered packages | Cost |
| **Vehicle Utilization** | Productive time (driving + serving) ÷ shift time; also idle-with-engine-on time (fuel waste) | Cost |
| **Overtime Rate** | % of shifts where End Shift > planned end; magnitude of overtime | Cost & driver welfare |
| **Mid-shift Breakdown Rate / MTBF** | Unscheduled stops with maintenance correlation per vehicle per 1,000 km | Maintenance cost & reliability |
| **Re-delivery Cost Index** | Extra km + time attributable to second/third attempts (from package-level loops) | Cost |

### 2.2 Bottleneck Identification Techniques

- **Performance-annotated process maps:** Color arcs/nodes by median and 90th-percentile durations. Long arcs between specific consecutive stops = travel bottleneck; long node dwell at "Arrive Customer  Delivery" = service bottleneck.
- **Dotted chart analysis** (cases on Y-axis, time on X-axis, events colored by type): instantly reveals temporal patterns — e.g., all routes slowing between 08:00–09:30 (rush hour), late depot departures cascading through the day, or end-of-shift compression.
- **Spatio-temporal heatmaps:** Aggregate "Low Speed" and idle events on the road network by hour-of-day  identify **traffic hotspots** and chronic parking-search zones (visible as GPS loops near a stop before "Arrive Customer").
- **Decomposed dwell-time analysis:** Split stop time into *parking search* (low-speed circling near destination), *walk/handover* (Arrive  Success scan), and *administrative* (Success  Depart). High variance in parking time at specific addresses (e.g., apartment blocks, business districts) localizes the problem.
- **Dimensional drill-down:** Slice every KPI by route zone, driver, vehicle, weekday, hour, and stop sequence position. Typical findings: stops late in the sequence have lower OTD (cascading delay); certain vans show longer travel times (underpowered/older vehicles); certain drivers consistently faster at identical stops.
- **Quantifying impact:** For each bottleneck, compute *delay contribution* = (actual  benchmark duration) × frequency. E.g., "Parking search in District X adds a median 6 min × 220 stops/week = ~22 driver-hours/week." This ranks bottlenecks by recoverable time/cost, prioritizing interventions.

## 3. Root Cause Analysis for Inefficiencies

Identifying *where* delays occur is necessary but insufficient; we must validate *why*. For each hypothesized root cause, a specific analysis:

| Root Cause Hypothesis | Validating Process Mining Analysis |
|---|---|
| **Suboptimal static route planning** | Conformance analysis: if drivers who deviate from planned sequences achieve *better* OTD/fuel KPIs, the plans are inferior to driver knowledge. Compare planned vs. actual distance/time systematically. |
| **Inaccurate travel time estimates** | Per road segment and hour, compare dispatch's planned leg durations vs. GPS-actual durations. Systematic underestimation (e.g., 25% during 8–9 AM) proves the planning engine ignores time-dependent traffic. |
| **Traffic congestion patterns** | Correlate "Low Speed Detected" episodes with location/time; regress route-level delay on exposure to identified hotspot corridors. If routes traversing corridor C between 8–9 AM are 30 min slower, congestion is causal and *predictable* — hence avoidable. |
| **High service-time variability** | Dwell-time distribution analysis per customer type/address. If B2B addresses with loading docks take 2 min but high-rise residential takes 9 ± 7 min, planning with a flat per-stop estimate guarantees lateness. Decision-tree / root-cause mining on stop attributes (building type, parcel size, signature requirement) explains variance. |
| **Mid-shift breakdowns** | Join unscheduled-stop events with maintenance logs and vehicle age/mileage. Survival analysis: do specific vehicles or usage patterns (high-mileage urban routes) predict in-shift failures? Quantify cascade cost (delayed/abandoned stops per breakdown). |
| **Driver behavior/skill differences** | **Comparative variant analysis**: control for route difficulty (same zones, similar stop counts), then compare driver cohorts on service time, parking time, sequence optimization, idle-engine time, and harsh-driving GPS signatures. Persistent gaps after controlling for context = skill/behavior, not assignment. |
| **Failed deliveries  rework loops** | Package-level loop analysis: failure rate by hour-of-day, customer segment, and time-window width. If failures spike for daytime residential deliveries with no notification sent, the cause is predictable absence + poor communication, not driver performance. Quantify total rework km/time. |

A key technique throughout is **benchmark-based variant comparison** ("process comparison"): split cases into top-quartile and bottom-quartile vehicle-days on OTD and cost, then statistically contrast their process maps, attribute distributions, and deviation patterns. Differences that survive controls are validated root causes.

## 4. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

### Strategy 1: Time-Dependent and Dynamic Routing

- **Targets:** Travel-time bottlenecks, cascading lateness from rush-hour exposure, fuel waste in congestion.
- **Root cause addressed:** Static route plans using flat travel-time estimates that ignore predictable, time-of-day-dependent congestion (validated by the planned-vs-actual leg duration analysis and spatio-temporal slowdown heatmaps).
- **Process mining support:** The mined six-month GPS history yields an empirical, segment-level, hour-by-hour travel-time matrix — far more accurate for *delivery vans in this specific area* than generic map data. Conformance analysis identifies which planned legs are systematically wrong; positive-deviance analysis reveals driver shortcuts worth codifying.
- **Implementation:** (a) Feed the empirical travel-time matrix into the route planner (time-dependent VRP); (b) sequence routes so congestion-prone corridors are traversed off-peak; (c) add intra-day re-optimization triggered by real-time delay detection (when actual arrival drifts > X min from plan, re-sequence remaining stops).
- **Expected KPI impact:** OTD +10–15 pts; travel time per route 10%; fuel per package 5–8%; overtime rate down materially.

### Strategy 2: Failed-Delivery Reduction via Predictive Time-Window Management and Customer Communication

- **Targets:** The rework loop (Failed  Return  Re-dispatch  Re-attempt), which the package-level analysis will likely show consumes a disproportionate share of km and capacity.
- **Root cause addressed:** Deliveries attempted when recipients are predictably absent, with no proactive notification; wide/unrealistic time-window promises.
- **Process mining support:** The log quantifies failure probability by address, customer segment, weekday, and hour. The mined arrival-time accuracy data shows how reliable narrow-window promises can be; the re-delivery cost index quantifies the prize.
- **Implementation:** (a) Predictive ETA notifications (SMS/app) with live narrowing windows, derived from the mined travel/service-time model; (b) schedule historically failure-prone residential stops into evening/weekend slots or offer pickup-point redirection at first risk signal; (c) sequence high-failure-risk stops earlier so a second same-day attempt is feasible.
- **Expected KPI impact:** First-attempt success rate +8–12 pts; re-delivery km 30–50%; effective stops-per-shift capacity increases without adding vehicles; customer complaints drop.

### Strategy 3: Usage-Based Predictive Maintenance Scheduling

- **Targets:** Mid-shift breakdowns and unscheduled stops, which cascade into abandoned stops, overtime, and emergency repair costs.
- **Root cause addressed:** Calendar-based maintenance that ignores actual vehicle stress — the GPS/maintenance correlation analysis will show breakdown risk concentrates in high-mileage, high-idle, stop-start urban vehicles.
- **Process mining support:** The integrated log links each vehicle's actual duty profile (km, idle hours, stop-start cycles, engine warnings like the V12 example) to subsequent failures. Survival/predictive models trained on this history flag at-risk vehicles *before* in-shift failure.
- **Implementation:** Condition/usage-triggered maintenance windows scheduled on low-demand days; rotate high-stress routes across the fleet to equalize wear; act on early warnings (engine light events) the same evening rather than waiting.
- **Expected KPI impact:** Mid-shift breakdown rate 40–60%; maintenance cost shifts from expensive reactive repair to cheaper planned service; fewer abandoned routes  OTD improvement; vehicle availability/utilization up.

### Strategy 4 (Bonus): Targeted Driver Coaching and Best-Practice Codification

- **Targets:** Service-time and parking-search variance; idle-engine fuel waste.
- **Root cause addressed:** Skill/knowledge gaps validated by controlled driver-cohort comparison (not raw league tables, which unfairly penalize hard routes).
- **Process mining support:** Variant analysis identifies *specific practices* of top performers (e.g., pre-sorting parcels by stop, known parking spots per address, efficient stop sequencing) — these become teachable, codified route notes embedded in the navigation app.
- **Expected KPI impact:** Average service time per stop 1–2 min (huge at 35 stops/day); idle fuel 10%; reduced performance spread across the fleet.

## 5. Operational Constraints and Continuous Monitoring

### 5.1 Respecting Operational Constraints

- **Driver working hours:** All re-routing and re-optimization runs against legal/contractual shift limits as hard constraints in the VRP solver. The mined log itself reveals current overtime patterns — optimized plans should be validated in simulation against historical demand to confirm routes complete within shifts at the 90th percentile, not just on average. Dynamic re-optimization must include a "stop-shedding" rule (return undelivered parcels) before hours-of-service violations occur.
- **Vehicle capacity:** Dispatch capacity data constrains parcel-to-route assignment; the mined data on actual stops-per-shift feasibility (not theoretical capacity) sets realistic load limits per route type — dense urban routes are time-constrained, suburban routes volume-constrained.
- **Customer time windows:** Time windows are hard constraints in routing; Strategy 2's predictive ETAs allow *negotiating* narrower but reliable windows rather than violating wide ones. Where mined data shows certain windows are structurally infeasible (e.g., everyone wanting 17:00–19:00), pricing/incentive levers can flatten demand.
- **Change management:** Drivers should be involved — the positive-deviance findings show their expertise; framing the project as "codifying what the best routes do" rather than surveillance is critical for adoption and data quality (drivers who feel monitored may game scanner events).

### 5.2 Continuous Monitoring Plan

Implement an automated pipeline (daily ETL from all four sources  event log  process mining dashboard) with three monitoring layers:

**Operational dashboard (daily/weekly):**
- OTD rate, first-attempt success rate, stops/hour, overtime — trended against pre-intervention baseline with control limits.
- Live conformance: % of routes deviating from plan beyond thresholds; today's emerging delay clusters on the map.

**Tactical dashboard (weekly/monthly):**
- Refreshed performance-annotated process map and spatio-temporal heatmaps to detect *new* traffic hotspots or service-time drift (cities change: construction, new residential towers).
- Variant distribution monitoring: is the "clean route" variant share growing? Are rework loops shrinking?
- Travel-time matrix recalibration: continuously compare planned vs. actual leg times and feed corrections back to the planner (closed-loop).
- Vehicle health indicators: idle ratios, warning-event rates, predicted maintenance risk per vehicle.

**Strategic review (quarterly):**
- Full root-cause re-analysis to confirm fixed causes stay fixed and to detect emerging ones (e.g., demand shifts changing route density).
- A/B evaluation of interventions: compare KPI trajectories of zones/drivers where strategies were rolled out vs. control groups, controlling for seasonality (the six-month baseline supports this).
- **Concept drift detection** on the process itself: statistically test whether the discovered process model changes over time, signaling when route plans, time matrices, or staffing models need redesign.

**Alerting:** Automated triggers — e.g., OTD in any zone drops > 5 pts week-over-week, a vehicle's breakdown-risk score exceeds threshold, or a route's conformance fitness falls — push alerts to dispatch for same-week corrective action.

### Summary

The core of this engagement is turning Speedy Parcels' fragmented telemetry into a unified, multi-level event log; using discovery and conformance to expose the gap between planned and actual operations; quantifying and ranking bottlenecks by recoverable cost; validating root causes through comparative and correlational analyses; and closing the loop with empirically calibrated routing, failure-prevention, maintenance, and coaching strategies — all sustained by a continuous process mining monitoring capability that keeps the operation data-driven as conditions evolve.
