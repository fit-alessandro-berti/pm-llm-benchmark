**1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics**

I would begin by importing the MES event logs into a process mining platform (e.g., Celonis, Disco, ProM, or PM4Py). The log is already in a near XES-compatible format with clear case IDs (Job ID), activities, timestamps, resources, and attributes (due date, priority, setup flag, planned vs. actual durations).

**Key process mining techniques and metrics:**

* **Process Discovery & Conformance:** Use the Inductive Miner or Split Miner to discover the actual job routing network (control-flow model). Apply multi-perspective process mining to incorporate resource and time perspectives. Perform conformance checking against any existing routing templates to quantify routing variability and unexpected loops/rework.

* **Performance Analysis (Time Perspective):**
  * **Flow time / Lead time / Makespan:** Calculate case-level throughput time (Job Release  final Quality Inspection or shipment). Derive distributions (mean, median, 95th percentile, variance) and use dotted charts or performance dashboards to visualize lead-time trends over time.
  * **Waiting/Queue Times:** Identify ôQueue Entryö  ôSetup Startö intervals per machine. Aggregate by work center and job type. Use waiting time decomposition to separate queue time from transport/setup.
  * **Resource Utilization:** Replay the log on the discovered model to compute utilization metrics: productive time (Task StartûEnd), setup time, idle time, and blocked time. Identify starvation periods (machine idle while upstream queues exist).
  * **Sequence-dependent Setup Times:** For each machine, extract the ordered sequence of jobs processed on it. Create a ôfrom-job-type  to-job-typeö matrix (using attributes such as material grade, geometry family, or previous-job ID). Calculate empirical setup time distributions for each pair. Use decision-tree or clustering analysis on job attributes to derive setup predictors.
  * **Tardiness and Schedule Adherence:** Compute lateness = Completion Date û Due Date for each job. Derive tardiness rate (% jobs > 0), mean tardiness, maximum tardiness, and service level (e.g., % on-time within 1 day). Use variant analysis to compare on-time vs. late job traces.
  * **Impact of Disruptions:** Filter log on ôBreakdown Start/Endö and ôPriority Changeö events. Measure the ôripple effectö by analyzing flow-time increase of all jobs active during a disruption window. Use causal process mining or Granger causality tests on time-series KPIs to quantify disruption impact.

These analyses produce a comprehensive diagnostic baseline quantifying the current chaotic state.

**2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies**

Process mining will almost certainly reveal the following pathologies:

* **Bottleneck Resources:** Bottleneck analysis (e.g., Celonis Process Explorer or PM4PyÆs bottleneck miner) will highlight machines with highest utilization + longest average queue times (likely certain CNC mills or heat-treatment furnaces). Their impact is measured via critical path contribution and queue-time correlation with overall lead time.

* **Poor Prioritization:** Variant analysis comparing ôHigh Priorityö vs. ôMedium/Lowö jobs will show that urgent jobs still experience significant waiting time, indicating that local dispatching rules (FCFS + EDD) fail to protect near-due-date work. ôWIP bubblesö around bottlenecks will be visible in animated process maps.

* **Suboptimal Sequencing and Setup Waste:** The setup-time matrix will reveal large variance; many long setups occur between dissimilar jobs. Setup time as % of total machine time will be surprisingly high (often 15û25% in job shops).

* **Starvation and Bullwhip Effect:** Resource utilization profiles will show alternating overload and starvation periods downstream of bottlenecks. Throughput diagrams and WIP level charts over time will demonstrate high variability and ôWIP wavesö propagating through the shop.

* **Disruption Amplification:** Late jobs will show statistically higher exposure to breakdown events, indicating that the system lacks effective recovery mechanisms.

These findings are evidenced through automated bottleneck detection, comparative variant analysis (on-time vs. late), and resource contention heatmaps derived directly from the event log.

**3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness**

Process mining helps separate scheduling logic problems from capacity or variability issues:

* **Static Dispatching Rules in Dynamic Environment:** Local rules cannot see downstream congestion or global due-date pressure. Conformance checking against an idealized ôglobal priorityö model will show massive deviations.

* **Lack of Real-time Visibility & Coordination:** High inter-departmental handoff delays and queue times at non-bottleneck stations indicate poor information flow. Social network analysis on operator/resource handoffs can reveal coordination gaps.

* **Inaccurate Estimates:** Compare planned vs. actual durations across job families. Systematic underestimation (especially setups) will be visible. Decision-tree analysis on actual duration using job attributes reveals missing predictors in current planning.

* **Sequence-dependent Setups Ignored:** The setup matrix will show that current sequences are essentially random with respect to setup time, proving the dispatching rules do not consider this factor.

* **Inadequate Disruption Handling:** Replay analysis around breakdown events will show that rerouting or priority bumping is inconsistent and often too late.

By correlating performance metrics with resource capacity data (from utilization analysis) versus scheduling decisions (from variant analysis), we can statistically attribute the majority of tardiness and WIP buildup to suboptimal scheduling logic rather than pure capacity shortage, although some true capacity bottlenecks will also be confirmed.

**4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies**

**Strategy 1: Multi-Criteria Dynamic Dispatching with Setup Awareness**

Replace local FCFS/EDD with a weighted scoring rule at each machineÆs queue:

Score(job) = w1À(DueDateSlack / RemainingProcessingTime) + w2ÀPriorityFactor + w3À(1 / EstimatedSetupTimeFromLastJob) + w4ÀDownstreamQueuePressure

Weights are derived via regression or simulation-based optimization using historical data. Process mining supplies:
- Empirical remaining processing time distributions per job family.
- Setup-time prediction model (lookup table or regression tree).
- Real-time downstream load extracted from current event log state.

This directly attacks poor prioritization and excessive setup times. Expected impact: 25û40% tardiness reduction, 15û20% lower setup time, reduced WIP via better flow.

**Strategy 2: Predictive Stochastic Scheduling with Bottleneck Forecasting**

Build a predictive model using historical distributions mined from the log:
- Task duration distributions conditioned on job attributes, operator, and machine state.
- Breakdown frequency and repair time distributions per machine.
- Probabilistic routing matrix.

Use these to feed a rolling-horizon stochastic scheduler (e.g., Sample Average Approximation or robust optimization) that generates proactive schedules. Incorporate predictive maintenance signals if sensor data can be correlated with breakdown events via process mining. The system periodically re-optimizes the schedule (every 2û4 hours) considering current shop state from the MES log.

This addresses inaccurate estimates and poor disruption response. Expected impact: more realistic due-date promises, 30% reduction in extreme tardiness, and proactive bottleneck avoidance.

**Strategy 3: Sequence Optimization via Job Family Batching at Bottlenecks**

For identified bottleneck resources, implement a lookahead sequencing optimizer (genetic algorithm or constraint programming) that minimizes total setup time for the next N jobs in the queue while respecting due-date windows. Use the setup-time matrix mined from history to define ôsimilarity classes.ö Allow limited preemption or insertion of urgent jobs with a calculated setup penalty.

Process mining provides the similarity classes and realistic time windows. This strategy is applied selectively only at high-impact bottlenecks. Expected impact: 20û35% reduction in setup time at bottlenecks, increased effective capacity, smoother flow, and lower overall lead times.

**5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement**

**Discrete-Event Simulation (DES) Validation:**

I would build a detailed DES model (using AnyLogic, Simio, or Python SimPy) parameterized directly from process mining results:
- Probabilistic routings and job-type mix.
- Empirical processing, setup, and transport time distributions.
- Machine breakdown and repair distributions.
- Arrival patterns of new jobs and urgent hot jobs.

Three scenarios would be tested:
1. Baseline (current dispatching rules) under normal load.
2. High-load period (20% increased order intake).
3. High-disruption scenario (increased breakdown frequency).

Performance measures (mean tardiness, % tardy, average WIP, machine utilization, lead-time variance) would be compared across the three proposed strategies using rigorous statistical experiments (replications, confidence intervals, ANOVA). Sensitivity analysis on key parameters would identify robust strategies.

**Continuous Monitoring Framework:**

Implement a live process mining dashboard that:
- Continuously computes the KPIs defined in Section 1 on a rolling 30-day window.
- Monitors process drift using concept drift detection techniques (e.g., on routing probabilities, setup-time distributions, or tardiness).
- Automatically triggers alerts when tardiness exceeds control limits or when new bottleneck patterns emerge.
- Feeds updated distributions back into the predictive models and dispatching weight optimizers on a weekly or monthly basis.

This creates a closed-loop ôProcess Mining  Scheduling Policy Optimization  Execution  Miningö system that continuously adapts to changing product mix, process variability, and resource conditions.

The overall approach transforms Precision Parts Inc. from reactive, rule-based scheduling to a data-driven, predictive, and adaptive scheduling system, directly addressing the root causes of high tardiness, excessive WIP, and unpredictable lead times while maximizing utilization of critical resources.
