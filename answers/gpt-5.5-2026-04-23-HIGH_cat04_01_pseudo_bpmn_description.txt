The pseudo-BPMN describes a customer request handling process in which incoming requests are classified as either **standard** or **custom**, processed through different validation or feasibility activities, optionally submitted for managerial approval, and then finalized through invoicing and customer confirmation.

---

## 1. Overall Process Summary

The process begins when a customer submits a request. The organization first identifies whether the request is a **standard request** or a **custom request**.

- **Standard requests** follow a more routine validation path involving credit and inventory checks.
- **Custom requests** require feasibility analysis to determine whether the organization can fulfill the requested customization.

If the request can proceed, the process then checks whether managerial approval is required. If approval is granted, or if no approval is needed, the final invoice is generated and a confirmation is sent to the customer. If approval is denied, the request is re-evaluated and may loop back for rework.

---

## 2. Step-by-Step Explanation

### Start Event

The process starts when a customer request is received.

---

## 3. Task A: Receive Customer Request

**Purpose:**  
This task captures the customer’s request and initiates the process.

Typical information gathered may include:

- Customer identity
- Requested product or service
- Quantity
- Delivery expectations
- Customization requirements
- Commercial terms
- Supporting documents or specifications

After the request is received, the process moves to a decision point to determine the request type.

---

## 4. XOR Gateway: Check Request Type

This is an **exclusive decision gateway**, meaning only one outgoing path is selected.

The gateway checks whether the request is:

1. **Standard**
2. **Custom**

Only one of these paths can be followed for a given request.

---

# Standard Request Path

## 5. Task B1: Perform Standard Validation

**Purpose:**  
For a standard request, the organization performs routine validation to confirm that the request can be processed under normal operating rules.

This may include checking:

- Whether the requested product or service is part of the standard catalog
- Whether pricing rules are already defined
- Whether terms and conditions are acceptable
- Whether the customer request matches standard fulfillment procedures

Once standard validation is complete, the process launches parallel checks.

---

## 6. AND Gateway: Run Parallel Checks

This is a **parallel gateway**, meaning multiple tasks are started at the same time.

In this case, two checks are performed in parallel:

1. Credit Check
2. Inventory Check

The purpose of running them in parallel is to reduce overall processing time, since both checks are independent and can happen simultaneously.

---

## 7. Task C1: Credit Check

**Purpose:**  
This task verifies whether the customer is financially eligible for the request.

It may involve checking:

- Credit limit
- Outstanding payments
- Payment history
- Risk category
- Required prepayment or deposit

---

## 8. Task C2: Inventory Check

**Purpose:**  
This task confirms whether the requested item or service capacity is available.

It may involve checking:

- Stock levels
- Warehouse availability
- Production capacity
- Reserved inventory
- Expected replenishment dates

---

## 9. AND Join: All Parallel Checks Completed

After the credit check and inventory check are launched in parallel, the process waits until **both** are completed.

This is the joining behavior of an AND gateway.

The process cannot continue to the next step until:

- The credit check is complete, and
- The inventory check is complete

Once both are finished, the process proceeds to delivery date calculation.

---

## 10. Task D: Calculate Delivery Date

**Purpose:**  
This task determines when the organization can deliver the requested product or service.

The delivery date may depend on:

- Inventory availability
- Shipping time
- Production lead time
- Customer location
- Internal scheduling constraints
- Results of the credit and inventory checks

After the delivery date is calculated, the standard request path is considered ready to converge with the successful custom request path.

---

# Custom Request Path

## 11. Task B2: Perform Custom Feasibility Analysis

**Purpose:**  
For a custom request, the organization must determine whether the requested customization is possible.

This may involve reviewing:

- Technical feasibility
- Engineering effort
- Special materials or components
- Production capability
- Legal or compliance constraints
- Cost implications
- Delivery impact

Custom requests require more analysis than standard requests because they are not covered by routine catalog or fulfillment rules.

---

## 12. XOR Gateway: Is Customization Feasible?

This is another **exclusive gateway**. It determines whether the custom request can proceed.

There are two possible outcomes:

1. **Yes, customization is feasible**
2. **No, customization is not feasible**

Only one path is taken.

---

## 13. If Feasible: Task E1 — Prepare Custom Quotation

**Purpose:**  
If the customization is feasible, the organization prepares a quotation specifically for the customer.

The custom quotation may include:

- Custom pricing
- Additional labor or engineering costs
- Special material costs
- Delivery timeline
- Assumptions and exclusions
- Terms and conditions
- Validity period of the quote

After the custom quotation is prepared, the request can proceed to the common approval decision step.

---

## 14. If Not Feasible: Task E2 — Send Rejection Notice

**Purpose:**  
If the customization is not feasible, the organization informs the customer that the request cannot be fulfilled.

The rejection notice may explain:

- Why the request cannot be completed
- Whether alternatives are available
- Whether the customer may revise the request
- Contact information for follow-up

After the rejection notice is sent, the process ends.

This path does **not** continue to invoicing or confirmation because the request has been rejected.

---

# Convergence of Standard and Custom Paths

## 15. After Standard or Custom Path Tasks Completed

The process converges after either:

- The **standard path** completes Task D: “Calculate Delivery Date”, or
- The **custom path** completes Task E1: “Prepare Custom Quotation”

At this point, the request is considered commercially or operationally ready for the next decision: whether approval is required.

The custom rejection path, Task E2, does not converge here because it ends the process.

In BPMN terms, this convergence could be represented by an **exclusive merge gateway**, because only one of the two paths — standard or custom — will arrive there for a given request.

---

# Approval Decision

## 16. XOR Gateway: Is Approval Needed?

This exclusive gateway determines whether the request must be reviewed by a manager before final invoicing.

There are two possible outcomes:

1. **Approval is needed**
2. **No approval is needed**

Only one path is followed.

Approval may be required based on business rules such as:

- High order value
- Large discounts
- Non-standard commercial terms
- Custom engineering work
- Customer credit risk
- Delivery date exceptions
- Strategic customer handling
- Margin below threshold
- Manual override requirements

---

## 17. If Approval Is Needed: Task F — Obtain Manager Approval

**Purpose:**  
A manager reviews the request before it can proceed.

The manager may evaluate:

- Pricing
- Risk
- Profitability
- Delivery feasibility
- Credit concerns
- Contractual terms
- Exceptions to normal policy

After manager review, the process moves to another decision gateway.

---

## 18. XOR Gateway: Is Approval Granted?

This exclusive gateway determines the result of the manager’s review.

There are two outcomes:

1. **Approval granted**
2. **Approval not granted**

---

## 19. If Approval Granted: Task G — Generate Final Invoice

If the manager approves the request, the process proceeds to final invoicing.

---

## 20. If Approval Not Granted: Task H — Re-evaluate Conditions

**Purpose:**  
If the manager does not approve the request, the organization must rework or reconsider the request.

This may involve:

- Adjusting pricing
- Changing delivery terms
- Revising the quotation
- Reducing discounts
- Rechecking inventory
- Modifying the requested customization
- Clarifying customer requirements
- Updating feasibility assumptions
- Negotiating alternative terms

After re-evaluation, the process loops back to the appropriate earlier task depending on the original request type.

---

# Loop-Back Logic

## 21. Loop Back to Task E1 or Task D

If approval is denied, the process does not immediately end. Instead, it loops back for rework.

The loop-back depends on the request type:

### For a Custom Request

The process loops back to:

**Task E1: Prepare Custom Quotation**

This means the custom quotation may need to be revised, for example by changing price, scope, delivery timeline, or assumptions.

### For a Standard Request

The process loops back to:

**Task D: Calculate Delivery Date**

This means the standard request conditions may need to be revised, especially if the rejection was related to delivery timing, availability, pricing assumptions, or operational constraints.

After the relevant rework task is completed, the request again reaches the approval decision.

This loop may repeat until:

- Approval is granted, or
- Approval is no longer needed, or
- The organization decides to cancel or reject the request.

The pseudo-BPMN does not explicitly show a cancellation path after repeated rejection, but in a real process one might include one.

---

# No Approval Needed Path

## 22. If No Approval Needed: Task G — Generate Final Invoice

If the request does not require approval, the process skips manager review and goes directly to invoice generation.

This means the request meets all predefined business rules and is allowed to proceed automatically.

---

## 23. Task G: Generate Final Invoice

**Purpose:**  
This task produces the official invoice for the customer.

The invoice may include:

- Products or services ordered
- Quantity
- Unit prices
- Discounts
- Taxes
- Delivery charges
- Payment terms
- Total amount due

Task G can be reached in two ways:

1. Directly, if no approval is needed
2. After manager approval is granted

---

## 24. Task I: Send Confirmation to Customer

**Purpose:**  
After the final invoice is generated, the customer receives confirmation.

The confirmation may include:

- Order confirmation
- Invoice details
- Delivery date
- Payment instructions
- Contact information
- Summary of agreed terms

This step formally communicates that the request has been accepted and processed.

---

## 25. End Event

The process ends after the confirmation is sent to the customer.

There is also an earlier end event if a custom request is found to be infeasible and a rejection notice is sent.

---

# Meaning of the Gateways

## XOR Gateways

An XOR gateway represents an **exclusive choice**. Only one path can be taken.

In this process, XOR gateways are used for:

1. Checking whether the request is standard or custom
2. Checking whether customization is feasible
3. Checking whether approval is needed
4. Checking whether approval is granted

Each of these decisions results in a single selected path.

---

## AND Gateway

An AND gateway represents **parallel execution**.

In this process, it is used to run:

- Credit Check
- Inventory Check

Both tasks are started together, and both must finish before the process can continue.

---

# Key Difference Between Standard and Custom Requests

| Area | Standard Request | Custom Request |
|---|---|---|
| Initial handling | Standard validation | Custom feasibility analysis |
| Complexity | Lower | Higher |
| Main concern | Confirm routine criteria | Determine whether request can be fulfilled |
| Checks | Credit and inventory checks | Feasibility review |
| Output before approval | Delivery date | Custom quotation |
| Possible early rejection | Not shown explicitly | Yes, if customization is infeasible |
| Rework loop | Back to delivery date calculation | Back to custom quotation preparation |

---

# When Approvals Are Triggered

Approval is triggered when the process determines that approval is needed.

This may happen when the request contains exceptions or risk factors, such as:

- High financial value
- Custom pricing
- Special discounts
- Customer credit issues
- Non-standard delivery commitment
- Low margin
- Special contractual obligations
- Unusual customization
- Policy exceptions

If approval is not needed, the process goes directly to invoice generation.

---

# When Rework Is Triggered

Rework is triggered when:

1. Approval is required, and
2. The manager does not grant approval.

In that case, the process goes to:

**Task H: Re-evaluate Conditions**

Then it loops back to either:

- **Task D** for standard requests, or
- **Task E1** for custom requests.

The purpose of the rework loop is to modify the request conditions so they can potentially meet approval requirements on a later review.

---

# Final Interpretation

The process is designed to handle both routine and non-routine customer requests in a controlled way.

- Standard requests are validated through predefined checks.
- Custom requests require feasibility analysis and may be rejected if they cannot be fulfilled.
- Successful requests from both paths converge before the approval decision.
- Some requests require manager approval based on risk, value, or exceptions.
- If approval is denied, the process loops back for rework.
- Once the request is approved or determined not to need approval, the invoice is generated and confirmation is sent to the customer.
