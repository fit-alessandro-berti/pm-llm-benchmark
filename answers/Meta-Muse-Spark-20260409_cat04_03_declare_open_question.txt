This DECLARE model is essentially a formal specification of a loan approval workflow. DECLARE uses constraints to define what *must*, *must not*, or *must follow* other activities in a process. With `support: 1.0, confidence: 1.0` everywhere, this model says every rule is mandatory with full certainty. Let's break down what it's actually enforcing in the real world.

### What each activity represents in practice

| Activity | Real-life meaning | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Receive_Application (A)** | Customer submits loan request + basic info | This is the legal and operational trigger. Nothing starts without it |
| **Preliminary_Credit_Check (B)** | Pull credit report, score, debt-to-income screening | First financial gate. Prevents wasting time on unqualified applicants |
| **Gather_Additional_Documents (C)** | Bank requests pay stubs, tax returns, ID, etc | Due diligence + KYC/AML compliance. Regulators require proof of income/identity |
| **Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package (D)** | Underwriting creates term sheet: rate, amount, fees | This is the actual product being sold to the customer |
| **Quality_Assurance_Review (E)** | Second-line check: did we follow policy, is data correct | Catches errors before legal commitment. Required by SOX, CFPB, Basel |
| **Authorize_Contract_Terms (F)** | Manager/lender signs off on final contract | Legal binding step. Establishes accountability |
| **Transfer_Funds (G)** | ACH/wire hits customer account | The bank is now financially exposed |
| **Notify_Customer (H)** | Email/call/portal update with decision or next steps | Customer experience + Truth in Lending Act disclosures |
| **Proceed_Without_Compliance (Z)** | Rogue path: skip checks, ignore policy | Explicitly forbidden. Represents fraud/operational risk |

### How the constraints shape the real-life process

DECLARE constraints come in 4 flavors: existence, relation, negative relation, and choice. Here's what your model enforces:

**1. Lifecycle boundaries**
- `init: Receive_Application` : Every case *must* start with an application. You can't run credit checks on ghosts. This prevents orphaned workflows and ensures audit trail starts at customer request.
- `absence: Proceed_Without_Compliance` : Z can never occur. This is a hard compliance gate baked into the model. If anyone tries, the process instance becomes invalid.

**2. Cardinality and uniqueness**  
- `exactly_one: Preliminary_Credit_Check` : Must happen once, no more, no less. In practice: you can't approve without a check, but running it twice wastes money on credit pulls and could violate FCRA if done without purpose. One and done.

**3. Mandatory existence**
- `existence: Receive_Application, Gather_Additional_Documents` : These must occur in every successful loan. You cannot fund a loan without docs. This encodes "no doc" and "low doc" loans out of policy.

**4. Sequencing and dependencies**

This is where it gets strict. Real implications:

| Constraint | Rule | Real-world impact |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `response: A → B` | If application received, credit check must follow eventually | Ensures no application sits idle. SLAs for initial review |
| `succession: B → C` | After credit check, you must gather docs. And C cannot happen unless B happened first | No docs requested for people who fail initial screen. Saves customer hassle |
| `precedence: E → F` | Can only authorize terms if QA review already happened | Four-eyes principle. Manager can't sign off on un-reviewed offer |
| `responded_existence: D → E` | If you assemble an offer, QA must exist somewhere in the trace | Offer can't go out without QA, but QA doesn't have to be immediate |
| `coexistence: C ↔ F` | Docs and authorization either both happen or neither does | If you collected docs, you must make a decision. Prevents "document graveyards" where customer submits info and hears nothing |
| `chainsuccession: E → D` | QA and Assemble must be directly adjacent, E then D | After QA passes, you must immediately build the offer. No gap where data goes stale |

**5. Negative constraints = guardrails**
- `noncoexistence: G, A` : Transfer_Funds and Receive_Application cannot both occur. This looks odd, but in DECLARE it means "if G happens, A cannot happen in the same trace" and vice versa. Practically: you cannot fund before an application exists. This blocks disbursement errors.
- `nonsuccession: H, B` : Notify_Customer cannot be followed by Preliminary_Credit_Check. You can't tell the customer something, then go back and run credit. Prevents re-underwriting after decision.
- `nonchainsuccession: F, H` : Authorize_Contract_Terms cannot be immediately followed by Notify_Customer. Forces a step in between - likely QA or Transfer - so you don't announce terms before they're final.

**6. Alternating and chaining rules for the tail end**
- `altresponse: G → H` : Each funds transfer must be followed by a customer notice before another transfer can happen. Prevents batch disbursements without per-disbursement communication.
- `altprecedence: H → G` : A customer notice must be preceded by a transfer, and you can't have two notices without a transfer in between. Together with altresponse, this enforces strict G-H-G-H alternation.
- `chainresponse: D → G` : After assembling an offer, the next event must be Transfer_Funds. This is very aggressive - it means no QA, no authorization between offer and funding. This likely conflicts with other rules and would make the model unsatisfiable. In practice you'd want E and F between D and G.

### How this maintains control and transparency

1. **Compliance by construction**: `absence: Z` + `exactly_one: B` + `existence: C` directly encode Reg B, ECOA, and AML requirements. The process can't complete if you skip them. Auditors can verify the model instead of sampling cases.
2. **Quality gates**: `precedence: E → F` and `chainsuccession: E → D` force QA into the critical path. Nothing gets to the customer without review. This reduces operational risk and buyback risk on mortgages.
3. **Auditability**: Because relations like `response` and `succession` are declared, every trace has to explain why B followed A. You get a full causal chain for regulators. "Why did we pull credit?" -> "Because A happened."
4. **Customer fairness**: `coexistence: C ↔ F` prevents taking documents and ghosting. `altresponse: G → H` ensures every disbursement is communicated. This aligns with UDAAP and Truth in Lending.

### Why a business would make it this stringent

**Regulatory drivers**
- **CFPB/Reg B & Z**: Adverse action notices, timing of disclosures, documented underwriting.
- **AML/KYC**: `Gather_Additional_Documents` is non-negotiable.
- **SOX/Model Risk**: `Quality_Assurance_Review` + `Authorize_Contract_Terms` gives separation of duties.
- **State licensing**: Many states require explicit approval before funding.

**Business goals**
1. **Reduce credit losses**: `exactly_one: Preliminary_Credit_Check` + `succession: B → C` ensures you screen early and only collect docs from viable applicants.
2. **Prevent fraud**: `absence: Proceed_Without_Compliance` and `noncoexistence: G, A` block the two biggest fraud vectors - skipping checks and funding fake applications.
3. **Operational efficiency**: `chainresponse` and `chainsuccession` remove wait states. Once QA passes, offer is assembled immediately. No rework.
4. **Litigation defense**: If sued, the bank can point to the DECLARE model and show that the violating path was mathematically impossible.

### Potential issues and tensions in this specific model

This model is *very* strict, possibly too strict. A few conflicts:

1. `chainresponse: D → G` says Assemble_Offer must be directly followed by Transfer_Funds. But `precedence: E → F` and `responded_existence: D → E` require QA and Authorization between D and G. These constraints together may make the model unsatisfiable - no trace can satisfy all rules.
2. `chainprecedence: F → B` says Authorize can only happen if preceded by Credit_Check. But with `succession: B → C` and `coexistence: C ↔ F`, the path is likely B → C → ... → F, which is fine. But if any other activity slips between B and F, chainprecedence fails.
3. `noncoexistence: G, A` as written would forbid any trace where both funding and application occur. That kills all successful loans. Likely intended as `nonchainsuccession` or the model needs a different interpretation.

In real deployments, you'd validate this model with a DECLARE checker or LTL simulation to catch conflicts before going to production.
