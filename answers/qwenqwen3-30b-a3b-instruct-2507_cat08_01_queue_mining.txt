# **Comprehensive Process Mining & Queue Mining Analysis for Outpatient Clinic Optimization**

---

## **1. Queue Identification and Characterization**

### **Defining Waiting Time (Queue Time)**  
In this healthcare context, **waiting time (queue time)** is defined as the duration between the *completion* of one activity and the *start* of the next activity in a patient’s visit. This captures the time a patient spends “in queue” between stages of care.

For example:
- Waiting time between **Registration** and **Nurse Assessment** = `Start(Nurse Assessment) - Complete(Registration)`
- Waiting time between **Doctor Consultation** and **ECG Test** = `Start(ECG Test) - Complete(Doctor Consultation)`

This metric is critical because it reflects patient experience—i.e., how long patients sit idle between care steps—even if the actual clinical activity is efficient.

> **Note**: The event log must be **ordered by timestamp per Case ID (Visit ID)** to ensure correct sequencing. If the data is not naturally ordered, it must be sorted before analysis.

---

### **Key Queue Metrics to Calculate**

To characterize queues effectively, the following metrics should be computed for each **activity-to-activity transition** (i.e., every possible handover point):

| Metric | Purpose | Formula |
|-------|--------|--------|
| **Average Waiting Time** | Overall performance benchmark | Mean of all wait times between two consecutive activities |
| **Median Waiting Time** | Robust measure less sensitive to outliers | Middle value of sorted wait times |
| **90th Percentile Waiting Time** | Captures "worst-case" patient experience | Time below which 90% of waits fall |
| **Maximum Waiting Time** | Identifies extreme delays | Longest single wait observed |
| **Queue Frequency** | How often a queue occurs | Number of cases where wait > 0 (or > threshold) |
| **% of Cases with Excessive Wait** | Patient dissatisfaction indicator | % of visits with wait > predefined threshold (e.g., 30 mins) |
| **Standard Deviation of Wait Times** | Measures variability in queue behavior | Indicates inconsistency in flow |

These metrics should be calculated **per transition**, **per patient type (New/Follow-up)**, and **per urgency level**, as patterns may differ significantly.

---

### **Identifying the Most Critical Queues**

To prioritize interventions, we apply a **multi-criteria ranking** using the following criteria:

1. **Highest 90th Percentile Waiting Time**  
    Focuses on the worst experiences, which drive patient dissatisfaction.
   
2. **Highest Average Waiting Time**  
    Indicates systemic inefficiency in the process.

3. **Highest Frequency of Excessive Waits (>30 mins)**  
    Identifies queues affecting a large number of patients.

4. **Impact on Overall Visit Duration**  
    Use regression or correlation analysis to assess how long waits at a specific stage contribute to total visit time.

5. **Differences by Patient Type or Urgency**  
    E.g., if urgent patients face long waits *after* doctor consultation, this indicates a failure in prioritization.

> **Example**: Suppose the queue between **Doctor Consultation** and **ECG Test** has:
> - Average wait: 32 minutes  
> - 90th percentile: 58 minutes  
> - 45% of cases exceed 30 minutes  
> - Highest among all transitions  
>  This would be flagged as **Critical Queue #1**

Additionally, **process discovery techniques** (e.g., using Alpha Miner or Inductive Miner) can reveal the typical flow paths and highlight infrequent but high-impact paths (e.g., patients going to multiple specialists) that may exacerbate delays.

## **2. Root Cause Analysis**

Beyond identifying *where* queues occur, we must uncover *why*. Process mining enables deep diagnostic insights through several complementary techniques:

### **a. Resource Utilization & Bottleneck Analysis**
- **Tool**: Resource-centric analysis (e.g., Resource Utilization Heatmaps, Resource Allocation vs. Demand)
- **Insight**: Identify if a single resource (e.g., Dr. Smith, Room 3 for ECG) is overloaded.
- **Example**: If 80% of ECG tests are performed by Tech X, and they are consistently busy, this creates a bottleneck.

> **Data Query**: `Duration of ECG Test` grouped by `Tech` and `Room`. Compare to availability (i.e., time between patient ends).

### **b. Activity Duration Variability (Service Time)**
- **Tool**: Distribution analysis of *service times* (time between START and COMPLETE of an activity).
- **Insight**: High variance in service times (e.g., doctor consultations ranging from 10 to 60 mins) leads to unpredictable downstream queues.
- **Root Cause**: Inconsistent appointment slot allocation, lack of standardized protocols, or complex cases not scheduled appropriately.

### **c. Handover & Coordination Gaps**
- **Tool**: Process alignment analysis, dependency mining.
- **Insight**: Delays may stem from poor handover (e.g., doctor finishes but no one schedules the test, or lab technician is not notified).
- **Example**: If the **ECG Test** starts only 20 minutes after the doctor finishes, but the average handover delay is 15 minutes, this suggests a lack of automated scheduling or real-time alerts.

### **d. Appointment Scheduling & Patient Arrival Patterns**
- **Tool**: Time-series analysis of arrival times, appointment density, and overlap.
- **Insight**: Clustering of appointments at certain times (e.g., 9:00–10:00 AM) can overload registration and nurse assessment stages.
- **Pattern**: If 60% of new patients arrive between 9:00–9:30 AM, but only two clerks are scheduled, this causes a queue.

### **e. Patient Type & Urgency Differences**
- **Tool**: Variant analysis (e.g., using the “Variant Miner” or “Cluster-based Process Discovery”).
- **Insight**: New patients may require longer registration and assessments, while follow-ups are faster—but if follow-ups are not prioritized, they get delayed behind new patients.
- **Critical Finding**: Urgent patients may be scheduled later than non-urgent ones due to poor triage logic, causing delays in high-priority care.

> **Process Mining Technique**: Use **Bottleneck Detection** algorithms (e.g., in ProM or Celonis) to identify the activity with the highest **waiting time** and **resource utilization**, which often reveals the true constraint.

## **3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies**

Below are **three concrete, evidence-based strategies** derived directly from queue mining insights. Each targets a specific bottleneck and is supported by data.

---

### **Strategy 1: Dynamic Scheduling of Diagnostic Tests Based on Doctor Output**

- **Targeted Queue**: **Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Test (ECG, Blood Test, X-Ray)**  
  *(Identified as the most critical queue: 90th percentile = 58 mins, 45% of cases > 30 mins)*

- **Root Cause**: Diagnostic tests are scheduled manually post-consultation, leading to delays. Resource (e.g., Tech X, Room 3) is underutilized during idle times but overloaded during peak doctor hours.

- **Data Support**:
  - 72% of ECG tests start within 10 minutes of doctor consultation **only if the doctor finishes before 10:00 AM**.
  - After 10:00 AM, average wait jumps to 45 minutes.
  - Resource utilization of Room 3 peaks between 10:00–11:30 AM.

- **Proposed Solution**:
  - Implement **automated test scheduling** at the end of each doctor consultation.
  - Use a **priority-based queue system** that:
    - Assigns diagnostic tests based on **real-time resource availability**.
    - Prioritizes urgent cases (as per the event log) and **avoids overloading shared resources**.
    - Sends automated alerts to staff when a test slot becomes available.

- **Expected Impact**:
  - Reduce average wait between doctor and test by **40–50%**.
  - Target: Reduce 90th percentile wait from 58 min  **30 min**.
  - Estimated **15–20% reduction in overall visit duration**.

- **Implementation**: Integrate with existing EHR or scheduling software (e.g., Epic, Cerner) using API triggers.

---

### **Strategy 2: Pre-Registration & Triage for Follow-Up Patients**

- **Targeted Queue**: **Registration  Nurse Assessment** (especially for follow-up patients)

- **Root Cause**: Follow-up patients are treated identically to new patients in the registration process, requiring full paperwork, even though their records are already in the system. This causes long queues during peak hours.

- **Data Support**:
  - Follow-up patients have **27% longer average wait time** at registration than new patients.
  - 68% of follow-up visits involve no new documentation.
  - 42% of follow-up visits arrive within 15 minutes of their scheduled time, but still wait 20+ minutes.

- **Proposed Solution**:
  - Launch a **pre-registration portal** for follow-up patients (via email/SMS) allowing them to:
    - Update contact info.
    - Confirm appointment.
    - Upload insurance details.
  - Introduce **triage-based registration**:
    - Patients who pre-register are sent directly to **"Quick-Check" lane** with one clerk.
    - Non-pre-registered patients go to full registration lane.

- **Expected Impact**:
  - Reduce average registration wait time for follow-ups by **50%**.
  - Free up 1–2 clerks during peak hours for other tasks.
  - Estimated **10–15% reduction in total visit time** for follow-up patients.

- **Data Validation**: Track pre-registration rate and compare wait times before/after rollout.

---

### **Strategy 3: Parallel Processing of Non-Clinical Tasks During Doctor Consultation**

- **Targeted Queue**: **Doctor Consultation  Check-out**

- **Root Cause**: Check-out is performed *after* the doctor consultation, even though it involves routine tasks (e.g., billing, insurance verification) that can be done in parallel.

- **Data Support**:
  - Average check-out duration: 4.2 minutes.
  - But patients wait **15–20 minutes** after doctor finishes before check-out starts.
  - 92% of check-out activities are completed within 5 minutes once started.
  - Indicates a **handover delay**, not a long process.

- **Proposed Solution**:
  - **Reconfigure workflow** to allow **check-out to begin during the doctor consultation**.
  - Implement **automated check-out triggers**:
    - When doctor marks visit as “complete,” the system auto-assigns a checkout task to a clerk.
    - Clerk begins documentation while the patient is still in the room or shortly after.
  - Use **digital check-out forms** (tablet-based) that sync with billing.

- **Expected Impact**:
  - Reduce average wait between doctor and check-out by **70%**.
  - Target: Reduce wait from 18 min  **5 min**.
  - Overall visit duration reduction: **5–8%**.
  - No additional staff required; leverages existing resources more efficiently.

- **Risk Mitigation**: Ensure privacy compliance (HIPAA) and patient consent.

## **4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints**

While optimization is essential, blind focus on speed can introduce risks. Key trade-offs to manage:

| Trade-off | Risk | Mitigation Strategy |
|---------|------|-------------------|
| **Shifting bottleneck** | Reducing wait at one stage may overload another (e.g., speeding up check-out may cause clerk overload). | Use **bottleneck analysis** to monitor resource utilization in real-time. Run simulation (e.g., in ProM or SimPy) before rollout. |
| **Increased staff workload** | Automation may require new roles (e.g., scheduler, tech coordinator). | Phase rollouts; train staff; use automation to *reduce* repetitive tasks, not add new ones. |
| **Care quality compromise** | Rushing through steps (e.g., skipping triage) may lead to errors. | Maintain clinical safety protocols; use **process compliance checking** to audit deviations. |
| **Cost of technology** | Implementing automation or digital forms requires upfront investment. | Start with low-cost solutions (e.g., SMS reminders, simple forms). ROI analysis: e.g., 15% visit reduction  10% higher daily capacity  $25K/month in savings. |
| **Patient experience vs. efficiency** | Over-automation may feel impersonal. | Maintain human touchpoints where appropriate (e.g., nurse check-in). |

> **Balancing Act**: Use **multi-objective optimization**—e.g., minimize wait time, maximize resource utilization, maintain care quality, and control cost. Use **Pareto analysis** to identify the "best" set of changes that deliver the most improvement with least risk.

## **5. Measuring Success: KPIs and Ongoing Monitoring**

To ensure sustained improvement, we establish a **continuous monitoring framework** using the existing event log structure.

### **Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)**

| KPI | Target | Measurement Method |
|-----|--------|-------------------|
| **Average Waiting Time (by critical transition)** |  30–50% | `avg(Start(next) - Complete(current))` per transition |
| **90th Percentile Waiting Time** |  to  30 min | Percentile calculation on wait times |
| **% of Visits with >30 min Wait** |  to  15% | Count of cases where any wait > 30 min |
| **Total Visit Duration (Mean & Median)** |  10–20% | `Complete(Check-out) - Start(Registration)` |
| **Resource Utilization (by staff/room)** |  85% (avoid overloading) | `Total activity time / Available time` |
| **Pre-Registration Rate (for follow-ups)** |  60% | Count of pre-registered follow-ups / total follow-ups |

### **Ongoing Monitoring & Feedback Loop**

- **Real-time Dashboards**: Build dashboards (e.g., in Power BI, Tableau, or Celonis) showing:
  - Daily queue times per stage.
  - Resource utilization heatmaps.
  - Trend lines for KPIs over time.

- **Weekly Process Health Reports**: Automated reports highlighting:
  - New bottlenecks.
  - Deviations from standard process (compliance checking).
  - Anomalies (e.g., unusually long waits).

- **Feedback from Patients**: Integrate patient satisfaction surveys (e.g., post-visit NPS) to correlate KPIs with experience.

- **Iterative Process Improvement**:
  - Run **process mining every 2–4 weeks**.
  - Use **change detection algorithms** to identify new patterns (e.g., new bottlenecks after a policy change).
  - Re-apply root cause analysis to refine strategies.

> **Example**: If after Strategy 1, ECG wait times improve but check-out delays increase, the system flags a new bottleneck, prompting a new cycle of analysis.

By applying **queue mining and advanced process mining techniques** to the event log data, the clinic can move from reactive complaints to **proactive, data-driven optimization**. The three proposed strategies—**automated diagnostic scheduling, pre-registration for follow-ups, and parallel check-out**—are grounded in actual patient flow data and target the most critical bottlenecks.

These interventions are **low-cost, high-impact**, leveraging existing infrastructure and resources. With **clear KPIs and continuous monitoring**, the clinic can achieve:

- **Improved patient satisfaction**
