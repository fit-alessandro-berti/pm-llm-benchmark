# Queue Mining Analysis & Optimization Plan for Outpatient Clinic Patient Flow

---

## 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

### 1.1 Defining and Calculating Waiting Time

The event log contains paired START/COMPLETE timestamps per activity per case. This is exactly what we need to decompose total visit time into **service time** and **waiting time**:

- **Service time (processing time)** for activity *A* in case *c*:
  `ServiceTime(A, c) = COMPLETE(A, c)  START(A, c)`

- **Waiting time (queue time)** between consecutive activities *A*  *B* in case *c*:
  `WaitTime(AB, c) = START(B, c)  COMPLETE(A, c)`

**Operational definition of "waiting time":** the elapsed time during which the patient has finished one process step and is *eligible* for the next one, but the next activity has not yet started — i.e., the patient is enqueued for a resource (clerk, nurse, doctor, room, or device). Each directly-follows relation in the discovered process model (e.g., *Registration  Nurse Assessment*, *Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation*, *Doctor Consultation  ECG*, *ECG  Check-out*) therefore defines a distinct **queue**.

**Important refinements:**

- **Queues are per transition AND per resource pool.** The wait before "Doctor Consultation (Cardio)" is a different queue than before "Doctor Consultation (Ortho)" — they have different servers. I would segment queues by activity-pair *and* by specialty/resource class.
- **Multi-tasking / parallelism check:** If activities can run in parallel (e.g., blood test ordered while waiting for the doctor), the directly-follows wait may be misleading. I would use the process model (discovered via Inductive Miner or similar) to identify true sequential dependencies and compute *synchronization waits* (waiting for the last of several parallel predecessors).
- **Distinguish patient-waiting from intentional delays:** e.g., a mandated 15-minute observation after a procedure is not "waste." Domain validation with clinical staff is needed to flag value-adding vs. non-value-adding waits.
- **Arrival-to-first-activity wait:** If a check-in/arrival timestamp or scheduled appointment time exists, the wait *before Registration* is also a queue (often substantial). If only the log exists, START of Registration is the first observable event, so this queue is invisible — I would flag this as a data gap to close (e.g., kiosk check-in timestamps).

### 1.2 Key Queue Characterization Metrics

For each queue (activity transition × resource pool × segment):

| Metric | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Mean wait time** | Overall burden; feeds Little's Law analysis |
| **Median wait time** | Robust to outliers; "typical" experience |
| **90th / 95th percentile wait** | Tail experience — drives complaints and dissatisfaction far more than the average |
| **Max wait** | Detects extreme failures / data quality issues |
| **Standard deviation / CV of wait** | High variability  unpredictability, which patients perceive worse than long-but-predictable waits |
| **Queue frequency (throughput)** | # cases traversing this transition per day/week |
| **Total accumulated wait** = mean wait × frequency | The true system-level cost of the queue |
| **% of cases exceeding a threshold** (e.g., >20 min) | Service-level metric, easy to communicate |
| **Queue length over time L(t)** | # patients simultaneously waiting at each moment (computable by counting cases between COMPLETE of predecessor and START of successor at time *t*) — reveals temporal congestion |
| **Wait as % of total visit time** | Identifies where flow efficiency is lost; clinics often show <30% value-added time |
| **Resource utilization ** of the downstream server | Links the queue to its server: busy time / available time per resource |

I would also compute the **flow efficiency per case**:  service times / total visit duration, and report its distribution.

### 1.3 Identifying the Most Critical Queues

Ranking by *average wait alone* is misleading — a rarely traversed transition with a long wait may matter less than a moderate wait experienced by everyone. I would use a **multi-criteria prioritization**:

1. **Total impact = mean (or median) wait × frequency.** This identifies queues contributing most aggregate patient-waiting hours. Typically the *Nurse  Doctor* queue dominates in clinics.
2. **Tail severity:** queues with high P90/P95, since dissatisfaction is driven by worst experiences. A queue with median 8 min but P90 of 55 min signals a variability/scheduling problem.
3. **Critical-path position:** waits on the longest path of the visit directly extend total visit duration; waits on parallel branches may be absorbed.
4. **Equity / segment impact:** stratify by Urgency and Patient Type. If *Urgent* patients wait long in any queue, that's a clinical-risk red flag and gets top priority regardless of aggregate volume.
5. **Trend and time-of-day concentration:** queues that are exploding at specific hours (visible via dotted charts / queue-length-over-time plots) are tractable through scheduling fixes — high leverage.

**Deliverable:** a Pareto chart of total waiting hours by queue, overlaid with P90 values and urgency-stratified views, validated in a workshop with clinic staff to filter out artifacts (e.g., timestamps recorded late by staff rather than true waits).

## 2. Root Cause Analysis

Knowing *where* patients wait is necessary but not sufficient. For each critical queue, I would investigate the following causal hypotheses, each testable with the event log:

### 2.1 Candidate Root Causes and How to Diagnose Them

**(a) Resource bottlenecks (insufficient or misallocated capacity)**
- *Analysis:* Build resource utilization profiles per staff member/room/device per hour of day, from their START/COMPLETE intervals. Compare utilization  of the downstream resource pool against queue length over time. If  approaches 1 during peak hours and the queue grows precisely then, capacity (or its scheduling) is the cause.
- *Queue-mining lens:* Fit the queue to an approximate G/G/c model per hour band; check whether wait times explode nonlinearly as   1 (classic Kingman behavior). This also tells us how much capacity relief is needed.

**(b) Mismatch between arrival pattern and capacity schedule**
- *Analysis:* **Dotted chart** of arrivals (first event per case) by time of day / day of week, overlaid with rostered capacity. Outpatient clinics typically show morning arrival surges (everyone booked at 9:00) while doctor capacity is flat — the queue inherits the surge and never drains until lunch.
- Compare *scheduled* slot patterns (if scheduling data can be joined) with *actual* arrival times to detect early/late arrival behavior and overbooking.

**(c) Service-time variability and case-mix effects**
- *Analysis:* Distribution of consultation durations per doctor and per patient type. If New-patient consults take 25 min vs. 12 min for Follow-ups but slots are uniform 15 min, every New patient pushes the schedule late, cascading waits onto everyone after them. Variant analysis stratified by Patient Type quantifies this.
- Per-resource comparison: does Dr. Smith's consult duration distribution differ materially from peers? Is the difference case-mix-adjusted or behavioral?

**(d) Handover/dependency friction and synchronization waits**
- *Analysis:* **Performance spectrum analysis** of transitions — visualize each case's hand-off as a line segment from COMPLETE(A) to START(B). Patterns reveal whether the queue is FIFO, whether batching occurs (e.g., nurse processes patients in bursts, lab collects samples in batches), or whether priority overtaking happens (urgent patients jumping queue).
- Detect **resource-dependent routing**: e.g., the ECG room is shared between two departments and waits spike when the other department's sessions run.

**(e) Queue discipline and prioritization problems**
- *Analysis:* Compare enqueue order (COMPLETE of predecessor) vs. dequeue order (START of successor). Frequent overtaking by Urgent patients is appropriate; overtaking patterns uncorrelated with urgency indicate disorganized queue management (e.g., paper-based lists, patients getting "lost").
- Check whether *Urgent* preemption inflates Normal patients' tail waits beyond acceptable levels.

**(f) Rework, loops, and ping-pong patterns**
- *Analysis:* **Variant analysis**: identify variants with loops (e.g., Doctor  Test  Doctor again, or Registration redone due to missing data). Loop variants consume duplicate doctor capacity and double-queue the patient. Quantify the share of cases and the added duration per loop.

**(g) Upstream blocking / downstream starvation**
- *Analysis:* Cross-correlate queue lengths across stages over time. If the doctor queue grows while nurses sit idle, the bottleneck is the doctor; if the doctor is idle while the nurse queue is long ("starvation"), the constraint is upstream. This Theory-of-Constraints view, computed directly from the log, identifies the *system* bottleneck rather than local ones.

### 2.2 Method Summary

| Technique | Question answered |
|---|---|
| Performance-annotated process model | Where are the big waits structurally? |
| Dotted chart / arrival analysis | When does demand exceed capacity? |
| Resource utilization & workload profiles | Is it a capacity or scheduling problem? |
| Performance spectrum | Batching, overtaking, queue discipline |
| Variant analysis (by type, urgency, specialty, day) | Which subpopulations drive the waits? |
| Decision/regression analysis on wait time | Which case/context attributes predict long waits? (e.g., regression tree: wait_before_doctor ~ hour-of-day + specialty + patient type + doctor + day) |
| Queueing-model fitting per stage | How sensitive is the wait to capacity/demand changes? |

## 3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

Assume the analysis confirms a typical pattern (consistent with the scenario): the dominant queues are **(Q1) Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation** (especially Cardiology, mornings), **(Q2) Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Tests (ECG/X-Ray)** due to shared rooms/techs, and **(Q3) arrival surge  Registration** in the 8:30–10:00 window.

### Strategy 1: Demand-Shaping via Data-Driven Appointment Scheduling (Heterogeneous Slots + Smoothed Templates)

- **Targets:** Q1 (doctor queue) and Q3 (registration surge).
- **Root cause addressed:** (b) morning arrival surge against flat capacity, and (c) uniform slot lengths mismatched to heterogeneous service times (New vs. Follow-up; specialty differences).
- **Data support:** Mined service-time distributions per (specialty × patient type) give empirically correct slot lengths (e.g., median 24 min for New cardiology vs. 11 min for Follow-up). The hourly queue-length curve identifies exactly which booking template slots cause cascading lateness. A **simulation model parameterized from the log** (arrival processes, service distributions, routing probabilities) can test alternative templates *before* deployment.
- **Concrete actions:** (i) Differentiated slot lengths by patient type/specialty; (ii) spread appointment start times across the morning instead of block-booking at 9:00; (iii) sequence-aware booking (place high-variance New patients at the start of sessions so their overruns can be absorbed, a known scheduling heuristic — validate via simulation); (iv) cap same-slot overbooking based on observed no-show rates per segment.
- **Expected impact:** Smoothing arrivals typically reduces peak queue length substantially; based on simulation, a realistic target is **25–40% reduction in mean wait before Doctor Consultation** and elimination of the 9:00–10:30 registration spike, with no added capacity cost.

### Strategy 2: Dynamic, Utilization-Based Resource Rostering and Flexible Cross-Cover

- **Targets:** Q1 and Q3.
- **Root cause addressed:** (a) capacity–demand misalignment in time: utilization analysis shows clerks/nurses at >90% utilization 9:00–11:00 but <50% in early afternoon; some doctors idle while colleagues' queues grow.
- **Data support:** Hour-by-hour heatmaps of demand (queue arrivals) vs. rostered capacity per role, mined from the log. Little's Law (L = W) per hour band quantifies how many additional servers in the peak window would bring wait below target.
- **Concrete actions:** (i) Shift one clerk and one nurse from afternoon to the 8:30–11:00 window (re-rostering, not hiring); (ii) "flex pool" rules: when the cardiology queue length exceeds a threshold (observable in real time), a cross-trained nurse takes over pre-consult tasks (history, vitals, ECG ordering) to shorten the doctor's effective service time; (iii) stagger doctor session start times to match the smoothed arrival template from Strategy 1.
- **Expected impact:** Removing the morning capacity deficit, queueing models predict disproportionate gains because waits are convex in utilization: reducing peak  from ~0.95 to ~0.85 can cut peak-hour waits **by 40–50%** at zero net staffing cost.

### Strategy 3: Flow Redesign — Parallelization and Pre-Emptive Test Ordering ("Test-Before-Consult" Protocols)

- **Targets:** Q2 (test queue) and the rework loop Doctor  Test  Doctor.
- **Root cause addressed:** (f) loop variants and (d) sequential dependencies that aren't clinically necessary. Variant analysis shows, e.g., that 70% of New cardiology patients get an ECG *after* consultation and a meaningful share return to the doctor afterward — consuming a second doctor slot and a second queue.
- **Data support:** Variant frequencies and durations quantify the prize: if the "consult  ECG  re-consult" variant takes 45 min longer on average and affects N patients/week, the savings are directly computable. Routing-probability analysis identifies patient profiles where the test is near-certain (predictable from referral reason/patient type), making protocolized pre-ordering safe.
- **Concrete actions:** (i) Standing orders: nurses perform ECG/basic bloods during/immediately after Nurse Assessment for defined patient profiles, so results are ready at consultation; (ii) parallelize Registration data-capture with Nurse Assessment via pre-visit digital check-in for Follow-ups; (iii) dedicated diagnostic time windows for the clinic in the shared ECG room, sized from observed demand patterns, to eliminate cross-department blocking.
- **Expected impact:** For affected variants, elimination of one full queue-and-service cycle — typically **30–60 minutes shorter visit** for those cases; reduced doctor demand (fewer second consult slots) further relieves Q1.

### Strategy 4 (supplementary): Real-Time Queue Visibility and Discipline Enforcement

- **Targets:** all queues; tail waits (P90).
- **Root cause addressed:** (e) disorganized queue discipline / "lost" patients identified via overtaking analysis in the performance spectrum.
- **Concrete actions:** Digital queue board with automatic next-patient call; urgency-aware priority rules made explicit; alerting when any individual patient's wait exceeds a threshold (catches lost patients). Patient-facing expected-wait displays — predictability itself improves satisfaction even before waits shrink.
- **Expected impact:** Targets the long tail: realistic goal of **halving the share of waits >45 minutes** and removing extreme outliers.

## 4. Trade-offs and Constraints

| Strategy | Key trade-offs / risks | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Differentiated/smoothed scheduling | Later appointment offerings may be less popular; reduced slack increases sensitivity to no-shows and late arrivals; revenue managers may resist fewer "booked" slots per session | Use no-show prediction from historical data to calibrate overbooking; pilot in one specialty; monitor access metrics (time-to-next-available-appointment) |
| Re-rostering / flex pool | Staff workload intensification in peak hours; cross-training cost; union/contract constraints; risk of burnout if peak capacity becomes the norm | Co-design rosters with staff; cap peak-shift frequency per person; monitor staff utilization to keep sustained  < ~85% |
| Pre-emptive testing / parallelization | **Unnecessary tests** if protocols over-trigger (cost + clinical appropriateness concern); patient safety if tests precede clinical judgment inappropriately | Restrict standing orders to high-probability profiles validated by clinicians; audit test-ordered-but-unused rate as a guardrail KPI |
| Dedicated diagnostic windows | May *shift the bottleneck* — the other department sharing the room now queues; equipment idle in dedicated slots if demand mispredicted | System-wide (not local) simulation before implementation; review utilization monthly |
| Any wait reduction at the doctor | Risk of compressing consultation time  care-quality erosion | Monitor service-time distributions: optimization must reduce *waits*, not consult durations; flag if consult times shrink anomalously |

**Balancing conflicting objectives.** I would make trade-offs explicit and quantitative:
1. Define **guardrail metrics** (consultation duration, rework/return rates, unnecessary-test rate, staff utilization ceiling, cost per visit) that must not deteriorate beyond agreed bounds — improvements only count if guardrails hold.
2. Use **simulation (digital twin parameterized from the event log)** to evaluate strategies on the joint objective surface (wait vs. cost vs. utilization) before committing, choosing Pareto-efficient configurations.
3. Recognize the **classic queueing trade-off**: very low waits require low utilization (expensive idle capacity). The clinic should set an explicit service-level target (e.g., "90% of patients wait <20 min at each stage") and find the minimum-cost configuration achieving it, rather than minimizing waits absolutely.
4. Prioritize **zero-cost levers first** (scheduling templates, sequencing, parallelization) before capacity additions.

## 5. Measuring Success

### 5.1 KPIs

**Primary outcome KPIs (patient flow):**
- Mean and **median total visit duration** (door-to-door), overall and by patient type/specialty.
- Mean, median, and **P90 waiting time per critical queue** (Q1, Q2, Q3).
- **% of patients meeting service-level target** (e.g., <20 min wait per stage; <2 h total visit for standard visits).
- **Flow efficiency** (value-added time / total time) per visit.
- Total accumulated waiting hours per week (system-level burden).

**Experience KPIs:**
- Patient satisfaction scores (overall and the specific "waiting time" survey item); complaint rate regarding waits.

**Guardrail KPIs (quality, cost, staff):**
- Consultation duration distributions (must not compress abnormally); unplanned return/re-consult rate within 7–30 days; unnecessary-test rate (ordered-but-unused).
- Resource utilization per role (target band, e.g., 70–85%); overtime hours; staff satisfaction pulse.
- Throughput (visits/day) and cost per visit — improvements must not come from seeing fewer patients.
- Time-to-next-available-appointment (access must not worsen).

### 5.2 Ongoing Monitoring

Because the event log structure already supports the analysis, **the same pipeline becomes the monitoring system**:

1. **Automated recurring queue-mining dashboard** (weekly/daily refresh): waits per transition, queue length over time, utilization heatmaps, P90 trends — segmented by specialty, patient type, urgency, hour, day.
2. **Before/after evaluation with statistical rigor:** define a pre-intervention baseline window; use control charts (e.g., XmR/EWMA on weekly median waits) to distinguish genuine shifts from noise; where possible, stagger rollout across specialties to create natural comparison groups (stepped-wedge logic).
3. **Bottleneck-shift surveillance:** monitor *all* queues, not just the targeted ones — a successful intervention upstream often surfaces a new constraint downstream. The Pareto of total waiting hours should be re-ranked monthly.
4. **Alerting:** real-time or near-real-time rules (queue length > threshold; individual wait > threshold) feeding operational responses (flex-pool activation per Strategy 2) and recording activations for review.
5. **Continuous recalibration:** re-fit arrival patterns, service-time distributions, and no-show rates quarterly and update scheduling templates and the simulation model accordingly — patient mix and demand evolve, so the optimization must be treated as a closed control loop, not a one-off project.
6. **Governance:** monthly flow review with clinical and operational leadership covering KPI trends, guardrail status, and prioritized next interventions — keeping the improvement cycle (mine  diagnose  simulate  implement  monitor) running.

**Success criteria (illustrative targets to agree with management):** within 6 months post-implementation — 30% reduction in median total visit duration, 40% reduction in P90 wait before doctor consultation, 90% of patients within stage-level wait targets, with all guardrail KPIs within bounds and no net increase in operating cost.
