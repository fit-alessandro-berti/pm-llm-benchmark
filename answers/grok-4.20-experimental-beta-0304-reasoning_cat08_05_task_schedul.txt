**1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics**

I would begin by applying a comprehensive process mining pipeline to the MES event logs, which contain the necessary attributes (case ID, activity, timestamp, resource, due date, priority, setup flag, planned vs. actual durations).

**Process Discovery and Reconstruction:**
- Use the Inductive Miner (directly-follows graph with noise filtering) and Heuristics Miner to discover the *actual* end-to-end job routing network and the machine-specific task execution sequences. This reveals the true precedence constraints and frequent vs. rare routing variants.
- Apply multi-perspective process mining (e.g., using PM4Py or Celonis) to create separate views: job-flow perspective, resource perspective, and time perspective.
- Perform sequence pattern mining on a per-machine basis by extracting the ordered list of jobs processed on each resource and calculating the setup time as the duration between "Task End" of job *n* and "Task Start" of job *n+1* when a setup flag is present.

**Key Metrics and Techniques:**
- **Job flow times, lead times, and makespan:** Calculate case-level throughput time (from "Job Released" to final "Task End"). Use performance analysis on the discovered process map to extract median, 90th percentile, and distribution of flow times. Differentiate between value-added time and waiting time.
- **Task waiting times:** Identify "Queue Entry" to "Setup Start" intervals per work center. Use bottleneck analysis (e.g., waiting time heatmap) to highlight machines with excessive queue times.
- **Resource utilization:** Apply resource performance analysis to compute utilization as (sum of setup + processing time) / total available time, broken down into productive, setup, idle, and breakdown states. Identify "starvation" periods where a machine is available but has no queued jobs while upstream bottlenecks exist.
- **Sequence-dependent setup times:** Create a job similarity matrix by mining historical job attributes (material type, dimensions, tolerances, previous operation). For each machine, build a transition matrix showing average and distribution of setup times between every pair of job types. Use this to quantify "setup waste" as a percentage of total machine time.
- **Schedule adherence and tardiness:** Perform conformance checking between the planned schedule (if partially available) and actual execution. Calculate tardiness as `max(0, actual_completion - due_date)` at both job and task levels. Compute metrics such as percentage of tardy jobs, mean tardiness, and maximum tardiness.
- **Impact of disruptions:** Filter cases containing breakdown events or priority changes. Use variant analysis to compare "disrupted" vs. "normal" process variants, measuring the delta in flow time and downstream propagation of delays. Apply causal process mining (e.g., using process cubes) to quantify how a breakdown on MILL-02 affects jobs waiting at downstream or upstream stations.

This analysis would produce a detailed "process reality" baseline, replacing the company's current intuition-based understanding with quantitative evidence.

**2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies**

The process mining results would almost certainly reveal several interconnected pathologies:

- **Bottleneck Resources:** Performance analysis would identify 1-3 critical machines (likely certain CNC mills or the heat treatment furnace) where utilization exceeds 85-90% and queue times are disproportionately high. Bottleneck analysis would show these resources control overall shop throughput (Theory of Constraints signature).

- **Poor Task Prioritization:** Variant analysis comparing on-time vs. late jobs would reveal that high-priority or near-due-date jobs frequently wait behind lower-priority jobs with earlier release times, indicating the current mix of FCFS and EDD is ineffective. "Critical ratio" or "slack time" metrics would show systematic inversion of priorities.

- **Suboptimal Sequencing and Setup Waste:** The sequence-dependent setup analysis would quantify excessive setup times caused by frequent "setup thrashing" — jumping between dissimilar job types. Machines with high setup percentages (e.g., >25% of available time) would be flagged.

- **Resource Starvation and Bullwhip Effect:** Waiting time analysis would show downstream flexible machines experiencing significant idle time while upstream bottlenecks are overloaded. WIP levels (measured via concurrent active cases) would show high variance and "WIP bubbles" propagating through the shop.

- **Disruption Amplification:** Cases containing breakdowns or hot jobs would show significantly higher tardiness and flow time variance, demonstrating that the current system has no effective recovery mechanisms.

These diagnoses would be supported by visual artifacts: process maps with performance overlays, bottleneck heatmaps, and variant comparison dashboards.

**3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness**

Process mining enables us to distinguish between scheduling logic failures and fundamental capacity/variability issues through careful filtering and comparative analysis.

**Primary Root Causes:**
- **Static Dispatching Rules in a Dynamic Environment:** The local FCFS/EDD rules ignore global state (downstream congestion, sequence-dependent setups, remaining job slack). Variant analysis shows that "optimal local" decisions frequently create globally suboptimal outcomes.
- **Lack of Real-Time Visibility and Coordination:** The absence of a holistic view means decisions at one work center do not account for the state of others. Resource contention analysis reveals periods where multiple high-priority jobs compete for the same bottleneck without intelligent arbitration.
- **Inaccurate or Ignored Estimates:** Comparison of planned vs. actual durations reveals systematic biases (e.g., operators consistently taking 10-15% longer on certain job types). Setup time estimates appear to be either missing or based on inaccurate averages rather than sequence-specific data.
- **Ineffective Disruption Handling:** Breakdown events show that "hot jobs" are inserted reactively, often displacing other near-due jobs without proper rescheduling of the affected downstream work.
- **Failure to Exploit Job Similarity:** No mechanism exists to group similar jobs for setup reduction, despite clear patterns in the historical data.

**Differentiation:** By creating process cubes sliced by load level, product family, and disruption frequency, we can isolate whether poor performance is primarily driven by scheduling logic (high variance within the same capacity conditions) versus capacity constraints (consistent bottlenecks even under moderate load). The presence of significant idle time at non-bottleneck resources while bottlenecks are overloaded strongly points to scheduling logic as the dominant issue.

**4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies**

**Strategy 1: Multi-Criteria Dynamic Dispatching with Setup Awareness**
- **Core Logic:** Replace static rules with a weighted composite score calculated at each resource when it becomes free:  
  `Score = w1*(DueDateSlack) + w2*(CriticalRatio) + w3*(EstimatedSetupTimeToNextJob) + w4*(DownstreamCongestionFactor)`
- **Use of Process Mining:** Weights are derived via regression or machine learning on historical data (predicting tardiness from different decision factors). The "EstimatedSetupTimeToNextJob" is looked up from the mined sequence-dependent setup matrix based on the last job processed on that machine.
- **Addresses:** Poor prioritization and setup waste. Expected impact: 25-40% reduction in tardiness and 15-20% reduction in total setup time.

**Strategy 2: Predictive Stochastic Scheduling with Digital Twin**
- **Core Logic:** Build a predictive model using historical distributions (conditioned on job characteristics, operator, and machine) to create probabilistic schedules. Use Monte Carlo simulation or reinforcement learning to generate robust schedules that perform well under uncertainty. Incorporate predictive maintenance signals derived from increasing setup or processing times.
- **Use of Process Mining:** Task duration distributions, routing probabilities, breakdown frequencies, and recovery patterns are all directly mined from the logs. The model is continuously updated.
- **Addresses:** Unpredictable lead times and poor disruption response. Expected impact: More accurate due date quotations (improved reliability) and proactive bottleneck avoidance, targeting 30%+ reduction in tardiness under high variability.

**Strategy 3: Bottleneck-Focused Setup Optimization via Intelligent Sequencing**
- **Core Logic:** At identified bottleneck resources, use a rolling-horizon optimization (genetic algorithm or constraint programming) that sequences the next *k* jobs to minimize total setup time while respecting due dates and priority constraints. Non-bottleneck resources use the dynamic dispatching rule. Implement "job similarity batching" where similar jobs are grouped when feasible.
- **Use of Process Mining:** The setup time transition matrix and job similarity clusters are direct outputs of the sequence mining. Bottleneck identification determines where this computationally intensive approach is justified.
- **Addresses:** Excessive setup times and bottleneck inefficiency. Expected impact: Significant reduction in bottleneck idle/setup time, increased effective capacity, and lower overall WIP.

**5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement**

**Simulation Framework:**
I would implement a detailed discrete-event simulation (using SimPy, AnyLogic, or Arena) parameterized directly from process mining outputs:
- Empirical distributions for processing times, setup times (sequence-dependent), and inter-arrival times.
- Routing probabilities and job mix from the discovered process model.
- Breakdown and repair distributions mined from resource events.
- Current vs. proposed dispatching/sequencing logic as alternative scheduling agents.

**Test Scenarios:**
- Baseline load vs. 20% increased load.
- Normal disruption frequency vs. high disruption periods.
- Different product mix scenarios (high variety vs. temporary low variety).
- "What-if" analysis on adding capacity to specific bottlenecks.

Performance would be evaluated across multiple KPIs (tardiness, mean flow time, WIP, utilization, setup percentage) using statistical significance testing across many replications.

**Continuous Improvement Framework:**
- Deploy the chosen strategy within the MES with an "analytics sandbox" layer.
- Implement ongoing process mining on streaming event data (using frameworks like ProM or commercial platforms with streaming capabilities).
- Track KPIs in real-time dashboards with automated alerts for drift detection (using concept drift techniques in process mining).
- Monthly re-mining of the setup matrix, duration distributions, and bottleneck analysis to adapt weights and parameters.
- Quarterly full re-evaluation using the simulation model with the latest mined data to determine if a strategy switch or hybridization is warranted.

This approach transforms the company's scheduling from a collection of local heuristics into a continuously learning, data-driven socio-technical system that directly addresses the root causes of their performance problems while remaining practical for a job shop environment.
