## Which log exhibits bias?

**Group B (the “unprotected” group) exhibits a clear, measurable bias.**

Group A is uniform: every candidate goes through the same stages, and the only inputs to the decision are the Skill, Personality, and Cultural Fit scores. There is no extra score adjustment.

Group B, by contrast, applies a **+5 “Association Boost” to Cultural Fit** whenever a candidate belongs to the **“LocalBusinessLeadersClub.”** Because club membership is also perfectly correlated with being a **Local Resident** in this log, the boost functions as a preference for a specific social/professional network.

---

## How the bias manifests

### 1. Different rules for otherwise similar candidates
Compare candidates who are very close on the objective dimensions but receive different treatment because of the association rule:

| Candidate | Skill | Personality | Raw Cultural Fit | Adjustment | Final Cultural Fit | Decision |
|-----------|------:|------------:|-----------------:|-----------:|-------------------:|----------|
| **P002** (Group A) | 78 | 75 | 60 | 0 | 60 | **Not Hired** |
| **U003** (Group B, club member) | 75 | 65 | 58 | **+5** | 63 | **Hired** |

U003 scores **lower on every dimension** than P002, yet is hired because of the +5 boost. That is a direct advantage conferred by club membership, not by merit.

### 2. Lower-performing candidates can outrank higher-performing ones
- **P003** (Group A): Skill 82, Personality 68, Cultural Fit 65  Hired  
- **U003** (Group B): Skill 75, Personality 65, Cultural Fit 58 (boosted to 63)  Hired  

P003 outperforms U003 on objective scores but both receive the same outcome. If the boost did not exist, U003’s lower profile would likely have led to a “Not Hired” decision.

### 3. The adjustment is tied to a non-job-related attribute
The +5 boost is not based on anything measured during the hiring steps (Skill Test, Personality Assessment, or Cultural Fit Check). It is based on **membership in an external professional association**. In the data, every club member is also a **Local Resident**, so the rule effectively favors local, networked candidates over non-local candidates who may lack access to such clubs.

## Implications for fairness and equity

| Fairness concern | What the log shows |
|------------------|--------------------|
| **Disparate treatment** | The process explicitly assigns different scores to candidates based on club membership. |
| **Disparate impact** | Because club membership is correlated with local residency and likely socioeconomic network, the rule systematically disadvantages the protected group (non-local/non-member candidates). |
| **Violation of meritocracy** | Hiring outcomes no longer depend solely on job-relevant scores; they are influenced by social capital. |
| **Lack of individual fairness** | Similar—or even stronger—candidates in Group A are rejected while weaker club-member candidates in Group B are hired. |
| **Reinforcement of privilege** | Candidates who already have access to elite local networks receive a bonus, which can perpetuate existing inequalities. |
| **Legal and reputational risk** | Using non-job-related associations that correlate with protected characteristics (e.g., place of origin, socioeconomic background) can raise discrimination concerns under employment-equity laws. |

---

## Bottom line

**Group B is the biased log.** The **+5 “Association Boost”** for the *LocalBusinessLeadersClub* introduces a non-merit advantage that changes hiring outcomes. It causes lower-scoring candidates to be hired over higher-scoring candidates and disproportionately benefits local, networked applicants. For a fair and equitable process, score adjustments should be based only on job-relevant criteria, applied consistently to all candidates, and documented in a way that can be audited.
