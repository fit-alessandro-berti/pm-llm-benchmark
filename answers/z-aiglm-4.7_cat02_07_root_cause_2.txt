### 2. Root Cause Analysis

By correlating the duration with the attributes (Complexity, Region, Resource) and the event logs, we can deduce the following root causes:

#### A. The Impact of "Request Additional Documents" Loops
The most significant driver of long lead times is the iteration of the **"Request Additional Documents"** activity.

*   **Fast Cases (2001, 2004):** 0 document requests. Process flows linearly from Evaluate to Approve.
*   **Moderate Case (2002):** 1 document request.
*   **Slow Cases (2003, 2005):** Multiple document requests.
    *   **Case 2003** had **2 requests**.
    *   **Case 2005** had **3 requests**.

**Analysis:** Every time a document is requested, the case effectively stalls, waiting for external input or internal processing before the next step. Case 2005 required three separate rounds of information gathering, creating a "stop-and-go" effect that extended the case duration by days.

#### B. Attribute: Complexity Level
There is a direct correlation between **Complexity** and **Duration**.

*   **Low Complexity:** Average duration ~1.5 hours. No extra document requests.
*   **Medium Complexity:** Duration ~26 hours. 1 extra document request.
*   **High Complexity:** Average duration ~2.5 days. 2+ extra document requests.

**Root Cause:** High complexity claims are not being handled correctly in the initial evaluation. Instead of gathering all necessary requirements upfront, the process is reactive. The adjusters evaluate the claim, realize something is missing, request it, wait, and then realize something *else* is missing.

#### C. Attribute: Resource (Adjuster Behavior)
While resources generally perform well on low-complexity tasks, specific resources struggle with high-complexity workflows.

*   **Case 2005 (The worst case):** Handled primarily by **Adjuster_Lisa**. She requested documents on April 1, April 2, and April 3. This indicates a "drip-feeding" approach where she identifies missing information piece by piece rather than conducting a thorough audit during the first evaluation.
*   **Case 2003 (Second worst):** Handled by **Adjuster_Mike**. Similar to Lisa, he made two requests (Apr 1 and Apr 1), showing an inability to lock down requirements in a single pass.
*   **Manager_Bill:** Handles approvals for the slowest cases (2003 and 2005). While he is consistent, the sheer volume of rework hitting his desk from the adjusters suggests he is dealing with cases that were not "approval ready."

#### D. Attribute: Region

*   **Region B:** Has a fast case (2004), a medium case (2002), and the slowest case (2005).

**Conclusion:** Region itself is not the primary root cause. Both regions are capable of speed. The performance issues are tied to the **Complexity** of the claims assigned to specific resources within those regions. ### 3. Explanations and Mitigation Strategies

#### Explanation
The primary root cause of the performance issues is **Inefficient Initial Evaluation (Rework Loops)**.

The process is designed to be linear (Submit $\to$ Evaluate $\to$ Approve). However, for High Complexity cases, the "Evaluate" step is failing to catch all missing data at once. Instead of performing a comprehensive check, the adjusters are identifying missing documents incrementally. This forces the process into a loop of `Request Info $\to$ Wait $\to$ Review $\to$ Request More`. This "wait time" accumulates, causing cases 2003 and 2005 to drag on for days.

#### Suggestions for Mitigation

1.  **Implement "First-Time-Right" Checklists for High Complexity:**
    *   Create a mandatory verification checklist for Adjusters handling **High Complexity** claims. This should require them to validate *all* required documents before moving the case forward. This aims to reduce the multiple "Request Additional Documents" events down to a single event (or zero).

2.  **Adjuster Training & Specialization:**
    *   **Adjuster_Lisa** and **Adjuster_Mike** show a pattern of incremental requests on complex cases. They should receive specific training on comprehensive claim auditing or handling high-complexity cases.
    *   Consider routing High Complexity claims exclusively to "Senior Adjusters" who may have a higher success rate of getting it right the first time.

3.  **Manager Escalation Protocol:**
    *   If a "Request Additional Documents" event occurs more than once on a single case, it should trigger an automatic alert to the Manager (e.g., Manager_Bill). The Manager can then intervene to conduct a joint review with the Adjuster to ensure the *third* request does not happen (as seen in Case 2005).

4.  **SLA Monitoring for Document Loops:**
    *   Implement a process KPI that tracks the "Number of Additional Document Requests" per case. Any case exceeding 1 request should be flagged as a process failure, regardless of how long it eventually takes. This shifts the focus from just "speed" to "process efficiency."
