# Loan Approval Process: A DECLARE Model Walkthrough

## Overview

The DECLARE model above formalizes a **loan approval process** as a set of declarative rules rather than a rigid flowchart. Each constraint captures a business rule—what *must* happen, what *must not* happen, and in what order. Taken together, the constraints describe the full lifecycle of a loan application from intake to disbursement.

Let me walk through the process step by step, connect each activity to the constraints that govern it, and then reflect on the business motivations and risks.

---

## Step-by-Step Process Description

### Step 1: Receive the Application
The process **must begin** with `Receive_Application` (enforced by the **init** constraint) and this activity **must occur** in every case (enforced by **existence**).

- A loan officer or online portal intakes the customer's application (personal details, desired loan amount, purpose, etc.).
- No other activity can legally precede this one—this ensures every downstream action is traceable to a formal request.

### Step 2: Preliminary Credit Check
Thanks to the **response** constraint (`Receive_Application  Preliminary_Credit_Check`), once an application is received, a credit check *must* eventually follow. The **exactly_one** constraint guarantees this check happens precisely once—no more, no less.

- The bank pulls credit bureau data, evaluates debt-to-income ratio, and scores the applicant.
- Performing it exactly once prevents duplicate credit inquiries (which can harm the customer's credit score) while still guaranteeing due diligence.

### Step 3: Gather Additional Documents
The **succession** constraint (`Preliminary_Credit_Check  Gather_Additional_Documents`) means:
- Document collection follows the credit check, AND
- Document collection is preceded by the credit check.

This activity is also mandatory (**existence**). The **chainprecedence** constraint (`Authorize_Contract_Terms  Preliminary_Credit_Check`) reinforces that credit assessment is a structural prerequisite for any later authorization.

- Staff request pay stubs, tax returns, IDs, proof of address, etc.
- Doing this *after* the credit check is efficient: if the preliminary check fails, the bank doesn't burden itself or the customer with unnecessary paperwork.

### Step 4: Assemble the Loan Offer Package  Quality Assurance Review
Here two constraints interact strongly:
- **altsuccession** (`Gather_Additional_Documents  Quality_Assurance_Review`): QA must follow document gathering, with no other QA occurring in between.
- **chainsuccession** (`Quality_Assurance_Review  Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package`): These two activities must occur in immediate sequence.
- **responded_existence** (`Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package  Quality_Assurance_Review`): Any offer package assembled triggers a QA review.

Practically, the bank assembles a proposed offer (interest rate, term, conditions), and QA immediately follows to validate regulatory compliance, internal policy alignment, and completeness.

### Step 5: Authorize Contract Terms
- **precedence** (`Quality_Assurance_Review  Authorize_Contract_Terms`): Authorization can only happen *after* QA has signed off.
- **coexistence** (`Gather_Additional_Documents  Authorize_Contract_Terms`): If one occurs, so must the other—no contract without documentation, no documents gathered in vain.

A senior officer or automated system formally approves the final loan terms. This is the "point of no return" for committing funds.

### Step 6: Transfer Funds
- **chainresponse** (`Assemble_Loan_Offer_Package  Transfer_Funds`): Funds flow out only after a proper offer exists.
- **noncoexistence** (`Transfer_Funds`  `Receive_Application`): This one is unusual—it states the two cannot coexist in a trace. In a real model this likely reflects a safeguard distinguishing cases (e.g., the *pair* is forbidden without all the intermediate steps in place). It forces architects to think carefully: funds should never exist as a standalone event tied directly to intake without every intermediate guard.

### Step 7: Notify the Customer
- **altresponse** (`Transfer_Funds  Notify_Customer`): After funds transfer, the customer must be notified.
- **altprecedence** (`Notify_Customer  Transfer_Funds`): Conversely, any notification about funding must have been preceded by the transfer.
- **nonsuccession** (`Notify_Customer`  `Preliminary_Credit_Check`): A notification must never be followed by a new credit check—this prevents re-opening closed decisions.
- **nonchainsuccession** (`Authorize_Contract_Terms`  `Notify_Customer` immediately): Notification cannot follow authorization *directly*; the fund transfer must come in between. This guarantees the customer is never told "your loan is approved and funded" before the money has actually moved.

### Throughout: Compliance Must Hold
The **absence** constraint forbids `Proceed_Without_Compliance` from ever occurring. This is the catch-all safeguard: regardless of timing, no step may bypass regulatory controls.

## Real-World Motivations Behind Each Rule

| Constraint Type | Business Rationale |
|---|---|
| **init / existence** | Auditability: every case has a clear starting point and mandatory touchpoints. |
| **exactly_one** (credit check) | Avoids over-pulling credit (regulatory, fair-lending) while guaranteeing due diligence. |
| **absence** (Proceed_Without_Compliance) | Hard regulatory guardrail (AML, KYC, consumer protection laws). |
| **response / precedence / succession** | Enforce logical ordering—risk is assessed *before* money moves. |
| **chain\*** constraints | Prevent tampering or unreviewed insertions between critical steps. |
| **coexistence** | Ensures documentation and authorization are tied together—no "phantom" approvals. |
| **noncoexistence / nonsuccession** | Fraud prevention; e.g., no disbursement without a proper application trail. |
| **alt\*** constraints | Prevent duplication—each key event has exactly one corresponding counterpart. |

The big-picture drivers are:

1. **Regulatory compliance** (Basel requirements, consumer lending laws, anti-money-laundering rules).
2. **Fraud prevention** (no payouts without documented, reviewed approvals).
3. **Risk management** (credit assessment precedes commitment).
4. **Operational efficiency** (documents collected only for viable applicants).
5. **Customer experience** (one credit check; timely, accurate notifications).

---

## Consequences of Violating the Model

If steps occurred out of order or were skipped, the results could be severe:

- **Skipping the Preliminary Credit Check**  The bank extends credit blindly, exposing itself to default risk and potentially violating prudential lending regulations.
- **Transferring Funds before Authorize_Contract_Terms**  Unauthorized disbursement; a direct fraud or embezzlement red flag, with possible clawback, legal liability, and reputational harm.
- **Skipping Quality Assurance Review**  Offers may contain errors (wrong APR, missing disclosures), leading to consumer-protection violations (e.g., TILA in the U.S.) and forced remediation.
- **Notifying the Customer before Transferring Funds**  Sets false expectations; if the transfer later fails, the customer experiences a broken promise, eroding trust.
- **Proceed_Without_Compliance occurring**  A fundamental breach: regulators could levy fines, suspend licenses, or open investigations.
- **Gathering Documents without later Authorization** (violating coexistence)  Wasted operational effort and, worse, unnecessary retention of sensitive personal data (a GDPR/privacy risk).
- **Re-running Preliminary_Credit_Check after Notify_Customer**  Suggests re-opening a closed case, a signal of instability, confusion, or manipulation.

---

## Summary

The DECLARE model paints a picture of a tightly controlled, auditable loan-approval workflow:

> **Receive  Credit Check  Gather Documents  Assemble Offer  QA Review  Authorize  Transfer Funds  Notify Customer**

Every constraint—positive (what must happen) or negative (what must not)—aligns with a real-world need for **trust, safety, legality, and efficiency**. Together they ensure that no loan gets funded without a documented, reviewed, authorized, and compliant trail—protecting the bank, the customer, and the regulatory ecosystem.
