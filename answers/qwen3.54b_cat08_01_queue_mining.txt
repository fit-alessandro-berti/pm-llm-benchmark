# Process Mining & Queue Optimization Strategy for Outpatient Clinic

## 1. Queue Identification and Characterization

To address patient dissatisfaction regarding waiting times, we must move beyond simple observation to quantitative analysis of the event log. Since our logs contain explicit **Start** and **Complete** timestamps for every activity instance, we can derive precise metrics on queuing behavior.

### Defining "Waiting Time" (Queue Time)
In this context, "waiting time" is defined as the temporal gap between the completion of one activity ($A_i$) and the start of the next sequential activity ($A_{i+1}$) for the same Case ID (Patient Visit).
*   **Formula:** $WaitTime = Timestamp(Start_{Next}) - Timestamp(Complete_{Current})$
*   **Zero Waiting:** If $Timestamp(Start_{Next}) \le Timestamp(Complete_{Current})$, it indicates a potential data error or parallel processing; we treat this as 0 wait.

### Key Metrics for Characterization
We will calculate the following metrics per queue (Activity Pair) and aggregate them across resources:
1.  **Average Waiting Time:** The arithmetic mean of all wait times between two consecutive activities. This provides a general sense of throughput speed.
2.  **Median Waiting Time:** More robust than the average against outliers (e.g., one patient waiting 4 hours while others wait 5 minutes).
3.  **Maximum/90th Percentile Waiting Time:** Critical for identifying "worst-case" experiences that drive dissatisfaction.
4.  **Queue Frequency:** The percentage of cases that experience a wait time exceeding a specific threshold (e.g., >15 mins).
5.  **Service Level Agreements (SLA) Violations:** Count of cases where wait time exceeds the acceptable limit defined by management or patient expectations.

### Identifying Critical Queues
Not all queues are equal. We prioritize "Critical Queues" using a weighted scoring model based on:
*   **Impact Score:** $Weight \times Severity$.
    *   *Severity:* Based on Patient Type and Urgency. (e.g., A "Urgent" patient waiting longer than a "New" patient is highly negative).
    *   *Frequency:* How often does this bottleneck occur?
*   **Resource Constraint:** Is the queue caused by a single resource (bottleneck) or multiple?
*   **Cycle Time Impact:** Does the wait extend the total visit duration significantly?

**Action:** We will generate a "Queue Heatmap" visualizing average wait times across all activity transitions. The top 3 queues with the highest *Impact Score* will be selected for deep-dive analysis (e.g., Waiting between Registration and Nurse Assessment, or between Doctor Consultation and Check-out).

## 2. Root Cause Analysis

Identifying *where* the queue happens is step one; understanding *why* requires deeper process mining techniques beyond simple time calculation.

### Potential Root Causes & Process Mining Techniques
1.  **Resource Bottlenecks (Staff/Room Utilization):**
    *   *Analysis:* We will analyze **Resource Flow** and **Utilization Metrics**. If "Dr. Smith" has a high utilization rate (>80%) but long wait times for his patients, the queue is caused by his availability, not the process flow itself.
    *   *Evidence:* High correlation between `Activity: Doctor Consultation` start time and `Resource: Dr. Smith` availability gaps.

2.  **Activity Dependencies & Handovers:**
    *   *Analysis:* We will use **Process Variant Analysis** to compare sequences. Are "New" patients forced to wait longer for Nurse Assessment than "Follow-up" patients? This suggests a rigid handover process or lack of pre-assessment data.
    *   *Evidence:* If `Registration` completes, but `Nurse Assessment` starts significantly later for New patients compared to Follow-ups, the root cause is likely manual data entry or triage delays.

3.  **Variability in Service Times:**
    *   *Analysis:* We will calculate the **Coefficient of Variation (CV)** of activity durations. High CV indicates unpredictable service times (e.g., some doctors take 10 mins, others 60 mins), which causes queues to build up unpredictably.
    *   *Evidence:* `Activity: Doctor Consultation` duration varies widely due to case complexity or patient preparation time.

4.  **Appointment Scheduling Policies:**
    *   *Analysis:* We will analyze the **Gap between Appointment Booking and Arrival**. If patients arrive significantly before their scheduled slot, they queue up earlier than expected. Conversely, if slots are too short, doctors run over, causing downstream waits.
    *   *Evidence:* Correlation between `Appointment Time` (derived from Case ID history) and actual `Registration Start Time`.

5.  **Patient Type & Urgency Segmentation:**
    *   *Analysis:* We will perform **Segment Analysis**. Do "Urgent" patients wait longer in the system due to lack of priority tagging? Or do they bypass queues effectively?
    *   *Evidence:* Comparison of `WaitTime` distribution between `Urgency: Normal` vs. `Urgency: Urgent`.

### Application of Process Mining Techniques
*   **Bottleneck Analysis:** Identify the specific resource (e.g., Room 3 for ECG) that limits flow.
*   **Conformance Checking:** Verify if the clinic is following standard operating procedures (SOPs). If patients are often routed through `Registration` before `Doctor Consultation`, but data suggests they could be grouped, we can identify deviations.

## 3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

Based on the analysis of the event log, here are three concrete strategies to mitigate queuing issues.

### Strategy 1: Dynamic Staffing & Resource Scheduling
*   **Target Queue:** Registration $\rightarrow$ Nurse Assessment (High variability in nurse availability).
*   **Root Cause:** Understaffing during peak hours and lack of visibility into real-time demand.
*   **Data Support:** The log shows a spike in `Registration` completion times followed by long gaps before `Nurse Assessment`. Resource analysis reveals "Clerk A" is often fully booked 30 mins before peak, creating a backlog for the next step.
*   **Proposed Action:** Implement a **Dynamic Scheduling Model**. Use historical data to predict arrival patterns (e.g., 10:00 AM - 12:00 PM are peaks). Adjust staff shifts or add temporary clerks based on predicted load rather than fixed schedules.
*   **Impact:** Expected reduction in average wait time for Registration $\rightarrow$ Nurse Assessment by 25% during peak hours, reducing total visit duration by approx. 10 minutes per patient.

### Strategy 2: Patient Segmentation & Triage Logic
*   **Target Queue:** Doctor Consultation (New vs. Follow-up).
*   **Root Cause:** Treating all patients equally in the scheduling queue, ignoring urgency and history. "Follow-up" patients may have less complex needs than "New" patients, but are currently queued behind them.
*   **Data Support:** Variant analysis shows `Patient Type: Follow-up` often experience shorter total visit durations than `Patient Type: New`. However, if "Urgent" patients are mixed with "Normal", the wait time for Urgent patients is high.
*   **Proposed Action:** Implement a **Priority-Based Scheduling System**. Create separate appointment slots or queues for "Urgent" and "New" patients. Pre-qualify "Follow-up" patients digitally to reduce on-site assessment time, allowing them to move faster through the Doctor Consultation queue.
*   **Impact:** Reduction in maximum wait time (P90) for Urgent patients by 40%. Improved satisfaction scores for "Urgent" patient segment.

### Strategy 3: Parallel Processing & Handshake Optimization
*   **Target Queue:** Nurse Assessment $\rightarrow$ Doctor Consultation.
*   **Root Cause:** Strict sequential processing where a patient must finish the nurse assessment *before* seeing the doctor, and the doctor cannot see patients until the nurse is done. This creates a "handshake" bottleneck.
*   **Data Support:** Timing analysis shows that `Nurse Assessment` often takes longer than expected due to documentation delays, but `Doctor Consultation` starts strictly after completion. If Nurse Assessment is purely administrative, this is inefficient.
*   **Proposed Action:** **Decouple Documentation.** Allow the Doctor to see the patient based on a preliminary "Triage Note" or allow parallel waiting (e.g., Doctor waits for specific test results while Nurse finishes notes). Alternatively, if the log shows `Nurse Assessment` involves data entry that delays the doctor, move data entry to pre-visit digital forms.
*   **Impact:** Potential reduction in overall visit cycle time by 15-20 minutes per patient by removing the sequential dependency bottleneck.

## 4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints

Implementing these strategies is not without risks. A balanced approach is required to avoid shifting bottlenecks or increasing costs.

### Potential Trade-offs
1.  **Wait Time vs. Care Quality:**
    *   *Risk:* Rushing patients (e.g., via Strategy 2) might reduce doctor-patient interaction time, potentially compromising diagnosis accuracy.
    *   *Mitigation:* Set minimum consultation time limits (KPIs) in the system to ensure quality is not sacrificed for speed. Monitor `Activity: Doctor Consultation` duration to ensure it doesn't drop below clinical standards.

2.  **Cost vs. Efficiency:**
    *   *Risk:* Dynamic staffing (Strategy 1) may require paying for temporary staff or overtime, increasing operational costs.
    *   *Mitigation:* Use data to identify if the cost of overtime exceeds the cost of lost revenue from patient dissatisfaction (churn). If patient volume is low during off-peak hours, dynamic scheduling should not increase headcount unnecessarily.

3.  **Process Rigidity vs. Flexibility:**
    *   *Risk:* Strict priority queues (Strategy 2) may cause "Urgent" patients to wait for administrative tasks that "Non-Urgent" patients skipped.
    *   *Mitigation:* Ensure the system allows "Fast Track" documentation for Urgent patients, so they don't get stuck in a long admin queue.

4.  **Shift of Bottleneck:**
    *   *Risk:* If we speed up Registration but Doctor Consultation remains slow, the bottleneck simply shifts downstream.
    *   *Mitigation:* Conduct **End-to-End Cycle Time** analysis. Optimize the system holistically; if Doctors are the bottleneck, staffing there is more critical than speeding up Registration.

## 5. Measuring Success

To ensure the optimization strategies yield sustained improvement, we must establish a continuous feedback loop using process mining.

### Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)
1.  **Average Patient Wait Time (APWT):** The mean time spent waiting between activities. *Target: Reduce by X% within 3 months.*
2.  **Total Visit Duration (TVD):** Sum of all activity durations + wait times. *Target: Reduce by Y minutes per patient.*
3.  **Queue Frequency:** % of patients waiting > Threshold (e.g., 15 mins). *Target: < 10%.*
4.  **Resource Utilization Efficiency:** % of staff time spent on direct patient care vs. admin/waiting.
5.  **Patient Satisfaction Score (CSAT):** Correlate wait time data with post-visit survey feedback to validate objective metrics against subjective experience.

### Ongoing Monitoring & Validation
*   **Continuous Logging:** Ensure the event log structure remains consistent over the six-month period (and beyond) to allow for historical trend analysis.
*   **Process Mining Dashboard:** Create a dashboard that visualizes the **Cycle Time Distribution** and **Resource Utilization Heatmap** in real-time or near real-time.
*   **Pre/Post Comparison:** After implementing Strategy 1, 2, or 3, re-run the queue mining analysis on the new data to compare against the baseline (first 6 months).
    *   *Metric:* Compare `Median Wait Time` before and after intervention.
*   **Exception Handling:** Monitor for "Anomalous" cases in the log where expected wait times are not met to investigate specific root causes of outliers (e.g., machine failure, staff absence) that might invalidate general trends.

By adhering to this data-driven framework, the clinic can transition from reactive complaint management to proactive flow optimization, ensuring operational efficiency without compromising patient care quality.
