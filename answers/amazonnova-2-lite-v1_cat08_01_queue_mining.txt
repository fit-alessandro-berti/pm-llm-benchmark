## **1. Queue Identification and Characterization**

### **Calculating Waiting Times (Queue Times)**

In this context, **waiting time** refers to the **time a patient spends idle between the completion of one activity and the start of the next activity** in their care pathway. This is the true “queue time” experienced by the patient. Using the event log, which includes both **START** and **COMPLETE** timestamps, the waiting time between two consecutive activities for a given patient visit can be calculated as follows:

**Waiting Time (Activity B - Activity A) = Start Time (Activity B) – Complete Time (Activity A)**

Using the sample data:
- Visit V1001:  
  - Complete time of `Registration`: `2024-10-21 09:08:45`  
  - Start time of `Nurse Assessment`: `2024-10-21 09:15:20`  
  - **Waiting time between Registration and Nurse Assessment = 09:15:20 – 09:08:45 = 6 minutes 35 seconds**

This calculation is applied systematically across all pairs of consecutive activities for every patient visit to build a comprehensive view of waiting times at each transition point.

### **Key Queue Metrics to Calculate**

To characterize the queues effectively, we calculate the following **descriptive statistics** for each inter-activity transition (e.g., Registration  Nurse Assessment, Nurse Assessment  Doctor Consultation, etc.):

| Metric                          | Purpose                                                                 | Calculation                                                                   |
|--------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Average Waiting Time**        | Overall load on the queue                                               | Mean of all waiting times for that transition                                |
| **Median Waiting Time**         | Central tendency; less sensitive to outliers                              | 50th percentile of waiting times                                              |
| **90th Percentile Waiting Time**| Captures extreme cases; reflects worst-case experiences                   | Value below which 90% of waits fall                                           |
| **Maximum Waiting Time**        | Identifies outlier delays; potential system failures                     | Largest observed wait time                                                   |
| **Queue Frequency**             | How often patients encounter waits > threshold (e.g., > 15 min)         | % of cases with wait > threshold                                             |
| **Cases with Excessive Waits**  | Highlights impact on patient satisfaction                                | Count or % of visits exceeding a defined threshold (e.g., > 30 min)           |
| **Resource Utilization**        | Correlates queue with resource usage (e.g., staff, room)                 | % of time resource is busy during activity execution                          |

### **Identifying the Most Critical Queues**

To prioritize queues for mitigation, we apply the following **criteria**:

1. **Longest Average/Median Waiting Time:**  
   Queues with high baseline delays indicate systemic issues. Focus on transitions where patients wait the most on average.

2. **High 90th Percentile Waits:**  
   Transitions with high variability and extreme delays disproportionately impact patient satisfaction and perceived quality of service.

3. **High Frequency of Excessive Waits:**  
   Even if average wait is moderate, if a large percentage of patients wait over a threshold (e.g., 30+ minutes), it is critical for patient experience.

4. **Impact on Vulnerable Patient Segments:**  
   Urgency or patient type (e.g., **Urgent** vs. **New** vs. **Follow-up**) may reveal disparities. A queue that disproportionately delays urgent cases requires immediate attention.

5. **Impact on Overall Visit Duration:**  
   Queues that occur early (e.g., after Registration) or in long sequences compound delays downstream, affecting the entire visit duration.

By combining these criteria, we identify **bottleneck queues** that are both quantitatively severe and qualitatively impactful.

## **2. Root Cause Analysis**

### **Potential Root Causes for Significant Queues**

Understanding the *why* behind queues is crucial. Potential root causes include:

#### **A. Resource Bottlenecks**
- **Staff Shortages or Imbalances:** A single nurse or doctor may handle a disproportionate patient load.
- **Room/Equipment Utilization:** Limited diagnostic rooms (e.g., ECG, X-ray) may create choke points.
- **Skill Mismatches:** Activities requiring specialized staff (e.g., cardiologists) may have limited availability.

**Process Mining Insight:**  
Use **Resource Utilization Analysis** to visualize how often and how long each resource is busy. Heatmaps of resource usage across time-of-day can reveal underutilized or overburdened resources.

#### **B. Activity Dependencies and Handovers**
- **Sequential Dependencies:** Activities that must be completed in strict order (e.g., Nurse  Doctor) create linear bottlenecks.
- **Poor Coordination:** Handover delays between departments or staff can be significant.

**Process Mining Insight:**  
Apply **Variant Analysis** to identify common and rare paths. Variants where patients take longer may reveal unnecessary handoffs or deviations.

#### **C. Variability in Activity Durations**
- **Service Time Variability:** Some activities (e.g., complex consultations) take significantly longer than others, blocking downstream resources.
- **Uneven Patient Complexity:** New patients often require more time than follow-ups.

**Process Mining Insight:**  
Calculate **service time distributions** per activity and resource. Overlay these with **waiting time distributions** to see if variability correlates with queue delays.

#### **D. Appointment Scheduling Policies**
- **Inflexible Blocks:** Fixed time slots may not align with actual demand.
- **Overbooking or Underbooking:** Poor forecasting leads to idle time or congestion.

**Process Mining Insight:**  
Use **Timeline Visualization** to view patient start times per activity. Overlay with resource schedules to detect scheduling mismatches.

#### **E. Patient Arrival Patterns**
- **Peak Hours:** High patient arrivals at specific times overwhelm resources.
- **Emergency Walk-ins:** Unplanned arrivals can disrupt scheduled flow.

**Process Mining Insight:**  
Use **Event Distribution Over Time** to visualize arrival patterns and correlate with queue buildup.

#### **F. Patient Type and Urgency Differences**
- **Urgent Patients:** May bypass some steps or receive priority, creating unpredictability in flow.
- **New vs. Follow-up:** New patients often have longer initial assessments.

**Process Mining Insight:**  
Segment event logs by **Patient Type** and **Urgency**, and compare queue metrics across segments.

## **3. Data-Driven Optimization Strategies**

### **Strategy 1: Dynamic Resource Allocation Based on Real-Time Queue Metrics**

- **Targets:**  
  Queues after **Registration  Nurse Assessment** and **Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Tests** (common handoff points).

- **Root Cause Addressed:**  
  Resource bottlenecks due to uneven staff distribution or room availability, especially during peak hours.

- **Data Support:**  
  - Resource utilization heatmaps show periods of high nurse or doctor idle time vs. peak demand.
  - Queue frequency and 90th percentile data identify peak strain times.

- **Implementation:**  
  - Introduce **flexible staffing models:** Cross-train staff to cover multiple roles during peak demand.
  - Use **real-time queue dashboards** to trigger on-demand reallocation of nurses or diagnostic techs.
  - Schedule **floating staff** during identified high-demand windows.

- **Expected Impact:**  
  - Reduce average waiting time for Nurse Assessment by **25%** during peak hours.
  - Reduce 90th percentile wait for diagnostic tests by **30%**.

---

### **Strategy 2: Revised Appointment Scheduling with Buffer Zones and Patient Stratification**

- **Targets:**  
  Queues after **Doctor Consultation  Check-out** and **Registration  First Consultation**.

- **Root Cause Addressed:**  
  Poor scheduling logic leading to clustering of patients and inefficient use of time.

- **Data Support:**  
  - Timeline visualizations showing patient arrival clustering.
  - Service time variability data for different patient types (New vs. Follow-up).
  - Historical wait time data by time-of-day and day-of-week.

- **Implementation:**  
  - **Stratified Scheduling:** Separate time blocks for **New**, **Follow-up**, and **Urgent** patients.
  - **Buffer Zones:** Introduce short buffers (10–15 min) between appointments to absorb variability.
  - **Staggered Starts:** Distribute patient start times evenly across the day rather than clustering in the morning.

- **Expected Impact:**  
  - Reduce overall visit duration by **15–20%**.
  - Decrease first-consult wait times by **40%** for new patients.

---

### **Strategy 3: Parallel Processing and Diagnostic Fast-Tracking**

- **Targets:**  
  Queues after **Doctor Consultation  Diagnostic Tests** (e.g., ECG, Blood Tests).

- **Root Cause Addressed:**  
  Sequential dependencies that force patients to wait for diagnostics after the consultation.

- **Data Support:**  
  - Variant analysis showing patients who undergo diagnostic tests waited significantly longer.
  - Service time data showing diagnostic tests are often scheduled late in the visit.

- **Implementation:**  
  - **Pre-emptive Diagnostic Scheduling:** For patients whose consultations are likely to require diagnostics (based on specialty or diagnosis), schedule the test **during** or **immediately after** the consultation.
  - **Parallel Processing:** Allow nurses to collect samples or schedule diagnostics **before** the doctor delivers the final diagnosis, where clinically appropriate.
  - **Dedicated Fast-Track Lanes:** For routine diagnostics (e.g., ECG, X-ray), establish fast-track lines staffed by dedicated techs.

- **Expected Impact:**  
  - Reduce post-consultation waiting time by **35%**.
  - Improve patient satisfaction scores related to diagnostic wait times.

## **4. Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints**

### **Potential Trade-offs and Mitigations**

| Strategy | Potential Trade-off | Mitigation Strategy |
|---------|---------------------|----------------------|
| **Dynamic Resource Allocation** | Increased labor costs due to overtime or hiring | Use data to justify cost vs. benefit; focus on peak hours only; cross-training reduces need for new hires |
| **Revised Scheduling** | Risk of underutilization during off-peak hours | Use historical demand data to optimize block sizes; leverage low-demand time for administrative tasks |
| **Parallel Processing** | Risk of overbooking diagnostics; potential delays if fast-track resources are full | Implement real-time capacity monitoring; maintain standard queue as backup; use predictive modeling for demand |

### **Balancing Objectives**

- **Cost vs. Service:** Prioritize queue reductions that yield **high patient satisfaction gains** with **low marginal cost** (e.g., scheduling changes vs. new hires).
- **Thoroughness of Care:** Ensure fast-tracking doesn’t compromise documentation or consultation quality. Use **patient feedback loops** and **quality audits** to monitor.
- **Staff Workload:** Avoid creating new bottlenecks by overburdening staff. Use **workload balancing analysis** to ensure equitable distribution.

**Balancing Framework:**
1. **Quantify impact** of each strategy on cost, wait time, and quality.
2. **Pilot changes** on a small scale before full rollout.
3. **Monitor KPIs** continuously post-implementation.

## **5. Measuring Success**

### **Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)**

| KPI | Description | Target |
|-----|-------------|--------|
| **Average Waiting Time (per transition)** | Mean time between activities | Reduce by 20–30% in top 3 bottlenecks |
| **90th Percentile Waiting Time** | Patient experience tail risk | Reduce by 25% |
| **Overall Visit Duration** | End-to-end time from Registration to Check-out | Reduce by 15–20% |
| **Patient Satisfaction Score (Post-Visit Survey)** | Measured via surveys (e.g., NPS, wait time satisfaction) | Increase by 10–15 points |
| **Resource Utilization Rate** | % of time resources are actively used | Optimize to 75–85% (avoid >90% to prevent bottlenecks) |
| **No-Show Rate** | % of scheduled appointments not honored | Monitor for increase due to scheduling changes |

### **Ongoing Monitoring and Continuous Improvement**

- **Real-Time Event Logging:** Continue capturing **start/complete timestamps** for all activities to enable **real-time queue monitoring**.
  
- **Dashboards:** Implement **operational dashboards** showing:
  - Current queue lengths
  - Wait time trends by hour/day
  - Resource utilization in real time

- **Monthly Process Audits:** Conduct **monthly queue mining** using the same event log to:
  - Re-calculate KPIs
  - Identify new or re-emerging bottlenecks
  - Validate effectiveness of implemented strategies

- **Feedback Loops:**
  - **Patient Feedback:** Regular surveys to assess perceived wait times.
  - **Staff Feedback:** Engage frontline staff to identify practical barriers to new processes.

- **Predictive Analytics:** Use historical data to **forecast demand** and **proactively adjust schedules** before peak times.
