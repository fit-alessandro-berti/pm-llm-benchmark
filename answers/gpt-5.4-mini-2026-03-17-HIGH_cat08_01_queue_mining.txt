## 1) Queue Identification and Characterization

### A. How to calculate waiting times from the event log

Because the log contains both **START** and **COMPLETE** timestamps, each patient visit can be reconstructed as a sequence of activities.

For each case (visit):

- **Service time for an activity** = `COMPLETE(activity) - START(activity)`
- **Queue / waiting time between activities** = `START(next activity) - COMPLETE(previous activity)`

So if a patient finishes registration at 09:08:45 and nurse assessment starts at 09:15:20, then the waiting time before nurse assessment is:

- `09:15:20 - 09:08:45 = 6 min 35 sec`

### Definition of “waiting time” in this context

In queue mining, waiting time is the **non-service time between two consecutive executed steps where the patient is ready for the next step but no resource is yet serving them**.

It typically includes:

- Waiting for a clerk after registration
- Waiting for a nurse after registration
- Waiting for a doctor after nurse assessment
- Waiting for diagnostics after consultation
- Waiting for check-out after the last clinical step

It does **not** include the time spent actively being served in the activity itself.

> If arrival time to the clinic is available, you can also compute the wait before the first activity:
> `START(Registration) - Arrival time`

### Important preprocessing notes

- Pair each START with its corresponding COMPLETE for the same case and activity.
- Order activities per case.
- Flag or correct:
  - overlapping timestamps
  - negative queue times
  - missing START/COMPLETE pairs
- Segment analysis by:
  - patient type
  - urgency
  - specialty
  - day of week
  - time of day
  - resource/staff member

### Metrics to characterize queues

For each queue type, e.g. Registration  Nurse, Nurse  Doctor, Doctor  ECG, etc., calculate:

1. **Average waiting time**
2. **Median waiting time**
3. **Maximum waiting time**
4. **90th percentile waiting time**
   - Very useful because waiting times are usually skewed
5. **Queue frequency**
   - Number of cases that experience that queue
6. **Total waiting minutes**
   - Sum of all waiting times for that queue across all cases
7. **Percentage of cases above a threshold**
   - e.g., `% waiting > 15 min`
8. **Share of total visit time spent waiting**
   - `Total waiting / Total cycle time`
9. **Variation / standard deviation**
   - High variation often signals unstable operations
10. **Segment-specific metrics**
   - New vs follow-up
   - Urgent vs normal
   - Specialty-specific
   - Resource-specific

### How to identify the most critical queues

Do not prioritize only by average waiting time. A queue is critical if it combines:

- **Long waits**
- **High volume**
- **High variability**
- **High downstream impact**
- **Clinical or fairness relevance**

A practical prioritization score could be based on:

- `Average wait × number of affected cases`
- plus weighting for:
  - urgent patients affected
  - p90 wait
  - downstream delay propagation
  - service-level breaches

### Recommended criteria for immediate attention

Focus first on queues that:

- have the **highest total waiting minutes**
- have **high p90 waiting time**, not just high averages
- affect **many patients**
- disproportionately affect **urgent** or **new** patients
- create **backlogs** that propagate to later activities
- occur at **resource bottlenecks** with high utilization

Example:
- A queue with 25-minute average wait but only 10 cases/day may be less urgent than a 12-minute queue affecting 150 cases/day.
- A queue that causes late doctor consultation also delays tests and check-out, so it deserves higher priority.

## 2) Root Cause Analysis

Finding the queue is only the first step. The real value comes from explaining **why** it happens.

### Likely root causes in this clinic

#### 1. Resource bottlenecks
Possible issues:
- too few clerks at registration during peak hours
- nurse understaffing
- limited doctor availability by specialty
- scarce rooms or equipment, such as ECG or x-ray units

Symptoms in the log:
- long waiting times before a specific activity
- high utilization of certain resources
- queues concentrated in certain shifts or days

#### 2. Activity dependencies and handovers
Some delays come from how work is handed over between roles:
- registration  nurse  doctor  diagnostics  doctor review  check-out
- every handover adds coordination delay
- some handovers may require room transfer or chart review

Symptoms:
- waits are high specifically after transitions between departments or resources
- certain handover combinations consistently perform worse

#### 3. Variability in service times
If activity durations vary widely:
- some doctors spend much longer per patient
- some nurses may take longer due to complexity or inconsistent work methods
- diagnostic tests may have unstable processing times

Symptoms:
- waiting time spikes follow long service-time cases
- queues build up after unpredictable activities

#### 4. Appointment scheduling policies
Scheduling may be creating artificial congestion:
- too many appointments booked at the same time
- slot lengths not aligned with patient complexity
- no reserved slots for urgent cases or complex new patients
- overbooking without adequate slack

Symptoms:
- arrivals cluster around the top of the hour
- queue peaks are systematic, not random
- certain patient types consistently wait longer

#### 5. Patient arrival patterns
Patients may arrive in waves:
- early morning surges
- post-lunch surges
- day-of-week effects
- seasonality over the six-month period

Symptoms:
- queues follow arrival peaks
- resource capacity is sufficient on average but not at peak times

#### 6. Differences by patient type or urgency
- New patients may need longer registration and assessment.
- Follow-up patients may need less work but still wait in the same queue.
- Urgent patients may be buried in the same queue as routine visits.

Symptoms:
- large performance differences by patient segment
- unfair waits for urgent cases
- process variants differ by type

### How process mining helps uncover root causes

Beyond simple queue calculation, use the following process mining analyses:

#### A. Resource analysis
Identify:
- utilization by staff member
- workload distribution
- idle time vs overload
- handover frequency

Useful for spotting:
- overburdened clerks
- uneven doctor load
- underused staff at certain times

#### B. Bottleneck analysis
Compare:
- queue length
- waiting time
- resource utilization
- service time

If a stage has:
- high waiting time
- high utilization
- stable service time

then it is likely a true capacity bottleneck.

#### C. Variant analysis
Compare fast vs slow cases:
- Which process variants are associated with long waits?
- Are long-wait cases routed differently?
- Do urgent or new patients follow more delay-prone paths?

This helps reveal whether the issue is:
- a particular pathway
- a specialty-specific process
- a patient-type-specific workflow

#### D. Case clustering / segmentation
Group visits by:
- patient type
- urgency
- specialty
- time of day
- resource route

Then compare queue performance across clusters.

#### E. Performance spectrum / timeline analysis
Shows where delays accumulate over the visit and whether they are isolated or cascading.

#### F. Calendar and arrival analysis
Identify:
- peak hours
- peak weekdays
- seasonal trends
- shift-based effects

#### G. Conformance to expected workflow
If the intended clinical pathway is known, compare it to the observed one.
Delays often arise where reality diverges from the designed process.

## 3) Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

Below are four concrete strategies. You only asked for three, but these work well together.

---

### Strategy 1: Dynamic staffing and cross-training at peak bottlenecks

**Targets**
- Registration queue
- Nurse assessment queue
- Check-out queue

**Root cause addressed**
- Resource bottlenecks
- Peak-hour demand spikes
- Uneven workload across staff

**How data supports it**
- Resource analysis shows which staff or shifts have the highest utilization and longest waits.
- Time-of-day analysis shows when arrivals exceed capacity.
- Queue mining reveals which stages create the most backlog.

**What to do**
- Reallocate staff during peak arrival windows.
- Use cross-trained float staff who can help registration or check-out.
- Stagger breaks and lunches to avoid simultaneous capacity drops.
- Use real-time queue thresholds to trigger temporary support.

**Expected impact**
- Often reduces peak waiting times by **15–35%** in high-volume administrative queues.
- Can lower p90 wait more than the mean wait, which is important for patient experience.

---

### Strategy 2: Redesign appointment templates and smooth arrivals

**Targets**
- Registration queue
- Doctor consultation queue
- Entire front end of the visit

**Root cause addressed**
- Appointment bunching
- Poor slot-length fit
- Mismatch between arrival patterns and staffing

**How data supports it**
- Historical event logs reveal actual service time distributions by patient type and specialty.
- Arrival-pattern analysis shows where congestion starts.
- Variant analysis may show that new patients take much longer than follow-ups, making one-size-fits-all slots inefficient.

**What to do**
- Use different slot lengths for:
  - new patients
  - follow-ups
  - complex specialty visits
- Reserve urgent slots each session.
- Stagger appointment times rather than booking many at the same start time.
- Consider controlled overbooking only where no-show rates justify it.

**Expected impact**
- Reduces arrival surges and downstream queues.
- Typical improvement: **10–30% lower average waiting time**, with especially strong impact on doctor consultation congestion.
- Also reduces late-day backlog and overtime.

---

### Strategy 3: Create fast-track or segmented pathways

**Targets**
- Urgent patient queue
- Follow-up queue
- Simple diagnostic or low-complexity cases

**Root cause addressed**
- Mixed patient complexity in one queue
- Urgent patients waiting behind routine patients
- Lack of triage-based flow design

**How data supports it**
- Patient-type and urgency segmentation often shows clear performance gaps.
- Variant analysis can show that urgent cases still follow the same slow route as routine cases.
- Queue mining can reveal that the same single queue is serving very different demand types.

**What to do**
- Separate urgent patients into a priority lane.
- Create a fast-track path for routine follow-ups.
- Dedicate certain time blocks or rooms to simpler cases.
- Use triage rules at registration or nurse assessment.

**Expected impact**
- Urgent patient waits can drop by **40–60%** if priority routing is currently absent.
- Total clinic throughput may improve if low-complexity cases are processed faster.
- Patient fairness improves, especially for time-sensitive visits.

---

### Strategy 4: Parallelize work and move preparatory tasks earlier

**Targets**
- Doctor  diagnostic test waiting
- Diagnostic  review waiting
- Some administrative delays

**Root cause addressed**
- Sequential dependencies that force unnecessary idle time
- Late ordering of tests or paperwork

**How data supports it**
- Process mining may show that tests are only ordered after long consultation steps, followed by long waiting before execution.
- If diagnostics are routinely needed for certain visit types, the order can sometimes be prepared earlier.

**What to do**
- Pre-order common tests based on appointment reason or referral type.
- Begin room prep or patient prep before the consultation ends where clinically appropriate.
- Run tests in parallel with other low-risk administrative steps.
- Use electronic ordering and automatic notification to reduce handover delay.

**Expected impact**
- Can reduce end-to-end visit duration by **10–20%** in pathways with frequent diagnostics.
- Particularly useful where test queues are causing long downstream waits.

## 4) Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints

Every improvement can create new issues. The goal is not just faster flow, but better overall performance.

### Key trade-offs

#### 1. Shifting bottlenecks elsewhere
If you improve registration, the bottleneck may move to nurses or doctors.
- Example: faster check-in may simply expose a doctor shortage later.

#### 2. Increased staffing cost
Dynamic staffing or dedicated fast tracks may require:
- extra staffing
- overtime
- reallocation from other departments

#### 3. Staff workload and burnout
Reducing waits by pushing staff harder can worsen workload and morale.

#### 4. Quality-of-care concerns
Overly aggressive throughput goals can reduce:
- consultation thoroughness
- patient communication quality
- documentation completeness

#### 5. Fairness and access concerns
Fast-track lanes may be perceived as unfair if not designed transparently.
Digital self-service may disadvantage older or less tech-savvy patients.

#### 6. Over-ordering or unnecessary parallelization
Ordering tests too early may reduce waiting but increase:
- waste
- unnecessary resource use
- patient inconvenience

### How to balance conflicting objectives

Use a **multi-objective approach**, not a single metric.

Balance:
- waiting time
- cycle time
- cost
- staff workload
- quality and safety
- patient equity

Practical methods:
- define service-level targets, such as:
  - urgent patients: wait under X minutes
  - routine registration p90 under Y minutes
- use **Pareto analysis** to compare scenarios
- run **discrete-event simulation** using the mined process model before deployment
- pilot changes in one specialty or shift before scaling
- monitor for unintended consequences, especially shifting bottlenecks

## 5) Measuring Success

### KPIs to track after implementation

#### Waiting and flow KPIs
- Average waiting time per queue
- Median waiting time per queue
- 90th percentile waiting time per queue
- Maximum waiting time
- Total waiting time per case
- Total visit duration / cycle time
- Waiting time as a percentage of total visit time
- % of cases exceeding SLA thresholds

#### Operational KPIs
- Queue length over time
- Resource utilization by staff/room/equipment
- Throughput per hour/day
- Overtime hours
- Idle time
- Number of backlogged cases

#### Experience and quality KPIs
- Patient satisfaction score
- Complaint volume
- No-show rate
- Left-without-being-seen rate
- Rework or repeated testing
- Delayed or incomplete documentation

### How to monitor continuously

Use the same event log structure on an ongoing basis:

1. **Automated extraction**
   - ingest new START/COMPLETE events weekly or daily

2. **Real-time or periodic KPI dashboards**
   - by queue
   - by specialty
   - by patient type
   - by urgency
   - by shift/time of day

3. **Control charts / trend monitoring**
   - detect drift or deterioration early

4. **Segmentation monitoring**
   - check whether improvements help all groups or only some

5. **Repeat process mining regularly**
   - e.g., monthly or quarterly
   - compare current process maps to baseline
   - look for new bottlenecks after changes

6. **Alert thresholds**
   - e.g., p90 wait exceeds limit
   - utilization exceeds safe threshold
   - urgent patients experience excess waits

### Success definition

A successful improvement program should show:

- lower average and p90 waits
- reduced total cycle time
- fewer high-wait cases
- no major cost increase
- no reduction in care quality
- no harmful shift of bottlenecks to another stage
