This visualization is a **Causal Net**, also called a C-net. It's a type of process model used in process mining and business process management to show how activities in a workflow are causally related — which ones must happen, which are optional, and which can loop.

### How to read the diagram

The **boxes** are activities, labeled `a` through `z`. The **black dots** on the arcs define the split and join behavior for each activity. The legend in the gray box tells you how to interpret those dots.

**Key concepts from the legend:**
- **XOR-split**: Choose exactly one of the outgoing paths
- **AND-split**: Take all outgoing paths in parallel 
- **OR-split**: Take one or more of the outgoing paths
- **XOR-join**: Triggered by exactly one incoming path
- **AND-join**: Wait for all incoming paths to complete
- **OR-join**: Triggered when one or more incoming paths complete

### Walking through this specific process

This looks like a request-handling workflow, maybe for insurance claims or customer service tickets.

**1. Start: Register request**
- `a: register request` is the start activity
- It has an OR-split with 3 dots going to `b`, `c`, `d`. That means after registering, you must do *one or more* of: examine thoroughly, examine casually, check ticket

**2. Investigation phase**
- `b: examine thoroughly`, `c: examine casually`, `d: check ticket` all run
- All three have arcs pointing to `e: decide`
- `e: decide` has an OR-join, meaning it can start once any of `b`, `c`, or `d` finishes. It doesn't have to wait for all of them

**3. Decision and outcome**
- `e: decide` has an XOR-split to `g` and `h`. So you either:
  - `g: pay compensation` OR 
  - `h: reject request`
- Both `g` and `h` then flow into `z: end` via an XOR-join

**4. The loop: Reinitiate**
- Here's the interesting part: `e: decide` also has an arc to `f: reinitiate request`
- `f` has an OR-split back to `b`, `c`, and `d`
- This means after a decision, the process can loop back to re-examine the case. That’s common if new info comes up or the first decision wasn’t final
- `f` also has an OR-join from `b`, `c`, `d`, so reinitiation can be triggered by any of those activities too

### What this model tells you
| Aspect | What it means here |
| --- | --- |
| **Flexibility** | The OR-splits/joins mean the process isn't rigid. You can do thorough OR casual exam, or both, plus ticket check |
| **Loops** | The `e → f → b/c/d → e` cycle allows rework. Real processes often need this |
| **Exclusive outcome** | The XOR-split after `decide` ensures you can't both pay and reject the same request |
| **No forced parallelism** | Because it uses OR instead of AND at the start, you don't *have* to do all three checks |

So in plain English: Someone registers a request. The system can then check the ticket, do a casual exam, and/or do a thorough exam. Once any of those are done, a decision is made. The decision is either pay out, reject, or kick it back for more investigation. If it's paid or rejected, the case ends.
