## 2. Root Cause Analysis

### Root Cause 1: Claim Complexity Drives Multiple Document Requests

| Complexity | Avg Duration | Avg # of Document Requests |
|------------|-------------|---------------------------|
| Low | ~1.46 hours | 0 |
| Medium | ~25.9 hours | 1 |
| High | ~62.7 hours | 2.5 |

**This is the strongest driver of long lead times.** There is a near-perfect correlation between complexity and duration. High-complexity claims trigger multiple rounds of "Request Additional Documents," and each round introduces significant waiting time (often overnight or multi-day delays while awaiting customer responses).

**Key bottleneck timestamps in high-complexity cases:**
- **Case 2003:** Gap of **23 hours** between the second document request (Apr 1, 17:00) and approval (Apr 2, 16:00)
- **Case 2005:** Gap of **29.5 hours** between the second request (Apr 2, 17:00) and the third request (Apr 3, 15:00), then another **19 hours** to approval (Apr 4, 10:00)

Each additional document request cycle adds roughly **1û1.5 days** of elapsed time.

### Root Cause 2: Resource-Specific Patterns

| Resource | Cases | Observations |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| **Adjuster_Lisa** | 2002, 2004, 2005 | Handled Case 2005 (longest case), made **3 document requests** across multiple days |
| **Adjuster_Mike** | 2001, 2003 | Handled Case 2003 with 2 document requests |
| **Manager_Bill** | 2003, 2005 | Approved *only* the two high-complexity cases; approval came with significant delays |
| **Manager_Ann** | 2001, 2002, 2004 | Approved low/medium cases promptly (within minutes to hours of prior step) |

**Key finding regarding Manager_Bill:**
- In Case 2003: The last document request was Apr 1 at 17:00, but Manager_Bill didn't approve until Apr 2 at 16:00 ù a **23-hour gap**.
- In Case 2005: The last document request was Apr 3 at 15:00, but Manager_Bill didn't approve until Apr 4 at 10:00 ù a **19-hour gap**.

By contrast, **Manager_Ann** approved cases within 15 minutes to a few hours of the preceding activity. This suggests **Manager_Bill may be a bottleneck** for high-complexity approvals ù possibly due to workload, availability, or the inherently longer review required for complex claims.

**Adjuster_Lisa** on Case 2005 issued three separate document requests over three days, suggesting either inefficiency in identifying all required documents upfront, or difficulty obtaining responses from the claimant.

### Root Cause 3: Region Analysis

| Region | Cases | Avg Duration |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| A | 2001 (Low), 2003 (High) | ~24.9 hours avg |
| B | 2004 (Low), 2002 (Medium), 2005 (High) | ~34.8 hours avg |

Region B has a slightly higher average, but this is **confounded by complexity distribution** ù Region B includes the most extreme case (2005, High complexity with 3 document requests). When controlling for complexity, **region alone does not appear to be an independent root cause**. Both regions handle low-complexity claims efficiently (~1.5 hours).

## 3. Summary of Root Causes and Recommendations

### Root Cause Summary

| Priority | Root Cause | Impact | Evidence |
|----------|-----------|--------|----------|
| **1 (Primary)** | High complexity  multiple document request cycles | Each cycle adds ~1û1.5 days | Cases 2003 (2 requests, 48 hrs) and 2005 (3 requests, 77 hrs) |
| **2 (Secondary)** | Manager_Bill as approval bottleneck for complex cases | 19û23 hour approval delays | Both high-complexity cases routed to Manager_Bill show overnight waits before approval |
| **3 (Contributing)** | Serial/iterative document requests by adjusters | Documents requested one-at-a-time across days instead of all at once | Adjuster_Lisa in Case 2005 made 3 separate requests over 3 days |

### Recommendations

1. **Implement Comprehensive Upfront Document Checklists for High-Complexity Claims**
   - Instead of iteratively requesting documents (one request  wait  discover another gap  request again), adjusters should use a **standardized checklist** at the evaluation stage for high-complexity claims to request **all needed documents in a single round**. This alone could reduce Case 2005's duration from ~77 hours to potentially ~25û30 hours.

2. **Establish SLAs and Escalation Paths for Manager Approvals**
   - Manager_Bill's approval delays of 19û23 hours suggest either capacity constraints or lack of urgency signals. Implement a **maximum approval turnaround time** (e.g., 4 hours for high-complexity claims) with automated escalation to an alternate manager if the SLA is breached.

3. **Enable Parallel Processing for Complex Claims**
   - Allow document collection and preliminary approval review to happen **in parallel** rather than strictly sequentially. For instance, the manager could begin reviewing available documentation while additional documents are still being gathered.

4. **Dedicated Fast-Track Resources for High-Complexity Claims**
   - Consider assigning **specialized senior adjusters** to high-complexity cases who are trained to identify all documentation needs in one pass, reducing the iterative back-and-forth cycle.

5. **Monitor and Balance Workload Across Managers**
   - If Manager_Bill is consistently assigned complex cases (which inherently take longer to review), rebalance the workload or provide additional support to prevent them from becoming a systemic bottleneck.

6. **Customer Communication Improvements**
   - The multi-day gaps between document requests and responses suggest slow customer turnaround. Implement **automated reminders**, digital upload portals, and clear deadlines to accelerate document submission by claimants.
