A standard P2P flow is usually:

**Create Purchase Requisition  Approve Purchase Requisition  Issue Purchase Order  Receive Goods  Vendor Invoice Received  Match Invoice to PO / goods receipt (2-way or 3-way match)  Release Payment  Close Case**

Using that as the baseline:

## Case-by-case assessment

### Case 1001
**Sequence:**  
Create PR  Approve PR  Issue PO  Receive Goods  Invoice Received  Match  Payment  Close

**Assessment:**  
This looks **normal** and consistent with standard P2P controls.

---

### Case 1002
**Sequence:**  
Create PR  **Issue PO**  Invoice Received  **Release Payment**  **Approve PR**  Receive Goods  Match Invoice to PO  Close

**Why this is anomalous:**

1. **PO issued before requisition approval**
   - The purchase order was created before the purchase requisition was approved.
   - This bypasses an important authorization control.

2. **Payment released before approval**
   - Payment happened before the requisition was even approved.
   - This is a major control failure: the company paid for something before formal internal authorization.

3. **Payment released before goods receipt**
   - Goods were received only after payment.
   - While prepayments can happen in special cases, they are not the standard P2P pattern and are riskier.

4. **Payment released before invoice matching**
   - The invoice was matched to the PO only after payment.
   - Normally, matching is done before payment to verify that the order, receipt, and invoice align.

**Why problematic:**  
This case suggests **control circumvention**, weak approval discipline, and risk of:
- unauthorized spending,
- paying for undelivered goods,
- duplicate or incorrect payments,
- fraud or maverick buying.

---

### Case 1003
**Sequence:**  
Create PR  Approve PR  Issue PO  **Release Payment**  Invoice Received  Receive Goods  Match Invoice to PO  Close

**Why this is anomalous:**

1. **Payment before invoice receipt**
   - In standard P2P, payment is triggered by an invoice.
   - Paying before even receiving the invoice is highly unusual.

2. **Payment before goods receipt**
   - The company paid before confirming delivery.
   - That exposes the buyer to non-delivery risk.

3. **Payment before matching**
   - Matching occurred after payment.
   - This defeats the purpose of invoice/PO/receipt controls.

**Why problematic:**  
This looks like a **prepayment without visible justification**. In some businesses advance payments exist, but they require explicit controls and are exceptions, not the norm. Risks include:
- overpayment,
- paying for goods not yet delivered,
- inability to detect invoice discrepancies before cash leaves,
- reduced auditability.

---

### Case 1004
**Sequence:**  
Create PR  Approve PR  **Receive Goods**  **Issue PO**  Invoice Received  Match  Payment  Close

**Why this is anomalous:**

1. **Goods received before purchase order issued**
   - Normally the PO is sent to the vendor before delivery.
   - Receiving goods before a PO suggests the order was not formally placed in the system in time.

**Why problematic:**  
This may indicate:
- **retroactive PO creation**,
- off-system or informal purchasing,
- emergency buying without proper procurement controls,
- weak commitment and budget control.

This is often considered a form of **maverick buying** or backdated documentation. Even though the later steps look orderly, the key issue is that the formal purchasing document came too late.

## Summary of anomalies

### Clearly normal
- **1001**

### Anomalous / risky
- **1002:** PO before approval; payment before approval, before goods receipt, and before matching.
- **1003:** Payment before invoice, before goods receipt, and before matching.
- **1004:** Goods receipt before PO issuance.

## Most serious problems
The most severe anomalies are in:

### **Case 1002**
Because multiple core controls are violated:
- authorization control,
- receipt confirmation before payment,
- matching before payment.

### **Case 1003**
Because payment is made before the basic payment trigger documents/events exist.

---

## Business interpretation
These anomalies matter because P2P controls are designed to ensure:
- only authorized purchases happen,
- the vendor actually delivered,
- the invoice is valid and accurate,
- payment is made only after verification.

When that order is broken, the organization becomes more exposed to:
- fraud,
- unauthorized procurement,
- duplicate or erroneous payments,
- audit findings,
- supplier disputes,
- weak financial control.
