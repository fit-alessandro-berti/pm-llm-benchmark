# 1) Queue Identification and Characterization

## 1.1 How to calculate waiting time from the event log

Because the log contains **start** and **complete** timestamps for each activity, we can reconstruct each patient visit as a sequence of activities and measure:

- **Service time** for an activity = `complete timestamp - start timestamp`
- **Waiting time between activities** = `start timestamp of next activity - complete timestamp of previous activity`

### Example
For V1001:

- Registration completes at 09:08:45
- Nurse Assessment starts at 09:15:20

So:

- **Queue/wait time before Nurse Assessment** = 6 min 35 sec

This is the core queue mining measure: the idle time between one step finishing and the next step beginning.

### What counts as “waiting time” here?
In this setting, waiting time is the **time a patient is ready for the next step but the next resource/activity is not yet available or not yet triggered**.

Depending on process design, this may include:
- Waiting in reception queue
- Waiting for nurse
- Waiting for doctor
- Waiting for diagnostic room/equipment
- Waiting for check-out

It may also include some non-value-added delays such as:
- Internal handoff delays
- Transport/walking delays if they are not explicitly modeled as activities
- Delays caused by scheduling gaps or batching

If the event log has both start and completion timestamps, we can separate:
- **Queue time** = time between previous completion and next start
- **Processing time** = time between start and completion of the same activity

---

## 1.2 Key metrics to characterize queues

For each queue between activity A and activity B, calculate:

### Central tendency
- **Average waiting time**
- **Median waiting time**

Median is especially useful because waiting times are usually skewed by extreme delays.

### Tail and variability
- **90th percentile waiting time**
- **95th percentile waiting time**
- **Maximum waiting time**
- **Standard deviation / IQR**

These show whether the queue is consistently bad or occasionally catastrophic.

### Frequency and prevalence
- **Queue frequency**: how often the wait occurs across cases
- **Percentage of cases experiencing waits above a threshold**  
  Example: % of visits with wait > 15 min before doctor consultation
- **Total accumulated waiting time** across all cases

This helps distinguish:
- A queue that is slightly long but affects everyone
- A queue that is extremely long but rare

### Impact-related metrics
- **Waiting time by patient type**: New vs Follow-up
- **Waiting time by urgency**: Normal vs Urgent
- **Waiting time by specialty**
- **Waiting time by resource**
- **Waiting time by day/time slot**

### Operational metrics
- **Resource utilization**
- **Queue length over time**
- **Throughput per hour**
- **Service time variability**
- **Arrival rate vs service rate**

---

## 1.3 How to identify the most critical queues

The “most critical” queues are not necessarily the longest average waits. I would rank queues using a combination of:

### 1. High average and high percentile wait
A queue with a high mean and high 90th percentile is both slow and unstable.

### 2. High frequency
A queue that affects many patients has greater overall operational and patient satisfaction impact.

### 3. High total waiting burden
Total wait = average wait × number of affected cases  
This captures the overall time lost to patients.

### 4. Strong impact on urgent or high-value patient groups
Even if not the longest overall, a queue is critical if it disproportionately affects:
- Urgent patients
- New patients
- Specialized pathways
- Patients likely to abandon care or complain

### 5. Downstream propagation effect
A queue is critical if it creates delays in later steps, e.g.:
- Delays before doctor consultation push back tests, checkout, and room turnover
- A bottleneck at nurse assessment causes cascading congestion

### Practical prioritization rule
I would create a priority score such as:

**Criticality Score =**
- 30% average wait
- 20% 90th percentile wait
- 20% frequency
- 20% total waiting burden
- 10% impact on urgent/new patients

This gives a balanced, data-driven ranking.

# 2) Root Cause Analysis

Once queues are identified, the next step is to explain **why** they occur.

## 2.1 Likely root causes in this clinic

### A. Resource bottlenecks
Examples:
- Too few nurses during peak arrival periods
- Limited number of physicians in a specialty
- Diagnostic rooms or equipment as constrained resources
- Checkout desk understaffed at end of clinic sessions

Indicators:
- High utilization
- Long queue build-up at specific times
- Resource-specific waits varying by staff member, room, or device

---

### B. Activity dependencies and handovers
Delays may occur because:
- Patients must complete one step before another can begin
- Handoffs between departments are slow
- Staff need manual confirmation before next activity starts

Indicators:
- Consistent gaps between activities
- Delays concentrated after certain handoffs
- Long idle time despite available downstream resources

---

### C. Variability in service times
Even if average service time is acceptable, high variability creates queues.

Examples:
- Some consultations take much longer than others
- Complex new patients take longer than follow-ups
- Certain nurses or doctors work slower/faster than others

Indicators:
- Wide dispersion in activity durations
- Long-tail service time distributions
- Resource-to-resource performance differences

---

### D. Appointment scheduling policies
Queues often come from mismatched scheduling and capacity:
- Overbooking in certain time slots
- Fixed schedules not aligned with actual arrival patterns
- Too many new patients scheduled together
- Diagnostic appointments clustered after consultations

Indicators:
- Arrival peaks preceding queue spikes
- Delays increasing at specific times of day
- High waiting despite average utilization not being extreme

---

### E. Patient arrival patterns
Patients may arrive:
- Early
- Late
- In bursts due to transportation or referrals
- Clustered around opening times

Indicators:
- Strong temporal peaks in arrivals
- Queues worsened at opening, lunch, or late afternoon
- Differences across weekdays or clinic sessions

---

### F. Patient-type differences
New patients often require:
- Longer registration
- More detailed nurse assessment
- More comprehensive consultation

Urgent patients may be inserted ahead, causing:
- Rescheduling and queue disruption for normal patients
- Higher variability in downstream flow

Indicators:
- Variant analysis showing different paths and durations
- Queue times stratified by patient type and urgency

---

## 2.2 How process mining helps identify root causes

### Resource analysis
Shows:
- Which staff members or rooms are most overloaded
- Which resources cause the longest waits
- Differences in performance among resources

Useful for finding:
- Understaffed stations
- Uneven work allocation
- Skill-based constraints

---

### Bottleneck analysis
Identifies:
- Where cases accumulate
- Which activity has the highest waiting backlog
- Where throughput slows most

Useful for:
- Determining whether registration, nurse assessment, doctor consultation, diagnostics, or checkout is the dominant bottleneck

---

### Variant analysis
Groups cases by route/sequence:
- New patient cardiology path
- Follow-up cardiology path
- Urgent pathways
- Patients requiring tests vs no tests

Useful for:
- Seeing which flow patterns consume the most time
- Finding paths with repeated delays
- Identifying special-case pathways that need separate handling

---

### Conformance and deviation analysis
Compares actual flow to desired process:
- Are patients skipping steps?
- Are there rework loops?
- Are diagnostics causing unexpected rerouting?

Useful for:
- Detecting inefficiencies and exceptions
- Seeing whether the process is too rigid or too fragmented

---

### Time-of-day and day-of-week analysis
Reveals:
- Peak congestion periods
- Staffing mismatches
- Session-specific overload

Useful for:
- Aligning staffing with demand patterns

---

### Correlation and segmentation analysis
By segmenting data by:
- Patient type
- Urgency
- Specialty
- Doctor
- Day/time

you can isolate which subpopulations experience the worst queues.

# 3) Data-Driven Optimization Strategies

Below are four concrete strategies.

---

## Strategy 1: Rebalance staffing and schedules around peak queues

### Targeted queues
- Registration
- Nurse Assessment
- Doctor Consultation
- Checkout

### Root cause addressed
- Resource bottlenecks and demand peaks

### What the analysis would show
- Queue buildup at specific times of day
- High utilization for certain staff/resources
- Certain stations consistently under-resourced during peak arrival windows

### Action
- Shift staff start times to match arrival peaks
- Add cross-trained floating staff for bottleneck stations
- Split high-demand roles by time block
- Reserve more capacity for known surge periods

### Expected impact
- Often reduces average wait in peak queues by **15–30%**
- Can reduce 90th percentile waits even more if surges are the cause

### Why this is data-driven
The event log can reveal:
- When queues start forming
- Which resources are overloaded
- How many patients are affected by each peak

---

## Strategy 2: Redesign appointment scheduling rules

### Targeted queues
- Registration
- Nurse Assessment
- Doctor Consultation
- Diagnostics

### Root cause addressed
- Arrival imbalance and batching

### What the analysis would show
- Certain time slots have disproportionately high waiting times
- New patients cause longer downstream delays than follow-ups
- Overbooking may create avoidable congestion

### Action
- Use differentiated booking templates:
  - Longer slots for new patients
  - Shorter slots for follow-ups
  - Protected urgent slots
- Stagger start times instead of clustering appointments on the hour
- Add buffer capacity after high-complexity cases
- Limit overbooking in already congested periods

### Expected impact
- **10–25% reduction** in average waiting time
- Better reduction in extreme delays if clustering is the issue
- Improved punctuality and predictability

### Why this is data-driven
Variants and time-slot analysis can identify:
- Which appointment types generate the most downstream congestion
- Which start times lead to worst queue growth

---

## Strategy 3: Introduce parallel processing and pre-work

### Targeted queues
- Waiting for doctor consultation
- Waiting for diagnostics
- Waiting for checkout

### Root cause addressed
- Sequential dependencies and avoidable idle time

### What the analysis would show
- Long gaps after registration or nurse assessment before doctor consultation
- Diagnostics are only ordered after consultation even when predictable
- Checkout waits because documents or billing are prepared late

### Action
- Perform pre-registration or pre-triage before arrival
- Have nurse collect standardized data in parallel with room assignment
- Pre-generate likely orders for common patient types
- Prepare discharge/check-out paperwork while patient is in final clinical step

### Expected impact
- Can reduce end-to-end visit time by **10–20%**
- Particularly effective for predictable pathways such as follow-up visits

### Why this is data-driven
Process mining shows:
- Where time is spent waiting between steps
- Which steps can safely be moved earlier or run in parallel
- Which variants benefit most from pre-processing

---

## Strategy 4: Specialty-specific routing and fast-track pathways

### Targeted queues
- Doctor Consultation
- Diagnostic tests
- Specialist Review

### Root cause addressed
- Mixed patient complexity and one-size-fits-all flow

### What the analysis would show
- New patients and complex cases are crowding the same queue as simpler follow-ups
- Urgent cases are disrupting standard flow
- Certain specialties have much higher variability than others

### Action
- Create fast-track lanes for low-complexity follow-ups
- Separate urgent flow from routine flow
- Split specialty pathways by complexity level
- Assign dedicated resources for high-volume specialties

### Expected impact
- **20–40% reduction** in waits for low-complexity follow-ups
- Better handling of urgent cases
- Less queue contamination from long, complex visits

### Why this is data-driven
Variant and segmentation analysis can show:
- Which cohorts are suitable for fast-track
- Which patients are causing the most queue distortion
- Where separate routing is justified

---

## Strategy 5: Add queue visibility and real-time orchestration

### Targeted queues
- All major queues, especially diagnostic and checkout bottlenecks

### Root cause addressed
- Poor coordination and lack of real-time control

### What the analysis would show
- Delays become visible only after patients have already waited too long

### Action
- Implement dashboards showing queue length and expected delay
- Trigger alerts when waiting exceeds thresholds
- Dynamically reassign staff to overloaded stations
- Prioritize patients based on urgency and wait duration

### Expected impact
- Faster reaction to demand spikes
- Lower extreme delays
- Improved patient communication and satisfaction

### Why this is data-driven
The same event log can support near-real-time monitoring and threshold-based intervention.

# 4) Consideration of Trade-offs and Constraints

Every optimization has trade-offs.

## 4.1 Possible negative side-effects

### Staffing changes
- Higher labor cost if more people are added
- Staff fatigue if workload is redistributed poorly
- Resistance from staff if schedules become less stable

### Scheduling redesign
- Shorter slots may increase clinical pressure
- More precise scheduling reduces flexibility
- Overly rigid templates may reduce patient access

### Fast-track pathways
- Risk of under-triaging complex patients
- Possible perception of unequal treatment
- Additional coordination complexity

### Parallelization
- More handoffs if done carelessly
- Potential loss of continuity
- Risk of duplication or missing information

### Real-time prioritization
- Can create fairness concerns
- May disadvantage non-urgent but long-waiting patients
- Requires reliable data and operational discipline

---

## 4.2 Balancing conflicting objectives

I would use a multi-objective approach with priorities such as:

1. **Patient safety and quality of care**
2. **Reduction in excessive waiting**
3. **Operational efficiency**
4. **Cost control**
5. **Staff workload and sustainability**

### Practical balancing methods
- Set service-level targets by patient category
  - e.g. urgent patients seen within X minutes
- Use differentiated queues rather than one universal queue
- Apply staffing changes only where data show sustained benefit
- Pilot interventions before full rollout
- Monitor for unintended bottleneck shifts

### Example
If adding a nurse reduces waits at registration but shifts congestion to doctors, the solution may be:
- partial staffing adjustment
- better triage
- faster pre-assessment
rather than simply adding more clerks

# 5) Measuring Success

## 5.1 KPIs to evaluate improvement

### Waiting time KPIs
- Average waiting time between key steps
- Median waiting time
- 90th percentile waiting time
- Maximum waiting time
- Percentage of patients waiting more than threshold

### Flow KPIs
- Total visit duration
- Time from arrival to registration completion
- Time from registration to doctor start
- Time from doctor completion to checkout completion
- Number of handoff delays

### Capacity and efficiency KPIs
- Resource utilization
- Queue length over time
- Throughput per hour/day
- Activity service time variability

### Experience and quality KPIs
- Patient satisfaction scores
- Complaint rate
- No-show or abandonment rate
- Revisit rate or escalation rate, if applicable

### Equity and fairness KPIs
- Wait times by patient type
- Wait times by urgency
- Wait times by specialty
- Wait times by day/time

This ensures improvements do not only help one group while hurting another.

---

## 5.2 Ongoing process monitoring

Using the same event-log structure, the clinic can establish continuous monitoring:

### Dashboards
- Real-time and weekly queue KPIs
- Bottleneck alerts when waiting exceeds thresholds
- Comparison of current vs baseline performance

### Control charts / trend monitoring
- Detect whether improvements are sustained
- Flag regressions early
- Separate normal variation from true deterioration

### Periodic re-mining
Every month or quarter:
- Recompute queue metrics
- Re-evaluate variants
- Check for new bottlenecks
- Validate whether interventions shifted congestion elsewhere

### Before-and-after evaluation
Compare:
- Baseline 6-month period
- Pilot period
- Post-implementation period

Use:
- Statistical significance testing where appropriate
- Stratified comparison by patient type and urgency
- Difference-in-differences if a pilot group exists

# Summary

A strong process-mining-based improvement program for this clinic would:

1. **Measure queue times precisely** using timestamps between activity completion and next activity start.
2. **Rank queues by impact**, not just by average delay, using frequency, percentiles, and patient-type impact.
3. **Diagnose root causes** through resource analysis, bottleneck analysis, variant analysis, and segmentation.
4. **Implement targeted interventions** such as staffing rebalancing, scheduling redesign, fast-track pathways, parallel pre-processing, and real-time queue control.
5. **Monitor success continuously** with KPIs tied to waiting time, throughput, quality, and fairness.
