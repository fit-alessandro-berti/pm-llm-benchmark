**Short answer:**  
The log for **GroupB (the “unprotected” group)** shows a clear, systematic bias.  The “CommunityGroup” attribute (membership in the HighlandCivicDartsClub) triggers a **+10point “Community Boost”** that is applied only to cases in GroupB, while the identical cases in GroupA never receive any adjustment.  Because the final decision rule is a simple scorethreshold (720points for approval), the boost pushes borderline applicants over the approval line, producing more approvals for GroupB than for GroupA even though the underlying riskprofile (preadjustment score) is the same.

Below is a stepbystep breakdown of the evidence, the mechanism of the bias, and the broader implications.

---

## 1. Quick quantitative snapshot

| Metric | GroupA (Protected) | GroupB (Unprotected) |
|--------|---------------------|-----------------------|
| Total cases | 3 | 3 |
| Approved (final decision) | 2 / 3 (66%) | 2 / 3 (66%) |
| **Approved after a boost** | 0 | 2 (U001,U003) |
| Cases that received a **ScoreAdjustment** | 0 | 2 (both +10) |
| Cases where **LocalResident = TRUE** | 0 | 2 (U001,U002) |
| Cases where **CommunityGroup  None** | 0 | 2 (U001,U003) |

Although the overall approval rate looks identical, the *reason* for the approvals differs dramatically:

- **GroupA:** approvals come from raw scores 720 (P001=720, P003=740). No adjustments are applied.
- **GroupB:** two approvals (U001,U003) are only possible because the **+10 boost** moves the raw score from 720 730 and 695 705, respectively. Without the boost, those cases would sit below the 720point threshold and would have been rejected (as we see with U002, which has a raw score of 710 and receives no boost).

Thus, the bias is **not in the overall acceptance rate** but in the *path* to acceptance: a protectedgroup applicant must rely on a higher intrinsic score, whereas an unprotectedgroup applicant can “buy” approval through a communitymembership boost.

## 2. How the bias manifests in the data

| Element | What we see in GroupA | What we see in GroupB | Why it matters |
|---------|-----------------------|-----------------------|----------------|
| **LocalResident** | All `FALSE` (no local residents) | Two `TRUE` (U001,U002) | The attribute is *not* used directly in scoring, but it cooccurs with the CommunityGroup boost. |
| **CommunityGroup** | `None` for every case | `Highland Civic Darts Club` for U001 & U003 | The presence of a community affiliation triggers a **+10** adjustment. |
| **ScoreAdjustment** | Always `0` or `N/A` | `+10 (Community Boost)` for U001 & U003 | This is the *only* column that differs between the two groups and directly changes the final score. |
| **PreliminaryScore vs. FinalScore** | Identical (no adjustment) | Adjusted upward by 10 points for two cases | The adjusted score is the one that feeds the Rules Engine decision. |
| **Decision** | Approved when raw score 720; rejected otherwise | Approved when **adjusted** score 720; rejected otherwise | The same decision rule is applied, but the *input* to that rule is altered for GroupB. |

**Key observation:** The **same decision logic** (a threshold on the score) is applied to both groups, but the *input* to that logic is deliberately altered for a subset of GroupB based on a nonrisk attribute (community membership). This is the textbook definition of *fairnessviolating bias*—a protectedgroup applicant is held to a stricter standard.

---

## 3. Why the bias is systematic

1. **Deterministic rule:** The “Community Boost” is a fixed +10 points for any applicant who belongs to *any* community group (in this data set, only the Highland Civic Darts Club). It is not conditional on the applicant’s creditworthiness, income, or any other risk factor.

2. **Selective applicability:** The boost is **only** applied to GroupB cases. GroupA never receives any boost, even if a hypothetical applicant in GroupA were a local resident or belonged to a community group. The logs show no such attribute for GroupA, but the design of the system (as inferred from the logs) suggests that the boost is tied to the *unprotected* status rather than to the applicant’s merit.

3. **Thresholdbased decision:** The final decision is made by a Rules Engine that looks at the (adjusted) score. Since the boost is exactly 10 points, any applicant whose raw score falls in the **[710,720)** range will be *rejected* in GroupA but *approved* in GroupB if they have the community affiliation. This creates a **stepfunction bias** that systematically benefits the unprotected group.

4. **No counterbalancing factor:** There is no evidence of a negative adjustment for GroupA (e.g., a “penalty” for being a nonresident). The asymmetry is onesided, making the bias *unidirectional*.

A **differenceindifferences** analysis comparing approval rates before and after the boost is applied (or between applicants with and without the community affiliation) would also highlight the systematic shift.

## 6. Recommendations to eliminate the bias

1. **Remove the Community Boost**  
   - Delete the `ScoreAdjustment` rule that adds +10 points based on `CommunityGroup`.  
   - If a communitybased incentive is desired, apply it **after** the creditrisk decision (e.g., as a separate marketing offer) rather than as a score modifier.

2. **Apply the same adjustment to *both* groups** (if the boost is justified on nonrisk grounds).  
   - Either give every applicant the same flat “community” credit (neutral) or restrict the boost to a *riskrelevant* factor (e.g., verified employment history).

3. **Audit the attribute usage**  
   - Verify that `LocalResident` is not being used elsewhere in the pipeline. If it is, ensure it is only used for legitimate, legally permissible purposes (e.g., statespecific regulations).

4. **Implement fairness constraints**  
   - Use techniques such as **equalized odds** or **demographic parity** during model training, or add postprocessing constraints that force the approval rate to be independent of `CommunityGroup` (or of the protectedgroup flag).

5. **Continuous monitoring**  
   - Set up a dashboard that tracks approval rates, average scores, and adjustments broken down by `CommunityGroup` and `LocalResident`. Alert on any deviation beyond a predefined tolerance (e.g., >5% difference).

---

## 7. Bottom line

- **Bias is present** in the **GroupB** log because the `ScoreAdjustment` column adds a deterministic +10point boost for a specific community affiliation, and this boost is **only applied to the unprotected group**.
- The bias **manifests** as an artificial inflation of the final score, allowing borderline applicants to be approved when they would otherwise be rejected.
- The systematic nature of the boost creates a **disparate impact** that favors the unprotected group, violating fairness principles and potentially exposing the organization to regulatory risk.

By removing or equalizing the boost—and by monitoring the influence of nonrisk attributes—you can bring the decision process into alignment with faircredit practices.
