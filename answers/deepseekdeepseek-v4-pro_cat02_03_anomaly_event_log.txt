The Procure-to-Pay (P2P) process follows a well-established sequence of controls designed to ensure that purchases are authorized, goods or services are received as expected, and payments are made only for valid, properly approved obligations. The standard flow is:

1. **Create Purchase Requisition** – Identification of need.
2. **Approve Purchase Requisition** – Authorization of the request.
3. **Issue Purchase Order** – Commitment to the supplier.
4. **Receive Goods** – Confirmation that goods/services were delivered.
5. **Vendor Invoice Received** – Supplier’s request for payment.
6. **Match Invoice to PO** – Validation of invoice against PO and goods receipt (three-way match).
7. **Release Payment** – Disbursement of funds.
8. **Close Case** – Finalization.

Deviations from this order often indicate breakdowns in internal controls, policy violations, or fraud risks. Below are the anomalies found in the event log.

---

### Anomaly 1: Case 1002 – Multiple Control Failures
**Observed sequence (abridged):**
- Issue Purchase Order (08:40) **before** Approve Purchase Requisition (next day 08:00)
- Release Payment (07:15) **before** Approve Purchase Requisition, Receive Goods, and Match Invoice to PO

**Why it is problematic:**
1. **Purchase Order issued without approved requisition** – This circumvents the segregation of duties. The person creating the PO can commit company funds without managerial approval, enabling maverick spending or fraudulent purchases.
2. **Payment released before goods receipt and invoice matching** – The three-way match (PO, goods receipt, invoice) is a fundamental anti-fraud control. By skipping it, the organization pays on an invoice without verifying that goods were actually received or that the price/quantity are correct. This creates a high risk of paying for undelivered goods, duplicate invoices, or inflated charges.
3. **Match performed after payment** – Doing the validation retrospectively defeats its purpose; discrepancies discovered later would require costly recovery efforts and signal a reactive rather than preventive control posture.

This case shows a complete breakdown of the standard P2P controls.

---

### Anomaly 2: Case 1003 – Payment Before Receipt and Match
**Observed sequence (abridged):**
- Release Payment (02-08) **before** Vendor Invoice Received (02-09), Receive Goods (02-10), and Match Invoice to PO (02-11)

**Why it is problematic:**
- **Payment released without receiving an invoice** is extremely unusual. In standard P2P, the invoice triggers the payment process. Paying before any invoice exists suggests either an anticipatory payment (which is rare and high-risk) or a process where the payment is triggered without proper documentation.
- **No goods receipt before payment** means the company pays without confirming delivery. This exposes the organization to risks such as non-delivery, damaged goods, or partial shipments.
- **No matching step before payment** eliminates the opportunity to detect pricing errors, duplicate billings, or quantity mismatches. Any errors found later through matching become after-the-fact corrections, which are more difficult and expensive to resolve.

This sequence is inconsistent with sound financial governance and exposes the organization to significant financial and fraud risk.

### Anomaly 3: Case 1004 – Goods Received Before Purchase Order
**Observed sequence (abridged):**
- Receive Goods (02-06 09:00) **before** Issue Purchase Order (02-06 09:15)

**Why it is problematic:**
- **Accepting goods without a purchase order** undermines the commitment control. A PO serves as a legal offer and establishes the terms (price, quantity, delivery date). Receiving goods without a PO means the organization has no prior contractual agreement and no established price baseline, which can lead to disputes, overpayments, or acceptance of unauthorized items.
- It indicates a break in the "no PO, no pay" policy. Without a PO, the accounts payable team later has nothing to match the invoice against, which can cause delays, unmatched invoices, and potential duplicate payments.
- This pattern is often seen in “maverick buying” where employees bypass procurement procedures, leading to fragmented spend, loss of volume discounts, and weakened spend controls.

Although the case eventually issues a PO after the fact and completes the remaining steps normally, the violation of sequence creates operational risk and signals a lack of process discipline.
