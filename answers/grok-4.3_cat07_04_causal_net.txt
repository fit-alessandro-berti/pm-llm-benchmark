### Main Diagram (Left)
This is a labeled workflow net with:
- **Transitions** (white squares labeled a–h and z) representing activities or tasks.
- **Places** (black circles) representing states or conditions.
- Directed arcs showing the flow of control (tokens).

**Process flow**:
- **Start**: Transition **a** ("register request").
- After registration, the process can execute **b** ("examine thoroughly"), **c** ("examine casually"), and/or **d** ("check ticket") in various combinations (showing choice and possible parallelism).
- These converge into transition **e** ("decide").
- From the decision point **e**, there is an exclusive choice:
  - **g** ("pay compensation")  **z** ("end"), or
  - **h** ("reject request")  **z** ("end").
- Transition **f** ("reinitiate request") creates a loop, allowing the process to return to earlier stages (e.g., re-examination or re-registration) instead of ending.

The net contains multiple choice points, loops, and synchronization points, which are common in real-world processes like insurance claims or service requests.

### Reference Box (Bottom Right)
This shows the six fundamental routing patterns used in workflow modeling:

**Splits** (top row):
- **XOR-split**: Exclusive choice (exactly one outgoing path).
- **AND-split**: Parallel split (all outgoing paths executed).
- **OR-split**: Inclusive choice (one or more outgoing paths).

**Joins** (bottom row):
- **XOR-join**: Exclusive merge (proceeds when one incoming path completes).
- **AND-join**: Synchronization (waits for all incoming paths).
- **OR-join**: Inclusive merge (proceeds when at least one relevant path completes).

These patterns explain how the arcs and connections in the main diagram implement choice, parallelism, and synchronization.
