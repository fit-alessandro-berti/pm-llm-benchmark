**Analysis of the Procure-to-Pay (P2P) Event Log**

I have examined all four cases against standard P2P best practices. In a typical compliant P2P process the logical and control sequence is approximately:

1. Create Purchase Requisition  
2. Approve Purchase Requisition  
3. Issue Purchase Order (only after approval)  
4. Receive Goods / Services  
5. Receive Vendor Invoice  
6. Match Invoice to PO & Goods Receipt (3-way match)  
7. Release / Execute Payment  
8. Close Case

Below are the clear anomalies identified in the log.

### Case 1002 – Multiple Serious Violations

**Anomalous sequence:**
- Create PR  **Issue PO**  Vendor Invoice Received  **Release Payment**  Approve PR  Receive Goods  Match Invoice to PO  Close

**Problems:**
- **Purchase Order issued before PR approval** — This is a major internal-control violation. The PO should never be sent to a vendor without proper approval.
- **Payment released before goods are received and before the invoice is matched** — The company paid the vendor on 2024-02-03, but goods were not received until 2024-02-04 and matching occurred even later. This creates significant risk of paying for goods that were never delivered or for incorrect amounts.
- **Approval of the requisition after the PO has already been issued and after payment** — The approval step is essentially meaningless at this point; it is a “rubber-stamp” after the fact.

**Risks:** Fraud, maverick buying, payment for non-delivered goods, loss of financial control, possible regulatory or audit issues.

---

### Case 1003 – Severe Timing Violations

**Anomalous sequence:**
- Create PR  Approve PR  Issue PO  **Release Payment**  Vendor Invoice Received  Receive Goods  Match Invoice to PO

**Problems:**
- **Payment released eight days before the vendor invoice was even received** and six days before goods were received.
- The 3-way match (PO–Goods–Invoice) is performed *after* the payment has already been made.

**Risks:** Paying without an invoice and without confirmation of receipt of goods is highly irregular and exposes the company to duplicate payments, overpayments, or paying for undelivered items. This is the opposite of standard “pay only after verification” controls.

---

### Case 1004 – Wrong Process Order (Goods before PO)

**Anomalous sequence:**
- Create PR  Approve PR  **Receive Goods**  Issue PO  Vendor Invoice Received  Match  Payment

**Problems:**
- Goods are recorded as received *before* a Purchase Order has been issued. In standard P2P, you cannot legitimately receive goods against a non-existent PO.
- The PO is created retroactively after the goods have already arrived.

**Risks:** This suggests either:
- An unapproved/maverick purchase that was later “regularized,” or
- A process breakdown where receiving happened outside the formal procurement channel.

This defeats the purpose of having a PO as a commitment and control document.

---

### Summary of Anomalies

| Case | Anomaly | Why Problematic |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| 1002 | PO issued before PR approval; Payment before goods receipt and matching | Violates approval controls and 3-way match; high risk of fraud and erroneous payment |
| 1003 | Payment released before invoice received and before goods received | Extremely poor cash control; pays without verification |
| 1004 | Goods received before PO issued | Defeats the PO as a commitment/control mechanism; indicates possible maverick buying |

**Cases 1002, 1003, and 1004 all deviate significantly from standard P2P best practices.** Only Case 1001 follows a normal, logical, and control-compliant sequence.

These anomalies would normally trigger investigation by internal audit, process mining alerts, or fraud-detection systems, as they increase financial risk, weaken segregation of duties, and undermine the integrity of the procure-to-pay process.
