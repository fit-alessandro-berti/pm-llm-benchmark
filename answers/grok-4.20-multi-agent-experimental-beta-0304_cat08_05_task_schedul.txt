**1. Analyzing Historical Scheduling Performance and Dynamics**

Process mining on the MES event logs begins with **event log preparation**. The provided log format already contains the necessary attributes: Case ID (JOB-), Activity (e.g., "Queue Entry (Cutting)", "Setup Start", "Task Start", "Task End", "Job Released", breakdowns, priority changes), Resource (Machine ID), Timestamp, and enriched attributes (Order Priority, Order Due Date, Setup Required, Planned vs. Actual Duration, Notes/previous job). I would filter for complete cases, handle timestamp precision, derive additional attributes (e.g., job type from routing patterns, similarity features for setups based on material/geometry inferred from task sequences or job metadata), and create a unified log distinguishing job-level events from task-level and resource events.

**Process discovery** techniques would reconstruct the actual flows:
- Use the Inductive Miner or Directly-Follows Graph (DFG) with frequency thresholds to discover the highly variable routing network, revealing common vs. rare job paths in this high-mix environment.
- For machine-specific views, project the log onto each resource to discover the *execution sequence* of tasks on that machine, highlighting the order in which different jobs are processed.

**Performance analysis** (replay-based) would quantify key metrics:
- **Job flow times, lead times, and makespan**: Replay events to calculate end-to-end flow time (Job Released to final completion), throughput time per job, and shop makespan. Derive distributions (histograms, percentiles) and correlate with job characteristics (priority, routing complexity). Lead time reliability can be measured via the "punctuality" metric (percentage of jobs within a promised window).
- **Task waiting times**: Identify "Queue Entry" to "Setup Start" intervals per work center/machine. Use dotted chart analysis or performance dashboard in tools like PM4Py or Celonis to visualize queue time distributions and identify congestion points over time.
- **Resource utilization**: Calculate busy time (Setup + Processing), idle time, and setup proportion from timestamps. Utilization = (productive + setup time) / (available time, accounting for shifts and breakdowns). Identify starvation (long idle periods following specific patterns) and overload.
- **Sequence-dependent setup times**: For each machine, extract consecutive task pairs. Use the "Notes" or derived "previous job" attribute to group setups by transition type (e.g., from JOB type A to B, or based on features like material family, tolerance level, or size bucket mined from historical patterns). Fit statistical models (regression trees or simple lookup tables) showing setup duration as a function of previous-to-current job similarity. Quantify average and variance of setups for frequent vs. rare sequences; this reveals the cost of poor sequencing.
- **Schedule adherence and tardiness**: Compute completion time vs. Due Date for each case. Metrics include mean/median tardiness, percentage of tardy jobs, maximum tardiness, and service level (e.g., % on-time). Use conformance checking (alignments or token-based replay) against a "planned" model derived from due dates and expected routings.
- **Impact of disruptions**: Filter or annotate logs for breakdown events ("Breakdown Start/End") and priority changes. Use case correlation or time-window analysis to measure downstream effects珻.g., increase in queue times or tardiness in the subsequent hours/days, ripple effects on specific machines. Causal process mining or interval analysis can quantify delay propagation.

These analyses provide a factual, quantitative baseline of the *as-is* scheduling dynamics rather than the assumed behavior of the current dispatching rules.

**2. Diagnosing Scheduling Pathologies**

Process mining will surface several interconnected pathologies from the current local dispatching approach (FCFS + EDD mix):

- **Bottleneck resources**: Bottleneck analysis (e.g., waiting time aggregation per resource, or the "critical path" in replayed cases) will identify machines with disproportionately high queue times and low utilization elsewhere. Impact is quantified by how much overall shop throughput is limited by these (e.g., using the "influence on flow time" metric). Specialized machines with long setups will show up clearly.
- **Poor task prioritization**: Variant analysis comparing "on-time" vs. "late" jobs (using case clustering or decision tree analysis on attributes like priority, due date slack). High-priority "hot jobs" may still experience significant waiting if lower-priority jobs are not preempted effectively or if they arrive into already congested queues. Frequency of priority changes and their success rate in reducing tardiness can be measured.
- **Suboptimal sequencing and setup waste**: Sequence pattern analysis on machines will reveal frequent high-setup transitions that could have been avoided. Calculate the "setup inflation factor" (actual total setup time vs. minimum possible if optimally sequenced using historical setup matrix).
- **Starvation and poor coordination**: Upstream bottlenecks or bad release decisions cause downstream resources to show long idle periods despite high overall WIP. Dotted charts or resource utilization timelines correlated across machines will reveal these.
- **Bullwhip/WIP variability**: Analyze WIP levels over time (number of open cases in queue or in-process) and their correlation with release patterns and disruptions. High variance in WIP indicates reactive "expediting" behavior.

Techniques like **bottleneck analysis**, **variant analysis**, **root cause analysis via decision trees** on case attributes (why certain variants are late), and **social network/resource interaction analysis** provide strong visual and statistical evidence. For example, replaying only late jobs may show they disproportionately wait at specific machines after certain disruption patterns.

**3. Root Cause Analysis of Scheduling Ineffectiveness**

Process mining helps distinguish **scheduling logic problems** from **capacity or variability issues**:

- **Limitations of static dispatching rules**: Local rules (FCFS/EDD) ignore global state (downstream load, sequence-dependent setups, current WIP). Conformance checking against the discovered model vs. a simulated "ideal rule" execution can highlight deviation points. Variant analysis often shows that rule performance degrades precisely during high-load or disruption periods.
- **Lack of real-time visibility and global view**: High queue times uncorrelated with machine utilization elsewhere indicate lack of load balancing. Process mining can show "information silos" where decisions at one center ignore others.
- **Inaccurate estimations**: Compare Planned vs. Actual durations across contexts (operator, job features, previous task). High variance or systematic bias (e.g., underestimation on certain machines) points to poor data-driven estimation. Setup times are likely treated as constants rather than sequence-dependent.
- **Ineffective disruption handling**: Analyze cases immediately following breakdowns or hot job insertions. If tardiness spikes disproportionately and recovery is slow, it indicates no robust rescheduling or lookahead.
- **Poor coordination**: Low synchronization between work centers (e.g., jobs arriving when downstream is overloaded) visible in cross-resource waiting patterns.

Process mining differentiates causes by:
- Using **performance spectrum analysis** or **time-based filtering** to isolate periods of high variability vs. steady state.
- **Correlation analysis** between external factors (load, disruption frequency) and KPIs.
- **Comparative process mining** (e.g., models before/after certain policy changes or across job types) to isolate whether the issue is rule-based or due to inherent low capacity/high variability. If capacity is the issue, utilization will be very high on bottlenecks with little idle time; if logic is the issue, there will be avoidable idle time, high setup waste, and poor prioritization even at moderate loads.

**4. Developing Advanced Data-Driven Scheduling Strategies**

**Strategy 1: Multi-Criteria Dynamic Dispatching Rules (Enhanced Priority Indexing)**  
Core logic: At each machine, when free, calculate a dynamic priority score for queued jobs: Score = w1*(Due Date Slack / Remaining Processing Time) + w2*(1 / Estimated Setup Time with previous job) + w3*Priority Factor + w4*Downstream Queue Penalty. Weights are tuned via historical data or simulation. Estimated setup time is looked up from the process-mined transition matrix based on the last completed job's characteristics.  
Process mining informs this by providing the setup matrix, remaining time distributions, and identifying which factors most discriminate late vs. on-time jobs (via decision trees).  
It addresses poor prioritization, suboptimal sequencing, and lack of global view. Expected impact: Reduced tardiness (especially for near-due jobs), lower total setup time, and moderated WIP by considering downstream load. 

**Strategy 2: Predictive Stochastic Scheduling with Lookahead**  
Core logic: Use process-mined task duration distributions (contextualized by job features, operator, machine state) and breakdown frequency models (time-between-failures and repair times derived from resource events) to generate probabilistic schedules or use simulation-based lookahead for dispatching. A rolling-horizon approach re-optimizes periodically or upon events (breakdown, new hot job). Predictive maintenance insights (early warning from patterns preceding breakdowns) can trigger proactive rescheduling.  
Process mining supplies the distributions, routing probabilities, and disruption models. It directly tackles inaccurate estimations and poor disruption response. Expected impact: More realistic due date promises, proactive bottleneck avoidance, lower tardiness variance, and improved lead time predictability.

**Strategy 3: Sequence-Optimization via Similarity-Based Batching/Sequencing at Bottlenecks**  
Core logic: For identified bottleneck machines, use an optimization heuristic (e.g., genetic algorithm or dispatching rule biased toward "similarity") that minimizes setup times by sequencing similar jobs (grouped by features mined from historical setup patterns珻.g., material family, geometry class). Allow controlled batching of compatible jobs while respecting due dates via a composite objective (setup minimization + weighted tardiness). Non-bottleneck machines use simpler rules.  
Informed by the sequence-dependent setup analysis and bottleneck identification from process mining. Addresses setup waste and bottleneck inefficiency. Expected impact: Significant reduction in non-productive setup time on critical resources, higher effective capacity, reduced overall lead times and WIP.

**5. Simulation, Evaluation, and Continuous Improvement**

**Discrete-event simulation (DES)** is essential for offline testing. Parameterize a high-fidelity model (using tools like SimPy, AnyLogic, or commercial manufacturing simulators) with:
- Routing probabilities and job arrival patterns from process mining.
- Task and setup time distributions (including sequence-dependence) fitted from the log.
- Resource capacities, breakdown/repair distributions, and operator availability.
- Current vs. proposed dispatching logic.

Test scenarios include: baseline load, high-load periods, increased disruption frequency, introduction of new job types, and varying hot job arrival rates. Compare KPIs (mean tardiness, % tardy jobs, average WIP, lead time std. dev., resource utilization, total setup time) across the current rules and the three proposed strategies. Use statistical experimental design (replications, ANOVA) for rigorous comparison. This quantifies expected gains and identifies robustness before deployment.

**Continuous monitoring framework**: Implement an ongoing process mining pipeline (daily/weekly) on streaming MES data:
- Track the same KPIs as in the initial analysis, with control charts for drift detection (e.g., in flow times, setup efficiencies, tardiness).
- Use concept drift detection techniques in process mining to identify when the process or performance changes significantly (new job mix, machine behavior, policy effects).
- Automated alerts for emerging bottlenecks or deteriorating prioritization effectiveness.
- Periodic re-mining of setup matrices, duration distributions, and bottleneck rankings to adapt rule weights or optimization models.
- Feedback loop: Log the decisions made by new strategies and mine their actual performance to refine (e.g., update weights in the dynamic rule via reinforcement learning elements or periodic re-optimization).

This creates a closed-loop system where process mining continuously validates and evolves the scheduling strategies, ensuring sustained improvement in tardiness, WIP, lead time predictability, and resource efficiency in the face of the job shop's inherent complexity and disruptions.
