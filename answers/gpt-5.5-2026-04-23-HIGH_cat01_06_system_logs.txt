### Grouping and Transformation Logic

- **Case identification:** I grouped events by the primary work object being handled:
  - `DOC-Document1-20241211` for editing `Document1.docx`.
  - `EMAIL-AnnualMeeting-20241211` for reading and replying to the Annual Meeting email.
  - `PDF-ReportDraft-20241211` for reviewing and annotating `Report_Draft.pdf`.
  - `XLS-Budget2024-20241211` for updating `Budget_2024.xlsx`.
  - `DOC-QuarterlyReport-20241211` for editing `Quarterly_Report.docx`.

- **Activity naming:** Low-level actions were converted into higher-level process activities:
  - `FOCUS` and target-side `SWITCH` events became activities such as **Activate Document**, **Access Email Inbox**, **Activate PDF**, or **Activate Workbook**.
  - `TYPING` became **Edit Document**, **Compose Email Reply**, or **Update Workbook**, depending on the application and object.
  - `SCROLL` became **Read Email** or **Review PDF**.
  - `CLICK` actions were interpreted using their action descriptions, e.g. **Open Email**, **Start Email Reply**, and **Send Email**.
  - `SAVE`, `CLOSE`, and `HIGHLIGHT` were mapped directly to meaningful activities such as **Save Document**, **Close Document**, and **Annotate PDF**.

- **Interleaving of work:** The event log preserves the fact that the user switched between multiple work items. For example, the `Document1.docx` case is interrupted by email handling, PDF review, and budget workbook updates, then resumed at `09:06:00` to insert a budget reference. This produces an event log that can be analyzed both by individual case and by overall user multitasking behavior.
